<p><b>The Boeing Company</b>, commonly known as <b>Boeing</b> (<span class="rt-commentedText nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'b' in 'buy'">b</span><span title="/oʊ/: 'o' in 'code'">oʊ</span><span title="/ɪ/: 'i' in 'kit'">ɪ</span><span title="/ŋ/: 'ng' in 'sing'">ŋ</span></span>/</a></span></span>), is an American <a href="/wiki/Multinational_corporation" title="Multinational corporation">multinational corporation</a> that designs, manufactures, and sells <a href="/wiki/Airplane" title="Airplane">airplanes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rotorcraft" title="Rotorcraft">rotorcraft</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rocket" title="Rocket">rockets</a>, <a href="/wiki/Satellite" title="Satellite">satellites</a>, <a href="/wiki/Telecommunications_equipment" title="Telecommunications equipment">telecommunications equipment</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Missile" title="Missile">missiles</a> worldwide. The company also provides leasing and product support services. Boeing is among the largest global <a href="/wiki/Aerospace_manufacturer" title="Aerospace manufacturer">aerospace manufacturers</a>; it is the second-largest <a href="/wiki/List_of_defense_contractors" title="List of defense contractors">defense contractor</a> in the world based on 2018 revenue,<sup id="cite_ref-DefenseNews_Top_100_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DefenseNews_Top_100-6">[6]</a></sup> and is the largest exporter in the United States by dollar value.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Boeing stock is included in the <a href="/wiki/Dow_Jones_Industrial_Average" title="Dow Jones Industrial Average">Dow Jones Industrial Average</a>. In 2017, Boeing recorded <span style="white-space: nowrap">US$</span>93.3<span class="nowrap"> </span>billion in sales, ranked 24th on the <i><a href="/wiki/Fortune_(magazine)" title="Fortune (magazine)">Fortune</a></i> magazine "Fortune 500" list (2018),<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> ranked 64th on the "<a href="/wiki/Fortune_Global_500" title="Fortune Global 500">Fortune Global 500</a>" list (2018),<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> and ranked 19th on the "World's Most Admired Companies" list (2018).<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> In 2019, Boeing's global reputation, commercial business, and financial rating suffered after the <a href="/wiki/Boeing_737_MAX_groundings" title="Boeing 737 MAX groundings">737 MAX was grounded</a> worldwide following two fatal crashes in <a href="/wiki/Lion_Air_Flight_610" title="Lion Air Flight 610">late 2018</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ethiopian_Airlines_Flight_302" title="Ethiopian Airlines Flight 302">early 2019</a>.

</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(1)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Boeing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History" data-section="1" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-1 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-1">
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/History_of_Boeing" title="History of Boeing">History of Boeing</a></div>
<p>The Boeing Company was started in 1916, when American lumber industrialist <a href="/wiki/William_E._Boeing" title="William E. Boeing">William E. Boeing</a> founded Aero Products Company in Seattle, Washington. Shortly before doing so, he and Conrad Westervelt created the <a href="/wiki/Boeing_Model_1" title="Boeing Model 1">"B&amp;W" seaplane</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-:1_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-14">[14]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> In 1917, the organization was renamed Boeing Airplane Company, with William Boeing forming Boeing Airplane &amp; Transport Corporation in 1928.<sup id="cite_ref-:1_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-14">[14]</a></sup> In 1929, the company was renamed <a href="/wiki/United_Aircraft_and_Transport_Corporation" title="United Aircraft and Transport Corporation">United Aircraft and Transport Corporation</a>, followed by the acquisition of several aircraft makers such as Avion, Chance Vought, Sikorsky Aviation, Stearman Aircraft, <a href="/wiki/Pratt_%26_Whitney" title="Pratt &amp; Whitney">Pratt &amp; Whitney</a>, and Hamilton Metalplane.<sup id="cite_ref-Britannica_Boe_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Britannica_Boe-2">[2]</a></sup></p><p>In 1931, the group merged its four smaller airlines into <a href="/wiki/United_Airlines" title="United Airlines">United Airlines</a>. In 1934, the manufacture of aircraft was required to be separate from air transportation.<sup id="cite_ref-:2_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:2-16">[16]</a></sup> Therefore, Boeing Airplane Company became one of three organizations to arise from dissolution of United Aircraft and Transport; the other two groups were <a href="/wiki/United_Aircraft" title="United Aircraft">United Aircraft</a> (now <a href="/wiki/United_Technologies" title="United Technologies">United Technologies</a>) and United Airlines.<sup id="cite_ref-Britannica_Boe_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Britannica_Boe-2">[2]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:2_16-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:2-16">[16]</a></sup></p><p>In 1960, the company bought Vertol Corporation, which at the time, was the biggest independent fabricator of <a href="/wiki/Helicopter" title="Helicopter">helicopters</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> During the 1960s and 1970s, the company diversified into industries such as outer space travel, marine craft, agriculture, energy production and transit systems.<sup id="cite_ref-Britannica_Boe_2-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Britannica_Boe-2">[2]</a></sup></p><p>In 1995, Boeing partnered with Russian, Ukrainian and Anglo-Norwegian organizations to create Sea Launch, a company providing commercial launch services sending satellites to geostationary orbit from floating platforms.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> In 2000, Boeing acquired the satellite segment of <a href="/wiki/Hughes_Electronics" title="Hughes Electronics">Hughes Electronics</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Britannica_Boe_2-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Britannica_Boe-2">[2]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p><p>Corporate headquarters were moved from Seattle to Chicago in 2001.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p><p>After two fatal crashes of the <a href="/wiki/Boeing_737_MAX" title="Boeing 737 MAX">Boeing 737 MAX</a> narrow-body passenger airplanes in 2018 and 2019, aviation regulators and airlines around the world <a href="/wiki/Boeing_737_MAX_groundings" title="Boeing 737 MAX groundings">grounded all 737 MAX</a> airliners.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> A total of 387 aircraft were grounded.<sup id="cite_ref-:10_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:10-22">[22]</a></sup> Boeing's reputation, business, and financial rating has suffered after these groundings, questioning Boeing's strategy, governance, and focus on profits and cost efficiency.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> The Wall Street Journal reported on May 5, 2019 that Boeing had known of the issue with the system for "about a year" before the crash in Indonesia.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> In December 2019, Boeing announced it will suspend 737 MAX production from January 2020.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> Soon after, on December 23, then CEO Dennis Muilenburg resigned and was replaced by <a href="/wiki/David_Calhoun" class="mw-redirect" title="David Calhoun">David Calhoun</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(2)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Divisions">Divisions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Boeing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Divisions" data-section="2" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-2 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-2">
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Ridley_Park_PA_Boeing.JPG" class="image"><noscript><img alt="Boeing plant in Ridley Park, Pennsylvania – a building with aluminum siding, parking lot in front, and a flagpole with seven flags" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9f/Ridley_Park_PA_Boeing.JPG/220px-Ridley_Park_PA_Boeing.JPG" decoding="async" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="3860" data-file-height="2573"></noscript><span class="lazy-image-placeholder" style="width: 220px;height: 147px;" data-src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9f/Ridley_Park_PA_Boeing.JPG/220px-Ridley_Park_PA_Boeing.JPG" data-alt="Boeing plant in Ridley Park, Pennsylvania – a building with aluminum siding, parking lot in front, and a flagpole with seven flags" data-width="220" data-height="147" data-class="thumbimage"> </span></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Ridley_Park_PA_Boeing.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Boeing plant in <a href="/wiki/Ridley_Park,_Pennsylvania" title="Ridley Park, Pennsylvania">Ridley Park, Pennsylvania</a></div></div></div>
<p>The corporation's three main divisions are Boeing Commercial Airplanes (BCA), Boeing Defense, Space &amp; Security (BDS), and Boeing Global Services.<sup id="cite_ref-Brief_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brief-29">[29]</a></sup></p>

<p>In 2006, the <a href="/wiki/UCLA" class="mw-redirect" title="UCLA">UCLA</a> Center for Environmental Risk Reduction released a study showing that Boeing's <a href="/wiki/Santa_Susana_Field_Laboratory" title="Santa Susana Field Laboratory">Santa Susana Field Laboratory</a>, a site that was a former Rocketdyne test and development site in the <a href="/wiki/Simi_Hills" title="Simi Hills">Simi Hills</a> of eastern <a href="/wiki/Ventura_County" class="mw-redirect" title="Ventura County">Ventura County</a> in <a href="/wiki/Southern_California" title="Southern California">Southern California</a>, had been contaminated by Rocketdyne with <a href="/wiki/Toxic" class="mw-redirect" title="Toxic">toxic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Radioactive_waste" title="Radioactive waste">radioactive waste</a>. Boeing agreed to a cleanup agreement with the EPA in 2017.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> Clean up studies and lawsuits are in progress.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Jet_biofuels">Jet biofuels</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Boeing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Jet biofuels" data-section="4" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Aviation_biofuel" title="Aviation biofuel">Aviation biofuel</a> and <a href="/wiki/Algae_fuel" title="Algae fuel">Algae fuel</a></div>
<p>The airline industry is responsible for about 11% of <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">greenhouse gases</a> emitted by the U.S. transportation sector.<sup id="cite_ref-boe_energy_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-boe_energy-32">[32]</a></sup> Aviation's share of the greenhouse gas emissions was poised to grow, as air travel increases and ground vehicles use more alternative fuels like ethanol and biodiesel.<sup id="cite_ref-boe_energy_32-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-boe_energy-32">[32]</a></sup> Boeing estimates that biofuels could reduce flight-related greenhouse-gas emissions by 60 to 80%.<sup id="cite_ref-boe_energy_32-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-boe_energy-32">[32]</a></sup> The solution blends algae fuels with existing <a href="/wiki/Jet_fuel" title="Jet fuel">jet fuel</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-boe_energy_32-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-boe_energy-32">[32]</a></sup></p><p>Boeing executives said the company was collaborating with Brazilian biofuels maker <a href="/wiki/Tecbio" class="mw-redirect" title="Tecbio">Tecbio</a>, <a href="/wiki/Aquaflow_Bionomic" class="mw-redirect" title="Aquaflow Bionomic">Aquaflow Bionomic</a> of New Zealand and other fuel developers around the world. As of 2007, Boeing had tested six fuels from these companies, and expected to test 20 fuels "by the time we're done evaluating them".<sup id="cite_ref-boe_energy_32-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-boe_energy-32">[32]</a></sup> Boeing also joined other aviation-related members in the <a href="/wiki/Algal_Biomass_Organization" class="mw-redirect" title="Algal Biomass Organization">Algal Biomass Organization</a> (ABO) in June 2008.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p><p><a href="/wiki/Air_New_Zealand" title="Air New Zealand">Air New Zealand</a> and Boeing are researching the <a href="/wiki/Jatropha" title="Jatropha">jatropha</a> plant to see if it is a sustainable alternative to conventional fuel.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> A two-hour test flight using a 50–50 mixture of the new biofuel with <a href="/wiki/Jet_fuel" title="Jet fuel">Jet A-1</a> in a Rolls Royce RB-211 engine of a 747-400 was completed on December 30, 2008.<sup id="cite_ref-:3_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:3-35">[35]</a></sup> The engine was then removed to be studied to identify any differences between the Jatropha blend and regular Jet A1. No effects on performances were found.<sup id="cite_ref-:3_35-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:3-35">[35]</a></sup></p><p>On August 31, 2010, Boeing worked with the U.S. Air Force to test the <a href="/wiki/Boeing_C-17" class="mw-redirect" title="Boeing C-17">Boeing C-17</a> running on 50% JP-8, 25% Hydro-treated Renewable Jet fuel and 25% of a Fischer–Tropsch fuel with successful results.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Electric_propulsion">Electric propulsion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Boeing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Electric propulsion" data-section="5" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>For NASA's N+3 future airliner program, Boeing has determined that <a href="/wiki/Hybrid_electric_vehicle" title="Hybrid electric vehicle">hybrid electric engine technology</a> is by far the best choice for its subsonic design. Hybrid electric propulsion has the potential to shorten takeoff distance and reduce noise.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
