</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(2)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=PVH_(company)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History" data-section="2" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-2 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-2">
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Trade_names_and_early_mergers">Trade names and early mergers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=PVH_(company)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Trade names and early mergers" data-section="3" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>The history of Phillips-Van Heusen (PVH) goes back in part to Dramin Jones, a <a href="/wiki/Prussia" title="Prussia">Prussian</a> immigrant who founded the shirt manufacturing company D. Jones &amp; Sons, c. 1857.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Separately, in 1881, Moses Phillips and his wife Endel began sewing shirts by hand and selling them from pushcarts to local anthracite coal-miners in <a href="/wiki/Pottsville,_Pennsylvania" title="Pottsville, Pennsylvania">Pottsville, Pennsylvania</a>. This grew into a shirt business in New York City that placed one of the first ever shirt advertisements in the <i><a href="/wiki/Saturday_Evening_Post" class="mw-redirect" title="Saturday Evening Post">Saturday Evening Post</a></i>. D. Jones &amp; Sons merged with M. Phillips &amp; Sons in 1907 under the name Phillips-Jones<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> after Dramin Jones's death in 1903. Later Isaac Phillips met John Van Heusen, resulting both in their most popular line of shirts (Van Heusen), and in the subsequent acquisition of Van Heusen by Phillips-Jones and its renaming to Phillips-Van Heusen in 1957.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> In 2011, Phillips-Van Heusen is renamed to PVH.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Acquisitions">Acquisitions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=PVH_(company)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Acquisitions" data-section="4" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>The Phillips-Jones Corporation received a patent for a self-folding collar in 1919; the corporation released the product to the public in 1921 and it became successful. The first collar-attached shirt was introduced in 1929. The Bass Weejun was introduced in 1936. Geoffrey Beene shirts were launched in 1982. In 1987, Phillips-Van Heusen acquired G.H. Bass. In 1995, the corporation acquired the <a href="/wiki/Izod" title="Izod">Izod</a> brand, followed by the Arrow brand in 2000, and the <a href="/wiki/Calvin_Klein" title="Calvin Klein">Calvin Klein</a> company in 2002.<sup id="cite_ref-Phillips-Van_Heusen_Company_History_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Phillips-Van_Heusen_Company_History-10">[10]</a></sup> In 2004, PVH began manufacturing clothing for the Donald J. Trump Signature Collection as part of a licensing agreement with <a href="/wiki/Donald_Trump" title="Donald Trump">Donald Trump</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Trump_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Trump-11">[11]</a></sup></p><p>After acquiring Superba, Inc., in January 2007, PVH now owns <a href="/wiki/Necktie" title="Necktie">necktie</a> licenses for brands such as Arrow, <a href="/wiki/DKNY" title="DKNY">DKNY</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tommy_Hilfiger_Licensing" class="mw-redirect" title="Tommy Hilfiger Licensing">Tommy Hilfiger</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nautica_(clothing_company)" title="Nautica (clothing company)">Nautica</a>, <a href="/wiki/Perry_Ellis" title="Perry Ellis">Perry Ellis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ted_Baker" title="Ted Baker">Ted Baker</a>, <a href="/wiki/Michael_Kors" title="Michael Kors">Michael Kors</a>, <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Abboud" title="Joseph Abboud">JOE Joseph Abboud</a>, <a href="/wiki/Original_Penguin" title="Original Penguin">Original Penguin</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jones_Apparel_Group" class="mw-redirect" title="Jones Apparel Group">Jones New York</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Phillips-Van_Heusen_Corp_PVH_(NYSE)_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Phillips-Van_Heusen_Corp_PVH_(NYSE)-12">[12]</a></sup> The corporation began making men's clothing under the <a href="/wiki/The_Timberland_Company" title="The Timberland Company">Timberland</a> name in 2008, with women's clothing following in 2009, under a licensing agreement.<sup id="cite_ref-Foolish_Forecast:_Phillips-Van_Heusen_Buttons_Up_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Foolish_Forecast:_Phillips-Van_Heusen_Buttons_Up-13">[13]</a></sup></p><p>On March 15, 2010, Phillips-Van Heusen acquired <a href="/wiki/Tommy_Hilfiger_Licensing" class="mw-redirect" title="Tommy Hilfiger Licensing">Tommy Hilfiger</a> for $3 billion.<sup id="cite_ref-NY_Times_News_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NY_Times_News-14">[14]</a></sup> In the third quarter of 2010 losses made on the "Van Heusen" brand led to the decision to pull it out of all European trading markets. As of March  2011<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=PVH_(company)&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup> the company sells no products under that name in Europe. All European staff became redundant as a result.
</p><p>In February 2013, PVH acquired <a href="/wiki/Warnaco_Group" title="Warnaco Group">Warnaco Group</a>, which manufactured the Calvin Klein underwear, jeans and sportswear lines under license, thus consolidating control of the Calvin Klein brand. The Warnaco acquisition also added the Warner's and Olga intimate apparel brands, as well the <a href="/wiki/Speedo" title="Speedo">Speedo</a> swimwear brand (the latter in North America only).  In November 2013 PVH sold the G.H. Bass brand and all of its assets, images and licenses to <a href="/wiki/G-III_Apparel_Group" title="G-III Apparel Group">G-III Apparel Group</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p><p>PVH ended its licensing agreement with Trump in July 2015, after <a href="/wiki/Macy%27s" title="Macy's">Macy's</a> discontinued sales of his Trump Signature Collection due to controversial comments that he made regarding illegal immigrants.<sup id="cite_ref-Trump_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Trump-11">[11]</a></sup></p><p>In 2017 <a href="/wiki/Forbes" title="Forbes">Forbes</a> ranked PVH, 25 out of 890 companies on the "Just company" list.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p><p>In March 2017, PVH acquired lingerie brand <a href="/wiki/True_%26_Co." title="True &amp; Co.">True &amp; Co.</a><sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p><p>In June 2018, PVH acquired the <a href="/wiki/Geoffrey_Beene" title="Geoffrey Beene">Geoffrey Beene</a> clothing brand, which PVH previously produced under license.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup>  On August 28, 2018, PVH announced that it would expand the Izod brand to portions of Europe beginning with the Fall/Winter 2018 collection.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p><p>In January 2020, PVH sold back the rights for the Speedo swimwear brand, previously marketed under "Speedo USA" and "Speedo North America", back to Speedo's international parent, the British <a href="/wiki/Pentland_Group" title="Pentland Group">Pentland Group</a>. The deal was in exchange for $170 million in cash.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(3)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution">Distribution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=PVH_(company)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Distribution" data-section="5" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-3 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-3">
<p>PVH provides products to many popular department stores, such as <a href="/wiki/JC_Penney" class="mw-redirect" title="JC Penney">JC Penney</a>, <a href="/wiki/Macy%27s" title="Macy's">Macy's</a>, <a href="/wiki/Myer" title="Myer">MYER</a>, <a href="/wiki/David_Jones_Limited" title="David Jones Limited">David Jones</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kohl%27s" title="Kohl's">Kohl's</a>, <a href="/wiki/Belk" title="Belk">Belk</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Dillard%27s" title="Dillard's">Dillard's</a> as well as online retailer <a href="/wiki/Amazon_(company)" title="Amazon (company)">Amazon</a> both through its own labels and <a href="/wiki/Private_label" title="Private label">private label</a> agreements. PVH also sells its products directly to customers through about 700 <a href="/wiki/Outlet_stores" class="mw-redirect" title="Outlet stores">outlet stores</a> under the brand names Van Heusen, Tommy Hilfiger, and Calvin Klein.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2019)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p><p>The Calvin Klein stores sell the full range of products at full price, differing from existing outlet stores. These stores are range from 10,000 square feet (930 m<sup>2</sup>) to 20,000 square feet (1,900 m<sup>2</sup>).<sup id="cite_ref-luxury_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-luxury-21">[21]</a></sup></p><p>Phillips-Van Heusen closed its Geoffrey Beene outlet retail division in 2008. Approximately 25 percent of the Geoffrey Beene outlet stores became Calvin Klein stores, while the remaining 75 percent of stores closed entirely.<sup id="cite_ref-closing_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-closing-22">[22]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-luxury_21-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-luxury-21">[21]</a></sup> The company continued to license the Geoffrey Beene brand name for Geoffrey Beene brand dress shirts and men's sportswear until 2018 when it acquired the brand outright. In 2015, PVH closed its Izod retail division due to an increasing competitive environment driven by more premium brands in the outlet retail channel; this did not affect Izod's wholesale business to department stores and online retailers.  Select Izod products are now available at some Van Heusen stores; initially this only included the golf line, but now includes products from Izod's other lines (mostly from the Advantage and Saltwater lines).  In July 2018, PVH launched a direct online sales platform on the Izod and Van Heusen websites where both brands are featured and can be cross-shopped on each other's site.
</p>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(4)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Marketing">Marketing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=PVH_(company)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Marketing" data-section="6" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-4 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-4">
<p>In October 2007, PVH took over naming rights to the <a href="/wiki/Meadowlands_Sports_Complex" title="Meadowlands Sports Complex">Meadowlands Sports Complex</a> arena in <a href="/wiki/East_Rutherford,_New_Jersey" title="East Rutherford, New Jersey">East Rutherford</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_Jersey" title="New Jersey">New Jersey</a>. The arena's name was changed to the <a href="/wiki/Izod_Center" class="mw-redirect" title="Izod Center">Izod Center</a>, and the change became effective on October 31, 2007. PVH paid about $1.4 million a year for two years, then paid $750,000 a year and included the arena employees IZOD uniforms.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> In 2015 IZOD center was permanently closed.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>

<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Environmental_practices">Environmental practices</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=PVH_(company)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Environmental practices" data-section="8" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>In July 2011, PVH—along with other major fashion and <a href="/wiki/Sportswear_(activewear)" class="mw-redirect" title="Sportswear (activewear)">sportswear</a> brands including <a href="/wiki/Nike,_Inc." title="Nike, Inc.">Nike</a>, <a href="/wiki/Adidas" title="Adidas">Adidas</a> and <a href="/wiki/Abercrombie_%26_Fitch" title="Abercrombie &amp; Fitch">Abercrombie &amp; Fitch</a>—was the subject of a report by the environmental group <a href="/wiki/Greenpeace" title="Greenpeace">Greenpeace</a> entitled "Dirty Laundry". PVH is accused of working with suppliers in <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a> who, according to the findings of the report, contribute to the pollution of the <a href="/wiki/Yangtze" title="Yangtze">Yangtze</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pearl_River_(China)" title="Pearl River (China)">Pearl Rivers</a>. Samples taken from one facility belonging to the <a href="/wiki/Youngor_Group" title="Youngor Group">Youngor Group</a> located on the <a href="/wiki/Yangtze_River_Delta" class="mw-redirect" title="Yangtze River Delta">Yangtze River Delta</a> and another belonging to the Well Dyeing Factory Ltd. located on a tributary of the <a href="/wiki/Pearl_River_Delta" title="Pearl River Delta">Pearl River Delta</a> revealed the presence of hazardous and persistent <a href="/wiki/Hormone_disruptor" class="mw-redirect" title="Hormone disruptor">hormone disruptor</a> chemicals, including <a href="/wiki/Alkylphenols" class="mw-redirect" title="Alkylphenols">alkylphenols</a>, <a href="/wiki/Perfluorinated_compound" title="Perfluorinated compound">perfluorinated compounds</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Perfluorooctane_sulfonate" class="mw-redirect" title="Perfluorooctane sulfonate">perfluorooctane sulfonate</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
