<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Two_Guys">Two Guys</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vornado_Realty_Trust&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Two Guys" data-section="3" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>The origins of the company can be traced back to the <a href="/wiki/Two_Guys" title="Two Guys">Two Guys</a> discount store chain, founded in 1947 by brothers Sidney and Herbert Hubschman.<sup id="cite_ref-change_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-change-2">[2]</a></sup> In 1959, <a href="/wiki/Two_Guys" title="Two Guys">Two Guys</a> acquired <a href="/wiki/Vornado_Air" title="Vornado Air">O. A. Sutton Corporation</a>, manufacturers of the Vornado line of electric fans, and the company was renamed Vornado Inc.<sup id="cite_ref-change_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-change-2">[2]</a></sup></p><p>By 1964, the company operated over 200 stores.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> In the 1970s, Vornado began divesting its retail operations. In 1978, the company sold 80 Two Guys locations in California.<sup id="cite_ref-change_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-change-2">[2]</a></sup></p><p>In 1980, Interstate Properties Inc., a <a href="/wiki/Real_estate_development" title="Real estate development">real estate development</a> company controlled by <a href="/wiki/Steven_Roth" title="Steven Roth">Steven Roth</a>, acquired an 18% stake in Vornado, since Roth became interested in the company's real estate holdings.<sup id="cite_ref-change_2-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-change-2">[2]</a></sup> In 1981, Interstate Properties took control of the firm after winning a <a href="/wiki/Proxy_fight" title="Proxy fight">proxy struggle</a> against existing management and closed additional stores, reducing the number of stores in operation to 12.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span id="Acquisition_of_Alexander.27s_.281986.E2.80.931995.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Acquisition_of_Alexander's_(1986–1995)">Acquisition of Alexander's (1986–1995)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vornado_Realty_Trust&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Acquisition of Alexander's (1986–1995)" data-section="4" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>In 1986, Interstate Properties and <a href="/wiki/Donald_Trump" title="Donald Trump">Donald Trump</a> each bought approximately 20% of <a href="/wiki/Alexander%27s" title="Alexander's">Alexander's</a>, a failing retailer whose real estate holdings included a store that occupied the entire block between <a href="/wiki/List_of_numbered_streets_in_Manhattan#Details##58th_Street" title="List of numbered streets in Manhattan">East 58th</a> and 59th streets and Lexington and <a href="/wiki/Third_Avenue" title="Third Avenue">Third Avenues</a> in <a href="/wiki/Manhattan" title="Manhattan">Manhattan</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p><p>In 1988, they each raised their stakes to 27% of the company, but Trump pledged his interest as collateral for a personal loan from <a href="/wiki/Citicorp" class="mw-redirect" title="Citicorp">Citicorp</a> and in 1991, Trump was forced to turn over his holdings to Citibank.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p><p>In 1992, Roth and Alexander's creditors forced Alexander's into bankruptcy.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p><p>Alexander's emerged from bankruptcy in 1993 as a <a href="/wiki/Real_estate_investment_trust" title="Real estate investment trust">real estate investment trust</a>. That same year, Vornado Inc. was converted into a <a href="/wiki/REIT" class="mw-redirect" title="REIT">REIT</a>, Vornado Realty Trust. In 1995, Vornado bought Citicorp's interest in Alexander's.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span id="Vornado_Air_licensing_deal_.281989.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Vornado_Air_licensing_deal_(1989)">Vornado Air licensing deal (1989)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vornado_Realty_Trust&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Vornado Air licensing deal (1989)" data-section="5" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>In 1989, the Vornado name was <a href="/wiki/Brand_licensing" title="Brand licensing">licensed</a> to <a href="/wiki/Vornado_Air" title="Vornado Air">Vornado Air</a>, LLC, a new company formed to manufacture heating and cooling equipment. Outside of the brand licensing agreement, the Trust has no modern relation to Vornado Air, LLC.
</p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span id="Acquisition_of_properties_from_Bernard_H._Mendik_.281997.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Acquisition_of_properties_from_Bernard_H._Mendik_(1997)">Acquisition of properties from Bernard H. Mendik (1997)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vornado_Realty_Trust&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Acquisition of properties from Bernard H. Mendik (1997)" data-section="6" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>In April 1997, the company acquired a company owned by <a href="/wiki/Bernard_H._Mendik" title="Bernard H. Mendik">Bernard H. Mendik</a> in a $654 million stock transaction that added a large portfolio of office buildings in Manhattan to Vornado's assets. Mendik became co-chairman of Vornado until his resignation in October 1998.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span id="World_Trade_Center_lease_.282001.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="World_Trade_Center_lease_(2001)">World Trade Center lease (2001)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vornado_Realty_Trust&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: World Trade Center lease (2001)" data-section="7" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>On February 15, 2001, the Port Authority announced that Vornado Realty Trust had won the bid for a 99-year lease for the <a href="/wiki/World_Trade_Center_(1973%E2%80%932001)" title="World Trade Center (1973–2001)">World Trade Center</a>, offering $3.25 billion.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Silverstein_Properties" title="Silverstein Properties">Silverstein Properties</a> was outbid by $30 million by Vornado. However, Vornado balked over lease terms and possible tax liabilities.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Silverstein signed a lease for the World Trade Center in April 2001,<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> 5 months before the <a href="/wiki/September_11_attacks" title="September 11 attacks">September 11 attacks</a>.
</p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span id="2001.E2.80.93present"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="2001–present">2001–present</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vornado_Realty_Trust&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: 2001–present" data-section="8" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>In 2001, the company acquired Charles E. Smith Commercial Realty, owner of a portfolio of assets primarily in <a href="/wiki/Arlington,_Virginia" class="mw-redirect" title="Arlington, Virginia">Arlington, Virginia</a>, in a $1.58 billion transaction. <a href="/wiki/Robert_H._Smith_(philanthropist)" title="Robert H. Smith (philanthropist)">Robert H. Smith</a> and <a href="/wiki/Robert_P._Kogod" title="Robert P. Kogod">Robert P. Kogod</a> were added to the board of directors of Vornado.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p><p>In 2005, the company bought a 32.5% interest in <a href="/wiki/Toys_%22R%22_Us" title='Toys "R" Us'>Toys "R" Us</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Toys "R" Us filed <a href="/wiki/Bankruptcy" title="Bankruptcy">bankruptcy</a> in 2018 and the investment was written off.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p><p>In 2013, Steven Roth replaced Michael D. Fascitelli as <a href="/wiki/CEO" class="mw-redirect" title="CEO">CEO</a> of Vornado. Fascitelli had been CEO since 2009.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p><p>In 2015, the company spun off its interest in Urban Edge Properties to its shareholders.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p><p>In 2017, the company merged its Charles E. Smith Companies subsidiary into the JBG Companies, forming <a href="/wiki/JBG_Smith" title="JBG Smith">JBG Smith</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
