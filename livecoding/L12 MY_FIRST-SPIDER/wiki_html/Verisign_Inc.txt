<p><b><a href="/wiki/VeriSign_Identity_Protection" class="mw-redirect" title="VeriSign Identity Protection">Verisign</a> Inc.</b> is an <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">American</a> company based in <a href="/wiki/Reston,_Virginia" title="Reston, Virginia">Reston, Virginia</a>, <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> that operates a diverse array of network infrastructure, including two of the <a href="/wiki/Internet" title="Internet">Internet</a>'s thirteen <a href="/wiki/Root_nameserver" class="mw-redirect" title="Root nameserver">root nameservers</a>, the authoritative registry for the <style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r886049734">.mw-parser-output .monospaced{font-family:monospace,monospace}</style><span class="monospaced"><a href="/wiki/.com" title=".com">.com</a></span>, <link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r886049734"><span class="monospaced"><a href="/wiki/.net" title=".net">.net</a></span>, and <link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r886049734"><span class="monospaced"><a href="/wiki/.name" title=".name">.name</a></span> generic <a href="/wiki/Top-level_domain" title="Top-level domain">top-level domains</a> and the <link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r886049734"><span class="monospaced"><a href="/wiki/.cc" title=".cc">.cc</a></span> and <link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r886049734"><span class="monospaced"><a href="/wiki/.tv" title=".tv">.tv</a></span> <a href="/wiki/Country-code_top-level_domain" class="mw-redirect" title="Country-code top-level domain">country-code top-level domains</a>, and the back-end systems for the <link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r886049734"><span class="monospaced"><a href="/wiki/.jobs" title=".jobs">.jobs</a></span>, <link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r886049734"><span class="monospaced"><a href="/wiki/.gov" title=".gov">.gov</a></span>, and <link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r886049734"><span class="monospaced"><a href="/wiki/.edu" title=".edu">.edu</a></span> <a href="/wiki/Top-level_domain" title="Top-level domain">top-level domains</a>. Verisign also offers a range of security services, including managed <a href="/wiki/DNS" class="mw-redirect" title="DNS">DNS</a>, <a href="/wiki/Distributed_denial-of-service" class="mw-redirect" title="Distributed denial-of-service">distributed denial-of-service</a> (DDoS) attack mitigation<sup id="cite_ref-Lombard_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lombard-7">[7]</a></sup> and cyber-threat reporting.

Symantec later sold this unit to <a href="/wiki/DigiCert" title="DigiCert">DigiCert</a> in 2017.<sup id="cite_ref-Fortune-Symantec_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fortune-Symantec-9">[9]</a></sup> On October 25, 2018, <a href="/wiki/Neustar" title="Neustar">NeuStar, Inc</a>. acquired VeriSign’s Security Service Customer Contracts. <sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> The acquisition effectively transferred Verisign Inc.’s <a href="/wiki/Denial-of-service_attack" title="Denial-of-service attack">Distributed Denial of Service</a> (DDoS) protection, Managed <a href="/wiki/DNS" class="mw-redirect" title="DNS">DNS</a>, DNS Firewall and fee-based Recursive DNS services customer contracts.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p><p>Verisign's former <a href="/wiki/Chief_financial_officer" title="Chief financial officer">chief financial officer</a> (CFO) Brian Robins announced in August 2010 that the company would move from its original location of <a href="/wiki/Mountain_View,_California" title="Mountain View, California">Mountain View</a>, <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a>, to <a href="/wiki/Dulles,_Virginia" title="Dulles, Virginia">Dulles</a> in <a href="/wiki/Northern_Virginia" title="Northern Virginia">Northern Virginia</a> by 2011 due to 95% of the company's business being on the <a href="/wiki/East_Coast_of_the_United_States" title="East Coast of the United States">East Coast</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> The company is <a href="/wiki/Delaware_General_Corporation_Law" title="Delaware General Corporation Law">incorporated in Delaware</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>

</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(1)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Verisign&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History" data-section="1" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-1 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-1">
<p>Verisign was founded in 1995 as a spin-off of the <a href="/wiki/RSA_Security" title="RSA Security">RSA Security</a> certification services business. The new company received licenses to key cryptographic patents held by RSA (set to expire in 2000) and a time-limited non-compete agreement. The new company served as a <a href="/wiki/Certificate_authority" title="Certificate authority">certificate authority</a> (CA) and its initial mission was "providing trust for the Internet and <a href="/wiki/Electronic_Commerce" class="mw-redirect" title="Electronic Commerce">Electronic Commerce</a> through our Digital Authentication services and products". Prior to selling its certificate business to <a href="/wiki/NortonLifeLock" title="NortonLifeLock">Symantec</a> in 2010, Verisign had more than 3 million certificates in operation for everything from military to financial services and retail applications, making it the largest CA in the world.
</p><p>In 2000, Verisign acquired <a href="/wiki/Network_Solutions" title="Network Solutions">Network Solutions</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> which operated the <link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r886049734"><span class="monospaced"><a href="/wiki/.com" title=".com">.com</a></span>, <link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r886049734"><span class="monospaced"><a href="/wiki/.net" title=".net">.net</a></span> and <link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r886049734"><span class="monospaced"><a href="/wiki/.org" title=".org">.org</a></span> TLDs under agreements with the Internet Corporation for Assigned Names and Numbers (<a href="/wiki/ICANN" title="ICANN">ICANN</a>) and the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Commerce" title="United States Department of Commerce">United States Department of Commerce</a>. Those core registry functions formed the basis for Verisign's naming division, which is now the company's largest and most significant business unit.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> In 2002, Verisign was charged with violation of the <a href="/wiki/Securities_Exchange_Act" class="mw-redirect" title="Securities Exchange Act">Securities Exchange Act</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Verisign divested the Network Solutions retail (<a href="/wiki/Domain_name_registrar" title="Domain name registrar">domain name registrar</a>) business in 2003, retaining the <a href="/wiki/Domain_name_registry" title="Domain name registry">domain name registry</a> (wholesale) function as its core Internet addressing business.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p><p>For the year ended December 31, 2010, Verisign reported revenue of $681 million, up 10% from $616 million in 2009.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> Verisign operates two businesses, Naming Services, which encompasses the operation of top-level domains and critical Internet infrastructure, and Network Intelligence and Availability (NIA) Services, which encompasses DDoS mitigation, managed DNS and threat intelligence.
</p><p>On August 9, 2010, <a href="/wiki/NortonLifeLock" title="NortonLifeLock">Symantec</a> completed its approximately $1.28 billion acquisition of Verisign's authentication business, including the Secure Sockets Layer (SSL) Certificate Services, the Public Key Infrastructure (PKI) Services, the Verisign Trust Services, the Verisign Identity Protection (VIP) Authentication Service, and the majority stake in Verisign Japan. The deal capped a multi-year effort by Verisign to narrow its focus to its core infrastructure and security business units.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> Following ongoing controversies regarding Symantec's handling of certificate validation, which culminated in <a href="/wiki/Google" title="Google">Google</a> untrusting Symantec-issued certificates in its <a href="/wiki/Google_Chrome" title="Google Chrome">Chrome web browser</a>, Symantec sold this unit to <a href="/wiki/DigiCert" title="DigiCert">DigiCert</a> in 2017.<sup id="cite_ref-Fortune-Symantec_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fortune-Symantec-9">[9]</a></sup></p><p>Verisign's share price tumbled in early 2014, hastened by the U.S. government's announcement that it would "relinquish oversight of the Internet's domain-naming system to a non-government entity".<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> Ultimately the <a href="/wiki/Internet_Corporation_for_Assigned_Names_and_Numbers" class="mw-redirect" title="Internet Corporation for Assigned Names and Numbers">Internet Corporation for Assigned Names and Numbers</a> chose to continue VeriSign's role as the root zone maintainer and the two entered into a new contract in 2016.
</p>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(2)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Naming_services">Naming services</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Verisign&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Naming services" data-section="2" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-2 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-2">
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Token_Verisign.JPG" class="image"><noscript><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3d/Token_Verisign.JPG/200px-Token_Verisign.JPG" decoding="async" width="200" height="121" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="1851" data-file-height="1119"></noscript><span class="lazy-image-placeholder" style="width: 200px;height: 121px;" data-src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3d/Token_Verisign.JPG/200px-Token_Verisign.JPG" data-alt="" data-width="200" data-height="121" data-class="thumbimage"> </span></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Token_Verisign.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Security_token" title="Security token">Security token</a> produced by Verisign</div></div></div>
<p>Verisign's core business is its naming services division. The division operates the authoritative domain name registries for two of the Internet's most important <a href="/wiki/Top-level_domains" class="mw-redirect" title="Top-level domains">top-level domains</a>, <link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r886049734"><span class="monospaced"><a href="/wiki/.com" title=".com">.com</a></span> and <link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r886049734"><span class="monospaced"><a href="/wiki/.net" title=".net">.net</a></span>. It is also the contracted registry operator for the <a href="/wiki/.name" title=".name">.name</a> and <a href="/wiki/.gov" title=".gov">.gov</a> <a href="/wiki/Top-level_domains" class="mw-redirect" title="Top-level domains">top-level domains</a> as well as the country code top-level domains <link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r886049734"><span class="monospaced"><a href="/wiki/.cc" title=".cc">.cc</a></span> (<a href="/wiki/Cocos_(Keeling)_Islands" title="Cocos (Keeling) Islands">Cocos Islands</a>) and <link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r886049734"><span class="monospaced"><a href="/wiki/.tv" title=".tv">.tv</a></span> (<a href="/wiki/Tuvalu" title="Tuvalu">Tuvalu</a>). In addition, Verisign is the primary technical subcontractor for the <link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r886049734"><span class="monospaced"><a href="/wiki/.edu" title=".edu">.edu</a></span>, and <link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r886049734"><span class="monospaced"><a href="/wiki/.jobs" title=".jobs">.jobs</a></span> <a href="/wiki/Top-level_domains" class="mw-redirect" title="Top-level domains">top-level domains</a> for their respective registry operators, which are non-profit organizations; in this role Verisign maintains the <a href="/wiki/Zone_file" title="Zone file">zone files</a> for these particular domains and hosts the domains from their domain servers.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> Registry operators are the "wholesalers" of Internet domain names, while <a href="/wiki/Domain_name_registrar" title="Domain name registrar">domain name registrars</a> act as the “retailers”, working directly with consumers to register a domain name address.
</p><p>Verisign also operates two of the Internet's thirteen "<a href="/wiki/Root_name_server" title="Root name server">root servers</a>" which are identified by the letters A-M (Verisign operates the “A” and “J” root servers). The root servers form the top of the hierarchical <a href="/wiki/Domain_Name_System" title="Domain Name System">Domain Name System</a> that supports most modern Internet communication. Verisign also generates the globally recognized <a href="/wiki/Root_zone_file" class="mw-redirect" title="Root zone file">root zone file</a> and is also responsible for processing changes to that file once they are ordered by <a href="/wiki/ICANN" title="ICANN">ICANN</a> via <a href="/wiki/Internet_Assigned_Numbers_Authority" title="Internet Assigned Numbers Authority">IANA</a> and approved by the <a href="/wiki/US_Department_of_Commerce" class="mw-redirect" title="US Department of Commerce">U.S. Department of Commerce</a>. Changes to the root zone were originally distributed via the A root server, but now they are distributed to all thirteen servers via a separate distribution system which Verisign maintains. Verisign is the only one of the 12 root server operators to operate more than one of the thirteen <a href="/wiki/Root_nameservers" class="mw-redirect" title="Root nameservers">root nameservers</a>. The A and J root servers are "<a href="/wiki/Anycast" title="Anycast">anycasted</a>” and are no longer operated from any of the company's own datacenters as a means to increase redundancy and availability and mitigate the threat of a <a href="/wiki/Single_point_of_failure" title="Single point of failure">single point of failure</a>. In 2016 the Department of Commerce ended its role in managing the Internet's DNS and transferred full control to ICANN. While this initially negatively impacted VeriSign's stock, ICANN eventually chose to contract with Verisign to continue its role as the root zone maintainer.
</p><p>VeriSign's naming services division dates back to 1993 when Network Solutions was awarded a contract by the <a href="/wiki/National_Science_Foundation" title="National Science Foundation">National Science Foundation</a> to manage and operate the civilian side of the Internet's domain name registrations.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> Network Solutions was the sole registrar for all of the Internet's non governmental generic top-level domains until 1998 when ICANN was established and the new system of competitive registrars was implemented. As a result of these new policies, Network Solutions divided itself into two divisions. The NSI Registry division was established to manage the authoritative registries that the company would still operate and was separated from the customer-facing registrar business that would have to compete with other registrars. The divisions were even geographically split with the NSI Registry moving from the corporate headquarters in Herndon, Virginia, to nearby Dulles, Virginia. In 2000, VeriSign purchased Network Solutions taking over its role in the Internet DNS. The NSI Registry division eventually became VeriSign's naming services division while the remainder of Network Solutions was later sold by Verisign in 2003 to Pivotal Equity Group.
</p>

Verisign began shopping that year for a new permanent home shortly after moving. They have since terminated their lease of their current space in two buildings at Lakeside@Loudoun Technology Center. The company completed its move at the end of November 2011. The new headquarters is located in the <a href="/wiki/Reston_Town_Center" title="Reston Town Center">Reston Town Center</a> development which has become a major commercial and business hub for the region. In addition to its Reston headquarters, Verisign owns three data center properties. One at 22340 Dresden Street in Dulles, Virginia not far from its corporate headquarters (within the large Broad Run Technology Park), one at 21 Boulden Circle in New Castle, Delaware, and a third in Fribourg, Switzerland. It also has leased server space in numerous internet data centers around the world where the DNS constellation resolution sites are located, mostly at major internet peering facilities. One such facility is at the <a href="/wiki/Equinix" title="Equinix">Equinix</a> Ashburn Datacenter in <a href="/wiki/Ashburn,_Virginia" title="Ashburn, Virginia">Ashburn, Virginia</a>, one of the world's largest datacenters and internet transit hubs.

</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(4)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Controversies">Controversies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Verisign&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Controversies" data-section="4" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-4 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-4">
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="2001:_Code_signing_certificate_mistake">2001: Code signing certificate mistake</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Verisign&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: 2001: Code signing certificate mistake" data-section="5" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>In January 2001, Verisign mistakenly issued two Class 3 <a href="/wiki/Code_signing" title="Code signing">code signing</a> certificates to an individual claiming to be an employee of <a href="/wiki/Microsoft" title="Microsoft">Microsoft</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> The mistake was not discovered and the certificates were not revoked until two weeks later during a routine audit. Because Verisign code-signing certificates do not specify a Certificate Revocation List Distribution Point, there was no way for them to be automatically detected as having been revoked, placing Microsoft's customers at risk.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Microsoft had to later release a special security patch in order to revoke the certificates and mark them as being fraudulent.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="2002:_Domain_transfer_law_suit">2002: Domain transfer law suit</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Verisign&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: 2002: Domain transfer law suit" data-section="6" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>In 2002, Verisign was sued for <a href="/wiki/Domain_slamming" class="mw-redirect" title="Domain slamming">domain slamming</a> – transferring domains from other registrars to themselves by making the registrants believe they were merely renewing their domain name. Although they were found not to have broken the law, they were barred from suggesting that a domain was about to expire or claim that a transfer was actually a renewal.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="2003:_Site_Finder_legal_case">2003: Site Finder legal case</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Verisign&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: 2003: Site Finder legal case" data-section="7" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>In September 2003, Verisign introduced a service called <a href="/wiki/Site_Finder" title="Site Finder">Site Finder</a>, which redirected Web browsers to a search service when users attempted to go to non-existent <link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r886049734"><span class="monospaced"><a href="/wiki/.com" title=".com">.com</a></span> or <link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r886049734"><span class="monospaced"><a href="/wiki/.net" title=".net">.net</a></span> domain names. <a href="/wiki/ICANN" title="ICANN">ICANN</a> asserted that Verisign had overstepped the terms of its contract with the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Commerce" title="United States Department of Commerce">U.S. Department of Commerce</a>, which in essence grants Verisign the right to operate the <a href="/wiki/Domain_name_system" class="mw-redirect" title="Domain name system">DNS</a> for <link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r886049734"><span class="monospaced">.com</span> and <link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r886049734"><span class="monospaced">.net</span>, and Verisign shut down the service. Subsequently, Verisign filed a lawsuit against ICANN in February 2004, seeking to gain clarity over what services it could offer in the context of its contract with ICANN. The claim was moved from federal to California state court in August 2004.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> In late 2005 Verisign and ICANN announced a proposed settlement which defined a process for the introduction of new registry services in the <link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r886049734"><span class="monospaced">.com</span> registry. The documents concerning these settlements are available at ICANN.org.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> The ICANN comments mailing list archive<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> documents some of the criticisms that have been raised regarding the settlement. Additionally Verisign was involved in the <link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r886049734"><span class="monospaced"><a href="/wiki/Sex.com" title="Sex.com">sex.com</a></span> matter<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (May 2019)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> decided by the Ninth circuit<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (May 2019)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup>.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="2003:_Gives_up_.org_domain">2003: Gives up <link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r886049734"><span class="monospaced">.org</span> domain</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Verisign&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: 2003: Gives up .org domain" data-section="8" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>In keeping with ICANN's charter to introduce competition to the domain name marketplace, Verisign agreed to give up its operation of <link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r886049734"><span class="monospaced"><a href="/wiki/.org" title=".org">.org</a></span> top-level domain in 2003 in exchange for a continuation of its contract to operate <link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r886049734"><span class="monospaced">.com</span>, which, at the time had more than 34 million registered addresses.
</p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="2005:_Retains_.net_domain">2005: Retains <link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r886049734"><span class="monospaced">.net</span> domain</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Verisign&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: 2005: Retains .net domain" data-section="9" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>In mid-2005, the existing contract for the operation of <link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r886049734"><span class="monospaced"><a href="/wiki/.net" title=".net">.net</a></span> expired and five companies, including Verisign, bid for management of it. Verisign enlisted numerous IT and telecom heavyweights including Microsoft, IBM, Sun Microsystems, MCI, and others, to assert that Verisign had a perfect record operating <link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r886049734"><span class="monospaced">.net</span>. They proposed Verisign continue to manage the <link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r886049734"><span class="monospaced">.net</span> DNS due to its critical importance as the domain underlying numerous "backbone" network services. Verisign was also aided by the fact that several of the other bidders were foreign-based, which raised concerns in national security circles. On June 8, 2005, ICANN announced that Verisign had been approved to operate <link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r886049734"><span class="monospaced">.net</span> until 2011. More information on the <link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r886049734"><span class="monospaced">.net</span> bidding process is available at <a href="/wiki/ICANN" title="ICANN">ICANN</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> On July 1, 2011, ICANN announced that VeriSign's approval to operate .net was extended another six years, until 2017.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="2010:_Data_breach_and_disclosure_controversy">2010: Data breach and disclosure controversy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Verisign&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: 2010: Data breach and disclosure controversy" data-section="10" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>In February 2012 Verisign revealed that their network security had been repeatedly breached in 2010. Verisign stated that the breach did not impact the <a href="/wiki/Domain_Name_System" title="Domain Name System">Domain Name System</a> (DNS) that they maintain, but would not provide details about the loss of data. Verisign was widely criticized for not disclosing the breach earlier and apparently attempting to hide the news in an October 2011 SEC filing.<sup id="cite_ref-pcworld1_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pcworld1-32">[32]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p><p>Because of the lack of details provided by Verisign, it was not clear whether the breach impacted the Certificate Signing business, acquired by Symantec in late 2010. According to Oliver Lavery, the Director of Security and Research for <a href="/wiki/NCircle_Entertainment" title="NCircle Entertainment">nCircle</a> "Can we trust any site using Verisign SSL certificates? Without more clarity, the logical answer is no”.<sup id="cite_ref-pcworld1_32-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pcworld1-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="2010:_Web_site_domain_seizures">2010: Web site domain seizures</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Verisign&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: 2010: Web site domain seizures" data-section="11" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>On November 29, 2010, the <a href="/wiki/U.S._Immigration_and_Customs_Enforcement" title="U.S. Immigration and Customs Enforcement">U.S. Immigration and Customs Enforcement</a> (U.S. ICE) issued seizure orders against 82 web sites with <link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r886049734"><span class="monospaced">.com</span> Internet addresses that were reported to be involved in the illegal sale and distribution of counterfeit goods.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> As registry operator for <link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r886049734"><span class="monospaced">.com</span>, Verisign performed the required takedowns of the 82 sites under order from law enforcement.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> <i>InformationWeek</i> reported that "Verisign will say only that it received sealed court orders directing certain actions to be taken with respect to specific domain names".<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> The removal of the 82 websites was cited as an impetus for the launch of "the Dot-P2P Project"<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> in order to create a decentralized DNS service without centralized registry operators. Following the disappearance of <a href="/wiki/WikiLeaks" title="WikiLeaks">WikiLeaks</a><sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> during the following week<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> and its forced move to wikileaks.ch, a Swiss domain, the <a href="/wiki/Electronic_Frontier_Foundation" title="Electronic Frontier Foundation">Electronic Frontier Foundation</a> warned of the dangers of having key pieces of Internet infrastructure such as DNS name translation under corporate control.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup></p>
