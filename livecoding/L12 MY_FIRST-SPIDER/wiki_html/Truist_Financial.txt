message</a>)</i></small></div></td></tr></tbody></table><p><b>Truist Financial Corp.</b> is an American <a href="/wiki/Bank_holding_company" title="Bank holding company">bank holding company</a> headquartered in <a href="/wiki/Charlotte,_North_Carolina" title="Charlotte, North Carolina">Charlotte, North Carolina</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> The company was formed in December 2019 as the result of the merger of <b>BB&amp;T</b> (Branch Banking and Trust Company) and <a href="/wiki/SunTrust_Banks" title="SunTrust Banks">SunTrust Banks</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-TruistReveal_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TruistReveal-4">[4]</a></sup> Its bank operates 2,049 <a href="/wiki/Branch_(banking)" title="Branch (banking)">branches</a> in 15 states and Washington, D.C., and offers consumer and commercial banking, securities brokerage, asset management, mortgage, and insurance products and services.<sup id="cite_ref-10K_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10K-1">[1]</a></sup> It is on the <a href="/wiki/List_of_largest_banks_in_the_United_States" title="List of largest banks in the United States">list of largest banks in the United States</a> by assets. Its subsidiary, McGriff Insurance Services (formerly known as BB&amp;T Insurance Services),<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> was one of the largest <a href="/wiki/Insurance_broker" title="Insurance broker">insurance brokers</a> in the world.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> In its history, it has made 106 <a href="/wiki/Mergers_and_acquisitions" title="Mergers and acquisitions">mergers and acquisitions</a>. </section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(1)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Truist_Financial&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History" data-section="1" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-1 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-1">
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Beginnings">Beginnings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Truist_Financial&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Beginnings" data-section="2" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>In 1872, Alpheus Branch and Thomas Jefferson Hadley founded the Branch and Hadley merchant bank in their hometown of <a href="/wiki/Wilson,_North_Carolina" title="Wilson, North Carolina">Wilson, North Carolina</a>. After many transactions, mostly with local farmers, Branch bought out Hadley's shares in 1887 and renamed the company Branch and Company, Bankers. Two years later, Branch, his father-in-law Gen. Joshua Barnes, Hadley, and three other men secured a charter from the <a href="/wiki/North_Carolina_General_Assembly" title="North Carolina General Assembly">North Carolina General Assembly</a> to operate the Wilson Banking and Trust Company. After many more name changes, the company finally settled on the name Branch Banking and Trust Company. Branch remained an active member in the company until his death in 1893. The 1903 <a href="/wiki/Branch_Banking_(Wilson,_North_Carolina)" class="mw-redirect" title="Branch Banking (Wilson, North Carolina)">Branch Banking and Trust Company Building</a> at Wilson was listed on the <a href="/wiki/National_Register_of_Historic_Places" title="National Register of Historic Places">National Register of Historic Places</a> in 1978.<sup id="cite_ref-nris_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nris-8">[8]</a></sup></p><p>BB&amp;T sold <a href="/wiki/Liberty_Bonds" class="mw-redirect" title="Liberty Bonds">Liberty Bonds</a> during <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a> and grew to have more than $4 million in <a href="/wiki/Assets" class="mw-redirect" title="Assets">assets</a> by 1923. BB&amp;T Insurance Services was added in 1922 and a mortgage division was added in 1923. Even though banks across the United States failed as a result of the <a href="/wiki/1929_Stock_Market_Crash" class="mw-redirect" title="1929 Stock Market Crash">1929 Stock Market Crash</a>, BB&amp;T survived; it was the only one to do so in the town of Wilson.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Merger_with_Southern_National_Bank">Merger with Southern National Bank</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Truist_Financial&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Merger with Southern National Bank" data-section="3" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>In 1995, the bank merged with <a href="/wiki/Southern_National_Bank" title="Southern National Bank">Southern National Bank</a>, another bank with roots in the eastern part of the state. This gave BB&amp;T 437 branches in 220 cities in the Carolinas and <a href="/wiki/Virginia" title="Virginia">Virginia</a>. The merged bank would be called BB&amp;T, though at first, the holding company was Southern National Corp. With $19 billion in assets, BB&amp;T would have the most North Carolina deposits and branches of any bank. Southern National head Glenn Orr and new BB&amp;T chairman John Allison said the merger would likely prevent out-of-state banks from taking over. Orr retired once the merger was complete.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> The headquarters became <a href="/wiki/BB%26T_Financial_Center" title="BB&amp;T Financial Center">BB&amp;T Financial Center</a> in Winston-Salem, North Carolina.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p><p>In 1997, Southern National Corp. took over <a href="/wiki/United_Carolina_Bank" title="United Carolina Bank">United Carolina Bank</a>, another eastern North Carolina-based bank, in a $985 million deal announced in November 1996. UCB had $4.5 billion in assets. 400 employees worked in Whiteville but despite losing the headquarters, the town would eventually have 500 BB&amp;T employees working at a 250-employee <a href="/wiki/Call_centre" title="Call centre">call center</a> and other operations.<sup id="cite_ref-Bartlett_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bartlett-12">[12]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Wahl_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wahl-14">[14]</a></sup> Starting September 22, 1997, 91 UCB branches began the process of changing to BB&amp;T, and 67 other branches of the two banks closed starting in October because they were close to other BB&amp;T locations.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p><p>On May 19, 1997, Southern National Corp. changed its name to BB&amp;T Corp. and its stock symbol SN to BBT.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Expansion">Expansion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Truist_Financial&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Expansion" data-section="4" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>The bank continued to expand nationwide through the 1990s, purchasing Fidelity Financial Bankshares,<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> First Financial of Petersburg, Md., Maryland Federal Bancorp,<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> and Franklin Bancorporation.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> In 1998, BB&amp;T acquired MainStreet Financial of <a href="/wiki/Martinsville,_Virginia" title="Martinsville, Virginia">Martinsville, Va.</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> and Mason-Dixon Bancshares of Westminster, Md.,<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> and further expanded into <a href="/wiki/Georgia_(U.S._state)" title="Georgia (U.S. state)">Georgia</a> and <a href="/wiki/West_Virginia" title="West Virginia">West Virginia</a> after purchasing First Liberty of Macon, Ga.,<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> and Matewan Bancshares.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Acquisitions">Acquisitions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Truist_Financial&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Acquisitions" data-section="5" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>In 2000, BB&amp;T purchased One Valley Bancorp, which was itself formed from a combination of community banks throughout West Virginia. This move gave BB&amp;T the largest bank presence in West Virginia.
</p><p>In 2002, BB&amp;T completed its acquisition of MidAmerica Bancorp of Louisville, Ky. (Bank of Louisville) and AREA Bancshares Corporation of Owensboro, Ky.,,<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p><p>In 2003, BB&amp;T completed its acquisition of First Virginia Banks of Falls Church, Va.
</p><p>In 2005, the bank acquired <a href="/wiki/Main_Street_Bank" title="Main Street Bank">Main Street Banks</a> of metro Atlanta for $622 million.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p><p>In 2006, the bank acquired Coastal Federal Bank, based in <a href="/wiki/Myrtle_Beach,_South_Carolina" title="Myrtle Beach, South Carolina">Myrtle Beach</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p><p>In February 2008, the company acquired Burkey Risk Services.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p><p>In April 2008, the company acquired UnionBanc Insurance Services.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p><p>In late 2008, the bank accepted $3.1 billion in <a href="/wiki/Bailout" title="Bailout">bailout</a> money through the sale of preferred shares to the U.S. Treasury's <a href="/wiki/Troubled_Asset_Relief_Program" title="Troubled Asset Relief Program">Troubled Asset Relief Program</a>. In June 2009, the bank repurchased the shares.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> Also in June 2009, its chairman, <a href="/wiki/John_A._Allison_IV" title="John A. Allison IV">John A. Allison IV</a> delivered a keynote address to a meeting of the <a href="/wiki/Competitive_Enterprise_Institute" title="Competitive Enterprise Institute">Competitive Enterprise Institute</a>, where he claimed to show how government regulation caused the <a href="/wiki/Financial_crisis_of_2007%E2%80%932008" class="mw-redirect" title="Financial crisis of 2007–2008">financial crisis of 2007–2008</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></p><p>In July 2008, the company acquired Puckett, Scheetz &amp; Hogan.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></p><p>On August 14, 2009, the bank acquired <a href="/wiki/Colonial_Bank_(United_States)" title="Colonial Bank (United States)">Colonial Bank</a> after its seizure by the <a href="/wiki/Federal_Deposit_Insurance_Corporation" title="Federal Deposit Insurance Corporation">FDIC</a>. This acquisition added more than 340 branches in <a href="/wiki/Alabama" title="Alabama">Alabama</a>, <a href="/wiki/Florida" title="Florida">Florida</a>, Georgia, <a href="/wiki/Nevada" title="Nevada">Nevada</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Texas" title="Texas">Texas</a>, along with approximately $22 billion in assets.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> BB&amp;T sold the Nevada branch to <a href="/wiki/U.S._Bancorp" title="U.S. Bancorp">U.S. Bancorp</a> in January 2010.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></p><p>In December 2008, the company acquired J. Rolfe Davis.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup></p><p>In November 2009, the company acquired Oswald Trippe.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup></p><p>In September 2011, the company acquired Liberty Benefit Insurance Services, an agency operating in California.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> The company also acquired Atlantic Risk Management.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup></p><p>In November 2011, the company acquired Precept, an employee benefits consulting firm.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup></p><p>In February 2012, the company acquired the life insurance and property and casualty insurance divisions of Crump Group.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup></p><p>In July 2012, the bank acquired <a href="/wiki/BankAtlantic" title="BankAtlantic">BankAtlantic</a>, and its $2.1 billion in loans and $3.3 billion in deposits.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> The company also began selling flood insurance online.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup></p><p>In December 2013, the bank acquired 21 <a href="/wiki/Citigroup" title="Citigroup">Citigroup</a> branches in Texas for $36 million, adding $1.2 billion in deposits.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup></p><p>In March 2014, the company acquired Woodbury &amp; Co., an insurance broker operating in the Carolinas.<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup></p><p>In April 2014, the company acquired Caledonian Insurance Group, which specialized in insurance brokerage services for the aviation industry.<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup></p><p>In September 2014, the bank acquired 41 more Citigroup branches, adding $2.3 billion in deposits.<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup></p><p>In April 2015, BB&amp;T subsidiary CRC Insurance Services acquired the assets of Napco LLC, a broker of catastrophic commercial insurance.<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup></p><p>In June 2015, the bank acquired Bank of Kentucky for $363 million, which added $1.9 billion in assets and gave BB&amp;T a presence in the Northern Kentucky-Cincinnati market and its first branches in <a href="/wiki/Ohio" title="Ohio">Ohio</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup> The company also sold American Coastal Insurance and increased its ownership in AMRisc.<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup></p><p>In August 2015, the bank acquired <a href="/wiki/Susquehanna_Bank" title="Susquehanna Bank">Susquehanna Bank</a> for $2.5 billion, adding 240 branches and $18.7 billion in assets, and marking the bank's entry into <a href="/wiki/Pennsylvania" title="Pennsylvania">Pennsylvania</a> and <a href="/wiki/New_Jersey" title="New Jersey">New Jersey</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup></p><p>In April 2016, the company acquired Swett &amp; Crawford, a wholesale insurance broker.<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup></p><p>In April 2016, the bank acquired <a href="/wiki/National_Penn" title="National Penn">National Penn</a> for $1.8 billion, which added 124 branches in Pennsylvania, New Jersey, and Maryland, $9.6 billion in assets, and $6.7 billion in deposits.<sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup></p><p>On August 29, 2018, BB&amp;T announced as many as 630 employees would move from its former corporate headquarters on West Nash Street in Wilson to a $35 million, 95,000-square-foot facility on Pine Street. On October 16, 2018, BB&amp;T announced plan to move 500 employees into a 100,000-square-foot, $10 million building in <a href="/wiki/Whiteville,_North_Carolina" title="Whiteville, North Carolina">Whiteville, N.C.</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Acquisition_of_SunTrust">Acquisition of SunTrust</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Truist_Financial&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Acquisition of SunTrust" data-section="6" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>On February 7, 2019, BB&amp;T reported that it would acquire <a href="/wiki/Atlanta" title="Atlanta">Atlanta</a>-based <a href="/wiki/SunTrust_Banks" title="SunTrust Banks">SunTrust Banks</a> in the biggest bank deal since the 2007-2009 financial crisis, to create the eighth-largest US bank. The bank also announced the move of its headquarters to <a href="/wiki/Charlotte,_North_Carolina" title="Charlotte, North Carolina">Charlotte, North Carolina</a>, retaining significant operations in Winston-Salem.<sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-suntrust_59-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-suntrust-59">[59]</a></sup> It was subsequently announced that Winston-Salem would be the bank's headquarters for community banking, while Atlanta would be the hub for wholesale banking.<sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup></p><p>On June 12, BB&amp;T and SunTrust announced that the merged company would be called <i>Truist Financial Corporation</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-TruistReveal_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TruistReveal-4">[4]</a></sup> This name resulted from research that included hiring <a href="/wiki/Interbrand" title="Interbrand">Interbrand</a>, seeking opinions of employees of both banks, and <a href="/wiki/Focus_group" title="Focus group">focus groups</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-AJC_61-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AJC-61">[61]</a></sup>  The new name drew criticism from analysts and customers of both banks on <a href="/wiki/Social_media" title="Social media">social media</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-62" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-CNBC_63-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CNBC-63">[63]</a></sup></p><p>On June 17, <a href="/wiki/Truliant_Federal_Credit_Union" title="Truliant Federal Credit Union">Truliant Federal Credit Union</a> of Winston-Salem filed suit claiming "<a href="/wiki/Trademark_infringement" title="Trademark infringement">trademark infringement</a>", complaining of potential confusion between the two companies’ respective names, including Truliant products with "Tru" in their names.<sup id="cite_ref-AJC_61-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AJC-61">[61]</a></sup></p><p>The merger was completed at midnight December 6, 2019.<sup id="cite_ref-64" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup> BB&amp;T CEO <a href="/wiki/Kelly_King" title="Kelly King">Kelly King</a> retained the same position with the new company. BB&amp;T customers could begin using SunTrust ATMs free of charge, and SunTrust customers could begin using BB&amp;T ATMs free of charge.<sup id="cite_ref-King_65-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-King-65">[65]</a></sup> The merged bank will continue to operate under the BB&amp;T and SunTrust names until the two banks' systems are combined, a process that could take as long as two years.<sup id="cite_ref-66" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup> On December 11, 2019, Truist officially exercised its option to purchase <a href="/wiki/Hearst_Tower_(Charlotte)" class="mw-redirect" title="Hearst Tower (Charlotte)">Hearst Tower</a> in downtown Charlotte from Cousins Properties. Truist will move its corporate headquarters to Hearst Tower, which will be renamed Truist Center.<sup id="cite_ref-67" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup> Truist had taken over 550,000 square feet of 965,000 square feet total.<sup id="cite_ref-68" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup> The bank announced the deal was completed March 31, 2020. However, due to <a href="/wiki/2019%E2%80%9320_coronavirus_pandemic" title="2019–20 coronavirus pandemic">COVID-19</a>, further actions will be delayed.<sup id="cite_ref-69" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup></p><p>Truist unveiled its logo in January 2020, with two Ts representing touch and technology, and a shade of purple combining the blue of SunTrust and the burgundy of BB&amp;T.<sup id="cite_ref-70" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup></p><p>In February of 2020 Truist announced it would be launching a nonprofit foundation called the Truist Foundation.<sup id="cite_ref-71" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup></p><p>In March 2020, Truist announced it would vacate its former headquarters building in Winston-Salem except for a branch office there, and would move employees in its community/retail hub to other locations in the city, including the Park Building on Cherry Street and two locations on Stratford Road.<sup id="cite_ref-72" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-72">[72]</a></sup></p>
