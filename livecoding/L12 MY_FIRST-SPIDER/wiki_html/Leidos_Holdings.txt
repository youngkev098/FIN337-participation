<p class="mw-empty-elt">
</p>
<p><b>Leidos</b>, formerly known as <b>Science Applications International Corporation</b> (SAIC),<sup id="cite_ref-AR2013_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AR2013-6">[6]</a></sup> is an American <a href="/wiki/Defense_company" class="mw-redirect" title="Defense company">defense</a>, <a href="/wiki/Aerospace_manufacturer" title="Aerospace manufacturer">aviation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Information_technology" title="Information technology">information technology</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Biomedical_research" class="mw-redirect" title="Biomedical research">biomedical research</a> company headquartered in <a href="/wiki/Reston,_Virginia" title="Reston, Virginia">Reston, Virginia</a>, that provides scientific, engineering, <a href="/wiki/Systems_integration" class="mw-redirect" title="Systems integration">systems integration</a>, and technical services. Leidos works extensively with the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Defense" title="United States Department of Defense">United States Department of Defense</a> (4th largest DoD contractor FY2012), the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Homeland_Security" title="United States Department of Homeland Security">United States Department of Homeland Security</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Intelligence_Community" title="United States Intelligence Community">United States Intelligence Community</a>, including the <a href="/wiki/National_Security_Agency" title="National Security Agency">NSA</a>, as well as other U.S. government civil agencies and selected commercial markets.

</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(1)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Leidos&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History" data-section="1" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-1 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-1">
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="As_SAIC">As SAIC</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Leidos&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: As SAIC" data-section="2" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:SAIC_Logo.svg" class="image"><noscript><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/60/SAIC_Logo.svg/220px-SAIC_Logo.svg.png" decoding="async" width="220" height="96" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="437" data-file-height="190"></noscript><span class="lazy-image-placeholder" style="width: 220px;height: 96px;" data-src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/60/SAIC_Logo.svg/220px-SAIC_Logo.svg.png" data-alt="" data-width="220" data-height="96" data-srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/60/SAIC_Logo.svg/330px-SAIC_Logo.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/60/SAIC_Logo.svg/440px-SAIC_Logo.svg.png 2x" data-class="thumbimage"> </span></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:SAIC_Logo.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>SAIC company logo (2010)</div></div></div>
<p>The company was founded by <a href="/wiki/John_Robert_Beyster" title="John Robert Beyster">J. Robert "Bob" Beyster</a> in 1969 in the <a href="/wiki/La_Jolla" title="La Jolla">La Jolla</a> neighborhood of <a href="/wiki/San_Diego" title="San Diego">San Diego</a>, <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a>, as Science Applications Incorporated (SAI).<sup id="cite_ref-BeysterEconomyBook_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BeysterEconomyBook-7">[7]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Glass2007_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Glass2007-8">[8]</a></sup> Beyster, a former scientist for the <a href="/wiki/Westinghouse_Electric_Corporation" title="Westinghouse Electric Corporation">Westinghouse</a> Atomic Power Division,<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Los_Alamos_National_Laboratory" title="Los Alamos National Laboratory">Los Alamos National Laboratory</a><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> who became the chairman of the Accelerator Physics Department of <a href="/wiki/General_Atomics" title="General Atomics">General Atomics</a> in 1957,<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> raised the money to start SAI by selling stock he had received from General Atomics, combined with funds raised from the early employees who bought stock in the young enterprise.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p><p>Initially the company's focus was on projects for the U.S. government related to nuclear power and weapons effects study programs.  The company was renamed <a href="/wiki/Science_Applications_International_Corporation" title="Science Applications International Corporation">Science Applications International Corporation</a> (SAIC) as it expanded its operations. Major projects during Beyster's tenure included work on <a href="/wiki/Radiation_therapy" title="Radiation therapy">radiation therapy</a> for the Los Alamos National Laboratory; technical support and management assistance to the development of the <a href="/wiki/Cruise_missile" title="Cruise missile">cruise missile</a> in the 1970s; the cleanups of the <a href="/wiki/Three_Mile_Island_Nuclear_Generating_Station" title="Three Mile Island Nuclear Generating Station">Three Mile Island Nuclear Generating Station</a> after its major <a href="/wiki/Three_Mile_Island_accident" title="Three Mile Island accident">accident</a>, and of the contaminated community of <a href="/wiki/Love_Canal" title="Love Canal">Love Canal</a>; design and performance evaluation of the <a href="/wiki/Stars_%26_Stripes_87" title="Stars &amp; Stripes 87">Stars &amp; Stripes 87</a>, the winning ship for the <a href="/wiki/1987_America%27s_Cup" title="1987 America's Cup">1987 America's Cup</a>; and the design of the first luggage inspection machine to pass new <a href="/wiki/Federal_Aviation_Administration" title="Federal Aviation Administration">Federal Aviation Administration</a> tests following the terrorist bombing of <a href="/wiki/Pan_American_flight_103" class="mw-redirect" title="Pan American flight 103">Pan American flight 103</a> over <a href="/wiki/Lockerbie,_Scotland" class="mw-redirect" title="Lockerbie, Scotland">Lockerbie, Scotland</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-LeidosHistory_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LeidosHistory-13">[13]</a></sup></p><p>Contrary to traditional business models, Beyster originally designed SAIC as an <a href="/wiki/Employee_ownership" class="mw-redirect" title="Employee ownership">employee-owned company</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-BeysterEconomyBook_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BeysterEconomyBook-7">[7]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Glass2007_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Glass2007-8">[8]</a></sup> This shared ownership was accompanied by shared responsibility and freedom in business development, and allowed SAIC to attract and retain highly educated and motivated employees that helped the company to grow and diversify. After Beyster's retirement in 2003, SAIC conducted an <a href="/wiki/Initial_public_offering" title="Initial public offering">initial public offering</a> of <a href="/wiki/Common_stock" title="Common stock">common stock</a> on October 17, 2006.<sup id="cite_ref-ipo_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ipo-14">[14]</a></sup> The offering of 86,250,000 shares of common stock was priced at $15.00 per share. The underwriters, <a href="/wiki/Bear_Stearns" title="Bear Stearns">Bear Stearns</a> and <a href="/wiki/Morgan_Stanley" title="Morgan Stanley">Morgan Stanley</a>, exercised <a href="/wiki/Overallotment_option" class="mw-redirect" title="Overallotment option">overallotment options</a>, resulting in 11.25 million shares. The IPO raised US$1.245B.<sup id="cite_ref-ipo_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ipo-14">[14]</a></sup> Even then, employee shares retained a privileged status, having ten times the voting power per share over common stock.<sup id="cite_ref-Bigelow2005_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bigelow2005-15">[15]</a></sup></p><p>In September 2009 SAIC relocated its corporate headquarters to their existing facilities in <a href="/wiki/Tysons_Corner,_Virginia" class="mw-redirect" title="Tysons Corner, Virginia">Tysons Corner</a> in <a href="/wiki/Unincorporated_area" title="Unincorporated area">unincorporated</a> <a href="/wiki/Fairfax_County,_Virginia" title="Fairfax County, Virginia">Fairfax County</a>, <a href="/wiki/Virginia" title="Virginia">Virginia</a>, near <a href="/wiki/McLean,_Virginia" title="McLean, Virginia">McLean</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p><p>In 2012 SAIC was ordered to pay $550 million to the <a href="/wiki/City_of_New_York" class="mw-redirect" title="City of New York">City of New York</a> for <a href="/wiki/Overbilling" title="Overbilling">overbilling</a> the city over a period of seven years on the CityTime contract.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> In 2014 Gerard Denault, SAIC's CityTime program manager, and his government contact were sentenced to 20 years in prison for fraud and bribery related to that contract.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="As_Leidos">As Leidos</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Leidos&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: As Leidos" data-section="3" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:2017_Capital_Pride_(Washington,_D.C.)_-_031.jpg" class="image"><noscript><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ce/2017_Capital_Pride_%28Washington%2C_D.C.%29_-_031.jpg/220px-2017_Capital_Pride_%28Washington%2C_D.C.%29_-_031.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="136" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="3484" data-file-height="2148"></noscript><span class="lazy-image-placeholder" style="width: 220px;height: 136px;" data-src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ce/2017_Capital_Pride_%28Washington%2C_D.C.%29_-_031.jpg/220px-2017_Capital_Pride_%28Washington%2C_D.C.%29_-_031.jpg" data-alt="" data-width="220" data-height="136" data-class="thumbimage"> </span></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:2017_Capital_Pride_(Washington,_D.C.)_-_031.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Leidos employees in 2017 <a href="/wiki/Capital_Pride_(Washington,_D.C.)" title="Capital Pride (Washington, D.C.)">Capital Pride</a> in Washington, D.C.</div></div></div>
<p>In August 2012, SAIC announced its plans to split into two publicly traded companies.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> The company <a href="/wiki/Corporate_spin-off" title="Corporate spin-off">spun off</a> about a third of its business, forming an approximately $4 billion-per-year service company focused on government services, including systems engineering, technical assistance, financial analysis, and program office support. The remaining part became a $7 billion-per-year IT company specializing in technology for the national security, health, and engineering sectors. The smaller company was led by Tony Moraco, who beforehand was leading SAIC's <i>Intelligence, Surveillance and Reconnaissance</i> group, and the bigger one was led by <a href="/wiki/John_P._Jumper" title="John P. Jumper">John P. Jumper</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> The split has allowed both companies to pursue more business, which it could not pursue as a single company which would have resulted in <a href="/wiki/Conflict_of_interest" title="Conflict of interest">conflicts of interest</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> In February 2013, it was announced that the smaller spin-off company would get the name "Science Applications International Corporation" and stay in the current headquarters, while the larger company would change its name to <i>Leidos</i>,<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> (created by clipping the word <i>ka<b>leidos</b>cope</i>) and would move its headquarters to <a href="/wiki/Reston,_Virginia" title="Reston, Virginia">Reston</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> The split was structured in a way that SAIC changed its name to Leidos, then spun off the new SAIC as a separate publicly-traded company. However, Leidos is the legal successor of the original SAIC and retains SAIC's pre-2013 stock price history.
</p><p>On September 27, 2013, SAIC changed its name to <i>Leidos</i> and <a href="/wiki/Corporate_spin-off" title="Corporate spin-off">spun off</a> a new and independent $4 billion government services and <a href="/wiki/Information_technology" title="Information technology">information technology</a> company which retained the <a href="/wiki/Science_Applications_International_Corporation" title="Science Applications International Corporation">Science Applications International Corporation</a> name; Leidos is the direct successor to the original SAIC.<sup id="cite_ref-Aitoro1_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Aitoro1-2">[2]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Aitoro2_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Aitoro2-3">[3]</a></sup> Before the split, Leidos employed 39,600 employees and reported $11.17 billion in revenue and $525 million net income for its fiscal year ended January 31, 2013,<sup id="cite_ref-AR2013_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AR2013-6">[6]</a></sup> making it number 240<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> on the <a href="/wiki/Fortune_500" title="Fortune 500">Fortune 500</a> list. In 2014, Leidos reported US$5.06 billion in revenue.<sup id="cite_ref-Aitoro2_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Aitoro2-3">[3]</a></sup></p><p>In August 2016, the deal to merge with the entirety of <a href="/wiki/Lockheed_Martin" title="Lockheed Martin">Lockheed Martin's</a> Information Systems &amp; Global Solutions (IS&amp;GS) business came to a close, more than doubling the size of Leidos and its portfolio, and positioning the company as the global defense industry's largest enterprise in the federal technology sector.<sup id="cite_ref-Forbes_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Forbes-26">[26]</a></sup> As of February 2019, the company has 32,000 employees.<sup id="cite_ref-leidos.com1_1-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-leidos.com1-1">[1]</a></sup> In 2018, Leidos reported US$10.19 billion in revenue. It ranked 311 on the 2019 Fortune 500 list.<sup id="cite_ref-fortune.com_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fortune.com-27">[27]</a></sup></p><p>In January 2020, Leidos purchased defense contractor <a href="/wiki/Dynetics" title="Dynetics">Dynetics</a> for approximately $1.65 billion.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(2)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Structure">Structure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Leidos&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Structure" data-section="4" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-2 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-2">
<p>Leidos has four central divisions: Civil, Health, Advanced Solutions, and Defense &amp; Intelligence. The Civil Division focuses on integrating aviation systems, securing transportation measures, modernizing IT infrastructure, and engineering energy efficiently. The Health Division focuses on optimizing medical enterprises, securing private medical data, and improving collection and data entry methods. The Advanced Solutions Division is centered around data analysis, integrating advanced defense and intelligence systems, and increasing surveillance and reconnaissance efficiency. The Defense &amp; Intelligence Division focuses on providing air service systems, geospatial analysis, cybersecurity, intelligence analysis, and supporting operations efforts.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(3)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Management">Management</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Leidos&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Management" data-section="5" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-3 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-3">
<ul><li>CEO: <a href="/wiki/Roger_Krone" title="Roger Krone">Roger Krone</a><sup id="cite_ref-wspa_krone_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wspa_krone-30">[30]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-leidos_krone_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-leidos_krone-31">[31]</a></sup></li></ul><p>After more than 30 years of Beyster's leadership, <a href="/wiki/Kenneth_C._Dahlberg" title="Kenneth C. Dahlberg">Kenneth C. Dahlberg</a> was named the CEO of SAIC in November 2003.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> In May 2005, the company changed its external tagline from <i>An Employee-Owned Company</i> to <i>From Science to Solutions</i>.
</p><p>The third CEO was <a href="/wiki/Walt_Havenstein" title="Walt Havenstein">Walt Havenstein</a>, who pushed for tighter integration of the company's historically autonomous divisions, which led to lower profit and revenue. The strategy was reversed by the fourth CEO, retired Air Force general <a href="/wiki/John_P._Jumper" title="John P. Jumper">John P. Jumper</a>, appointed in 2012.<sup id="cite_ref-Censer_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Censer-33">[33]</a></sup> On July 1, 2014 Leidos announced that <a href="/wiki/Roger_Krone" title="Roger Krone">Roger Krone</a> would become its CEO on July 14, 2014.<sup id="cite_ref-wspa_krone_30-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wspa_krone-30">[30]</a></sup> As of 2019, Krone is the Chairman and CEO of Leidos.<sup id="cite_ref-leidos_krone_31-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-leidos_krone-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(4)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Headquarters">Headquarters</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Leidos&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Headquarters" data-section="6" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-4 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-4">
<p>In January 2018, Leidos announced it would move within <a href="/wiki/Reston,_VA" class="mw-redirect" title="Reston, VA">Reston, VA</a>, a quarter mile from 11951 Freedom Drive to 1750 Presidents Street in a new building due for completion in early 2020, part of the new "Reston Gateway" area.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup></p>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(5)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Operations">Operations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Leidos&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Operations" data-section="7" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-5 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-5">
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Defense_Intelligence_Agency" title="Defense Intelligence Agency">Defense Intelligence Agency</a> (DIA) transitioned a Remote Viewing Program to SAIC in 1991 which was renamed <a href="/wiki/Stargate_Project" title="Stargate Project">Stargate Project</a>.
</p><p>In March 2001 SAIC defined the concept for the <a href="/wiki/National_Security_Agency" title="National Security Agency">NSA</a> <a href="/wiki/Trailblazer_Project" title="Trailblazer Project">Trailblazer Project</a>. In 2002, NSA contracted SAIC for $280 million to produce a "technology demonstration platform"<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (February 2014)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> for the agency's project, a "<a href="/wiki/Digital_Network_Intelligence" title="Digital Network Intelligence">Digital Network Intelligence</a>" system to analyze data carried on computer networks. Other project participants included <a href="/wiki/Boeing" title="Boeing">Boeing</a>, <a href="/wiki/Computer_Sciences_Corporation" title="Computer Sciences Corporation">Computer Sciences Corporation</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Booz_Allen_Hamilton" title="Booz Allen Hamilton">Booz Allen Hamilton</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> According to science news site <a href="/wiki/PhysOrg.com" class="mw-redirect" title="PhysOrg.com">PhysOrg.com</a>, Trailblazer was a continuation of the earlier <a href="/wiki/ThinThread" title="ThinThread">ThinThread</a> program.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> In 2005, NSA director <a href="/wiki/Michael_Hayden_(general)" title="Michael Hayden (general)">Michael Hayden</a> told a Senate hearing that the Trailblazer program was several hundred million dollars over budget and years behind schedule.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup></p><p>In fiscal year 2003, SAIC did more than $2.6 billion in business with the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Defense" title="United States Department of Defense">United States Department of Defense</a>, making it the ninth-largest defense contractor in the United States. Other large contracts included a bid for <a href="/wiki/Information_technology" title="Information technology">information technology</a> for the <a href="/wiki/2004_Olympics" class="mw-redirect" title="2004 Olympics">2004 Olympics</a> in Greece.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup></p><p>From 2001 to 2005, SAIC was the primary contractor for the <a href="/wiki/FBI" class="mw-redirect" title="FBI">FBI</a>'s unsuccessful <a href="/wiki/Virtual_Case_File" title="Virtual Case File">Virtual Case File</a> project.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup></p><p>During fiscal year 2012 (latest figure available), SAIC had more than doubled its business with the DoD to $5,988,489,000, and was the 4th-largest defense contractor on the annual list of the top 100.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> Leidos ranked 292 on the 2017 Fortune 500 list.<sup id="cite_ref-fortune.com_27-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fortune.com-27">[27]</a></sup></p>

<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="As_SAIC_2">As SAIC</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Leidos&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: As SAIC" data-section="11" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>Then-SAIC had as part of its management and on its board of directors, many well-known ex-government personnel including <a href="/wiki/Melvin_Laird" title="Melvin Laird">Melvin Laird</a>, Secretary of Defense in the <a href="/wiki/Nixon_administration" class="mw-redirect" title="Nixon administration">Nixon administration</a>; <a href="/wiki/William_Perry" title="William Perry">William Perry</a>, Secretary of Defense for Bill Clinton; <a href="/wiki/John_M._Deutch" title="John M. Deutch">John M. Deutch</a>, President Clinton's CIA Director; Admiral <a href="/wiki/Bobby_Ray_Inman" title="Bobby Ray Inman">Bobby Ray Inman</a> who served in various capacities in the NSA and CIA for the Ford, Carter and Reagan administrations; and <a href="/wiki/David_Kay" title="David Kay">David Kay</a> who led the search for weapons of mass destruction after the 1991 Gulf War and served under the Bush Administration after the 2003 Iraq invasion.  In 2012, 26 out of 35 SAIC Inc. lobbyists previously held government jobs.<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup></p><p>In June 2001 the <a href="/wiki/Federal_Bureau_of_Investigation" title="Federal Bureau of Investigation">FBI</a> paid SAIC $122 million to create a <a href="/wiki/Virtual_Case_File" title="Virtual Case File">Virtual Case File</a> (VCF) software system to speed up the sharing of information among agents. But the FBI abandoned VCF when it failed to function adequately. <a href="/wiki/Robert_Mueller" title="Robert Mueller">Robert Mueller</a>, FBI Director, testified to a congressional committee, "When SAIC delivered the first product in December 2003 we immediately identified a number of deficiencies – 17 at the outset. That soon cascaded to 50 or more and ultimately to 400 problems with that software ... We were indeed disappointed."<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup></p><p>In 2005, then-SAIC executive vice president <a href="/wiki/Arnold_L._Punaro" title="Arnold L. Punaro">Arnold L. Punaro</a> claimed that the company had "fully conformed to the contract we have and gave the taxpayers real value for their money." He blamed the FBI for the initial problems, saying the agency had a parade of program managers and demanded too many design changes. He stated that during 15 months that SAIC worked on the program, 19 different government managers were involved and 36 contract modifications were ordered.<sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup> "There were an average of 1.3 changes every day from the FBI, for a total of 399 changes during the period," Punaro said.<sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup></p><p>In 2011-2012, then-SAIC was among the 8 top contributors to federal candidates, parties, and outside groups with $1,209,611 during the 2011–2012 election cycle according to information from the Federal Election Commission. The top candidate recipient was Barack Obama.<sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="As_Leidos_2">As Leidos</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Leidos&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: As Leidos" data-section="12" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>In a heavily redacted report dated January 3, 2018, the <a href="/wiki/Office_of_the_Inspector_General,_U.S._Department_of_Defense" title="Office of the Inspector General, U.S. Department of Defense">Inspector General for the Department of Defense</a> determined that a supervisor at Leidos made “inappropriate sexual and racial comments to” a female contractor, and that when she complained of a <a href="/wiki/Hostile_work_environment" title="Hostile work environment">hostile work environment</a>, Leidos retaliated by excluding her from further work on an additional contract.<sup id="cite_ref-Capaccio2018_59-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Capaccio2018-59">[59]</a></sup> The report found  that Leidos's claim that the contract employee “exhibited poor performance throughout her employment" lacked supporting evidence. It recommended that <a href="/wiki/U.S._Secretary_of_Defense" class="mw-redirect" title="U.S. Secretary of Defense">U.S. Secretary of Defense</a> <a href="/wiki/Jim_Mattis" title="Jim Mattis">Jim Mattis</a> “consider appropriate action against Leidos” such as “compensatory damages, including back pay, employee benefits and other terms and conditions of employment” that the contractor would have received under the additional contract.
</p>
