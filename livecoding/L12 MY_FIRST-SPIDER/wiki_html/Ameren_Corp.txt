</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(1)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ameren&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History" data-section="1" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-1 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-1">
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Origins">Origins</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ameren&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Origins" data-section="2" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>Prior to the formation of Ameren, the first major development in the history of its constituent parts occurred in 1929, when the <a href="/wiki/Bagnell_Dam" title="Bagnell Dam">Bagnell Dam</a> was completed on the Osage River and generated almost 175 megawatts of hydroelectricity for Missouri's <b>Union Electric Company</b>. The dam also created the <a href="/wiki/Lake_of_the_Ozarks" title="Lake of the Ozarks">Lake of the Ozarks</a> with 1,400 miles (2,300 km) of shoreline.<sup id="cite_ref-ameren1902-2002_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ameren1902-2002-8">[8]</a></sup></p><p>In 1931, Union Electric Light and Power sought additional generating sources (<a href="/wiki/Interurban" title="Interurban">interurbans</a> being one need) and the company began buying power from the <a href="/wiki/Lock_and_Dam_No._19" title="Lock and Dam No. 19">Keokuk, IA dam</a>, 150 miles (240 km) north of St. Louis.  Union Electric later bought the dam, providing 134 megawatts of hydroelectricity carried over a longer distance than had ever been achieved before.
</p><p>By the 1950s Union Electric owned gas operations in and around <a href="/wiki/Alton,_Illinois" title="Alton, Illinois">Alton, Illinois</a>, and acquired other utilities to become the third largest distributor of natural gas in Missouri.<sup id="cite_ref-ameren1902-2002_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ameren1902-2002-8">[8]</a></sup></p><p>In 1952, Ameren's second major constituent, the <b>Central Illinois Public Service Company</b>, became a major pooled energy power distributor with its future Ameren mate, Union Electric Company. The arrangement formed the Midwest Power Pool system. The CIPS <a href="/wiki/Meredosia,_Illinois" title="Meredosia, Illinois">Meredosia, Illinois</a> Power Station became a key contributor to the pool, which also included the later Ameren subsidiary <b>Illinois Power Company</b>.<sup id="cite_ref-ameren1902-2002_8-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ameren1902-2002-8">[8]</a></sup></p><p>In 1963 Union Electric completed construction of one of the largest pumped storage plants at that time, the then-350-megawatt Taum Sauk Plant, in <a href="/wiki/Reynolds_County,_Missouri" title="Reynolds County, Missouri">Reynolds County, Missouri</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-ameren1902-2002_8-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ameren1902-2002-8">[8]</a></sup></p><p>In 1984 Union Electric added nuclear energy to the mix, when the <a href="/wiki/Callaway_Nuclear_Generating_Station" title="Callaway Nuclear Generating Station">Callaway Nuclear Generating Station</a> began providing 1,143 megawatts of power from <a href="/wiki/Callaway_County,_Missouri" title="Callaway County, Missouri">Callaway County, Missouri</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-ameren1902-2002_8-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ameren1902-2002-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Ameren_history">Ameren history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ameren&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Ameren history" data-section="3" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>In 1995 shareholders of both CIPSCO Inc. and of its neighboring utility of twice its size, the <a href="/wiki/S%26P_500" class="mw-redirect" title="S&amp;P 500">S&amp;P 500</a>-listed Union Electric Company, approved the merger of the two companies, which were to then be combined as <b>Ameren Corporation</b>.<sup id="cite_ref-Ameren-Jan-1998-8-K_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ameren-Jan-1998-8-K-4">[4]</a></sup> Both of those former utilities had traded publicly on the New York Stock Exchange, under ticker symbol CIP and UEP, respectively. At the time of the merger, Union Electric had assets of nearly US$600 million, but still carried nearly US$1.8 billion in long-term debt, although down from US$2.5 billion which it had accumulated by the 1980s.<sup id="cite_ref-S&amp;Pguide9604_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-S&amp;Pguide9604-9">[9]</a></sup> CIPSCO had assets of about US$210 million, but still carried nearly half of US$1 billion in long-term debt, which it had also accumulated by the 1980s.<sup id="cite_ref-S&amp;Pguide9604_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-S&amp;Pguide9604-9">[9]</a></sup></p><p>The merger was completed on December 31, 1997, when the two public companies became one, as Ameren Corporation, which then began to trade publicly on the New York Stock Exchange with the ticker symbol AEE.<sup id="cite_ref-Ameren-Jan-1998-8-K_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ameren-Jan-1998-8-K-4">[4]</a></sup></p><p>The name Ameren comes from the combination of American electric
</p><p>Following the merger, Union Electric began doing business as <b>AmerenUE</b>, now known as Ameren Missouri. Today, with nine power plants Ameren Missouri serves 1.2 million power customers and 110,000 gas customers, primarily in Missouri, where more than half of its customers reside in the <a href="/wiki/St._Louis" title="St. Louis">St. Louis</a> metropolitan area. It also served Iowa as well through the mid-1990s, and served adjoining parts of Illinois until 2010.
</p><p>The former CIPSCO Inc. utility, Central Illinois Public Service Company, became Ameren's other operating company, doing business as <b>AmerenCIPS</b>.<sup id="cite_ref-Ameren-Jan-1998-8-K_4-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ameren-Jan-1998-8-K-4">[4]</a></sup></p><p>In 2000, Ameren formed the holding company, <b>AmerenEnergy Resources</b>.<sup id="cite_ref-Ameren-Mar-2010-8-K_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ameren-Mar-2010-8-K-10">[10]</a></sup> It contained two further subsidiaries, <b>AmerenEnergy Marketing</b>, and <b>AmerenEnergy Generating</b>.<sup id="cite_ref-Ameren-Mar-2000-10-K_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ameren-Mar-2000-10-K-11">[11]</a></sup></p><p>In 2002, Ameren Corporation announced a voluntary retirement program, which was offered to approximately 1,000 of Ameren's 7,400 current employees, expecting to realize significant long-term savings.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p><p>In 2003, Ameren acquired <a href="/wiki/Peoria,_Illinois" title="Peoria, Illinois">Peoria</a>-based CILCORP, Inc. and its leading subsidiary, <b>Central Illinois Light Company</b>, from <a href="/wiki/AES_Corporation" title="AES Corporation">AES Corporation</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Ameren-Jan-2003-8-K_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ameren-Jan-2003-8-K-13">[13]</a></sup> CILCORP had traded on the NYSE with ticker symbol CER prior to its acquisition by AES, and by the mid-1990s had become a member of the S&amp;P Small Cap 600 index. CILCO had been another pioneer utility in the region, which had paid a dividend since 1921. By 1996, it had grown to over US$150 million in assets, and carried US$330 million in long-term debt.<sup id="cite_ref-S&amp;Pguide9604_9-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-S&amp;Pguide9604-9">[9]</a></sup> Following the 2003 Ameren acquisition, that utility began doing business as <b>AmerenCILCO</b>.<sup id="cite_ref-Ameren-Jan-2003-8-K_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ameren-Jan-2003-8-K-13">[13]</a></sup></p><p>At the end of 2003, Ameren's chairman and chief executive, Charles Mueller, retired and was succeeded in both positions by Gary Rainwater, the company's president and chief operating officer the past two years.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p><p>In 2004, Ameren acquired (from <a href="/wiki/Dynegy" title="Dynegy">Dynegy</a> Inc.) its third partner from the 1952 Midwest Power Pool system, <b>Illinois Power Company</b>.<sup id="cite_ref-Ameren-Mar-2004-8-K_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ameren-Mar-2004-8-K-15">[15]</a></sup> That utility had traded publicly on the NYSE under the ticker symbol IPC through the 1980s, and paid dividends since 1947. As of the late 1980s, the company generated electricity and natural gas, almost entirely from coal plants, with less than 1% fueled from oil and gas. By then, with about $360 million in assets, it carried long-term debt of over US$2 billion.<sup id="cite_ref-S&amp;Pguide8712_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-S&amp;Pguide8712-16">[16]</a></sup> In 1991, Illinois Power  became the subsidiary of holding company Illinova Corp., which traded on the NYSE with ticker symbol ILN. Illinova had grown to an S&amp;P Midcap 400 stock by 1996, with over US$415 million in assets, and had brought the IP utility's debt down to US$1.8 billion by then.<sup id="cite_ref-S&amp;Pguide9604_9-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-S&amp;Pguide9604-9">[9]</a></sup> In a merger completed February 1, 2000, Illinova Corp. became a wholly owned subsidiary of Dynegy Inc. (<a href="/wiki/New_York_Stock_Exchange" title="New York Stock Exchange">NYSE</a>: <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://www.nyse.com/quote/XNYS:DYN">DYN</a>), in which <a href="/wiki/Chevron_Corporation" title="Chevron Corporation">Chevron Corporation</a> also took a 28% stake. Dynegy in turn had been created in June 1998, from the merger of Chevron's natural gas and natural gas liquids businesses with Dynegy's predecessor, NGC Corp. (former ticker NGL). NGC had been an integrated natural gas services company around since 1994.<sup id="cite_ref-chevron_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-chevron-17">[17]</a></sup> Following Ameren's acquisition of Illinois Power, that subsidiary began doing business as <b>AmerenIP</b>.<sup id="cite_ref-Ameren-Feb-2004-8-K_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ameren-Feb-2004-8-K-18">[18]</a></sup> The IP acquisition made Ameren the major investor-owned power company in downstate Illinois.
</p><p>In December 2004, Ameren announced that Patrick T. Stokes, the president and chief executive officer of Anheuser-Busch Cos., Inc., was elected to the Ameren board of directors.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p><p>In 2009, AmerenUE signed an agreement to purchase 102 megawatts (MW) of wind power from phase II of <a href="/wiki/Horizon_Wind_Energy" class="mw-redirect" title="Horizon Wind Energy">Horizon Wind Energy</a>'s Pioneer Prairie Wind Farm in Iowa.  That's enough to power 26,000 households.  The power AmerenUE is purchasing will tie into the Midwest Independent System Operator (MISO) transmission grid, of which the company is a member, fulfilling AmerenUE's commitment to add 100 megawatts of renewable capacity to serve its Missouri customers by 2010.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p><p>On October 1, 2010 Ameren's three Illinois operating companies merged to become Ameren Illinois Company.<sup id="cite_ref-Ameren-Mar-2010-8-K_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ameren-Mar-2010-8-K-10">[10]</a></sup> The merger resulted in a single operating company providing power to most of downstate Illinois. However, the Ameren Illinois service territory is split into three rate zones corresponding to the service territories of the three former Illinois operating companies—Zone I (the former AmerenCIPS), Zone II (the former AmerenCILCO) and Zone III (the former AmerenIP). On the same date, AmerenUE changed its name to Ameren Missouri Company.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> The former Illinois portion of the AmerenUE territory was transferred to Ameren Illinois Zone I.
</p><p>In 2015, Ameren became the first major energy company to open an Innovation Center at the <a href="/wiki/Research_Park,_University_of_Illinois_at_Urbana-Champaign" class="mw-redirect" title="Research Park, University of Illinois at Urbana-Champaign">Research Park, University of Illinois at Urbana-Champaign</a>.
</p><p>In 2016, Ameren was named 10th on <a href="/wiki/Business_Insider" title="Business Insider">Business Insider</a>’s 10 best energy companies to work for in America list. In the report, seventy four percent of employees said their job has high meaning.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>

</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(3)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Taum_Sauk_pumped_storage_plant">Taum Sauk pumped storage plant</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ameren&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Taum Sauk pumped storage plant" data-section="5" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-3 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-3">
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Taum_Sauk_pumped_storage_plant" class="mw-redirect" title="Taum Sauk pumped storage plant">Taum Sauk pumped storage plant</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Taum_Sauk_reservoir-FERC_12-22-05_01.jpg" class="image"><noscript><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/76/Taum_Sauk_reservoir-FERC_12-22-05_01.jpg/220px-Taum_Sauk_reservoir-FERC_12-22-05_01.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="400" data-file-height="300"></noscript><span class="lazy-image-placeholder" style="width: 220px;height: 165px;" data-src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/76/Taum_Sauk_reservoir-FERC_12-22-05_01.jpg/220px-Taum_Sauk_reservoir-FERC_12-22-05_01.jpg" data-alt="" data-width="220" data-height="165" data-class="thumbimage"> </span></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Taum_Sauk_reservoir-FERC_12-22-05_01.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A large section of the Taum Sauk upper reservoir failed, draining over a billion gallons of water in less than half an hour.</div></div></div>
<p>Ameren Missouri owns the <a href="/wiki/Taum_Sauk_pumped_storage_plant" class="mw-redirect" title="Taum Sauk pumped storage plant">Taum Sauk pumped storage plant</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> which failed on December 14, 2005, causing extensive damage to the east fork of the Black River and to <a href="/wiki/Johnson%27s_Shut-Ins_State_Park" title="Johnson's Shut-Ins State Park">Johnson's Shut-Ins State Park</a>.  Consequently, <a href="/wiki/FERC" class="mw-redirect" title="FERC">FERC</a> fined Ameren $15 million.  The State of Missouri has sued Ameren for actual and punitive damages, alleging Ameren recklessly operated the plant and put financial considerations from sale of power to other companies over safety, maintenance and engineering.  The plant was operated by remote control with no one onsite during pumping operations. Rebuilding the upper reservoir of the AmerenUE Taum Sauk pumped-storage hydroelectric plant cost $450 million.  The upper reservoir was constructed with roller compacted concrete (RCC); and online in 2010, this 54.5-acre (221,000 m<sup>2</sup>) reservoir is the largest RCC dam in North America.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup>
The 450-megawatt Taum Sauk pumped storage hydroelectric plant began generating electricity again on April 21, 2010.
</p>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(4)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Environmental_issues">Environmental issues</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ameren&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Environmental issues" data-section="6" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-4 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-4">
<p>In 2011 the United States Environmental Protection Agency (EPA) filed suit (Case No. 4:11 CV 77 RWS) against Ameren Missouri concerning excess sulfur (SO2) emissions from the Rush Island Plant in Festus, Missouri <cite><i>United States v. Ameren Missouri</i></cite>, 229 F.Supp.3d 906 (2017) 48295 (D.C., E.D. Missouri, Eastern Division. 01/23/17).  
EPA alleged Ameren violated the Clean Air Act, the Missouri State Implementation Plan, and Ameren's Rush Island Plant Title V Permit when it undertook major modifications at the Rush Island Plant in 2007 through 2010 without obtaining the required permits under the Prevention of Significant Deterioration (PSD) provisions of the New Source Review (NSR) program.
</p><p>In January 2017 the District Court found that Ameren "...violated the Clean Air Act without obtaining the necessary permits, installing best-available pollution technology, and otherwise meeting applicable requirements." Judge Rodney Sippel entered a finding of liability in January 2017, and the court proceedings currently underway will establish remedies.
</p><p>Ameren has a history of supporting the adoption of <a href="/wiki/Electric_vehicle" title="Electric vehicle">electric vehicles</a>. In 2016, the company attempted to launch a pilot program to install electric vehicle charging stations but state utility regulators turned down oversight of the project. As a result, Ameren canceled the program because it would not recover its investments. However, in early 2018 Ameren tried again, this time launching a program called "Charge Ahead" which incentivizes the installation of around 1,200 charging stations. In addition, the program creates incentives for the adoption of commercial electric vehicles.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p><p>In nineteenth and twentieth centuries a local utility company operated a coal gasification plant at the corner of Ash and Orr streets in <a href="/wiki/Columbia,_Missouri" title="Columbia, Missouri">Columbia, Missouri</a>, that was subsequently demolished. The company was purchased by the Ameren Corporation. The <a href="/wiki/Gasification" title="Gasification">gasification</a> process contaminated the soil and some groundwater in the area with <a href="/wiki/Carcinogenic" class="mw-redirect" title="Carcinogenic">carcinogenic</a> chemicals. In June 2014 Ameren was removing contaminated soil from the area, and expected to finish by September 2014.<sup id="cite_ref-mgp20140521_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mgp20140521-27">[27]</a></sup> As of 2019 the city is considering purchasing the lot to create a public park.<sup id="cite_ref-Business_Times_Ameren_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Business_Times_Ameren-28">[28]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Missourian_Ameren_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Missourian_Ameren-29">[29]</a></sup> Ameren has been criticized by the North Village Board of Directors for owning "a whole city block that is in disrepair and for an aversion to commerce."<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p><p>Ameren Missouri sponsors the Adopt-the-Shoreline program, which enables people to “adopt” portions of the shoreline for litter control. They also control other shoreline activities in the <a href="/wiki/Lake_of_the_Ozarks" title="Lake of the Ozarks">Lake of the Ozarks</a> such as the regulation of boat docks, seawalls and other structures within the Osage Project boundary on the lake and downstream from <a href="/wiki/Bagnell_Dam" title="Bagnell Dam">Bagnell Dam</a>.  The company maintains the Lake and Shoreline Protection Hotline that anyone who wishes can use to report neglected docks or other concerns they may have about the lake.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(5)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Former_manufactured_gas_plant">Former manufactured gas plant</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ameren&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Former manufactured gas plant" data-section="7" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-5 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-5">
<p>A predecessor of Ameren operated a coal gasification plant at the corner of Ash and Orr streets in <a href="/wiki/Columbia,_Missouri" title="Columbia, Missouri">Columbia, Missouri</a>, which was subsequently demolished. The gasification process contaminated the soil and some groundwater in the area with potentially cancer-causing chemicals. Ameren has since finished removing contaminated soil as of September 2014.<sup id="cite_ref-mgp20140521_27-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mgp20140521-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
