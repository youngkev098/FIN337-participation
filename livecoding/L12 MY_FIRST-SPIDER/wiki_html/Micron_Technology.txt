For for the Russian microelectronics company, see <a href="/wiki/Mikron_Group" title="Mikron Group">Mikron Group</a>.</div>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">For other uses, see <a href="/wiki/Micron_(disambiguation)" class="mw-disambig" title="Micron (disambiguation)">Micron (disambiguation)</a>.</div>
<p><b>Micron Technology, Inc.</b> is an American producer of <a href="/wiki/Computer_memory" title="Computer memory">computer memory</a> and <a href="/wiki/Computer_data_storage" title="Computer data storage">computer data storage</a> including <a href="/wiki/Dynamic_random-access_memory" title="Dynamic random-access memory">dynamic random-access memory</a>, <a href="/wiki/Flash_memory" title="Flash memory">flash memory</a>, and <a href="/wiki/USB_flash_drive" title="USB flash drive">USB flash drives</a>. It is headquartered in <a href="/wiki/Boise,_Idaho" title="Boise, Idaho">Boise, Idaho</a>. Its consumer products are marketed under the brands Crucial<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>  and Ballistix. Micron and <a href="/wiki/Intel" title="Intel">Intel</a> together created <a href="/wiki/IM_Flash_Technologies" title="IM Flash Technologies">IM Flash Technologies</a>, which produces <a href="/wiki/NAND_flash_memory" class="mw-redirect" title="NAND flash memory">NAND flash memory</a>. It owned <a href="/wiki/Lexar" title="Lexar">Lexar</a> between 2006<sup id="cite_ref-lexar_acquire_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-lexar_acquire-4">[4]</a></sup> and 2017.<sup </section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(1)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Micron_Technology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History" data-section="1" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-1 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-1">
<h3 class="in-block"><span id="1978.E2.80.931999"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="1978–1999">1978–1999</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Micron_Technology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: 1978–1999" data-section="2" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>Micron was founded in Boise, Idaho, in 1978<sup id="cite_ref-Milestones_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Milestones-6">[6]</a></sup> by Ward Parkinson, Joe Parkinson, Dennis Wilson, and Doug Pitman as a semiconductor design consulting company.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Startup funding was provided by local Idaho businessmen Tom Nicholson, Allen Noble, Rudolph Nelson, and Ron Yanke. Later it received funding from Idaho billionaire <a href="/wiki/J._R._Simplot" title="J. R. Simplot">J. R. Simplot</a>, whose fortune was made in the <a href="/wiki/Potato" title="Potato">potato</a> business. In 1981, the company moved from consulting to manufacturing with the completion of its first <a href="/wiki/Semiconductor_fabrication_plant" title="Semiconductor fabrication plant">wafer fabrication</a> unit ("Fab 1"), producing 64K DRAM chips.
</p><p>In 1984, the company <a href="/wiki/Initial_public_offering" title="Initial public offering">went public</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p><p>In 1994, founder Joe Parkinson retired as <a href="/wiki/Chief_executive_officer" title="Chief executive officer">CEO</a> and <a href="/wiki/Steve_Appleton" title="Steve Appleton">Steve Appleton</a> took over as <a href="/wiki/Chairman" class="mw-redirect" title="Chairman">Chairman</a>, <a href="/wiki/President_(corporate_title)" title="President (corporate title)">President</a>, and CEO.<sup id="cite_ref-Milestones_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Milestones-6">[6]</a></sup></p><p>A 1996 3-way merger among <a href="/wiki/Zeos" title="Zeos">ZEOS International</a>, Micron Computer, and Micron Custom Manufacturing Services (MCMS) increased the size and scope of the company;<sup id="cite_ref-Milestones_6-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Milestones-6">[6]</a></sup> this was followed rapidly with the 1997 acquisition of NetFrame Systems, in a bid to enter the mid-range server industry.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span id="2000.E2.80.93present"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="2000–present">2000–present</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Micron_Technology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: 2000–present" data-section="3" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>In 2000, <a href="/wiki/Gurtej_Sandhu" title="Gurtej Sandhu">Gurtej Singh Sandhu</a> and Trung T. Doan at Micron initiated the development of <a href="/wiki/Atomic_layer_deposition" title="Atomic layer deposition">atomic layer deposition</a> high-k <a href="/wiki/Thin_film" title="Thin film">films</a> for <a href="/wiki/Dynamic_random-access_memory" title="Dynamic random-access memory">DRAM</a> memory devices. This helped drive cost-effective implementation of <a href="/wiki/Semiconductor_memory" title="Semiconductor memory">semiconductor memory</a>, starting with <a href="/wiki/90_nanometer" class="mw-redirect" title="90 nanometer">90-nm</a> <a href="/wiki/Semiconductor_node" class="mw-redirect" title="Semiconductor node">node</a> DRAM.<sup id="cite_ref-ieee_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ieee-10">[10]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Pitch <a href="/wiki/Double_patterning" class="mw-redirect" title="Double patterning">double-patterning</a> was also pioneered by Gurtej Singh Sandhu at Micron during the 2000s, leading to the development of <a href="/wiki/32_nanometer" class="mw-redirect" title="32 nanometer">30-nm</a> class <a href="/wiki/NAND_flash" class="mw-redirect" title="NAND flash">NAND flash</a> memory, and it has since been widely adopted by NAND flash and <a href="/wiki/RAM_memory" class="mw-redirect" title="RAM memory">RAM memory</a> manufacturers worldwide.<sup id="cite_ref-ieee_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ieee-10">[10]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p><p>Micron and Intel created a joint venture in 2005, based in IM Flash Technologies in <a href="/wiki/Lehi,_Utah" title="Lehi, Utah">Lehi</a>, Utah.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> The two companies formed another joint venture in 2011, <a href="/wiki/IM_Flash_Singapore" title="IM Flash Singapore">IM Flash Singapore</a>, in Singapore.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> In 2012, Micron became sole owner of this second joint venture.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p><p>In 2006, Micron acquired <a href="/wiki/Lexar" title="Lexar">Lexar</a>, an American manufacturer of digital media products.<sup id="cite_ref-lexar_acquire_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-lexar_acquire-4">[4]</a></sup></p><p>The company again changed leadership in June 2007 with COO <a href="/wiki/Mark_Durcan" title="Mark Durcan">Mark Durcan</a> becoming President.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p><p>In 2008, Micron spun off <a href="/wiki/Aptina" title="Aptina">Aptina Imaging</a>, which was acquired by <a href="/wiki/ON_Semiconductor" title="ON Semiconductor">ON Semiconductor</a> in 2014. Micron retained a stake in the spinoff.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> The core company suffered setbacks, however, requiring layoffs of 15 percent of its workforce in October 2008,<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> during which period the company also announced the purchase of <a href="/wiki/Qimonda" title="Qimonda">Qimonda</a>'s stake in <a href="/wiki/Inotera" title="Inotera">Inotera</a> technologies for $400 million.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> The trend of layoffs and acquisitions continued in 2009 with the termination of an additional 2,000 employees,<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> and the acquisition of the FLCOS microdisplay company Displaytech.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> Micron agreed to buy flash-chip maker <a href="/wiki/Numonyx" title="Numonyx">Numonyx</a> for $1.27 billion in stock in February 2010.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p><p>On February 3, 2012, the CEO, <a href="/wiki/Steve_Appleton" title="Steve Appleton">Steve Appleton</a>, died in a small <a href="/wiki/Lancair" title="Lancair">Lancair</a> plane crash in Boise, Idaho.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> Mark Durcan replaced Appleton as the CEO shortly thereafter,<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> eliminating his former title of President.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p><p>In the 2012–2014 period, Micron again went through an acquisition-layoff cycle, becoming the majority shareholder of Inotera Memories, purchasing <a href="/wiki/Elpida_Memory" title="Elpida Memory">Elpida Memory</a><sup id="cite_ref-elpida_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-elpida-30">[30]</a></sup> for $2 billion and the remaining shares in Rexchip, a PC memory chip manufacturing venture between <a href="/wiki/Powerchip" title="Powerchip">Powerchip</a> and Elpida Memory for $334 million,<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> while announcing plans to lay off approximately 3,000 workers.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> Through the Elpida acquisition, Micron became a major supplier to <a href="/wiki/Apple_Inc." title="Apple Inc.">Apple Inc.</a> for the <a href="/wiki/IPhone" title="IPhone">iPhone</a> and <a href="/wiki/IPad" title="IPad">iPad</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-elpida_30-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-elpida-30">[30]</a></sup></p><p>In December 2016, Micron finished acquiring the remaining 67% of <a href="/wiki/Inotera" title="Inotera">Inotera</a>’s stake and made Inotera a 100% subsidiary of Micron in Taiwan.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></p><p>In April 2017, Micron announced <a href="/wiki/Sanjay_Mehrotra" title="Sanjay Mehrotra">Sanjay Mehrotra</a> as the new President and CEO to replace <a href="/wiki/Mark_Durcan" title="Mark Durcan">Mark Durcan</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></p><p>In June 2017, Micron announced it was discontinuing the Lexar retail removable media storage business and putting some or all it up for sale.<sup id="cite_ref-lexar_discontinue_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-lexar_discontinue-38">[38]</a></sup> In August of that year the Lexar brand was acquired by Longsys, a flash memory company based in Shenzhen, China.<sup id="cite_ref-lexar_sale_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-lexar_sale-5">[5]</a></sup></p><p>On December 5, 2017, Micron sued rivals United Microelectronics Corporation and Fujian Jinhua Integrated Circuit Co. (JHICC) in the United States District Court for the Northern District of California, alleging infringement on its DRAM patents and intellectual property rights.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup></p><p>In May 2018, Micron Technology and Intel launched QLC NAND memory to increase storage density.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> The company ranked 150th on the <a href="/wiki/Fortune_500" title="Fortune 500">Fortune 500</a> list of largest United States corporations by revenue.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup></p><p>In February 2019, the first <a href="/wiki/MicroSD_card" class="mw-redirect" title="MicroSD card">microSD card</a> with a storage capacity of 1 <a href="/wiki/Terabyte" title="Terabyte">terabyte</a> (TB) was announced by Micron.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup></p><p>As of March 2020, 3.84TB Micron 5210 Ion is the cheapest large capacity SSD in the world.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup></p>
