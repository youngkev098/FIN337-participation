<p><b>Prologis, Inc.</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Real_estate_investment_trust" title="Real estate investment trust">real estate investment trust</a> headquartered in <a href="/wiki/San_Francisco,_California" class="mw-redirect" title="San Francisco, California">San Francisco, California</a> that invests in logistics facilities, with a focus on the consumption side of the global supply chain.<sup id="cite_ref-10K_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10K-1">[1]</a></sup> The company was formed through the merger of AMB Property Corporation and ProLogis in June 2011, which made Prologis the largest industrial real estate company in the world.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-wsj-rents_3-0" </section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(1)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Prologis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History" data-section="1" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-1 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-1">
<p>Prologis was formed in 2011 through the merger of AMB and ProLogis, both multinational real estate companies based in the United States.<sup id="cite_ref-giants_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-giants-7">[7]</a></sup></p><p>In 1983, <a href="/wiki/Hamid_R._Moghadam" class="mw-redirect" title="Hamid R. Moghadam">Hamid R. Moghadam</a> and Doug Abbey used a $50,000 line of credit to found Abbey, Moghadam and Company,<sup id="cite_ref-future_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-future-8">[8]</a></sup> an investment manager serving institutions.<sup id="cite_ref-robaton_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-robaton-9">[9]</a></sup> They were joined by T. Robert Burke in 1984 and established AMB Property Corporation, which invested in office, industrial and community <a href="/wiki/Shopping_centers" class="mw-redirect" title="Shopping centers">shopping centers</a> on behalf of large <a href="/wiki/Institutional_investor" title="Institutional investor">institutional investors</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-future_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-future-8">[8]</a></sup> During the <a href="/wiki/Savings_and_loan_crisis" title="Savings and loan crisis">savings and loan crisis</a>, the company avoided significant financial repercussions by investing in industrial parks and shopping centers, and began to exit the office market in 1987.<sup id="cite_ref-future_8-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-future-8">[8]</a></sup> AMB launched its first private equity fund in 1989, which focused on industrial and retail properties.<sup id="cite_ref-robaton_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-robaton-9">[9]</a></sup></p><p>In late 1997,<sup id="cite_ref-robaton_9-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-robaton-9">[9]</a></sup> AMB became a <a href="/wiki/Public_company" title="Public company">public company</a> via an <a href="/wiki/Initial_public_offering" title="Initial public offering">initial public offering</a>, with more than US$2.8 billion under management.<sup id="cite_ref-future_8-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-future-8">[8]</a></sup></p><p>In 1999, the company sold its retail business to focus solely on the industrial sector. The company made its first overseas investment in 2002, developing a facility for <a href="/wiki/Procter_%26_Gamble" title="Procter &amp; Gamble">Procter &amp; Gamble</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-robaton_9-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-robaton-9">[9]</a></sup> That year, AMB initiated an international expansion program focused on buying and developing distribution facilities near global trade hubs, particularly in growth markets such as <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">Brazil</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">Mexico</a>, and <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-future_8-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-future-8">[8]</a></sup> AMB added an internal development division in 2004. By 2011, AMB was focusing on expanding its operations in China and Brazil.<sup id="cite_ref-robaton_9-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-robaton-9">[9]</a></sup></p><p>Security Capital Industrial Trust (SCI), a legacy company to Prologis, was formed in 1991. SCI became a <a href="/wiki/Public_company" title="Public company">public company</a> via an <a href="/wiki/Initial_public_offering" title="Initial public offering">initial public offering</a> on the <a href="/wiki/New_York_Stock_Exchange" title="New York Stock Exchange">New York Stock Exchange</a> in 1994.<sup id="cite_ref-future_8-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-future-8">[8]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-robaton_9-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-robaton-9">[9]</a></sup> The company first expanded outside the United States in 1996 with acquisitions in Mexico<sup id="cite_ref-future_8-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-future-8">[8]</a></sup> and in 1997 it acquired properties in both Mexico and <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-robaton_9-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-robaton-9">[9]</a></sup> SCI invested in the <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Netherlands</a> in the summer of 1997 and then expanded into <a href="/wiki/Paris" title="Paris">Paris</a>, <a href="/wiki/Stockholm" title="Stockholm">Stockholm</a>, <a href="/wiki/Brussels" title="Brussels">Brussels</a>, <a href="/wiki/Warsaw" title="Warsaw">Warsaw</a>, <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">London</a>, <a href="/wiki/Berlin" title="Berlin">Berlin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Milan" title="Milan">Milan</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Madrid" title="Madrid">Madrid</a>. SCI purchased 33% of Insight Inc. in October 1997 to help solve supply-chain problems for clients. SCI also expanded into the refrigerated distribution business in 1997, purchasing <a href="/wiki/Christian_Salvesen" title="Christian Salvesen">Christian Salvesen</a> and renaming it as CS Integrated LLC. SCI acquired Texas Cold Storage and Continental Freezer in 1997 and secured the primary contract to distribute food for <a href="/wiki/Kroger_Co." class="mw-redirect" title="Kroger Co.">Kroger Co.</a> in <a href="/wiki/Illinois" title="Illinois">Illinois</a>. For refrigeration abroad, SCI purchased <a href="/wiki/Posten_Norge" title="Posten Norge">Frigoscandia AB</a>, then Europe's largest distributor of refrigerated goods.<sup id="cite_ref-recognition_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-recognition-10">[10]</a></sup> SCI later exited the cold-storage business.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p><p>Prologis, Inc. was incorporated on November 24, 1997. SCI officially changed its name to ProLogis in July 1998. SCI was active in 84 markets in 12 countries by July 1998, with a market capitalization of nearly $5 billion.<sup id="cite_ref-recognition_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-recognition-10">[10]</a></sup> In November 1998, Prologis Trust acquired Meridian Industrial Trust for $862.5 million in stock. At the time, Prologis was the "largest owner of industrial and warehouse properties in the United States."<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> ProLogis formed its first property funds in 1999, and entered the Japanese market in 2001. In 2003, ProLogis was added to the <a href="/wiki/S%26P_500_Index" title="S&amp;P 500 Index">S&amp;P 500 Index</a> and entered the Chinese market,<sup id="cite_ref-future_8-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-future-8">[8]</a></sup> and in 2004 the company acquired Keystone Industrial Trust for $1.6 billion.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> In the same year, ProLogis formed its first joint venture in China with Suzhou Logistics Center Co. Ltd.<sup id="cite_ref-future_8-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-future-8">[8]</a></sup> With headquarters in <a href="/wiki/Aurora,_Colorado" title="Aurora, Colorado">Aurora, Colorado</a>, at the time, in 2005 ProLogis completed a merger with <a href="/wiki/Catellus_Development_Corporation" title="Catellus Development Corporation">Catellus Development Corporation</a>, a North American industrial development company, for $4.9 billion. ProLogis ranked first in the 2005 Top 25 Industrial Owners survey.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> In 2006, ProLogis became a <a href="/wiki/Fortune_1000" title="Fortune 1000">Fortune 1000</a> company and the ProLogis European Properties Fund became a <a href="/wiki/Public_company" title="Public company">public company</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-future_8-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-future-8">[8]</a></sup></p><p>In the midst of problems with debt<sup id="cite_ref-forbesbiggest_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-forbesbiggest-15">[15]</a></sup> following aggressive expansion<sup id="cite_ref-giants_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-giants-7">[7]</a></sup> and heavy borrowing,<sup id="cite_ref-robaton_9-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-robaton-9">[9]</a></sup> ProLogis' CEO Jeffrey Schwartz was replaced with Walter Rakowich in 2008.<sup id="cite_ref-forbesbiggest_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-forbesbiggest-15">[15]</a></sup> Rakovich afterwards implemented a cost-cutting effort involving raising capital and selling assets.<sup id="cite_ref-giants_7-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-giants-7">[7]</a></sup></p><p>In December 2008, ProLogis sold a portfolio of assets to the <a href="/wiki/Government_of_Singapore_Investment_Corporation" class="mw-redirect" title="Government of Singapore Investment Corporation">Government of Singapore Investment Corporation</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-giants_7-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-giants-7">[7]</a></sup></p><p>It sold its China operations and some of its Japanese interests to <a href="/wiki/GIC_Private_Limited" title="GIC Private Limited">GIC Private Limited</a> for $1.3 billion in 2009.<sup id="cite_ref-future_8-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-future-8">[8]</a></sup></p><p>In August 2009, ProLogis secured financing from <a href="/wiki/Deutsche_Pfandbriefbank" title="Deutsche Pfandbriefbank">Deutsche Pfandbriefbank</a> and an unidentified German bank for two of its funds.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p><p><a href="/wiki/The_Blackstone_Group" title="The Blackstone Group">The Blackstone Group</a> bought a portfolio of assets from the company in late 2010 for $1 billion.<sup id="cite_ref-giants_7-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-giants-7">[7]</a></sup></p><p>The company acquired Catellus Development in 2005 for $3.6 billion.<sup id="cite_ref-giants_7-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-giants-7">[7]</a></sup></p><p>In January 2011, the company sold a portfolio of Catellus retail and mixed-use assets to affiliates of <a href="/wiki/TPG_Capital" title="TPG Capital">TPG Capital</a> for $505 million, including rights to the Catellus name.<sup id="cite_ref-finkelstein_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-finkelstein-17">[17]</a></sup></p><p>In March 2011, prior to the merger with ProLogis, AMB Property formed a €470 million joint venture with <a href="/wiki/Allianz" title="Allianz">Allianz</a> Real Estate.<sup id="cite_ref-reuters-amb_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-reuters-amb-18">[18]</a></sup></p><p>In January 2011, AMB Property Corporation agreed to buy the larger ProLogis for $8.7 billion, with the new entity named Prologis.<sup id="cite_ref-reuters-amb_18-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-reuters-amb-18">[18]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-forbesbiggest_15-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-forbesbiggest-15">[15]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-nyt-amb_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nyt-amb-19">[19]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-japanrents_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-japanrents-20">[20]</a></sup> The companies announced that the new Prologis would be based in <a href="/wiki/San_Francisco" title="San Francisco">San Francisco</a>, AMB's hometown, but would maintain an office in <a href="/wiki/Denver,_Colorado" class="mw-redirect" title="Denver, Colorado">Denver, Colorado</a>, where ProLogis was based.<sup id="cite_ref-giants_7-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-giants-7">[7]</a></sup> Completed in June 2011,<sup id="cite_ref-robaton_9-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-robaton-9">[9]</a></sup> the merger  was one of the biggest real-estate deals since the <a href="/wiki/Great_Recession" title="Great Recession">Great Recession</a>, and it created the largest industrial real estate company in the world.<sup id="cite_ref-forbesbiggest_15-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-forbesbiggest-15">[15]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-giants_7-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-giants-7">[7]</a></sup> With total market value estimated at $24 billion,<sup id="cite_ref-nyt-amb_19-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nyt-amb-19">[19]</a></sup> the new Prologis had around $46 billion in assets under management and logistics and distribution facilities in North America, Europe, Asia and South America.<sup id="cite_ref-forbesbiggest_15-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-forbesbiggest-15">[15]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-giants_7-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-giants-7">[7]</a></sup> Clients of the new company included <a href="/wiki/DHL" title="DHL">DHL</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kuehne_%2B_Nagel" title="Kuehne + Nagel">Kuehne + Nagel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Home_Depot_Inc." class="mw-redirect" title="Home Depot Inc.">Home Depot Inc.</a>, <a href="/wiki/Unilever" title="Unilever">Unilever</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-giants_7-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-giants-7">[7]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/FedEx" title="FedEx">FedEx</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-robaton_9-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-robaton-9">[9]</a></sup> AMB CEO <a href="/wiki/Hamid_R._Moghadam" class="mw-redirect" title="Hamid R. Moghadam">Hamid R. Moghadam</a> and ProLogis CEO Walter Rakowich were appointed as the new company's co-chiefs, with Moghadam to become the sole CEO in 2013.<sup id="cite_ref-giants_7-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-giants-7">[7]</a></sup></p><p>In August 2011, the company sold a 378,298-square-foot industrial property in <a href="/wiki/Grove_City,_Ohio" title="Grove City, Ohio">Grove City, Ohio</a> for $12.4 million.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> The company also acquired a warehouse in <a href="/wiki/Tolleson,_Arizona" title="Tolleson, Arizona">Tolleson, Arizona</a> for $9.95 million.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p><p>In February 2012, the company sold a 3.5 million square foot portfolio in the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a> to <a href="/wiki/The_Blackstone_Group" title="The Blackstone Group">The Blackstone Group</a> for $335 million.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p><p>In June 2012, the company sold the 10 buildings in <a href="/wiki/Memphis,_Tennessee" title="Memphis, Tennessee">Memphis, Tennessee</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p><p>In November 2012, the company sold a 1.98 million square portfolio in  <a href="/wiki/Minneapolis%E2%80%93Saint_Paul" title="Minneapolis–Saint Paul">Minneapolis–Saint Paul</a> for $96.6 million.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p><p>On February 14, 2013, Nippon Prologis REIT, Inc. (NPR), a Japanese real estate investment trust formed by Prologis, successfully completed an IPO on the <a href="/wiki/Tokyo_Stock_Exchange" title="Tokyo Stock Exchange">Tokyo Stock Exchange</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-nippon-ipo_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nippon-ipo-26">[26]</a></sup> In March 2013, Prologis formed the Prologis European Logistics Partners Sarl joint venture with <a href="/wiki/Norges_Bank_Investment_Management" class="mw-redirect" title="Norges Bank Investment Management">Norges Bank Investment Management</a> for US$3.1 billion.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p><p>In August 2013, in partnership with <a href="/wiki/The_Blackstone_Group" title="The Blackstone Group">The Blackstone Group</a> the company acquired a 17 million square foot portfolio for $960 million from <a href="/wiki/Lehman_Brothers" title="Lehman Brothers">Lehman Brothers</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p><p>In September 2013, the company sold 3 warehouses in <a href="/wiki/Tempe,_Arizona" title="Tempe, Arizona">Tempe, Arizona</a> for $22 million.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p><p>In November 2013, Prologis announced plans to spend "as much as $600 million a year to develop warehouses in Japan," its biggest market after the United States, where it had customers such as <a href="/wiki/Amazon.com" class="mw-redirect" title="Amazon.com">Amazon.com</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nippon_Express" title="Nippon Express">Nippon Express</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-japanrents_20-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-japanrents-20">[20]</a></sup></p><p>In the fourth quarter of 2013, the company acquired a 1.8 million square foot portfolio in <a href="/wiki/Nashville,_Tennessee" title="Nashville, Tennessee">Nashville, Tennessee</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p><p>In April 2014, the company acquired a 117,600 square foot warehouse in <a href="/wiki/Hanover,_Maryland" title="Hanover, Maryland">Hanover, Maryland</a> for $8.65 million.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p><p>In May 2014, the company acquired two industrial buildings in <a href="/wiki/Charlotte,_North_Carolina" title="Charlotte, North Carolina">Charlotte, North Carolina</a> for $17.5 million.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p><p>In June 2014, the company completed the <a href="/wiki/Corporate_spin-off" title="Corporate spin-off">corporate spin-off</a> of FIBRA Prologis, the company’s Mexican affiliate also known as Prologis Property Mexico SA, raising about 7 billion pesos ($541 million).<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> The company also sold a 59-property portfolio to TPG for $375 million.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></p><p>In September 2014, the company sold a former <a href="/wiki/Big_Lots" title="Big Lots">Big Lots</a> warehouse in <a href="/wiki/Columbus,_Ohio" title="Columbus, Ohio">Columbus, Ohio</a> for $8.5 million.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></p><p>At the end of 2014, Prologis owned 2,853 properties, with the bulk in North America and around 600 in Europe and Asia.<sup id="cite_ref-wsj-tobuy_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wsj-tobuy-37">[37]</a></sup></p><p>In March 2015, the company acquired an 18-acre development site in <a href="/wiki/La_Vergne,_Tennessee" title="La Vergne, Tennessee">La Vergne, Tennessee</a> for $2.35 million.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></p><p>Prologis had reduced its number of managed funds from 23 to 11 by the spring of 2015, and was managing "$29 billion in real-estate assets through joint ventures and a series of funds backed by institutional investors."<sup id="cite_ref-wsj-tobuy_37-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wsj-tobuy-37">[37]</a></sup> That quarter, the company sold a <a href="/wiki/Silicon_Valley" title="Silicon Valley">Silicon Valley</a> industrial park to <a href="/wiki/Facebook,_Inc." title="Facebook, Inc.">Facebook, Inc.</a><sup id="cite_ref-wsj-rents_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wsj-rents-3">[3]</a></sup></p><p>With corporate headquarters remaining in San Francisco, in April 2015 Prologis announced a plan to move offices from east <a href="/wiki/Denver,_Colorado" class="mw-redirect" title="Denver, Colorado">Denver, Colorado</a>, to a new building in downtown Denver<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> in 2017.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup></p><p>In June 2015, Prologis partnered with <a href="/wiki/Norges_Bank_Investment_Management" class="mw-redirect" title="Norges Bank Investment Management">Norges Bank Investment Management</a> to buy KTR Capital Partners for $5.9 billion, expanding Prologis' real estate portfolio in the United States through the Prologis U.S. Logistics Venture.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup></p><p>In October 2015, Prologis acquired Morris Realty Associates' portfolio of logistics and retail properties in the United States for $820 million. Prologis sold the retail asset portion of this portfolio to an affiliate of <a href="/wiki/The_Blackstone_Group" title="The Blackstone Group">The Blackstone Group</a> for $374 million.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup></p><p>By late 2016, Prologis had built large multistory warehouses in Japan, <a href="/wiki/Singapore" <a href="/wiki/Seattle" title="Seattle">Seattle</a>, <a href="/wiki/Washington_(state)" title="Washington (state)">Washington</a>, in November 2016, which was the first multi-story warehouse in the country.<sup id="cite_ref-wsj-buildfirst_45-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wsj-buildfirst-45">[45]</a></sup> The following month, Prologis also outlined plans to build a three-story
