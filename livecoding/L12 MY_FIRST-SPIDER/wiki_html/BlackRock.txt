institutional <a href="/wiki/Asset_manager" class="mw-redirect" title="Asset manager">asset manager</a>, BlackRock is the world's largest asset manager, with $7.4 trillion in <a href="/wiki/Assets_under_management" title="Assets under management">assets under management</a> as of end-Q4 2019.<sup id="cite_ref-:2_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:2-3">[3]</a></sup> </section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(1)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=BlackRock&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History" data-section="1" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-1 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-1">
<h3 class="in-block"><span id="1988.E2.80.931997"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="1988–1997">1988–1997</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=BlackRock&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: 1988–1997" data-section="2" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>BlackRock was founded in 1988 by <a href="/wiki/Laurence_D._Fink" title="Laurence D. Fink">Larry Fink</a>, <a href="/wiki/Robert_S._Kapito" title="Robert S. Kapito">Robert S. Kapito</a>, <a href="/wiki/Susan_Wagner" title="Susan Wagner">Susan Wagner</a>, Barbara Novick, Ben Golub, Hugh Frater, Ralph Schlosstein, and Keith Anderson<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> to provide institutional clients with asset management services from a risk management perspective.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Fink, Kapito, Golub and Novick had worked together at <a href="/wiki/First_Boston" title="First Boston">First Boston</a>, where Fink and his team were pioneers in the <a href="/wiki/Mortgage-backed_security" title="Mortgage-backed security">mortgage-backed securities</a> market in the United States.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>  During Fink's tenure, he had lost $100 million as head of First Boston. That experience was the motivation to develop what he and the others considered to be excellent risk management and fiduciary practices. Initially, Fink sought funding (for initial operating capital) from Pete Peterson of <a href="/wiki/The_Blackstone_Group" title="The Blackstone Group">The Blackstone Group</a> who believed in Fink's vision of a firm devoted to risk management. Peterson called it Blackstone Financial Management.<sup id="cite_ref-:4_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:4-10">[10]</a></sup> In exchange for a 50 percent stake in the bond business, initially Blackstone gave Fink and his team a $5 million credit line. Within months, the business had turned profitable, and by 1989 the group's assets had quadrupled to $2.7 billion. The percent of the stake owned by Blackstone also fell to 40%, compared to Fink's staff.<sup id="cite_ref-:4_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:4-10">[10]</a></sup></p><p>By 1992, Blackstone had a stake equating to about 35% of the company, and Schwarzman and Fink were considering selling shares to the public.<sup id="cite_ref-bb-heroic_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bb-heroic-11">[11]</a></sup> The firm adopted the name BlackRock in 1992, and by the end of that year, BlackRock was managing $17 billion in assets. At the end of 1994, BlackRock was managing $53 billion.<sup id="cite_ref-main-history_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-main-history-12">[12]</a></sup> In 1994, Blackstone Group's <a href="/wiki/Stephen_A._Schwarzman" title="Stephen A. Schwarzman">Stephen A. Schwarzman</a> and Fink had an internal dispute over methods of compensation<sup id="cite_ref-bb-heroic_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bb-heroic-11">[11]</a></sup> and over equity.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Fink wanted to share equity with new hires, to lure talent from <a href="/wiki/Bank" title="Bank">banks</a>, unlike Schwarzman, who did not want to further lower Blackstone's stake.<sup id="cite_ref-bb-heroic_11-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bb-heroic-11">[11]</a></sup> They agreed to part ways, so the BlackRock partners (Sue Wagner) orchestrated a deal to sell part of the company.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> In June 1994 Blackstone sold a mortgage-securities unit with $23 billion in assets to <a href="/wiki/PNC_Financial_Services_Group" class="mw-redirect" title="PNC Financial Services Group">PNC Bank Corp.</a> for $240 million.<sup id="cite_ref-fortune-admired_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fortune-admired-13">[13]</a></sup> The unit had traded mortgages and other fixed-income assets, and during the sales process the unit changed its name from Blackstone Financial Management to BlackRock Financial Management.<sup id="cite_ref-bb-heroic_11-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bb-heroic-11">[11]</a></sup> Schwarzman remained with Blackstone, while Fink went on to become chairman and CEO of BlackRock Inc.<sup id="cite_ref-bb-heroic_11-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bb-heroic-11">[11]</a></sup> In 1998, PNC’s equity, liquidity, and mutual fund activities were merged into BlackRock.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span id="1999.E2.80.932009"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="1999–2009">1999–2009</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=BlackRock&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: 1999–2009" data-section="3" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>BlackRock went public in 1999 at $14 a share<sup id="cite_ref-corporate-history_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-corporate-history-14">[14]</a></sup> on the <a href="/wiki/New_York_Stock_Exchange" title="New York Stock Exchange">New York Stock Exchange</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-main-history_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-main-history-12">[12]</a></sup> By the end of 1999, BlackRock was managing $165 billion in assets.<sup id="cite_ref-main-history_12-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-main-history-12">[12]</a></sup> BlackRock grew both organically and by acquisition. In August 2004, BlackRock made its first major acquisition, buying <a href="/w/index.php?title=State_Street_Research_%26_Management%27s_holding&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="State Street Research &amp; Management's holding (page does not exist)">State Street Research &amp; Management's holding</a> company SSRM Holdings, Inc. from <a href="/wiki/MetLife" title="MetLife">MetLife</a> for $325 million in cash and $50 million in stock. The acquisition raised BlackRock's assets under management from $314 billion to $325 billion.<sup id="cite_ref-Updated-Source_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Updated-Source-15">[15]</a></sup> The deal included the mutual-fund business State Street Research &amp; Management in 2005.<sup id="cite_ref-fortune-admired_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fortune-admired-13">[13]</a></sup> BlackRock merged with <a href="/wiki/Merrill_Lynch" class="mw-redirect" title="Merrill Lynch">Merrill Lynch Investment Managers</a> (MLIM) in 2006,<sup id="cite_ref-main-history_12-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-main-history-12">[12]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-econ-ml_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-econ-ml-16">[16]</a></sup> halving PNC's ownership and giving <a href="/wiki/Merrill_Lynch" class="mw-redirect" title="Merrill Lynch">Merrill Lynch</a> a 49.5% stake in the company.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> In October 2007, BlackRock acquired the fund-of-funds business of <a href="/w/index.php?title=Quellos_Capital_Management&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Quellos Capital Management (page does not exist)">Quellos Capital Management</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-quellos_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-quellos-18">[18]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p><p>The U.S. government contracted with BlackRock to help resolve the fallout of the <a href="/wiki/Late-2000s_financial_crisis" class="mw-redirect" title="Late-2000s financial crisis">financial meltdown of 2008</a>. According to <i><a href="/wiki/Vanity_Fair_(magazine)" title="Vanity Fair (magazine)">Vanity Fair</a></i>, the financial establishment in <a href="/wiki/Washington,_D.C." title="Washington, D.C.">Washington</a> and on <a href="/wiki/Wall_Street" title="Wall Street">Wall Street</a> believed BlackRock was the best choice for the job.<sup id="cite_ref-vfair-job_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-vfair-job-20">[20]</a></sup> In 2009, BlackRock first became the No. 1 asset manager worldwide.<sup id="cite_ref-fortune-admired_13-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fortune-admired-13">[13]</a></sup> In April 2009, BlackRock acquired R3 Capital Management, LLC and took control of the $1.5 billion fund.<sup id="cite_ref-r3-nyt_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-r3-nyt-21">[21]</a></sup> On 12 June 2009, <a href="/wiki/Barclays" title="Barclays">Barclays</a> sold its Global Investors unit (BGI), which included its exchange traded fund business, <a href="/wiki/IShares" title="IShares">iShares</a>, to BlackRock for US$13.5 billion. Through the deal, Barclays attained a near-20% stake in BlackRock.<sup id="cite_ref-gaurdian-ishares_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gaurdian-ishares-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span id="2010.E2.80.932019"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="2010–2019">2010–2019</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=BlackRock&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: 2010–2019" data-section="4" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>In 2010, Ralph Schlosstein, the CEO of <a href="/wiki/Evercore_Partners" class="mw-redirect" title="Evercore Partners">Evercore Partners</a> and a BlackRock founder, called BlackRock "the most influential financial institution in the world."<sup id="cite_ref-bb-power_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bb-power-23">[23]</a></sup> On 1 April 2011, due to <a href="/wiki/Sanofi" title="Sanofi">Sanofi's</a> acquisition of <a href="/wiki/Genzyme" title="Genzyme">Genzyme</a>, BlackRock replaced it on the <a href="/wiki/S%26P_500" class="mw-redirect" title="S&amp;P 500">S&amp;P 500</a> index.<sup id="cite_ref-bweek-replace_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bweek-replace-24">[24]</a></sup></p><p>In 2013, <i>Fortune</i> listed BlackRock on its annual list of the world's 50 Most Admired Companies.<sup id="cite_ref-fortune-admired_13-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fortune-admired-13">[13]</a></sup> In 2014, <i>The Economist</i> said that BlackRock's $4 trillion under management made it the "world's biggest asset manager", and it was larger than the world's largest bank, the <a href="/wiki/Industrial_and_Commercial_Bank_of_China" title="Industrial and Commercial Bank of China">Industrial and Commercial Bank of China</a> with $3 trillion.<sup id="cite_ref-econ-br_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-econ-br-6">[6]</a></sup> In May of the same year, BlackRock invested in <a href="/wiki/Snapdeal" title="Snapdeal">Snapdeal</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p><p>In December 2014 a BlackRock managing director in London was banned by the British <a href="/wiki/Financial_Conduct_Authority" title="Financial Conduct Authority">Financial Conduct Authority</a> for failing the "fit and proper" test, because he paid £43,000 to avoid prosecution for dodging train fares. BlackRock said, "Jonathan Burrows left BlackRock earlier this year. What he admitted to the FCA is totally contrary to our values and principles."<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-sky-fca_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sky-fca-27">[27]</a></sup></p><p>At the end of 2014, the <a href="/wiki/Sovereign_Wealth_Fund_Institute" title="Sovereign Wealth Fund Institute">Sovereign Wealth Fund Institute</a> reported that 65% of Blackrock's assets under management were made up of <a href="/wiki/Institutional_investor" title="Institutional investor">institutional investors</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-sfw-institute_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sfw-institute-28">[28]</a></sup></p><p>By June 30, 2015, BlackRock had US$4.721 trillion of assets under management.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> On August 26, 2015, BlackRock entered into a definitive agreement to acquire <a href="/wiki/FutureAdvisor" class="mw-redirect" title="FutureAdvisor">FutureAdvisor</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-future-advisor_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-future-advisor-30">[30]</a></sup> a digital wealth management provider with reported assets under management of $600 million.<sup id="cite_ref-future-tech_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-future-tech-31">[31]</a></sup> Under the deal, FutureAdvisor would operate as a business within BlackRock Solutions (BRS).<sup id="cite_ref-future-advisor_30-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-future-advisor-30">[30]</a></sup> BlackRock announced in November 2015 that they would wind down the BlackRock Global Ascent hedge fund after losses. The Global Ascent fund had been its only dedicated global macro fund, as BlackRock was "better known for its mutual funds and exchange traded funds." At the time, BlackRock managed $51 billion in hedge funds, with $20 billion of that in funds of hedge funds.<sup id="cite_ref-fund-losses_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fund-losses-32">[32]</a></sup></p><p>In March 2017, the <i><a href="/wiki/Financial_Times" title="Financial Times">Financial Times</a></i> announced that BlackRock, after a six-month review led by <a href="/wiki/Mark_Wiseman" title="Mark Wiseman">Mark Wiseman</a>, had initiated a restructuring of its $8bn actively-managed fund business, resulting in the departure of seven portfolio managers and a $25m charge in Q2, replacing certain funds with quantitative investment strategies.<sup id="cite_ref-ft-cuts_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ft-cuts-33">[33]</a></sup> In May 2017, BlackRock increased its stake in both <a href="/wiki/CRH_plc" title="CRH plc">CRH plc</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bank_of_Ireland" title="Bank of Ireland">Bank of Ireland</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-it-stake_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-it-stake-34">[34]</a></sup> By April 2017, <a href="/wiki/IShares" title="IShares">iShares</a> business accounted for $1.41tn, or 26 percent, of BlackRock's total assets under management, and 37 percent of BlackRock’s base fee income.<sup id="cite_ref-ft-record_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ft-record-35">[35]</a></sup> In April 2017, BlackRock backed the inclusion of mainland Chinese shares in MSCI's global index for the first time.<sup id="cite_ref-ft-china-shares_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ft-china-shares-36">[36]</a></sup></p><p>Between October and December 2018, BlackRock's assets dropped by US$468bn and fell below $6tn. It was the largest decline between quarters since September 2011.<sup id="cite_ref-:2_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:2-3">[3]</a></sup></p><p>As of 2019, BlackRock holds 4.81% of <a href="/wiki/Deutsche_Bank" title="Deutsche Bank">Deutsche Bank</a>, making it the single largest shareholder.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> This investment goes back to at least 2016.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></p><p>In May 2019, BlackRock received criticism for the environmental impact of its holdings.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> It is counted among the top three shareholders in every oil "<a href="/wiki/Supermajors" class="mw-redirect" title="Supermajors">supermajor</a>" except <a href="/wiki/Total_S.A." title="Total S.A.">Total</a>, and it is among the top 10 shareholders in 7 of the 10 biggest coal producers.
</p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="2020">2020</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=BlackRock&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: 2020" data-section="5" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>In  his 2020 annual open letter,<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> CEO Fink announced <a href="/wiki/Sustainability" title="Sustainability">environmental sustainability</a> as core goal for BlackRock's future investment decisions.<sup id="cite_ref-:5_41-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:5-41">[41]</a></sup> BlackRock disclosed plans to sell US$500 million in <a href="/wiki/Coal" title="Coal">coal</a> investments.<sup id="cite_ref-:6_42-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:6-42">[42]</a></sup></p>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(2)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Finances">Finances</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=BlackRock&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Finances" data-section="6" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-2 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-2">

<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="iShares">iShares</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=BlackRock&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: iShares" data-section="8" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>BlackRock's largest division is <a href="/wiki/IShares" title="IShares">iShares</a>, a family of over 800 exchange-traded funds (ETFs) that comprises more than $1 trillion in assets under management. iShares is the largest provider of ETFs in the U.S. and in the world.
</p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="BlackRock_Solutions">BlackRock Solutions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=BlackRock&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: BlackRock Solutions" data-section="9" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>In 2000, BlackRock launched BlackRock Solutions, the analytics and risk management division of BlackRock, Inc. The division grew from the <a href="/wiki/Aladdin_(BlackRock)" title="Aladdin (BlackRock)">Aladdin System</a> (which is the enterprise investment system), Green Package (which is the Risk Reporting Service) PAG (portfolio analytics) and AnSer (which is the interactive analytics). BlackRock Solutions (BRS) serves two roles within BlackRock. First, BlackRock Solutions is the in-house investment analytics and “process engineering” department for BlackRock which works with their portfolio management teams, risk and quantitative analysis, business operations and every other part of the firm that touches the investment process.  Second, BlackRock Solutions (BRS) and the three primary divisions are services that offered to institutional clients. As of 2013, the platform had nearly 2,000 employees.<sup id="cite_ref-economist.com_46-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-economist.com-46">[46]</a></sup></p><p>BlackRock differentiates itself from other asset managers by claiming its risk management is not separate. Risk management is the foundation and cornerstone of the firm's entire platform.<sup id="cite_ref-economist.com_46-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-economist.com-46">[46]</a></sup> Aladdin keeps track of 30,000 investment portfolios, including BlackRock's own along with those of competitors, banks, <a href="/wiki/Pension_fund" title="Pension fund">pension funds</a>, and insurers. According to <i><a href="/wiki/The_Economist" title="The Economist">The Economist</a></i>, as at December 2013, the platform monitors almost 7 percent of the world’s $225 trillion of financial assets.<sup id="cite_ref-economist.com_46-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-economist.com-46">[46]</a></sup></p><p>BlackRock Solutions was retained by the <a href="/wiki/U._S._Treasury_Department" class="mw-redirect" title="U. S. Treasury Department">U. S. Treasury Department</a> in May 2009<sup id="cite_ref-bb-power_23-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bb-power-23">[23]</a></sup> to manage the <a href="/wiki/Toxic_asset" title="Toxic asset">toxic mortgage assets</a> (i.e. to analyze, unwind, and price) that were owned by <a href="/wiki/Bear_Stearns" title="Bear Stearns">Bear Stearns</a>, <a href="/wiki/American_International_Group" title="American International Group">AIG, Inc.</a>, <a href="/wiki/Freddie_Mac" title="Freddie Mac">Freddie Mac</a>, <a href="/wiki/Morgan_Stanley" title="Morgan Stanley">Morgan Stanley</a>, and other financial firms that were affected in the 2008 financial crisis.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="ESG_investing">ESG investing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=BlackRock&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: ESG investing" data-section="10" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>In 2017, BlackRock expanded its presence in sustainable investing and <a href="/wiki/Environmental,_social_and_corporate_governance" title="Environmental, social and corporate governance">environmental, social and corporate governance</a> (ESG) with new staff<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> and products both in USA<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> and Europe<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup> with the aim to lead the evolution of the financial sector in this regard.<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup></p><p>BlackRock started using its weight to draw attention to environmental and diversity issues by means of official letters to CEOs and shareholder votes together with <a href="/wiki/Activist_investor" class="mw-redirect" title="Activist investor">activist investors</a> or investor networks<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup> like the <a href="/wiki/Carbon_Disclosure_Project" title="Carbon Disclosure Project">Carbon Disclosure Project</a>, which in 2017 backed a successful shareholder resolution for <a href="/wiki/ExxonMobil" title="ExxonMobil">ExxonMobil</a> to act on <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" class="mw-redirect" title="Climate change">climate change</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup> In 2018, it asked <a href="/wiki/Russell_1000" class="mw-redirect" title="Russell 1000">Russell 1000</a> companies to improve <a href="/wiki/Gender_diversity" title="Gender diversity">gender diversity</a> on their <a href="/wiki/Board_of_directors" title="Board of directors">board of directors</a> if they had less than 2 women on them.<sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup></p><p>After discussions with firearms manufacturers and distributors, on April 5, 2018, BlackRock introduced two new <a href="/wiki/Exchange-traded_fund" title="Exchange-traded fund">exchange-traded funds</a> (ETFs) that exclude stocks of gun makers and large gun retailers, <a href="/wiki/Walmart" title="Walmart">Walmart</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dick%E2%80%99s_Sporting_Goods" class="mw-redirect" title="Dick’s Sporting Goods">Dick’s Sporting Goods</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kroger" title="Kroger">Kroger</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sturm_Ruger" class="mw-redirect" title="Sturm Ruger">Sturm Ruger</a>, <a href="/wiki/American_Outdoor_Brands_Corporation" title="American Outdoor Brands Corporation">American Outdoor Brands Corporation</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Vista_Outdoor" title="Vista Outdoor">Vista Outdoor</a>, and removing the stocks from their seven existing <a href="/wiki/Environmental,_social_and_corporate_governance" title="Environmental, social and corporate governance">environmental, social and corporate governance</a> (ESG) funds, in order “to provide more choice for clients seeking to exclude firearms companies from their portfolios.”<sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-59" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup></p>
<h4 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Contributions_to_global_warming">Contributions to global warming</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=BlackRock&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Contributions to global warming" data-section="11" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h4>
<p>Despite BlackRock's attempts to model itself as a sustainable investor, one report shows that BlackRock is the world’s largest investor in coal plant developers, holding shares worth $11 billion among 56 coal plant developers.<sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup> Another report shows that BlackRock owns more oil, gas, and thermal coal reserves than any other investor with total reserves amounting to 9.5 gigatonnes of CO2 emissions – or 30 percent of total energy-related emissions from 2017.<sup id="cite_ref-61" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup> Concerned about <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a>, environmental groups including the Sierra Club,<sup id="cite_ref-62" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup> and Amazon Watch <sup id="cite_ref-63" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup> launched a campaign called BlackRock's Big Problem in September 2018.<sup id="cite_ref-:3_64-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:3-64">[64]</a></sup>  In this campaign, these groups assert that BlackRock is the "biggest driver of climate destruction on the planet", due in part to its refusal to <a href="/wiki/Fossil_fuel_divestment" title="Fossil fuel divestment">divest from fossil fuel companies</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-:3_64-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:3-64">[64]</a></sup> On January 10th, 2020 a group of climate activists rushed inside the Paris offices of Black Rock France painting walls and floors with warnings and accusations on the responsibility of the company in the <a href="/wiki/Effects_of_global_warming" title="Effects of global warming">current climate and social crises</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-65" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup></p><p>On January 14, 2020, BlackRock CEO <a href="/wiki/Laurence_D._Fink" title="Laurence D. Fink">Laurence Fink</a> declared that environmental sustainability would be a key goal for investment decisions. BlackRock announced that it would sell $500 million worth of coal-related assets, and create funds that would avoid fossil-fuel stocks, two moves that would drastically shift the company's investment policy.<sup id="cite_ref-:5_41-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:5-41">[41]</a></sup> Environmentalist <a href="/wiki/Bill_McKibben" title="Bill McKibben">Bill McKibben</a> called this a "huge, if not final, win for activists."<sup id="cite_ref-:6_42-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:6-42">[42]</a></sup></p>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(4)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Public_perception">Public perception</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=BlackRock&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Public perception" data-section="12" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-4 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-4">
<p>In his 2018 annual letter to shareholders, BlackRock CEO Laurence D. Fink stated that other CEOs should be aware of their impact on society. Anti-war organizations were discontent with Fink’s statement,<sup id="cite_ref-66" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup> because BlackRock is the largest investor in weapon manufacturers through its iShares U.S. Aerospace and Defense ETF.<sup id="cite_ref-67" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup></p><p>In May 2018, anti-war organizations held a demonstration<sup id="cite_ref-68" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup> outside the annual BlackRock shareholder’s meeting in Manhattan, New York.
</p>
