</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(1)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lowe%27s&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History" data-section="1" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-1 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-1">

Jim took on Carl Buchan as a partner in 1943.<sup id="cite_ref-Williams_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Williams-10">[10]</a></sup></p><p>Buchan anticipated the dramatic increase in construction after <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, and under his management, the store focused on <a href="/wiki/Household_hardware" title="Household hardware">hardware</a> and building materials.<sup id="cite_ref-auto_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-auto-11">[11]</a></sup> Before then, the product mix had also included <a href="/wiki/Notions_(sewing)" class="mw-redirect" title="Notions (sewing)">notions</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dry_good" class="mw-redirect" title="Dry good">dry goods</a>, <a href="/wiki/Horse_tack" title="Horse tack">horse tack</a>, <a href="/wiki/Snuff_(tobacco)" title="Snuff (tobacco)">snuff</a>, produce, and groceries. The company bought a second location in <a href="/wiki/Sparta,_North_Carolina" title="Sparta, North Carolina">Sparta, North Carolina</a> in 1949.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p><p>In 1952, Buchan became the sole owner of Lowe's. In 1954, Jim Lowe started the <a href="/wiki/Lowes_Foods" title="Lowes Foods">Lowes Foods</a> grocery store chain.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p><p>By 1955, Buchan quickly expanded the company by opening stores in the North Carolina cities of <a href="/wiki/Asheville,_North_Carolina" title="Asheville, North Carolina">Asheville</a>, <a href="/wiki/Charlotte,_North_Carolina" title="Charlotte, North Carolina">Charlotte</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Durham,_North_Carolina" title="Durham, North Carolina">Durham</a>. More stores opened through the 1950s.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2020)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p><p>In 1961, Buchan died of a <a href="/wiki/Heart_attack" class="mw-redirect" title="Heart attack">heart attack</a> at age 44.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> His five-man executive team, which included Robert Strickland and Leonard Herring, took the company public in 1961.<sup id="cite_ref-auto_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-auto-11">[11]</a></sup> By 1962, Lowe's operated 21 stores and reported annual revenues of $32 million.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Lowe's began trading on the <a href="/wiki/New_York_Stock_Exchange" title="New York Stock Exchange">New York Stock Exchange</a> in 1979.<sup id="cite_ref-Williams_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Williams-10">[10]</a></sup></p><p>Lowe's suffered in the 1980s due to market conditions and increasing competition from the new <a href="/wiki/Big-box_store" title="Big-box store">big-box</a> store chain, <a href="/wiki/The_Home_Depot" title="The Home Depot">The Home Depot</a>. For a while, Lowe's resisted adopting the mega-store format partly because its management believed the smaller towns where Lowe's mostly operated would not even support huge stores. However, Lowe's eventually adopted the big-box format to survive.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2020)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p><p>Lowe's has since grown nationally, as it was aided by the purchase of the <a href="/wiki/Renton,_Washington" title="Renton, Washington">Renton, Washington</a>–based <b>Eagle Hardware &amp; Garden</b> company in 1999.<sup id="cite_ref-lndeag_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-lndeag-16">[16]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> The first store outside the United States was in <a href="/wiki/Hamilton,_Ontario" title="Hamilton, Ontario">Hamilton, Ontario</a>, Canada.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p><p>According to its website, Lowe's has operated/serviced more than 2,355 locations in the United States, Canada, and Mexico alone,<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> although the Mexican stores were closed in the late 2010s.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>

<p>In 1998, Lowe's purchased the Wilkes Mall in <a href="/wiki/Wilkesboro,_North_Carolina" title="Wilkesboro, North Carolina">Wilkesboro, North Carolina</a>, to serve as the company's headquarters. In 2002, Lowe's acquired full control over the 440,000 square feet (41,000 m<sup>2</sup>) building after the lease of the 10 remaining mall tenants vacated the property. A year later, Lowe's constructed and relocated the corporate headquarters to a new, 350-acre campus in <a href="/wiki/Mooresville,_North_Carolina" title="Mooresville, North Carolina">Mooresville, North Carolina</a>, a suburb of <a href="/wiki/Charlotte,_North_Carolina" title="Charlotte, North Carolina">Charlotte</a>. The new facility contains a five-story, and two seven-story buildings. The building has a central atrium and two office wings; the atrium houses a food court, a five-story spiral staircase, and meeting and reception rooms. A 7-acre (2.8 ha) lake flows underneath the headquarters building.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2020)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p><p>Lowe's maintained its former headquarters in Wilkesboro, where it employs over 2,400 people. In 2011, Lowe's invested $10 million in improvements in renovations in the property, including a full service food court, coffee shop, health center, as well as gating the entire property with an addition of a guardhouse.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p><p>Lowe's Companies Canada is based in <a href="/wiki/Boucherville" title="Boucherville">Boucherville</a>, <a href="/wiki/Quebec" title="Quebec">Quebec</a>, after the merger with Rona. Prior to 2016, Lowe's Canada was headquartered in <a href="/wiki/Toronto" title="Toronto">Toronto</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ontario" title="Ontario">Ontario</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Torontooff_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Torontooff-24">[24]</a></sup></p><p>Lowe's operates customer contact centers in Mooresville and Wilkesboro, North Carolina, as well as Indianapolis, Indiana, Albuquerque, New Mexico, and India.
</p><p>On June 27, 2019, Lowe's announced a $153 million, 23-story tower in <a href="/wiki/South_End_(Charlotte_neighborhood)" title="South End (Charlotte neighborhood)">South End</a> which will be completed by 2020 and eventually house 2000 employees, 400 of which will move from the headquarters. The state of North Carolina is providing $54 million in incentives provided Lowe's meets certain goals.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(4)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Environmental_record">Environmental record</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lowe%27s&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Environmental record" data-section="4" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-4 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-4">
<p>Lowe's won eight consecutive <a href="/wiki/Energy_Star" title="Energy Star">Energy Star</a> awards from 2003 to 2010, including four Energy Star Partner of the Year awards for educating consumers about the benefits of energy efficiency. On March 1, 2010, Lowe's also became the first winner of the Energy Star Sustained Excellence Award in Retail, to recognize its contribution to reducing greenhouse gas emissions by promoting energy-efficient products and educating consumers and employees on the value of the Energy Star program.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> In 2000, Lowe's released a policy promising that all wood products sold would not be sourced from rainforests.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> However, according to a 2006 report released by the Environmental Investigation Agency, wood used in flooring by Armstrong Flooring that Lowe's was selling had been coming from the forests of <a href="/wiki/Indonesia" title="Indonesia">Indonesia</a>’s remote Papua Province, where some <a href="/wiki/Logging" title="Logging">logging</a> was estimated to be illegal.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> According to the Telepak report, which was the sole basis of the EIA allegations, Armstrong commissioned an independent audit of Kreasi's merbau supplies over the last 18 month which found documentation relating to the source of the company's merbau logs in Papua, and based on this, Armstrong decided to continue to trade in merbau supplied by Kreasi. The cited report dates to 2006 and Lowe's continues to claim its wood products come from known sources; is legally harvested and traded, and more<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(5)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Advertising_and_sponsorships">Advertising and sponsorships</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lowe%27s&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Advertising and sponsorships" data-section="5" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-5 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-5">
<p>In January 2019, Lowes became the official Sponsor of the <a href="/wiki/National_Football_League" title="National Football League">National Football League</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Racing_sponsorships">Racing sponsorships</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lowe%27s&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Racing sponsorships" data-section="6" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Jimmie_Johnson%27s_pit_box.jpg" class="image"><noscript><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0b/Jimmie_Johnson%27s_pit_box.jpg/220px-Jimmie_Johnson%27s_pit_box.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="146" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="3008" data-file-height="2000"></noscript><span class="lazy-image-placeholder" style="width: 220px;height: 146px;" data-src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0b/Jimmie_Johnson%27s_pit_box.jpg/220px-Jimmie_Johnson%27s_pit_box.jpg" data-alt="" data-width="220" data-height="146" data-class="thumbimage"> </span></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Jimmie_Johnson%27s_pit_box.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The Lowe's-sponsored team of <a href="/wiki/Jimmie_Johnson" title="Jimmie Johnson">Jimmie Johnson</a>'s pit box</div></div></div>
<p>Lowe's purchased <a href="/wiki/Naming_rights" title="Naming rights">naming rights</a> of <a href="/wiki/Charlotte_Motor_Speedway" title="Charlotte Motor Speedway">Charlotte Motor Speedway</a> in <a href="/wiki/Charlotte,_North_Carolina" title="Charlotte, North Carolina">Charlotte, North Carolina</a>, in 1998, and the speedway changed names to Lowe's Motor Speedway. After the ten year naming rights expired, Lowe's extended naming rights by one year.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> After the one-year extension expired, Lowe's discontinued naming rights, and as of the 2010 racing season the racetrack returned to its original name.
</p><p>The corporation was the primary sponsor for 7-time <a href="/wiki/NASCAR" title="NASCAR">NASCAR</a> <a href="/wiki/Sprint_Cup_Series" class="mw-redirect" title="Sprint Cup Series">Sprint Cup Series</a> champion <a href="/wiki/Jimmie_Johnson" title="Jimmie Johnson">Jimmie Johnson</a> in the No. 48 Chevrolet Camaro ZL1 and it was a part-time sponsor of the former No. 5 <a href="/wiki/Nationwide_Series" class="mw-redirect" title="Nationwide Series">Nationwide Series</a> car; both teams are owned by <a href="/wiki/Hendrick_Motorsports" title="Hendrick Motorsports">Hendrick Motorsports</a>.  Prior to the current sponsorship deal with <a href="/wiki/Hendrick_Motorsports" title="Hendrick Motorsports">Hendrick Motorsports</a>, Lowe's was the sponsor of the No. 31 <a href="/wiki/Chevrolet" title="Chevrolet">Chevrolet</a> for <a href="/wiki/Richard_Childress_Racing" title="Richard Childress Racing">Richard Childress Racing</a> driven by <a href="/wiki/Mike_Skinner_(racing_driver)" title="Mike Skinner (racing driver)">Mike Skinner</a> and <a href="/wiki/Robby_Gordon" title="Robby Gordon">Robby Gordon</a> from 1997 to 2001. Prior to the RCR deal, Lowe's was the primary sponsor of the No. 11 <a href="/wiki/Ford" class="mw-redirect" title="Ford">Ford</a> driven by <a href="/wiki/Brett_Bodine" title="Brett Bodine">Brett Bodine</a> for <a href="/wiki/Junior_Johnson_%26_Associates" title="Junior Johnson &amp; Associates">Junior Johnson &amp; Associates</a> and later his own team, <a href="/wiki/Brett_Bodine_Racing" title="Brett Bodine Racing">Brett Bodine Racing</a> (after Bodine bought the No. 11 from Johnson). One of the company's earliest forays into racing sponsorship was in 1979, when it sponsored the No. 2 <a href="/wiki/Buick" title="Buick">Buick</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Rod_Osterlund" title="Rod Osterlund">Rod Osterlund</a> team in what was then known the Winston Cup Series, for the <a href="/wiki/Talladega_500" class="mw-redirect" title="Talladega 500">Talladega 500</a>. The car was driven in that race by <a href="/wiki/David_Pearson_(racing_driver)" title="David Pearson (racing driver)">David Pearson</a>, subbing for injured rookie <a href="/wiki/Dale_Earnhardt" title="Dale Earnhardt">Dale Earnhardt</a>. Lowe's would continue with sporadic car sponsorship throughout the next two decades until the aforementioned Bodine sponsorship in 1995. On March 14, 2018, Lowe's announced it would end sponsorship of the No. 48 car after the 2018 season thereby ending all involvement in Motorsports.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2020)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p><p>Lowe's also sponsored <a href="/wiki/Fernandez_Racing" class="mw-redirect" title="Fernandez Racing">Fernandez Racing</a> in the <a href="/wiki/American_Le_Mans_Series" title="American Le Mans Series">American Le Mans Series</a> and previously sponsored the team in the <a href="/wiki/Rolex_Sports_Car_Series" title="Rolex Sports Car Series">Rolex Sports Car Series</a>;  in that series, Lowe's sponsored the No. 99 Gainsco Stallings Racing Pontiac in events where Johnson was driving.
</p><p>In 2006, Lowe's contracted designer <a href="/wiki/Marianne_Cusato" title="Marianne Cusato">Marianne Cusato</a> to develop and offer affordable house plans for the hurricane-affected Gulf region. Lowe's is the exclusive retailer for both the plans and building materials for the Lowe's <a href="/wiki/Katrina_Cottage" title="Katrina Cottage">Katrina Cottage</a>. They offer easy construction and affordability, as well as the possibility of expansion. Moreover, they meet all international building codes and exceed hurricane codes. Lowe's discontinued the Katrina Cottage line in 2011.
</p><p>Lowe's has a wide variety of television and radio commercials. A significant number of different racing-inspired commercials can be seen and are often played outside of television race coverage. Before 2010, when he was replaced by Ben Yannette, <a href="/wiki/Gene_Hackman" title="Gene Hackman">Gene Hackman</a>'s voice could be heard on many commercial advertisements for Lowe's.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="All-American_Muslim_advertising_controversy"><i>All-American Muslim</i> advertising controversy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lowe%27s&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: All-American Muslim advertising controversy" data-section="7" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>Lowe's withdrew its advertising from the <a href="/wiki/TLC_(TV_channel)" class="mw-redirect" title="TLC (TV channel)">TLC</a> reality television show <i><a href="/wiki/All-American_Muslim" title="All-American Muslim">All-American Muslim</a></i> in December 2011.<sup id="cite_ref-wapo_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wapo-34">[34]</a></sup> A spokesperson for Lowe's said that "we understand the program raised concerns, complaints, or issues from multiple sides of the viewer spectrum, which we found after doing research of news articles and blogs covering the show".<sup id="cite_ref-wapo_34-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wapo-34">[34]</a></sup></p><p>Lowe's faced a backlash from several quarters, including Muslim-American and Arab-American organizations. The <a href="/wiki/American-Arab_Anti-Discrimination_Committee" title="American-Arab Anti-Discrimination Committee">American-Arab Anti-Discrimination Committee</a> called upon members to contact Lowe's to urge it to reverse its position.<sup id="cite_ref-aaadc_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-aaadc-35">[35]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles" title="Los Angeles">Los Angeles</a> chapter of the <a href="/wiki/Council_on_American-Islamic_Relations" class="mw-redirect" title="Council on American-Islamic Relations">Council on American-Islamic Relations</a> met to consider possible actions, including <a href="/wiki/Boycott" title="Boycott">boycotts</a> and protests. Several celebrities also called for a boycott.<sup id="cite_ref-backlash_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-backlash-36">[36]</a></sup> California State Senator <a href="/wiki/Ted_Lieu" title="Ted Lieu">Ted Lieu</a> called Lowe's decision "naked religious bigotry" and said he would consider legislative action if Lowe's did not apologize to Muslim-Americans and reinstate the ads.<sup id="cite_ref-CNNSenator_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CNNSenator-37">[37]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Abraham_Foxman" title="Abraham Foxman">Abraham Foxman</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Anti-Defamation_League" title="Anti-Defamation League">Anti-Defamation League</a> expressed similar views.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Keith_Ellison" title="Keith Ellison">Keith Ellison</a>, the first Muslim elected to the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Congress" title="United States Congress">United States Congress</a>, criticized Lowe's decision to "uphold the beliefs of a fringe hate group and not the creed of the <a href="/wiki/First_Amendment_to_the_United_States_Constitution" title="First Amendment to the United States Constitution">First Amendment</a>".<sup id="cite_ref-backlash_36-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-backlash-36">[36]</a></sup> Representative <a href="/wiki/John_Conyers" title="John Conyers">John Conyers</a> of <a href="/wiki/Michigan" title="Michigan">Michigan</a> called on Lowe's to apologize. Michigan state representative <a href="/wiki/Rashida_Tlaib" title="Rashida Tlaib">Rashida Tlaib</a> contacted the company's corporate headquarters, reporting that Lowe's declined to change its decision.<sup id="cite_ref-aaadc_35-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-aaadc-35">[35]</a></sup></p>

<table class="plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-move" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">It has been suggested that this section be <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Splitting" title="Wikipedia:Splitting">split</a> out  into another article titled <i><a href="/wiki/Lowe%27s_Canada" class="mw-redirect" title="Lowe's Canada">Lowe's Canada</a></i>. (<a href="/wiki/Talk:Lowe%27s" title="Talk:Lowe's">Discuss</a>) <small><i>(August 2018)</i></small></div></td></tr></tbody></table><p>Based in <a href="/wiki/Toronto" title="Toronto">Toronto</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ontario" title="Ontario">Ontario</a>, Lowe's opened its first three stores in Canada on December 10, 2007, in <a href="/wiki/Hamilton,_Ontario" title="Hamilton, Ontario">Hamilton</a>, <a href="/wiki/Brampton" title="Brampton">Brampton</a> and <a href="/wiki/Brantford" title="Brantford">Brantford</a>. On February 1, 2008, they opened three more stores in <a href="/wiki/Toronto" title="Toronto">Toronto</a>, <a href="/wiki/East_Gwillimbury,_Ontario" class="mw-redirect" title="East Gwillimbury, Ontario">East Gwillimbury</a>, and a second store in Brampton as well as a new location in Maple (Vaughan).<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> Currently, additional stores are under construction, with 19 now open in the province of Ontario. Lowe's also expanded into western Canada, starting with three new stores in <a href="/wiki/Calgary" title="Calgary">Calgary</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alberta" title="Alberta">Alberta</a>. One of the three locations opened in late September 2010.  The other two opened in early 2011. There are now stores in <a href="/wiki/British_Columbia" title="British Columbia">British Columbia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Manitoba" title="Manitoba">Manitoba</a> and <a href="/wiki/Saskatchewan" title="Saskatchewan">Saskatchewan</a>. To date (2018) Lowe's has 62 locations in Canada. Each store represents an average investment of $20.5 million (US$20.4 million).<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup></p><p>In February 2013, Lowe's Canada hired former <a href="/wiki/Walmart_Canada" title="Walmart Canada">Walmart Canada</a> and <a href="/wiki/Loblaw_Companies" title="Loblaw Companies">Loblaw Companies</a> executive Sylvain Prud'homme as CEO.<sup id="cite_ref-fp-homme_41-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fp-homme-41">[41]</a></sup></p><p>On May 11, 2015, Lowe's Canada announced that it would acquire the leases of 13 former <a href="/wiki/Target_Canada" title="Target Canada">Target Canada</a> stores, as well as an Ontario distribution centre, for $151 million.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup></p><p>In 2012, Lowe's attempted to buy <a href="/wiki/Rona,_Inc." title="Rona, Inc.">Rona, Inc.</a>, a Quebec-based hardware chain. However, the deal was met with objections from Rona shareholders (particularly the <a href="/wiki/Caisse_de_d%C3%A9p%C3%B4t_et_placement_du_Qu%C3%A9bec" title="Caisse de dépôt et placement du Québec">Caisse de dépôt et placement du Québec</a>) and operators of its franchised locations over concerns that the company could centralize its supply operations in the United States, and was eventually called off.<sup id="cite_ref-fp-homme_41-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fp-homme-41">[41]</a></sup> On February 3, 2016, Rona announced that it had accepted an offer to be acquired by Lowe's for <a href="/wiki/Canadian_dollar" title="Canadian dollar">CDN$</a>3.2 billion, pending regulatory and shareholder approval. The division will remain under the leadership of Sylvain Prud'homme, but will be operated out of Rona's headquarters in Boucherville. Lowe's plans to maintain Rona's retail banners, "key" executives, and the "vast majority of its current employees" post-acquisition.<sup id="cite_ref-cbc-lowesrona_43-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cbc-lowesrona-43">[43]</a></sup></p>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(7)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Australia">Australia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lowe%27s&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Australia" data-section="9" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-7 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-7">
<p>Trading as <a href="/wiki/Masters_Home_Improvement" title="Masters Home Improvement">Masters Home Improvement</a>, the first store opened in <a href="/wiki/Braybrook,_Victoria" title="Braybrook, Victoria">Braybrook, Victoria</a> to <a href="/wiki/Tradesmen" class="mw-redirect" title="Tradesmen">tradesmen</a> on August 31, 2011, and the general public on Masters was a <a href="/wiki/Joint_venture" title="Joint venture">joint venture</a> between Lowe's and <a href="/wiki/Woolworths_Group_(Australia)" title="Woolworths Group (Australia)">Woolworths</a> to compete against the <a href="/wiki/Wesfarmers" title="Wesfarmers">Wesfarmers</a> owned <a href="/wiki/Bunnings_Warehouse" title="Bunnings Warehouse">Bunnings Warehouse</a>, which operates in the large bigbox format similar to Lowe's and Home Depot.
</p><p>On 18 January 2016, Woolworths announced that it intended to "either sell or wind up" all its home improvement area, including the Masters hardware chain. Chairman Gordon Cairns said that it would take years to become profitable and that the ongoing losses could not be sustained. The windup involved Woolworths buying back a 33.3 per cent interest in the venture, held by the Lowe's subsidiary WDR Delaware Corporation. As of 28 August 2016<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Lowe%27s&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, all stores were due to cease trading on, or before, December 11, 2016.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2020)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(8)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Lawsuits">Lawsuits</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lowe%27s&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Lawsuits" data-section="10" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-8 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-8">
<p>Lowe's was involved in a small cluster of <a href="/wiki/Class_action_lawsuit" class="mw-redirect" title="Class action lawsuit">class action lawsuits</a> that revolved around the Lowe's employee payment system. The cases focused on a pay practice known as "variable rate overtime". Variable rate overtime has the effect of paying a decreasing overtime rate the more hours a person works in a week. The suits alleged that salaried managers who worked 40 to 50 hours per week were improperly compensated for that time. The variable rate overtime ended in the first quarter of 2006.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2020)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p><p>The first case was filed in October 2002 by employees of the Lowe's store in <a href="/wiki/Shawnee,_Kansas" title="Shawnee, Kansas">Shawnee, Kansas</a>. In September 2005, the cases were certified as class action. Lowe's has four similar cases in <a href="/wiki/New_York_(state)" title="New York (state)">New York</a>, <a href="/wiki/Indiana" title="Indiana">Indiana</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pennsylvania" title="Pennsylvania">Pennsylvania</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Ohio" title="Ohio">Ohio</a>. The lawsuits for New York, Indiana, and Kansas resulted in an out-of-court settlement on September 22, 2006.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> The lawsuit in Pennsylvania became a class-action lawsuit in June 2004, with 550 employees.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> The case in Ohio was filed by ten former Lowe's employees, in August 2004.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup></p><p>Lowe's faced multiple lawsuits from its Loss Prevention Managers citing that they were classified as exempt employees and therefore denied <a href="/wiki/Overtime" title="Overtime">overtime</a> pay. The managers asserted that they were forced to work a minimum of 48 hours per week which saved Lowe's and its investors millions of dollars every year. Lawsuits have been settled for $2.95 million in <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://archive.is/20130129080112/http://www.mybackwages.com/verdicts-settlements/lowes-loss-prevention-managers.aspx">California</a> and $6.2 million in Texas.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup></p><p>In 2014, Lowe's faced a class action lawsuit from former and current Human Resource Managers. Similarly to the lawsuit regarding its Loss Prevention Managers, the lawsuit stated that the HR Managers were improperly and illegally classified as exempt employees and were, therefore, denied overtime even though they were required to work 48-hour weeks.<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup></p>
