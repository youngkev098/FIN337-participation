</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(1)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Western_Digital&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History" data-section="1" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-1 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-1">
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="1970s">1970s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Western_Digital&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: 1970s" data-section="2" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>

Unsourced material may be challenged and removed.<br><small><span class="plainlinks"><i>Find sources:</i> <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="//www.google.com/search?as_eq=wikipedia&amp;q=%22Western+Digital%22">"Western Digital"</a> – <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="//www.google.com/search?tbm=nws&amp;q=%22Western+Digital%22+-wikipedia">news</a> <b>·</b> <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="//www.google.com/search?&amp;q=%22Western+Digital%22+site:news.google.com/newspapers&amp;source=newspapers">newspapers</a> <b>·</b> <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="//www.google.com/search?tbs=bks:1&amp;q=%22Western+Digital%22+-wikipedia">books</a> <b>·</b> <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="//scholar.google.com/scholar?q=%22Western+Digital%22">scholar</a> <b>·</b> <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://www.jstor.org/action/doBasicSearch?Query=%22Western+Digital%22&amp;acc=on&amp;wc=on">JSTOR</a></span></small></span>  <small class="date-container"><i>(<span class="date">January 2019</span>)</i></small><small class="hide-when-compact"><i> (<a href="/wiki/Help:Maintenance_template_removal" title="Help:Maintenance template removal">Learn how and when to remove this template message</a>)</i></small></div></td></tr></tbody></table><p>Western Digital was founded on April 23, 1970, by Alvin B. Phillips, a <a href="/wiki/Motorola" title="Motorola">Motorola</a> employee, as <b>General Digital</b>, initially a manufacturer of <a href="/wiki/MOSFET" title="MOSFET">MOS</a> test equipment.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> It rapidly became a speciality <a href="/wiki/Semiconductor" title="Semiconductor">semiconductor</a> maker, with start-up capital provided by several individual investors and industrial giant <a href="/wiki/Emerson_Electric" title="Emerson Electric">Emerson Electric</a>. Around July 1971, it adopted its current name and soon introduced its first product, the <i>WD1402A</i> <a href="/wiki/Universal_asynchronous_receiver/transmitter" class="mw-redirect" title="Universal asynchronous receiver/transmitter">UART</a>.
</p><p>The company was financed by <a href="/wiki/Emerson_Electric_Company" class="mw-redirect" title="Emerson Electric Company">Emerson Electric Company</a> and investors, to sell <a href="/wiki/Calculator" title="Calculator">calculator</a> chips through the early years of the 1970s, and by 1975, Western Digital was the largest independent calculator chip maker in the world. The <a href="/wiki/1973_oil_crisis" title="1973 oil crisis">oil crisis</a> of the mid-1970s and the bankruptcy of its biggest calculator customer, Bowmar Instrument,<sup id="cite_ref-NY_Times_Feb_1975_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NY_Times_Feb_1975-5">[5]</a></sup> changed its fortunes, however, and in 1976 Western Digital declared Chapter 11 bankruptcy. After this, Emerson Electric withdrew their support of the company.
</p><p>Western Digital introduced several products during the late 1970s, including the <i><a href="/wiki/MCP-1600" title="MCP-1600">MCP-1600</a></i> multi-chip, <a href="/wiki/Microcode" title="Microcode">microcoded</a> <a href="/wiki/Central_processing_unit" title="Central processing unit">CPU</a>. The MCP-1600 was used to implement <a href="/wiki/Digital_Equipment_Corporation" title="Digital Equipment Corporation">DEC</a>'s <a href="/wiki/PDP-11" title="PDP-11">LSI-11</a> system and their own <a href="/wiki/Pascal_MicroEngine" title="Pascal MicroEngine">Pascal MicroEngine</a> microcomputer which ran the <a href="/wiki/UCSD_Pascal" title="UCSD Pascal">UCSD p-System</a> Version III and <a href="/wiki/UCSD_Pascal" title="UCSD Pascal">UCSD Pascal</a>. However, the WD integrated circuit that arguably drove Western's forward integration was the <i><a href="/wiki/Western_Digital_FD1771" title="Western Digital FD1771">FD1771</a></i>,<sup id="cite_ref-Computer_Oct_1976_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Computer_Oct_1976-6">[6]</a></sup> one of the first single-chip floppy disk drive formatter/controllers, which could replace significant amounts of TTL logic.
</p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="1980s">1980s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Western_Digital&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: 1980s" data-section="3" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>The FD1771 and its kin were Western Digital's first entry into the data storage industry; by the early 1980s, they were making <a href="/wiki/Hard_disk_drive" title="Hard disk drive">hard disk drive</a> controllers, and in 1983, they won the contract to provide <a href="/wiki/IBM" title="IBM">IBM</a> with controllers for the <a href="/wiki/IBM_Personal_Computer/AT" title="IBM Personal Computer/AT">PC/AT</a>. That controller, the <i>WD1003</i>, became the basis of the <a href="/wiki/AT_Attachment" class="mw-redirect" title="AT Attachment">ATA</a> interface (which Western Digital developed along with <a href="/wiki/Compaq" title="Compaq">Compaq</a> and <a href="/wiki/Control_Data_Corporation" title="Control Data Corporation">Control Data Corporation's</a> MPI division, now owned by <a href="/wiki/Seagate_Technology" title="Seagate Technology">Seagate Technology</a>), starting in 1986. Throughout most of the 1980s, the family of controllers based on the WD1003 provided the bulk of Western Digital's revenues and profits, and for a time generated enormous corporate growth.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Paradise-VGA-1MB-89.png" class="image"><noscript><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/47/Paradise-VGA-1MB-89.png/220px-Paradise-VGA-1MB-89.png" decoding="async" width="220" height="159" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="1970" data-file-height="1428"></noscript><span class="lazy-image-placeholder" style="width: 220px;height: 159px;" data-src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/47/Paradise-VGA-1MB-89.png/220px-Paradise-VGA-1MB-89.png" data-alt="" data-width="220" data-height="159" data-class="thumbimage"> </span></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Paradise-VGA-1MB-89.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Western Digital Paradise VGA card, 8-bit <a href="/wiki/Industry_Standard_Architecture" title="Industry Standard Architecture">ISA</a> bus, circa 1989</div></div></div>
<p>Much of the mid-to-late 1980s saw an effort by Western Digital to use the profits from their ATA storage controllers to become a general-purpose <a href="/wiki/OEM" class="mw-redirect" title="OEM">OEM</a> hardware supplier for the PC industry. As a result, Western Digital purchased a number of hardware companies. These included <a href="/wiki/Video_card" title="Video card">graphics cards</a> (through its <i>Paradise</i> subsidiary, purchased 1986, which became Western Digital Imaging), core logic chipsets (by purchasing Faraday Electronics Inc. in 1987), SCSI controller chips for disk and tape devices (by purchasing ADSI in 1986), networking (<i>WD8003</i>, <i>WD8013</i> <a href="/wiki/Ethernet" title="Ethernet">Ethernet</a> and <i>WD8003S</i> <a href="/wiki/StarLAN" title="StarLAN">StarLAN</a>). They did well (especially Paradise, which produced one of the best <a href="/wiki/Video_Graphics_Array" title="Video Graphics Array">VGA</a> cards of the era), but storage-related chips and disk controllers were their biggest money makers. In 1986, they introduced the <i>WD33C93</i> single-chip <a href="/wiki/SCSI" title="SCSI">SCSI</a> interface, which was used in the first 16-bit <a href="/wiki/Bus_mastering" title="Bus mastering">bus mastering</a> SCSI host adapter, the <i>WD7000 "FASST"</i>; in 1987 they introduced the <i>WD37C65</i>, a single-chip implementation of the PC/AT's floppy disk controller circuitry, and the grandfather of modern <a href="/wiki/Super_I/O" title="Super I/O">super I/O</a> chips; in 1988 they introduced the <i>WD42C22 "Vanilla"</i>, the first single-chip ATA hard disk controller.
</p><p>1988 also brought what would be the biggest change in Western Digital's history. That year, Western Digital bought the hard drive production assets of PC hardware maker <a href="/wiki/Tandon_Corporation" title="Tandon Corporation">Tandon</a>; the first products of that union under Western Digital's own name were the "Centaur" series of ATA and XT attachment drives.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>

<p>By 1991, things were starting to slow down, as the PC industry moved from <a href="/wiki/ST-506" class="mw-redirect" title="ST-506">ST-506</a> and <a href="/wiki/Enhanced_Small_Disk_Interface" title="Enhanced Small Disk Interface">ESDI</a> drives to ATA and SCSI, and thus were buying fewer hard disk controller boards. That year saw the rise of Western Digital's <i>Caviar</i> drives, brand new designs that used the latest in <a href="/wiki/Embedded_servo" title="Embedded servo">embedded servo</a> and computerized diagnostic systems.
</p><p>Eventually, the successful sales of the Caviar drives resulted in Western Digital starting to sell some of its divisions. Paradise was sold to <a href="/wiki/Philips" title="Philips">Philips</a>, and since disappeared. Its networking and floppy drive controller divisions went to SMC Networks and its SCSI chip business went to Future Domain, which was later bought out by market leader <a href="/wiki/Adaptec" title="Adaptec">Adaptec</a>. Around 1995, the technological lead that the Caviar drives had enjoyed was eclipsed by newer offerings from other companies, especially <a href="/wiki/Quantum_Corp." class="mw-redirect" title="Quantum Corp.">Quantum Corp.</a>, and Western Digital fell into a slump.
</p><p>Products and ideas of this time did not go far. The <i>Portfolio</i> drive (a 3-inch (76 mm) <a href="/wiki/Form_factor_(electronics)" title="Form factor (electronics)">form factor</a> model, developed with <a href="/wiki/JT_Storage" title="JT Storage">JT Storage</a>) was a flop, as was the SDX hard disk to <a href="/wiki/CD-ROM" title="CD-ROM">CD-ROM</a> interface. Western Digital's drives started to slip further behind competing products, and quality began to suffer; <a href="/wiki/Custom_built_PC" class="mw-redirect" title="Custom built PC">system builders</a> and PC enthusiasts who used to recommend Western Digital above all else, were going to the competition, particularly <a href="/wiki/Maxtor" title="Maxtor">Maxtor</a>, whose products had improved significantly by the late 1990s.
</p><p>In an attempt to turn the tide in 1998, Western Digital recruited the help of <a href="/wiki/IBM" title="IBM">IBM</a>. This agreement gave Western Digital the rights to use certain IBM technologies, including <a href="/wiki/Giant_magnetoresistance" title="Giant magnetoresistance">giant magneto-resistive (GMR)</a> heads and access to IBM production facilities. The result was the <i>Expert</i> line of drives, introduced in early 1999. The idea worked, and Western Digital regained respect in the press and among users, even despite a recall in 2000 (which was due to bad motor driver chips). Western Digital later broke ties to IBM.
</p>

<p>In 2001, Western Digital became the first manufacturer to offer mainstream ATA hard disk drives with 8 <a href="/wiki/Mebibyte" title="Mebibyte">MiB</a> of <a href="/wiki/Disk_buffer" title="Disk buffer">disk buffer</a>. At that time, most desktop hard disk drives had 2 MB of buffer. Western Digital labeled these 8 MB models as "Special Edition" and distinguished them with the <i>JB</i> code (the 2 MB models had the <i>BB</i> code). The first 8 MB cache drive was the 100 <a href="/wiki/Gigabyte" title="Gigabyte">GB</a> WD1000JB, followed by other models starting with 40 GB capacity. Western Digital advertised the JB models for cost-effective <a href="/wiki/File_server" title="File server">file servers</a>.  In October 2001, Western Digital restated its prior year results to reflect the adoption of SEC Staff Accounting Bulletin No.101 and the reclassification of Connex and SANavigator results as discontinued operations.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p><p>In 2003, Western Digital acquired most of the assets of bankrupt one-time market leading magnetic hard drive read-write head developer Read-Rite Corporation. In the same year, Western Digital offered the first 10,000 <a href="/wiki/Revolutions_per_minute" title="Revolutions per minute">rpm</a> <a href="/wiki/Serial_ATA" title="Serial ATA">Serial ATA</a> HDD, the WD360GD "<a href="/wiki/WD_Raptor" class="mw-redirect" title="WD Raptor">Raptor</a>", with a capacity of 36 GB and an average access time of less than six milliseconds. Soon, the 74 GB WD740GD followed, which was also much quieter. In 2004, Western Digital redesigned its logo for the first time since 1997, with the design of new logo focusing on the company's initials ("WD").<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> In 2005, Western Digital released the 150 GB version, the WD1500, which was also available in a special version with a transparent window enabling the user to see the drive's heads move over the platters while the drive read and wrote data. As of 2004<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Western_Digital&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, the <a href="/wiki/Western_Digital_Raptor" title="Western Digital Raptor">Western Digital Raptor</a> drives have a five-year warranty, making them a more attractive choice for inexpensive <a href="/wiki/File_server" title="File server">storage servers</a>, where a large number of drives in constant use increases the likelihood of a drive failure.
</p><p>In 2006, Western Digital introduced its My Book line of mass market external hard drives that feature a compact book-like design. On October 7, 2007, Western Digital released several editions of a single 1 <a href="/wiki/Terabyte" title="Terabyte">TB</a> hard drive, the largest in its My Book line.
</p><p><span id="GREEN-POWER"></span>
In 2007, Western Digital acquired magnetic media maker Komag. Also in the same year, Western Digital adopted <a href="/wiki/Perpendicular_recording" title="Perpendicular recording">perpendicular recording</a> technology in its line of notebook and desktop drives. This allowed it to produce notebook and desktop drives in the largest classes of the time. Western Digital also started to produce the energy efficient GP (Green Power) range of drives.
</p><p>In 2007, Western Digital announced the WD GP drive touting rotational speed "between 7200 and 5400 rpm", which is technically correct while also being misleading; the drive spins at 5405 rpm, and the Green Power spin speed is not variable.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> WD GP drives are programmed to unload the heads whenever idle for a very short period of time.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>  Many Linux installations write to the file system a few times a minute in the background.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> As a result, there may be 100 or more load cycles per hour, and the 300,000 load cycles rating of a WD GP drive may be exceeded in less than a year.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Two_Western_Digital_VelociRaptor_1_TB_SATA_10,000_rpm_3.5-inch_HDDs.jpg" class="image"><noscript><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/38/Two_Western_Digital_VelociRaptor_1_TB_SATA_10%2C000_rpm_3.5-inch_HDDs.jpg/220px-Two_Western_Digital_VelociRaptor_1_TB_SATA_10%2C000_rpm_3.5-inch_HDDs.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="1024" data-file-height="768"></noscript><span class="lazy-image-placeholder" style="width: 220px;height: 165px;" data-src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/38/Two_Western_Digital_VelociRaptor_1_TB_SATA_10%2C000_rpm_3.5-inch_HDDs.jpg/220px-Two_Western_Digital_VelociRaptor_1_TB_SATA_10%2C000_rpm_3.5-inch_HDDs.jpg" data-alt="" data-width="220" data-height="165" data-class="thumbimage"> </span></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Two_Western_Digital_VelociRaptor_1_TB_SATA_10,000_rpm_3.5-inch_HDDs.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Two third-generation 1 TB <a href="/wiki/VelociRaptor" class="mw-redirect" title="VelociRaptor">VelociRaptors</a> in IcePack mounting frames</div></div></div>
<p>On April 21, 2008, Western Digital announced the next generation of its 10,000 rpm SATA WD Raptor series of hard drives. The new drives, called WD <a href="/wiki/VelociRaptor" class="mw-redirect" title="VelociRaptor">VelociRaptor</a>, featured 300 GB capacity and 2.5-inch (64 mm) platters enclosed in the IcePack, a 3.5-inch (89 mm) mounting frame with a built-in heat sink. Western Digital said that the new drives are 35 percent faster than the previous generation. On September 12, 2008, Western Digital shipped a 500 GB 2.5-inch (64 mm) notebook hard drive which is part of their Scorpio Blue series of notebook hard drives.
</p><p>On January 27, 2009, Western Digital shipped the first 2 TB internal hard disk drive.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>  On March 30, 2009, they entered the <a href="/wiki/Solid-state_drive" title="Solid-state drive">solid-state drive</a> market with the acquisition of Siliconsystems, Inc. Its acquisition was unsuccessful, and few years later Western Digital discontinued all solid-state storage products based on Siliconsystems design (<i>SiliconEdge</i> and <i>SiliconDrive</i> families of SSDs and memory cards), but its inventions was used later in development of various other solid-state storage products, with larger developments going on after 2016 acquisition of SanDisk.
</p><p>On July 27, 2009, Western Digital announced the first 1 TB mobile hard disk drive, which shipped as both a Passport series portable USB drive as well as a Scorpio Blue series notebook drive.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p><p>In October 2009, Western Digital announced the shipment of first 3 TB internal hard disk drive, which has 750 GB-per-platter density with SATA interface.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="2010s"><span id="ULTRASTAR-HE6"></span><span id="RED"></span>2010s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Western_Digital&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: 2010s" data-section="6" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>In March 2011, Western Digital agreed to acquire the storage unit of <a href="/wiki/Hitachi" title="Hitachi">Hitachi</a>, <a href="/wiki/HGST" title="HGST">HGST</a>, for about $4.3 billion of which $3.5 billion was paid in cash and the rest with 25 million shares of Western Digital.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Western_Digital_%22Red%22_4_TB_SATA_NAS-optimized_3.5-inch_HDD.jpg" class="image"><noscript><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ee/Western_Digital_%22Red%22_4_TB_SATA_NAS-optimized_3.5-inch_HDD.jpg/220px-Western_Digital_%22Red%22_4_TB_SATA_NAS-optimized_3.5-inch_HDD.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="1024" data-file-height="768"></noscript><span class="lazy-image-placeholder" style="width: 220px;height: 165px;" data-src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ee/Western_Digital_%22Red%22_4_TB_SATA_NAS-optimized_3.5-inch_HDD.jpg/220px-Western_Digital_%22Red%22_4_TB_SATA_NAS-optimized_3.5-inch_HDD.jpg" data-alt="" data-width="220" data-height="165" data-class="thumbimage"> </span></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Western_Digital_%22Red%22_4_TB_SATA_NAS-optimized_3.5-inch_HDD.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Western Digital "Red" 4 TB, a <a href="/wiki/Network-attached_storage" title="Network-attached storage">NAS</a>-optimized 3.5-inch SATA HDD</div></div></div>
<p>In March 2012, Western Digital completed the acquisition of HGST and became the largest traditional hard drive manufacturer in the world; to address the requirements of regulatory agencies, in May 2012 Western Digital divested assets to manufacture and sell certain 3.5-inch hard drives for the desktop and consumer electronics markets to <a href="/wiki/Toshiba" title="Toshiba">Toshiba</a>, in exchange for one of its 2.5-inch hard drive factories in Thailand.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p><p>In February 2014, Western Digital announced a new "Purple" line of hard disk drives for use in <a href="/wiki/Video_surveillance" class="mw-redirect" title="Video surveillance">video surveillance</a> systems, with capacities from 1 to 4 TB. They feature internal optimizations for applications that involve near-constant disk writing, and "AllFrame" technology which is designed to reduce write errors.<sup id="cite_ref-pcworld-purplehdd_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pcworld-purplehdd-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Western_Digital_My_Passport_Ultra.jpg" class="image"><noscript><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b5/Western_Digital_My_Passport_Ultra.jpg/220px-Western_Digital_My_Passport_Ultra.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="124" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="5312" data-file-height="2988"></noscript><span class="lazy-image-placeholder" style="width: 220px;height: 124px;" data-src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b5/Western_Digital_My_Passport_Ultra.jpg/220px-Western_Digital_My_Passport_Ultra.jpg" data-alt="" data-width="220" data-height="124" data-class="thumbimage"> </span></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Western_Digital_My_Passport_Ultra.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A red redesigned My Passport Ultra Drive with a storage of 2TB</div></div></div>
<p>In October 2015, after being required to operate the company autonomously from WD, the <a href="/wiki/Ministry_of_Commerce_(China)" title="Ministry of Commerce (China)">Chinese Ministry of Commerce</a> issued a decision allowing the company to begin integrating HGST into its main business, but under the condition that it maintain the HGST brand and sales team for at least two more years.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> The HGST brand was definitely phased out in 2018, and since then, all HGST-branded products are just branded Western Digital.
</p><p>In May 2016, Western Digital acquired <a href="/wiki/SanDisk" title="SanDisk">SanDisk</a> for US$19 billion.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-verge-wdsandisk_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-verge-wdsandisk-21">[21]</a></sup> In the summer of 2017, Western Digital licensed the <a href="/wiki/Fusion-io" title="Fusion-io">Fusion-io</a>/SanDisk ION Accelerator software to One Stop Systems.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> In August 2017, Western Digital acquired cloud storage provider Upthere, with the intention to continue building out the service.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> In September 2017, Western Digital acquired <a href="/wiki/Tegile_Systems" title="Tegile Systems">Tegile Systems</a>, maker of <a href="/wiki/Flash_memory" title="Flash memory">flash memory</a> <a href="/wiki/Storage_array" class="mw-redirect" title="Storage array">storage arrays</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p><p>In April 2017, Western Digital moved its headquarters from Irvine, California to San Jose, California.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> In December 2017, Western Digital reached an agreement with Toshiba about the sale of the jointly owned NAND production facility in Japan.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> In May 2018, Toshiba reached an agreement with the Bain consortium about the sale of that chip unit.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p><p>In July 2018, Western Digital announced their plan to close their hard disk production facility in <a href="/wiki/Kuala_Lumpur" title="Kuala Lumpur">Kuala Lumpur</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> The company ranked 158th on the 2018 <a href="/wiki/Fortune_500" title="Fortune 500">Fortune 500</a> of the largest United States corporations by revenue.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p><p>In June 2019, <a href="/wiki/Kioxia" title="Kioxia">Toshiba Memory</a> (currently Kioxia) experienced a power cut at one of its factories in Yokkaichi, Japan, resulting in the loss of at least 6 exabytes of flash memory, with some sources estimating the loss as high as 15 exabytes. Western Digital used (and still uses) Kioxia's facilities for making its own flash memory chips.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p>

<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Hard_drives">Hard drives</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Western_Digital&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Hard drives" data-section="8" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>Self-encrypting Western Digital hard drives have been reported to have severe faults and to be easy to decrypt.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></p><p>Western Digital was the last manufacturer of <a href="/wiki/Parallel_ATA" title="Parallel ATA">parallel ATA</a> hard disk drives for laptops (2.5-inch form factor) and desktop PCs (3.5-inch form factor), producing them until December 2013.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> Furthermore, they were the only manufacturer that have 250 GB and 320 GB in 2.5-inch form factor.
</p><p>Western Digital Purple hard drives are designed for write-heavy workloads; for instance, security applications (ex: recording video). These drives feature AllFrame technology, which attempts to reduce video frame loss, <a href="/wiki/Error_recovery_control" title="Error recovery control">time limited error recovery</a>, and support for the ATA streaming command set.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></p><p>Western Digital sells data center software and system solutions.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Use_plain_English#Buzzwords" title="Wikipedia:Use plain English"><span title="The material near this tag may use buzzwords designed solely to impress you or obscure meaning. (September 2019)">buzzword</span></a></i>]</sup><sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> Including an enterprise-class Ultrastar product line.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> In October 2017, Western Digital shipped the world's first 14 TB HDD, the helium-filled HGST Ultrastar Hs14.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Consumer_products">Consumer products</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Western_Digital&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Consumer products" data-section="9" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>Western Digital sells consumer storage products under the WD brand, with product families called <a href="/wiki/My_Passport" title="My Passport">My Passport</a>, <a href="/wiki/Western_Digital_My_Book" title="Western Digital My Book">My Book</a>, <a href="/wiki/WD_TV" title="WD TV">WD TV</a>, and <a href="/wiki/My_Cloud" title="My Cloud">My Cloud</a>. While traditionally these products have used HDDs, Western Digital has started to offer SSD versions, such as the My Passport SSD, its first portable SSD.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> In September 2015, Western Digital released My Cloud OS 3, a platform that enables connected HDDs to sync between PCs and mobile devices.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup></p><p>Western Digital also sells consumer data technology products under the <a href="/wiki/SanDisk" title="SanDisk">SanDisk</a> and <a href="/wiki/G-Technology" title="G-Technology">G-Technology</a> product brands, as well as cloud storage services under the Upthere brand. Through Western Digital's acquisition of Upthere, the company offers personal cloud storage through the Upthere Home app and UpOS operating system.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup></p><p>Under the SanDisk brand, Western Digital offers mobile storage products, cards and readers, USB flash drives, SSDs and MP3 players. Most of Western Digital's consumer flash memory products are offered through SanDisk. The SanDisk iXpand product family, including the iXpand Flash Drive and iXpand Base, is made specifically for use with the <a href="/wiki/Apple_Inc." title="Apple Inc.">Apple</a> <a href="/wiki/IPhone" title="IPhone">iPhone</a> and <a href="/wiki/IPad" title="IPad">iPad</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> The 400<span class="nowrap"> </span>GB SanDisk Ultra microSDXC UHS-I card was designed primarily for use in <a href="/wiki/Android_(operating_system)" title="Android (operating system)">Android</a> smartphones that include an expansion slot.<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup></p><p>Under the G-Technology brand, Western Digital offers HDD, SSD, platforms and systems products designed specifically for creative professionals.<sup id="cite_ref-MacRumors_51-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MacRumors-51">[51]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-MacRumors_51-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MacRumors-51">[51]</a></sup></p>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(3)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Corporate_affairs">Corporate affairs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Western_Digital&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Corporate affairs" data-section="10" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-3 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-3">
<p>Western Digital Capital is Western Digital's investment arm.<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup> It has contributed funding for data technology companies such as Elastifile and <a href="/wiki/Avere_Systems" title="Avere Systems">Avere Systems</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Lawsuits">Lawsuits</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Western_Digital&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Lawsuits" data-section="11" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Binary_prefix#Legal_disputes" title="Binary prefix">Binary prefix § Legal disputes</a></div>
<p>Lawsuits have been filed against various manufacturers including Western Digital,<sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup> related to the claimed capacity of their drives. The drives are labelled using the convention of 10<sup>3</sup> (1,000) bytes to the kilobyte, resulting in a perceived capacity shortfall when reported by most operating systems, which tend to use 2<sup>10</sup> (1,024) bytes to the kilobyte.<sup id="cite_ref-ars_59-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ars-59">[59]</a></sup></p><p>While Western Digital maintained that they used "the indisputably correct industry standard for measuring and describing storage capacity", and that they "cannot be expected to reform the software industry", they agreed to settle in March 2006,<sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup> with a $30 refund to affected customers in the form of backup and recovery software of the same value.<sup id="cite_ref-ars_59-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ars-59">[59]</a></sup></p>
