<p><b>The Hershey Company</b>, commonly known as <b>Hershey's</b>, is an American <a href="/wiki/Multinational_corporation" title="Multinational corporation">multinational</a> company and one of the largest <a href="/wiki/Chocolate" title="Chocolate">chocolate</a> manufacturers in the world. It also manufactures baked products, such as cookies, cakes, milk shakes, drinks and many more.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Its headquarters are in <a href="/wiki/Hershey,_Pennsylvania" title="Hershey, Pennsylvania">Hershey, Pennsylvania</a>, which is also home to <a href="/wiki/Hersheypark" title="Hersheypark">Hersheypark</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hershey%27s_Chocolate_World" title="Hershey's Chocolate World">Hershey's Chocolate World</a>. It was founded by <a href="/wiki/Milton_S._Hershey" title="Milton S. Hershey">Milton S. Hershey</a> in 1894 as the Hershey Chocolate Company, a subsidiary of his <a href="/wiki/Lancaster_Caramel_Company" title="Lancaster Caramel Company">Lancaster Caramel Company</a>. <h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_Years">Early Years</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Hershey_Company&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Early Years" data-section="2" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>After an <a href="/wiki/Apprenticeship" title="Apprenticeship">apprenticeship</a> to a confectioner in 1873, <a href="/wiki/Milton_S._Hershey" title="Milton S. Hershey">Milton S. Hershey</a> founded a candy shop in <a href="/wiki/Philadelphia" title="Philadelphia">Philadelphia</a>. This candy shop was only open for six years, after which Hershey apprenticed with another confectioner in Denver, where he learned to make <a href="/wiki/Caramel" title="Caramel">caramel</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> After another failed business attempt in New York, Hershey returned to Pennsylvania, where in 1886 he founded the <a href="/wiki/Lancaster_Caramel_Company" title="Lancaster Caramel Company">Lancaster Caramel Company</a>. The use of fresh milk in caramels proved successful,<sup id="cite_ref-Reference_For_Business.com_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Reference_For_Business.com-12">[12]</a></sup> and in 1900, after seeing chocolate-making machines for the first time at the 1893 <a href="/wiki/World%27s_Columbian_Exposition" title="World's Columbian Exposition">World's Columbian Exposition</a> in Chicago, Hershey sold his caramel company for $1,000,000<sup id="cite_ref-Reference_For_Business.com_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Reference_For_Business.com-12">[12]</a></sup> (equal to $30,732,000 today), and began to concentrate on chocolate manufacturing, stating to people who questioned him, "Caramels are just a fad, but chocolate is a permanent thing."
</p><p>In 1896, Milton built a milk-processing plant so he could create and refine a recipe for milk chocolate candies.  In 1899, he developed the Hershey process, which is less sensitive to milk quality than traditional methods. In 1900, he began manufacturing <a href="/wiki/Hershey_bar" title="Hershey bar">Hershey's Milk Chocolate Bars</a>, also called Hershey's Bars or Hershey Bars.
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Hershey_cocoa_ad_ww1.png" class="image"><noscript><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e7/Hershey_cocoa_ad_ww1.png/220px-Hershey_cocoa_ad_ww1.png" decoding="async" width="220" height="264" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="2292" data-file-height="2746"></noscript><span class="lazy-image-placeholder" style="width: 220px;height: 264px;" data-src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e7/Hershey_cocoa_ad_ww1.png/220px-Hershey_cocoa_ad_ww1.png" data-alt="" data-width="220" data-height="264" data-class="thumbimage"> </span></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Hershey_cocoa_ad_ww1.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Hershey's Cocoa ad from 1918</div></div></div>
<h3 class="in-block"><span id="Hershey.2C_Pennsylvania"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Hershey,_Pennsylvania">Hershey, Pennsylvania</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Hershey_Company&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Hershey, Pennsylvania" data-section="3" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>In 1903, Hershey began construction of a chocolate plant in his hometown of Derry Church, Pennsylvania, which later came to be known as <a href="/wiki/Hershey,_Pennsylvania" title="Hershey, Pennsylvania">Hershey, Pennsylvania</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Reference_For_Business.com_12-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Reference_For_Business.com-12">[12]</a></sup> The town was an inexpensive place for the workers and their families to live; however the factory itself was built without windows so that employees would not be distracted.  To increase employee morale, Milton provided leisure activities and created what would later become Hersheypark to make sure the citizens enjoyed themselves.  The milk chocolate bars manufactured at this plant proved popular, and the company grew rapidly.
</p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span id="Hershey.27s_Kisses"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Hershey's_Kisses">Hershey's Kisses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Hershey_Company&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Hershey's Kisses" data-section="4" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>In 1907, he introduced a new candy - bite-sized, flat-bottomed, conical-shaped pieces of chocolate that he named "<a href="/wiki/Hershey%27s_Kiss" class="mw-redirect" title="Hershey's Kiss">Hershey's Kiss</a>".  At first they were individually wrapped by hand in squares of aluminum foil. The introduction of machine wrapping in 1921 simplified the process while adding the small paper ribbon to the top of the package to indicate that it was a genuine Hershey product.<sup id="cite_ref-Reference_For_Business.com_12-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Reference_For_Business.com-12">[12]</a></sup>  Today, 80 million of the candies are produced daily.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2019)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>  Other products introduced included <a href="/wiki/Mr._Goodbar" title="Mr. Goodbar">Mr. Goodbar</a> (1925), containing peanuts in milk chocolate, Hershey's Syrup (1926), semisweet chocolate chips (1928), and the <a href="/wiki/Krackel" title="Krackel">Krackel</a> bar containing <a href="/wiki/Crisped_rice" class="mw-redirect" title="Crisped rice">crisped rice</a> (1938).
</p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Unionization">Unionization</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Hershey_Company&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Unionization" data-section="5" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>Labor unrest came to Hershey in the late 1930s as a <a href="/wiki/Congress_of_Industrial_Organizations" title="Congress of Industrial Organizations">Congress of Industrial Organizations</a>-backed union attempted to organize the factory workers.  A failed sit-down strike in 1937 ended in violence, as loyalist workers and local dairy farmers beat many of the strikers as they attempted to leave the plant.  By 1940, an affiliate of the <a href="/wiki/American_Federation_of_Labor" title="American Federation of Labor">American Federation of Labor</a> had successfully organized Hershey's workers under the leadership of <a href="/w/index.php?title=John_Shearer_(unionist)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="John Shearer (unionist) (page does not exist)">John Shearer</a>, who became the first president of Local Chapter Number 464 of the <a href="/wiki/Bakery,_Confectionery,_Tobacco_Workers_and_Grain_Millers%27_International_Union" title="Bakery, Confectionery, Tobacco Workers and Grain Millers' International Union">Bakery, Confectionery, Tobacco Workers and Grain Millers' International Union</a>.  Local 464 still represents the Hershey workforce.
</p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span id="M.26M.27s"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="M&amp;M's">M&amp;M's</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Hershey_Company&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: M&amp;M's" data-section="6" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>Shortly before <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bruce_Murrie" class="mw-redirect" title="Bruce Murrie">Bruce Murrie</a>, son of long-time Hershey's president <a href="/w/index.php?title=William_F.R._Murrie&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="William F.R. Murrie (page does not exist)">William F.R. Murrie</a>, struck a deal with <a href="/wiki/Forrest_Mars,_Sr." class="mw-redirect" title="Forrest Mars, Sr.">Forrest Mars</a> to create a hard sugar-coated chocolate that would be called <a href="/wiki/M%26M%27s" title="M&amp;M's">M&amp;M's</a> (for Mars and Murrie).  Murrie had  20% interest in the confection, which used Hershey chocolate during the <a href="/wiki/Rationing" title="Rationing">rationing</a> era during World War II.  In 1948, Mars bought out Murrie's interest and became one of Hershey's main competitors.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="21st_century">21st century</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Hershey_Company&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: 21st century" data-section="7" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>In June 2006, Philadelphia city councilman Juan Ramos called for Hershey's to stop marketing "Ice Breakers Pacs", a kind of mint, due to the resemblance of its packaging to a kind that was used for illegal street drugs.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p><p>In September 2006, <a href="/wiki/ABC_News" title="ABC News">ABC News</a> reported that several Hershey chocolate products were reformulated to replace <a href="/wiki/Cocoa_butter" title="Cocoa butter">cocoa butter</a> with vegetable oil as an <a href="/wiki/Emulsifier" class="mw-redirect" title="Emulsifier">emulsifier</a>. According to the company, this change was made to reduce the costs of producing the products instead of raising their prices or decreasing the sizes. Some consumers complained that the taste was different, but the company stated that in the company-sponsored blind taste tests, about half of consumers preferred the new versions. As the new versions no longer met the <a href="/wiki/Food_and_Drug_Administration" title="Food and Drug Administration">Food and Drug Administration</a>'s official definition of "milk chocolate", the changed items were relabeled from stating they were "milk chocolate" and "made with chocolate" to "chocolate candy" and "chocolatey."<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p><p>In April 2015, the Hershey chocolate plant on East Chocolate Avenue in Hershey Pennsylvania was demolished to make way for mixed-use development.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p><p>A 2016 attempt to sell Hershey to <a href="/wiki/Mondelez_International" title="Mondelez International">Mondelez International</a> was scuttled because of objections by the Hershey Trust.<sup id="cite_ref-cnbc.com_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cnbc.com-17">[17]</a></sup></p><p>In October 2019, Hershey's partnered with <a href="/wiki/Yuengling" title="Yuengling">Yuengling</a> to produce a limited release collaboration beer titled Yuengling Hershey's Chocolate Porter, becoming Hershey's first licensed beer partnership.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span id="Reese.27s_Peanut_Butter_Cups"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Reese's_Peanut_Butter_Cups">Reese's Peanut Butter Cups</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Hershey_Company&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Reese's Peanut Butter Cups" data-section="8" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/H.B._Reese" class="mw-redirect" title="H.B. Reese">Harry Burnett Reese</a> invented <a href="/wiki/Reese%27s_Peanut_Butter_Cups" title="Reese's Peanut Butter Cups">Reese's Peanut Butter Cups</a> after founding the H.B. Reese Candy Company in 1923.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> Reese died on May 16, 1956 in <a href="/wiki/West_Palm_Beach,_Florida" title="West Palm Beach, Florida">West Palm Beach, Florida</a> leaving the company to his six sons.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> On July 2, 1963 the H.B. Reese Candy Company was acquired by the Hershey Chocolate Corporation in a tax free stock-for-stock merger. In 2017 after 54-years of stock splits, the original 666,316 shares of Hershey common stock received by the Reese family represented 16 million Hershey shares valued at over $1.8 billion that pay annual cash dividends of $42 million.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> In 1969, only 6-years after the Reese/Hershey merger, Reese's Peanut Butter Cups became The Hershey Company's top seller.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> As of September 20, 2012, Reese's is the best-selling candy brand in the United States with sales of $2.603 billion, and is the fourth-best-selling candy brand globally with sales of $2.679 billion—only $76 million (2.8%) of its sales are from outside the United States market.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span id="Cadbury.27s"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Cadbury's">Cadbury's</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Hershey_Company&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Cadbury's" data-section="9" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>In 1988, Hershey's acquired the rights to manufacture and distribute many <a href="/wiki/Cadbury" title="Cadbury">Cadbury</a>-branded products in the United States (except gum and mints, which are part of <a href="/wiki/Mondelez_International" title="Mondelez International">Mondelēz International</a>).  In 2015, they sued a British importer to halt imports of British Cadbury chocolate, angering consumers.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> A <a href="/wiki/Merger" class="mw-redirect" title="Merger">merger</a> between Mondelēz and Hershey's was considered but abandoned in 2016 after Hershey's turned down a $23 billion cash-and-stock bid.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Krave_Jerky">Krave Jerky</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Hershey_Company&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Krave Jerky" data-section="10" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>In 2005, Krave Jerky was founded by Jon Sebastiani after he trained for a marathon and looked for a healthy source of energy.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup>  Alliance Consumer Growth, a <a href="/wiki/Private_equity" title="Private equity">private equity</a> group, invested in Krave Jerky in 2012.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> Hershey's purchased the company in 2015 for $240 million.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(2)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span id="Milton_Hershey_School_.28MHS.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Milton_Hershey_School_(MHS)">Milton Hershey School (MHS)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Hershey_Company&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Milton Hershey School (MHS)" data-section="11" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-2 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-2">
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Milton_Hershey_School" title="Milton Hershey School">Milton Hershey School</a></div>
<p>Unable to have children of his own, Milton S. Hershey founded the Hershey Industrial School in 1909 for white orphaned boys.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> In 1918, three years after the death of his wife, Milton Hershey donated around $60 million to the boarding school in trust, as well as 40% of the Hershey Company's common stock.<sup id="cite_ref-:1_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-31">[31]</a></sup> The school's initial purpose was to train young men in trades, but eventually shifted to focus on preparation for college.<sup id="cite_ref-:1_31-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-31">[31]</a></sup> The Hershey Trust Company has exercised voting rights for the school, and has been a trustee since its founding.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p><p>Many of its designs resemble Hershey chocolate products, such as the Hershey Kisses street lights.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> Milton Hershey was involved in the school's operations until his death in 1945. The Hershey Industrial School was renamed the Milton Hershey School in 1951.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(3)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Manufacturing_plants">Manufacturing plants</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Hershey_Company&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Manufacturing plants" data-section="12" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-3 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-3">
<p>The first plant outside Hershey opened on June 15, 1963, in <a href="/wiki/Smiths_Falls,_Ontario" class="mw-redirect" title="Smiths Falls, Ontario">Smiths Falls, Ontario</a>, Canada, and the third opened on May 22, 1965, in <a href="/wiki/Oakdale,_California" title="Oakdale, California">Oakdale, California</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Hershey's.com_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hershey's.com-35">[35]</a></sup> In February and April 2007, Hershey's announced that the Smiths Falls<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> and Oakdale<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-modbeeclosing_39-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-modbeeclosing-39">[39]</a></sup> plants would close in 2008, being replaced in part by a new facility in <a href="/wiki/Monterrey" title="Monterrey">Monterrey, Mexico</a>. The Oakdale factory closed on February 1, 2008.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> Hershey chocolate factory in São Roque, Brazil, was opened in August 2002. Hershey's Asia operations are largely supplied by their plant in <a href="/w/index.php?title=Mandideep,_Madhya_Pradesh&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Mandideep, Madhya Pradesh (page does not exist)">Mandideep, Madhya Pradesh</a>, <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup></p><p>Hershey also has plants in <a href="/wiki/Stuarts_Draft,_Virginia" title="Stuarts Draft, Virginia">Stuarts Draft, Virginia</a>; <a href="/wiki/Lancaster,_Pennsylvania" title="Lancaster, Pennsylvania">Lancaster, Pennsylvania</a>; <a href="/wiki/Hazleton,_Pennsylvania" title="Hazleton, Pennsylvania">Hazleton, Pennsylvania</a>; <a href="/wiki/Memphis,_Tennessee" title="Memphis, Tennessee">Memphis, Tennessee</a>; <a href="/wiki/Robinson,_Illinois" title="Robinson, Illinois">Robinson, Illinois</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Guadalajara,_Jalisco" class="mw-redirect" title="Guadalajara, Jalisco">Guadalajara</a>, Mexico.
</p><p>Visitors to Hershey can experience <a href="/wiki/Hershey%27s_Chocolate_World" title="Hershey's Chocolate World">Hershey's Chocolate World</a> visitors center and its simulated tour ride. Public tours were once operated in the Pennsylvania and California factories, which ended in Pennsylvania in 1973 as soon as Hershey's Chocolate World opened,<sup id="cite_ref-HCA_42-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HCA-42">[42]</a></sup> and later in California following the September 11, 2001, attacks, due to security concerns.<sup id="cite_ref-modbeeclosing_39-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-modbeeclosing-39">[39]</a></sup></p><p>On September 18, 2012, Hershey opened a new and expanded West Hershey plant. The plant was completed at a budget of $300 million.<sup id="cite_ref-LDN_43-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LDN-43">[43]</a></sup></p><p>On March 9, 2018, Hershey broke ground to expand its <a href="/wiki/Kit_Kat" title="Kit Kat">Kit Kat</a> manufacturing facility in <a href="/wiki/Hazle_Township,_Luzerne_County,_Pennsylvania" title="Hazle Township, Luzerne County, Pennsylvania">Hazle Township, Pennsylvania</a>. The expansion project has a $60 million budget and is expected to create an additional 111 jobs at the facility.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup></p>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(4)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_sales_and_acquisitions">Other sales and acquisitions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Hershey_Company&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Other sales and acquisitions" data-section="13" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-4 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-4">
<p>In 1969, Hershey received a license from <a href="/wiki/Rowntree%27s" title="Rowntree's">Rowntree's</a> to manufacture and market <a href="/wiki/Kit_Kat" title="Kit Kat">Kit Kat</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rolo" title="Rolo">Rolo</a> in the United States. As of September 2013, Hershey continued to make and market these brands in the U.S. under license from <a href="/wiki/Nestl%C3%A9" title="Nestlé">Nestlé</a>, owners of the Rowntree brand. That license would be revoked and revert to Nestlé if Hershey were sold.<sup id="cite_ref-cnbc.com_17-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cnbc.com-17">[17]</a></sup>
In 1977, Hershey acquired Y&amp;S Candies, founded in 1845, and became the makers of <a href="/wiki/Twizzlers" title="Twizzlers">Twizzlers</a> <a href="/wiki/Licorice" class="mw-redirect" title="Licorice">licorice</a> candies.
In 1986, Hershey's began a brief foray into <a href="/wiki/Throat_lozenge" title="Throat lozenge">cough drops</a> when it acquired the <a href="/wiki/Luden%27s" title="Luden's">Luden's</a> cough drops brand. By 2001, though, the brand had been sold to Pharmacia (now part of <a href="/wiki/Pfizer" title="Pfizer">Pfizer</a>),<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> and Luden's eventually became a product of <a href="/wiki/Prestige_Brands" title="Prestige Brands">Prestige Brands</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> Hershey's kept Luden's <a href="/wiki/5th_Avenue_(candy)" title="5th Avenue (candy)">5th Avenue</a> bar. In 1988, Hershey's acquired the rights to manufacture and distribute many <a href="/wiki/Cadbury-Schweppes" class="mw-redirect" title="Cadbury-Schweppes">Cadbury</a>-branded products in the United States (except gum and mints, which are part of Mondelēz International).  In 1996, Hershey purchased the American operations of the <a href="/wiki/Leaf_Candy_Company" class="mw-redirect" title="Leaf Candy Company">Leaf Candy Company</a> from <a href="/wiki/Huhtam%C3%A4ki" title="Huhtamäki">Huhtamäki</a>.
</p><p>In 1999, the Hershey Pasta Group was divested to several equity partners to form the <a href="/wiki/New_World_Pasta" title="New World Pasta">New World Pasta</a> company (now part of <a href="/wiki/Ebro_Foods" title="Ebro Foods">Ebro Foods</a>).
</p><p>On July 25, 2002, it became public knowledge that the Hershey Trust Company was seeking to sell its controlling interest in the Hershey Foods Corporation. The value of Hershey stock skyrocketed 25% with over 19 million shares trading that day. Over the following 55 days, widespread press coverage, as well as pressure from Pennsylvania Attorney General Mike Fisher, the community of Hershey, and Dauphin County Orphans' Court Senior Judge Warren G. Morgan, led to the sale being abandoned. The seven Hershey trustees who voted to sell Hershey Foods on September 17, 2002, for US$12.5 billion to the <a href="/wiki/William_Wrigley_Jr._Company" class="mw-redirect" title="William Wrigley Jr. Company">William Wrigley Jr. Company</a> (now part of <a href="/wiki/Mars_Incorporated" class="mw-redirect" title="Mars Incorporated">Mars Incorporated</a>) were removed by Attorney General Fisher and Judge Morgan.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> Ten of the 17 trustees were forced to resign and four new members who lived locally were appointed. The former Pennsylvania Attorney General, LeRoy S. Zimmerman, became the new chairman of the reconstituted Milton Hershey School Trustees. Mr. Zimmerman has publicly committed to having the Milton Hershey School Trust always retain its interest in The Hershey Company.
</p><p>In December 2004, Hershey acquired the <a href="/wiki/Mauna_Loa_Macadamia_Nut_Corp." class="mw-redirect" title="Mauna Loa Macadamia Nut Corp.">Mauna Loa Macadamia Nut Corp.</a> from <a href="/wiki/The_Shansby_Group" class="mw-redirect" title="The Shansby Group">The Shansby Group</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup></p><p>In July 2005, Hershey acquired the <a href="/wiki/Berkeley,_California" title="Berkeley, California">Berkeley, California</a>-based boutique chocolate-maker <a href="/wiki/Scharffen_Berger_Chocolate_Maker" title="Scharffen Berger Chocolate Maker">Scharffen Berger</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> In November 2005, Hershey acquired <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Schmidt_Confections" title="Joseph Schmidt Confections">Joseph Schmidt Confections</a>, the San Francisco-based chocolatier, and in November 2006, Hershey acquired <a href="/wiki/Dagoba_Organic_Chocolate" class="mw-redirect" title="Dagoba Organic Chocolate">Dagoba Organic Chocolate</a>, a boutique chocolate maker based in <a href="/wiki/Ashland,_Oregon" title="Ashland, Oregon">Ashland, Oregon</a>.
</p><p>In December 2011, Hershey reached an agreement to acquire Brookside Foods Ltd., a privately held confectionery company based in Abbotsford, British Columbia.<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup></p><p>In 2015, Hershey announced that they had acquired Krave Jerky, marking the company's first foray outside of the confectionery market in more than a decade.<sup id="cite_ref-nytimes_krave_51-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nytimes_krave-51">[51]</a></sup></p><p>In 2016, Hershey acquired barkTHINS, a New York-based chocolate snack foods company which expected to generate between $65 million and $75 million in revenue for that year, for $290 million.<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:0_29-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-29">[29]</a></sup></p><p>In 2017, Hershey acquired Amplify Snack Brands, Austin, Texas based maker of <a href="/wiki/SkinnyPop" title="SkinnyPop">SkinnyPop</a>, in an all-cash transaction valued at approximately $1.6 billion.<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup></p><p>In September 2018, Hershey announced to buy Pirate Brands from <a href="/wiki/B%26G_Foods" title="B&amp;G Foods">B&amp;G Foods</a> for $420 million in an all-cash deal.<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup></p><p>In August 2019, Hershey announced it would purchase protein bar maker One Brands LLC for $397 million.<sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup></p>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(5)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Product_recalls">Product recalls</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Hershey_Company&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Product recalls" data-section="14" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-5 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-5">
<ul><li>In July 1998, a number of 100 g (3.5 oz) milk chocolate bars being sold for fundraising events were recalled because they may have contained traces of almonds not listed in the ingredients.<sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup></li>
<li>In November 2006, the Smiths Falls production plant in Ontario temporarily shut down and several products were voluntarily recalled after concerns over <i><a href="/wiki/Salmonella" title="Salmonella">Salmonella</a></i> contamination possibly found in soy <a href="/wiki/Lecithin" title="Lecithin">lecithin</a> within their production line. It was believed that most of the products involved in the recall never made it to the retail level.<sup id="cite_ref-59" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup></li></ul></section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(6)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Philanthropy">Philanthropy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Hershey_Company&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Philanthropy" data-section="15" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-6 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-6">
<p>Hershey has made large contributions to education. One of their most notable contributions was the <a href="/wiki/Elizabethtown_College" title="Elizabethtown College">Elizabethtown College</a> Honors Program.<sup id="cite_ref-61" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup> The program was established in 1999 and is funded partially through the endowment.
</p><p>In 2015, Hershey announced a commitment with Clinton Global Initiative to help build a sustainable supply chain to support basic nutrition for children in Ghana.<sup id="cite_ref-62" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup></p><p>Hershey's long-term focus on children and families has yielded long-standing partnerships with organizations such as <a href="/wiki/Children%27s_Miracle_Network_Hospitals" title="Children's Miracle Network Hospitals">Children's Miracle Network</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ronald_McDonald_House_Charities" title="Ronald McDonald House Charities">Ronald McDonald House</a>, and <a href="/wiki/United_Way_Worldwide" title="United Way Worldwide">United Way</a> (UW). In 2016, the company donated more than $486,200 to those organizations.<sup id="cite_ref-63" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup></p>

<p>Hershey has been criticized for not having programs to ensure sustainable and ethical <a href="/wiki/Cocoa_bean" title="Cocoa bean">cocoa</a> purchase, lagging behind its competitors in <a href="/wiki/Fair_trade" title="Fair trade">fair trade</a> measures.<sup id="cite_ref-64" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup></p><p>The "Raise the Bar, Hershey!" campaign was launched in September 2010 by Global Exchange, <a href="/wiki/Green_America" title="Green America">Green America</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Oasis_Trust" class="mw-redirect" title="Oasis Trust">Oasis Trust</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/International_Labor_Rights_Forum" title="International Labor Rights Forum">International Labor Rights Forum</a>. The purpose of the Raise the Bar Campaign was to pressure Hershey to commit "to take immediate action to eliminate forced and child labor [...] from Hershey's cocoa supply"; "to sourcing 100% Fair Trade Certified cocoa beans by 2012 for at least one of its top five selling chocolate bars [...] making at least one additional top five selling bar 100% Fair Trade Certified every two years thereafter"; and that "the majority of Hershey's cocoa across all products will be Fair Trade Certified by 2022". Pressure was particularly directed at <a href="/wiki/Whole_Foods_Market" title="Whole Foods Market">Whole Foods Market</a>, which announced on October 3, 2012, that it would cease carrying Hershey's <a href="/wiki/Scharffen_Berger_Chocolate_Maker" title="Scharffen Berger Chocolate Maker">Scharffen Berger</a> line.<sup id="cite_ref-GA_press_release_65-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GA_press_release-65">[65]</a></sup> The Campaign stated, "Whole Foods' decision follows more than 40 natural food retailers and coops publicly expressing concern about carrying Scharffen Berger and <a href="/wiki/Dagoba_Chocolate" title="Dagoba Chocolate">Dagoba</a> products as a consequence of the giant chocolate maker's refusal to address child labor in its supply chain".<sup id="cite_ref-GA_press_release_65-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GA_press_release-65">[65]</a></sup> The same day, Hershey's announced it would "source 100 percent certified cocoa for its global chocolate product lines by 2020 and accelerate its programs to help eliminate child labor in the cocoa regions of West Africa".<sup id="cite_ref-66" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup></p><p>In 2019, Hershey announced that they could not guarantee that their chocolate products were free from child slave labor, as they could trace only about 50% of their purchasing back to the farm level. The <i><a href="/wiki/Washington_Post" class="mw-redirect" title="Washington Post">Washington Post</a></i> noted that the commitment taken in 2001 to eradicate such practices within 4 years had not been kept, neither at the due deadline of 2005, nor within the revised deadlines of 2008 and  2010, and that the result was not likely to be achieved for 2020 either.<sup id="cite_ref-67" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup></p>
