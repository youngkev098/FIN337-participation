			<div id="bodyContent" class="content"><div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><script>function mfTempOpenSection(id){var block=document.getElementById("mf-section-"+id);block.className+=" open-block";block.previousSibling.className+=" open-block";}</script><div class="mw-parser-output"><section class="mf-section-0" id="mf-section-0"><p><b>Zions Bancorporation</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Bank_holding_company" title="Bank holding company">bank holding company</a> headquartered in <a href="/wiki/Salt_Lake_City,_Utah" class="mw-redirect" title="Salt Lake City, Utah">Salt Lake City, Utah</a>. Zions Bancorporation originated as Keystone Insurance and Investment Co., a Utah Corporation, in April 1955. In April 1960, Keystone, together with several individual investors, acquired a 57.5 percent interest in Zions First National Bank from the LDS Church. In 1965, the name of the company was changed to Zions Bancorporation. (However, it operated as Zions Utah Bancorporation from 1966 to 1987.) The first public offering of shares in Zions Bancorporation was made in January 1966. There continued to be some minority shareholders until April 1972, when the company exchanged the remaining minority shares for common shares. In 2018, Zions Bancorporation merged into its bank subsidiary, ZB, N.A., which was then renamed Zions Bancorporation, N.A. Zions Bancorporation now operates as a national bank doing business under eight local brands, rather than as a holding company.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a </section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(1)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Zions_Bancorporation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History" data-section="1" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-1 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-1">
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Zions_footprint.png" class="image"><noscript><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fb/Zions_footprint.png/200px-Zions_footprint.png" decoding="async" width="200" height="169" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="1820" data-file-height="1541"></noscript><span class="lazy-image-placeholder" style="width: 200px;height: 169px;" data-src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fb/Zions_footprint.png/200px-Zions_footprint.png" data-alt="" data-width="200" data-height="169" data-class="thumbimage"> </span></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Zions_footprint.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Zions retail footprint</div></div></div> 
<p>The bank's history dates back to the founding of Zion's Savings Bank and Trust Company, which <a href="/wiki/Brigham_Young" title="Brigham Young">Brigham Young</a> opened in the fall of 1873 during the <a href="/wiki/Utah#Mormon_settlement" title="Utah">Mormon settlement of Utah</a>. It was Utah's first chartered savings bank and trust company.
</p><p>During the <a href="/wiki/Panic_of_1893" title="Panic of 1893">Panic of 1893</a>, the bank managed not only to remain solvent, but continue to grow.<sup id="cite_ref-snlhistory_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-snlhistory-4">[4]</a></sup></p><p>During the early 20th century, Zions financed such firms as:<sup id="cite_ref-snlhistory_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-snlhistory-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<ul><li>Bingham Copper Company (later <a href="/wiki/Kennecott_Utah_Copper" title="Kennecott Utah Copper">Kennecott Utah Copper</a>)</li>
<li>Salt Lake and Los Angeles Railroad Company (now <a href="/wiki/Union_Pacific_Railroad" title="Union Pacific Railroad">Union Pacific Railroad</a>)</li>
<li>Big Cottonwood Power Company (later Utah Power and today <a href="/wiki/Rocky_Mountain_Power" class="mw-redirect" title="Rocky Mountain Power">Rocky Mountain Power</a>)</li>
<li>Salt Lake Gas Company (later Mountain Fuel Supply Company and today <a href="/wiki/Questar_Corporation_(gas_company)" title="Questar Corporation (gas company)">Questar Gas</a>)</li></ul><p>The <a href="/wiki/Panic_of_1907" title="Panic of 1907">Panic of 1907</a> was the lone interruption in the steady growth of Zions.<sup id="cite_ref-snlhistory_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-snlhistory-4">[4]</a></sup> However, deposits grew from $2 million in 1901 to $9 million in 1918.<sup id="cite_ref-snlhistory_4-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-snlhistory-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Great_Depression">Great Depression</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Zions_Bancorporation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Great Depression" data-section="2" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>On the morning of February 15, 1932, 3 years after the <a href="/wiki/Wall_Street_Crash_of_1929" title="Wall Street Crash of 1929">Wall Street Crash of 1929</a>, customers began a run on the bank, waiting in lines that ran out of the building and onto the street. Tellers were instructed to honor all withdrawal requests. In 2.5 days, a total of $1.5 million was withdrawn. Near the end of the second day, bank (and The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter Day Saints) president <a href="/wiki/Heber_J._Grant" title="Heber J. Grant">Heber J. Grant</a> placed a sign in the bank's window that read, in part:
</p>
<blockquote>
<p>[The bank] is in a very strong, clean, liquid condition. It can pay off every depositor in full. Fear of its failure is not only without foundation, but positively foolish. There is not a safer bank in the State or the Nation.<sup id="cite_ref-snlhistory_4-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-snlhistory-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>Lines of customers that had been as long as a city block began to dwindle, and within five or six days many customers returned to deposit their money. By month's end, total deposits were more than withdrawals, and Zions had survived the <a href="/wiki/Great_Depression" title="Great Depression">Great Depression</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-creation_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-creation-5">[5]</a></sup></p><p>In 1957, Zion's merged with Utah Savings and Trust Company and First National Bank of Salt Lake City.<sup id="cite_ref-creation_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-creation-5">[5]</a></sup> The surviving institution was named Zions First National Bank.
</p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span id="1960.E2.80.932007"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="1960–2007">1960–2007</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Zions_Bancorporation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: 1960–2007" data-section="3" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>In 1960, Keystone Insurance and Investment Company, which had been founded in 1955, bought a majority stake in Zions from <a href="/wiki/The_Church_of_Jesus_Christ_of_Latter-day_Saints" title="The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints">The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints</a> and changed its name to Zions Utah Bancorporation. It kept that name until 1987, when it was shortened to Zions Bancorporation, its present name, to reflect the growing presence of the company outside of Utah.<sup id="cite_ref-snlhistory_4-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-snlhistory-4">[4]</a></sup></p><p>In January 1966, Zions became a <a href="/wiki/Public_company" title="Public company">public company</a> via an <a href="/wiki/Initial_public_offering" title="Initial public offering">initial public offering</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-creation_5-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-creation-5">[5]</a></sup></p><p>The bank expanded into <a href="/wiki/Nevada" title="Nevada">Nevada</a> in 1985 with the purchase of Nevada State Bank.<sup id="cite_ref-snlhistory_4-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-snlhistory-4">[4]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p><p>The bank entered <a href="/wiki/Arizona" title="Arizona">Arizona</a> in 1986 with the acquisition of Mesa Bank.<sup id="cite_ref-snlhistory_4-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-snlhistory-4">[4]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p><p>It expanded into <a href="/wiki/Colorado" title="Colorado">Colorado</a> and <a href="/wiki/New_Mexico" title="New Mexico">New Mexico</a> in 1996 by acquiring Aspen Bancshares, which operated Pitkin County Bank and Trust, Valley National Bank of Cortez, and Centennial Savings Bank.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p><p>Zions made many acquisitions in 1997 and 1998.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p><p>In 1997, Zions acquired Vectra Banking Corporation and Tri-State Finance Corporation of Colorado.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Zions also acquired Tri-State Bank of Idaho.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> It also bought 27 branches in Arizona, Idaho, Nevada, and Utah from <a href="/wiki/Wells_Fargo" title="Wells Fargo">Wells Fargo</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p><p>Zions expanded into <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a> in 1997 and <a href="/wiki/Washington_(U.S._state)" class="mw-redirect" title="Washington (U.S. state)">Washington</a> in 1998, <a href="/wiki/Oregon" title="Oregon">Oregon</a> in 2005.   In October 1998, Zions merged its three California holdings--<a href="/wiki/The_Sumitomo_Bank" title="The Sumitomo Bank">Sumitomo Bank of California</a>, San Diego-based Grossmont Bank and First Pacific National Bank--to create <a href="/wiki/California_Bank_and_Trust" title="California Bank and Trust">California Bank and Trust</a>.
</p><p>In 1999, the company bid for <a href="/wiki/First_Security_Corporation" title="First Security Corporation">First Security Corporation</a> but lost to <a href="/wiki/Wells_Fargo" title="Wells Fargo">Wells Fargo</a> after the <a href="/wiki/U.S._Securities_and_Exchange_Commission" title="U.S. Securities and Exchange Commission">U.S. Securities and Exchange Commission</a> forced Zions to restate its results in prior years due to the way it accounted for acquisitions.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Despite the unsuccessful pursuit of First Security, Zions became the largest bank headquartered in Utah.
</p><p>In 2001, the company was added to the <a href="/wiki/S%26P_500_Index" title="S&amp;P 500 Index">S&amp;P 500 Index</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p><p>In 2005, the company expanded into <a href="/wiki/Texas" title="Texas">Texas</a> with the purchase of <a href="/wiki/Amegy_Bank" class="mw-redirect" title="Amegy Bank">Amegy Bank</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span id="2008.E2.80.93present"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="2008–present">2008–present</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Zions_Bancorporation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: 2008–present" data-section="4" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>On October 27, 2008, Zions received a $1.4 billion investment from the U.S. government as a result of the <a href="/wiki/Troubled_Asset_Relief_Program" title="Troubled Asset Relief Program">Troubled Asset Relief Program</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Zions-Bancorporation-Nov-2008-10-Q_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Zions-Bancorporation-Nov-2008-10-Q-17">[17]</a></sup> It repaid the final $700 million on September 26, 2012,<sup id="cite_ref-Zions-Bancorporation-Sep-2012-8-K_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Zions-Bancorporation-Sep-2012-8-K-18">[18]</a></sup> and the government realized a profit of $253 million from its investment in the company.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p><p>On August 31, 2010, Zions announced the sale of its NetDeposit subsidiary to <a href="/wiki/BankServ" title="BankServ">BankServ</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Zions-Bancorporation-Oct-2010-8-K_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Zions-Bancorporation-Oct-2010-8-K-21">[21]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p><p>On June 1, 2015, Zions Bancorporation announced a corporate restructuring, which included the consolidation of seven bank charters into a single charter and $120 million in expense reduction initiatives.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Zions-Bancorporation-Jun-2015-8-K_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Zions-Bancorporation-Jun-2015-8-K-24">[24]</a></sup></p><p>In November 2017, Zions announced plans to merge the holding company and its banking subsidiary ZB, N.A. into one Zions Bancorporation, N.A.<sup id="cite_ref-Zions-Bancorporation-Nov-2017-8-K_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Zions-Bancorporation-Nov-2017-8-K-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
