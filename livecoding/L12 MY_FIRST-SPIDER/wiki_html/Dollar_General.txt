</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(1)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dollar_General&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History" data-section="1" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-1 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-1">
<h3 class="in-block"><span id="J.L._Turner_and_Son:_1939.E2.80.931964"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="J.L._Turner_and_Son:_1939–1964">J.L. Turner and Son: 1939–1964</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dollar_General&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: J.L. Turner and Son: 1939–1964" data-section="2" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:282px;"><a href="/wiki/File:J.L._Turner_and_Son_Building.jpg" class="image"><noscript><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c8/J.L._Turner_and_Son_Building.jpg/280px-J.L._Turner_and_Son_Building.jpg" decoding="async" width="280" height="186" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="6016" data-file-height="4000"></noscript><span class="lazy-image-placeholder" style="width: 280px;height: 186px;" data-src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c8/J.L._Turner_and_Son_Building.jpg/280px-J.L._Turner_and_Son_Building.jpg" data-alt="" data-width="280" data-height="186" data-class="thumbimage"> </span></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:J.L._Turner_and_Son_Building.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>J.L. Turner and Son Building in <a href="/wiki/Scottsville,_Kentucky" title="Scottsville, Kentucky">Scottsville, Kentucky</a>.</div></div></div>
<p>Dollar General has its origin in <a href="/wiki/Scottsville,_Kentucky" title="Scottsville, Kentucky">Scottsville, Kentucky</a>, with <a href="/wiki/James_Luther_Turner" title="James Luther Turner">James Luther "J.L." Turner</a> and his son <a href="/wiki/Cal_Turner" title="Cal Turner">Cal Turner</a>. James Turner's father died in an accident in 1902 when James was only 11. James had to quit school and never completed his education so he could work the family farm and help provide for his mother and siblings. After two unsuccessful attempts at retailing, James became a traveling dry goods salesman for a Nashville wholesale grocer. James left the sales job after 10 years and settled his family in <a href="/wiki/Scottsville,_Kentucky" title="Scottsville, Kentucky">Scottsville, Kentucky</a>. During the <a href="/wiki/Great_Depression" title="Great Depression">Great Depression</a>, he began buying and liquidating bankrupt general stores. James' only child <a href="/wiki/Cal_Turner" title="Cal Turner">Cal Turner</a> accompanied his father to these closeouts at a young age, gaining valuable business knowledge and skills.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p><p>In October 1939, James and Cal opened <b>J.L. Turner and Son</b> with an initial investment of $5,000 each. The switch to retailing resulted in annual sales above $2 million by the early 1950s. By the mid-1950s Turner had 35 department stores in Kentucky and Tennessee. In 1955 Cal Turner developed his idea of a retail store selling goods for a dollar, based on the Dollar Days promotions held at other department stores, by converting Turner's Department Store in <a href="/wiki/Springfield,_Kentucky" title="Springfield, Kentucky">Springfield, Kentucky</a> into the first Dollar General Store.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> In 1964 J.L. Turner died leaving his son Cal Turner to succeed him.
</p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span id="1968.E2.80.932002"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="1968–2002">1968–2002</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dollar_General&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: 1968–2002" data-section="3" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>The company Cal Turner co-founded went public as <b>Dollar General Corporation</b> in 1968, posting annual sales of more than $40 million and net income in excess of $1.5 million. In 1977, Cal Turner, Jr., who joined the company in 1965 as the third generation Turner, succeeded his father as president of Dollar General. Cal Jr.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> led the company until his retirement in 2002. Under his leadership, the company grew to more than 6,000 stores and $6 billion in sales. In 1997 a <a href="/wiki/Distribution_center" title="Distribution center">distribution center</a> was established in <a href="/wiki/South_Boston,_Virginia" title="South Boston, Virginia">South Boston, Virginia</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p><p>In 2000 Dollar General opened a new corporate headquarters in <a href="/wiki/Goodlettsville,_Tennessee" title="Goodlettsville, Tennessee">Goodlettsville, Tennessee</a>. By the end of 2000 sales at Dollar General exceeded $4 billion.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> The distribution center in <a href="/wiki/Homerville,_Georgia" title="Homerville, Georgia">Homerville, Georgia</a>, was closed in April 2000 and operations were moved to a new distribution center in <a href="/wiki/Alachua,_Florida" title="Alachua, Florida">Alachua, Florida</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p><p>Cal Jr. retired in 2002 and was succeeded by <a href="/wiki/David_Perdue" title="David Perdue">David Perdue</a> on April 2, 2003.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span id="2003.E2.80.93present"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="2003–present">2003–present</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dollar_General&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: 2003–present" data-section="4" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>Dollar General entered the grocery market with the establishment of Dollar General Market in 2003.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> In 2004 Dollar General expanded to low-cost Asian markets by opening a sourcing office in <a href="/wiki/Hong_Kong" title="Hong Kong">Hong Kong</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p><p>On June 21, 2007, CEO David Perdue announced his resignation leaving David Bere as interim CEO.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> One month later all shares of Dollar General <a href="/wiki/Stock" title="Stock">stock</a> were acquired by private equity investors for $22 per share. An investment group consisting of affiliates of <a href="/wiki/Kohlberg_Kravis_Roberts" title="Kohlberg Kravis Roberts">Kohlberg Kravis Roberts</a> (KKR), <a href="/wiki/GS_Capital_Partners" class="mw-redirect" title="GS Capital Partners">GS Capital Partners</a> (an affiliate of <a href="/wiki/Goldman_Sachs" title="Goldman Sachs">Goldman Sachs</a>), <a href="/wiki/Citigroup" title="Citigroup">Citigroup</a> Private Equity and other co-investors completed an acquisition of Dollar General Corporation for $6.9 billion.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p><p>As a part of the transition to a privately held company, Dollar General assessed each location at the end of its lease against a model known as "EZ Stores". This assessment included evaluating whether the location had a loading dock, garbage dumpsters, adequate parking, and acceptable profitability. Stores that did not pass this evaluation were relocated or closed. Over 400 stores were closed as part of this initiative.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Dollar_General,_Davidson_Rd,_GA125,_Lowndes_County.JPG" class="image"><noscript><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/81/Dollar_General%2C_Davidson_Rd%2C_GA125%2C_Lowndes_County.JPG/250px-Dollar_General%2C_Davidson_Rd%2C_GA125%2C_Lowndes_County.JPG" decoding="async" width="250" height="188" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="4608" data-file-height="3456"></noscript><span class="lazy-image-placeholder" style="width: 250px;height: 188px;" data-src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/81/Dollar_General%2C_Davidson_Rd%2C_GA125%2C_Lowndes_County.JPG/250px-Dollar_General%2C_Davidson_Rd%2C_GA125%2C_Lowndes_County.JPG" data-alt="" data-width="250" data-height="188" data-class="thumbimage"> </span></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Dollar_General,_Davidson_Rd,_GA125,_Lowndes_County.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Construction of a new Dollar General store in <a href="/wiki/Lowndes_County,_Georgia" title="Lowndes County, Georgia">Lowndes County, Georgia</a>, in 2015.</div></div></div>
<p>Dollar General filed on August 20, 2009, for an <a href="/wiki/Initial_public_offering" title="Initial public offering">initial public offering</a> of up to $750 Million turning the company once again into a publicly traded corporation.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> In 2013 Dollar General started selling cigarettes in response to its competitor Family Dollar selling cigarettes in 2012.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> Dollar General's 12th distribution center opened on May 31, 2014, in <a href="/wiki/Bethel,_Berks_County,_Pennsylvania" title="Bethel, Berks County, Pennsylvania">Bethel, Pennsylvania</a>, to serve the northeast and midwest stores.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> On August 18, 2014, Dollar General lodged a competing bid of $9.7 billion against <a href="/wiki/Dollar_Tree" title="Dollar Tree">Dollar Tree</a> for <a href="/wiki/Family_Dollar" title="Family Dollar">Family Dollar</a>. The bid was rejected on August 20, 2014, by the Family Dollar board, which said it would proceed with the deal with Dollar Tree.<sup id="cite_ref-BidRebuffed_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BidRebuffed-25">[25]</a></sup></p><p>On June 3, 2015, Chief Operating Officer Todd Vasos replaced Rick Dreiling as chief executive. Dreiling remained as senior advisor and chairman until his retirement in January 2016.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> Dollar General's 13th distribution center opened in <a href="/wiki/San_Antonio,_Texas" class="mw-redirect" title="San Antonio, Texas">San Antonio, Texas</a>, on June 6, 2016, with a local investment of $100 million and the creation of over 500 jobs.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> In September 2015, the Janesville City Council, in Wisconsin, approved an agreement to bring a Dollar General distribution center to the town. The center created more than 500 jobs in the area and became the 14th Dollar General distribution center.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a href="/wiki/File:DollarGeneralDC2018.jpg" class="image"><noscript><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c0/DollarGeneralDC2018.jpg/250px-DollarGeneralDC2018.jpg" decoding="async" width="250" height="156" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="3748" data-file-height="2342"></noscript><span class="lazy-image-placeholder" style="width: 250px;height: 156px;" data-src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c0/DollarGeneralDC2018.jpg/250px-DollarGeneralDC2018.jpg" data-alt="" data-width="250" data-height="156" data-class="thumbimage"> </span></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:DollarGeneralDC2018.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Dollar General distribution center in 2018.</div></div></div>
<p>On September 15, 2016, Dollar General announced plans to hire 10,000 new employees and open 900 new stores in fiscal 2016 and 1,000 in fiscal 2017. Dollar General had operated 13,000 stores as of August 2016.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> Dollar Express and all of its 323 locations were acquired by Dollar General in April 2017.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> In January 2017 Dollar General opened a concept store in Nashville called DGX. The DGX store concept focuses on urban shoppers and is geared toward instant consumption items such as a coffee station and a soda fountain. The following month another DGX store opened in <a href="/wiki/Raleigh,_North_Carolina" title="Raleigh, North Carolina">Raleigh, North Carolina</a>, and in September a third DGX opened in <a href="/wiki/Philadelphia" title="Philadelphia">Philadelphia</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Jackson,_Georgia" title="Jackson, Georgia">Jackson, Georgia</a>, Dollar General opened its 15th distribution center in fall 2017 to serve stores in Georgia and the surrounding states.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> In 2017, Dollar General began construction for its 16th distribution center in <a href="/wiki/Amsterdam,_New_York" class="mw-redirect" title="Amsterdam, New York">Amsterdam, New York</a>. The distribution center was to cost $91 million and was expected to create 400 low-skilled jobs in <a href="/wiki/Montgomery_County,_New_York" title="Montgomery County, New York">Montgomery County, New York</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> Dollar General planned to open 900 new stores in 2018.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> Also in 2017, Dollar General acquired Dollar Express, a spinoff from the Family Dollar-Dollar Tree deal, and converted the store.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></p><p>A second distribution center will be established in <a href="/wiki/Texas" title="Texas">Texas</a> with a planned opening in 2019. The distribution center will be located in <a href="/wiki/Longview,_Texas" title="Longview, Texas">Longview, Texas</a>, and will bring 400 low-skilled jobs to the area.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> Dollar General opened its 15,000th store in <a href="/wiki/Wilmington,_North_Carolina" title="Wilmington, North Carolina">Wilmington, North Carolina</a> in July 2018. Dollar General hosted a community celebration and donated $15,000 to five local schools to support literacy and education initiatives.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(2)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Major_sponsorships">Major sponsorships</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dollar_General&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Major sponsorships" data-section="5" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-2 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-2">
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Auto_racing">Auto racing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dollar_General&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Auto racing" data-section="6" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:292px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Road_America_2013_Nationwide_20_Brian_Vickers.jpg" class="image"><noscript><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/64/Road_America_2013_Nationwide_20_Brian_Vickers.jpg/290px-Road_America_2013_Nationwide_20_Brian_Vickers.jpg" decoding="async" width="290" height="143" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="1848" data-file-height="910"></noscript><span class="lazy-image-placeholder" style="width: 290px;height: 143px;" data-src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/64/Road_America_2013_Nationwide_20_Brian_Vickers.jpg/290px-Road_America_2013_Nationwide_20_Brian_Vickers.jpg" data-alt="" data-width="290" data-height="143" data-class="thumbimage"> </span></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Road_America_2013_Nationwide_20_Brian_Vickers.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Brian_Vickers" title="Brian Vickers">Brian Vickers</a>' 2013 Nationwide Series car at <a href="/wiki/Road_America" title="Road America">Road America</a></div></div></div>
<p>For several years, Dollar General has had a connection with motorsports, particularly in <a href="/wiki/NASCAR" title="NASCAR">NASCAR</a>. The company has previously been a primary sponsor for <a href="/wiki/Joe_Gibbs_Racing" title="Joe Gibbs Racing">Joe Gibbs Racing</a>. Dollar General sponsored <a href="/wiki/Brian_Vickers" title="Brian Vickers">Brian Vickers</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Nationwide_Series" class="mw-redirect" title="Nationwide Series">Nationwide Series</a> in 2013. Dollar General became a primary sponsor for <a href="/wiki/Matt_Kenseth" title="Matt Kenseth">Matt Kenseth</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Sprint_Cup_Series" class="mw-redirect" title="Sprint Cup Series">Sprint Cup Series</a> starting in 2013.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> Dollar General and Turner (formerly Braun Racing) have been partnered together since 2008, with the team previously sponsoring cars for <a href="/wiki/Frank_Cicci_Racing" title="Frank Cicci Racing">Frank Cicci Racing</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kevin_Harvick_Incorporated" title="Kevin Harvick Incorporated">Kevin Harvick Incorporated</a>. In 2010, Dollar General sponsored some races in the <a href="/wiki/Camping_World_Truck_Series" class="mw-redirect" title="Camping World Truck Series">Camping World Truck Series</a> for <a href="/wiki/Kyle_Busch_Motorsports" title="Kyle Busch Motorsports">Kyle Busch Motorsports</a> with <a href="/wiki/Kyle_Busch" title="Kyle Busch">Kyle Busch</a> in the No. 18 <a href="/wiki/Toyota_Tundra" title="Toyota Tundra">Toyota Tundra</a> and sponsored Kyle Busch's Motorsports No. 51 Toyota Tundra for four races in 2014 with Busch driving three and <a href="/wiki/Erik_Jones" title="Erik Jones">Erik Jones</a> driving one.<sup id="cite_ref-kbm31814_39-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kbm31814-39">[39]</a></sup> Dollar General was the title sponsor for Nationwide Series races held in <a href="/wiki/Dollar_General_300" class="mw-redirect" title="Dollar General 300">Charlotte</a> every fall, <a href="/wiki/Dollar_General_300_(Chicagoland)" class="mw-redirect" title="Dollar General 300 (Chicagoland)">Chicagoland</a> every summer, and <a href="/wiki/Dollar_General_200" class="mw-redirect" title="Dollar General 200">Phoenix</a> in the spring. On May 23, 2016, Dollar General announced they would withdraw its sponsorship from NASCAR at the end of the 2016 season.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup></p><p>Dollar General is also active in the <a href="/wiki/IndyCar_Series" title="IndyCar Series">IndyCar Series</a> since 2008, serving as the primary sponsor for owner/driver <a href="/wiki/Sarah_Fisher" title="Sarah Fisher">Sarah Fisher</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Sarah_Fisher_Racing" class="mw-redirect" title="Sarah Fisher Racing">Sarah Fisher Racing</a> team.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> In 2010, both Fisher and <a href="/wiki/Graham_Rahal" title="Graham Rahal">Graham Rahal</a> drove part-time for the team finishing 9th at the <a href="/wiki/Honda_Grand_Prix_of_St._Petersburg" class="mw-redirect" title="Honda Grand Prix of St. Petersburg">Honda Grand Prix of St. Petersburg</a>. Fisher also led the field at the <a href="/wiki/Peak_Antifreeze_%26_Motor_Oil_Indy_300" class="mw-redirect" title="Peak Antifreeze &amp; Motor Oil Indy 300">Peak Antifreeze &amp; Motor Oil Indy 300</a> at Chicagoland Speedway. In 2011, Dollar General continued to sponsor Sarah Fisher Racing, the team was still part-time but <a href="/wiki/Ed_Carpenter_(racing_driver)" title="Ed Carpenter (racing driver)">Ed Carpenter</a> drove for nines races starting at the <a href="/wiki/2011_Indianapolis_500" title="2011 Indianapolis 500">2011 Indianapolis 500</a>. Since 2012 Dollar General is no longer a sponsor for Sarah Fisher's Sarah Fisher Racing.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Sports">Sports</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dollar_General&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Sports" data-section="7" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>Dollar General became the sponsor of the <a href="/wiki/Dollar_General_Bowl" class="mw-redirect" title="Dollar General Bowl">Dollar General Bowl</a>, formerly the <a href="/wiki/GoDaddy" title="GoDaddy">GoDaddy</a> Bowl, in <a href="/wiki/Mobile,_Alabama" title="Mobile, Alabama">Mobile, Alabama</a> on August 17, 2016.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> In May 2019, Dollar  General withdrew its title sponsorship of the Mobile bowl game.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup></p>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(3)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Store_brands">Store brands</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dollar_General&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Store brands" data-section="8" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-3 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-3">
<p>Dollar General created its abbreviation, the letters "DG", as a <a href="/wiki/Store_brand" class="mw-redirect" title="Store brand">store brand</a> for "inexpensive" household products sold through the Dollar General stores. DG is also the company's <a href="/wiki/NYSE" class="mw-redirect" title="NYSE">NYSE</a> <a href="/wiki/Ticker_symbol" title="Ticker symbol">ticker symbol</a>.
</p><p>In June, 2010, Dollar General outbid <a href="/wiki/Dolce_and_Gabbana" class="mw-redirect" title="Dolce and Gabbana">Dolce and Gabbana</a> for the "DG.com" domain, purchased in a private sale from <a href="/wiki/EMC_Corporation" class="mw-redirect" title="EMC Corporation">EMC Corporation</a>, successor to the defunct <a href="/wiki/Data_General" title="Data General">Data General</a> computer manufacturer. Dollar General became the 107th largest company in the world to own its initials as a 2-letter Internet address. The DG symbol is used by the company for a variety of in-house products called DG Home,<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> DG Baby,<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> DG Health, and DG Body.
</p><p>In 2017, Dollar General secured an exclusive contract to sell <a href="/wiki/Jolt_Cola" title="Jolt Cola">Jolt Cola</a> in its stores.<sup id="cite_ref-Geek.com_47-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Geek.com-47">[47]</a></sup></p><p>Dollar General uses the Clover Valley store brand for grocery products and the Smart &amp; Simple brand for low-end discount products. Other Dollar General brands specific to one department include Good &amp; Smart (snack foods, formerly Heartland Harvest), Sweet Smiles (bulk candy), Nature's Menu, Forever Pals and Heartland Farms (pet food and products, formerly EverPet), Gentle Steps (diapers), Studio Selection (beauty, hair and skin care), TrueLiving (housewares), Comfort Bay (towels, blankets and pillows), Open Trails (men's apparel), Zone Pro (sportswear) and <a href="/w/index.php?title=Bobbie_Brooks&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Bobbie Brooks (page does not exist)">Bobbie Brooks</a> (women's apparel).<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup></p>

</ul><h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Rexall">Rexall</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dollar_General&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Rexall" data-section="9" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>The brand name Rexall was first established in 1903 by <a href="/wiki/Louis_K._Liggett" title="Louis K. Liggett">Louis K. Liggett</a> and gradually became a powerhouse as a <a href="/wiki/Pharmaceutical_drug" class="mw-redirect" title="Pharmaceutical drug">pharmaceutical drug</a> store chain.<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> In March 2010 Dollar General became the exclusive retailer for <a href="/wiki/Rexall" title="Rexall">Rexall</a> products. Rexall vitamins and supplements began appearing at Dollar General stores in March and by Fall 2010 a full line of Rexall products was available at Dollar General.<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup></p>

<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Board_of_directors">Board of directors</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dollar_General&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Board of directors" data-section="11" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>Dollar General Board of Directors as of July 2017 are: Michael M. Calbert (Chairman of the Board), Todd Vasos (CEO), Warren Bryant, Sandra Cohen, Patricia Fili-Krushel, Paula Price, William Rhodes III, and David Rickard.<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup></p>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(5)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Operations">Operations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dollar_General&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Operations" data-section="12" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-5 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-5">
<p>Dollar General has over 15,000 stores in 44 states,<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup> and approximately 129,000 employees.<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup> Dollar General also has 15 <a href="/wiki/Distribution_center" title="Distribution center">distribution centers</a> in 14 states with 2 additional centers either under construction or planned.<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup> Since 2017, DG has opened stores in North Dakota. As of early 2020, DG does not have stores in six states: Alaska, Hawaii, Idaho, Montana, Washington, and Wyoming.

<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Dolgencorp">Dolgencorp</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dollar_General&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Dolgencorp" data-section="14" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>Dolgencorp is a wholly owned subsidiary of Dollar General Corporation. Dollar General brand products are manufactured under the Dolgencorp subsidiary.<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Dollar_General_Global_Sourcing">Dollar General Global Sourcing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dollar_General&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Dollar General Global Sourcing" data-section="15" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>In 2004 a Dollar General office was opened in <a href="/wiki/Hong_Kong" title="Hong Kong">Hong Kong</a> to oversee the global sourcing operations through exporting and importing products of Dollar General related goods.<sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Dollar_General_Literacy_Foundation">Dollar General Literacy Foundation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dollar_General&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Dollar General Literacy Foundation" data-section="16" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>Since 1993, Dollar General has provided funding of literacy and education programs through its subsidiary Dollar General Literacy Foundation. Every year the Dollar General Literacy Foundation awards funds to nonprofit organizations, schools and libraries within a 20-mile radius of a Dollar General store or distribution center. It has awarded over $135 million in grants to nonprofit organizations as of July 2017.<sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup> In 2018 the Dollar General Literacy Foundation had awarded $8.3 million to over 1,000 nonprofit organizations, schools and libraries. The Dollar General Literacy Foundation also celebrated its 25th anniversary in 2018.<sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup></p>

<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Perpetuating_economic_distress">Perpetuating economic distress</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dollar_General&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Perpetuating economic distress" data-section="18" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>Dollar General, along with other <a href="/wiki/Dollar_store" class="mw-redirect" title="Dollar store">dollar store</a> chains, while "sometimes [filling] a need in cash-strapped communities" where <a href="/wiki/Supermarket" title="Supermarket">supermarkets</a> have closed, are regarded not "merely a byproduct of economic distress. They’re a cause of it.” Dollar store chains, in "capitalizing on a series of powerful economic and social forces—white flight, the recent recession, the so-called “<a href="/wiki/Retail_apocalypse" title="Retail apocalypse">retail apocalypse</a>”—all of which have opened up gaping holes in food access...might not be causing these inequalities per se, they appear to be perpetuating them". The rapid growth in dollar stores across the USA have created a “dollar store belt”. Studies found that dollar stores lacked fresh produce and nutritious food, and were less affordable per unit than big box retailers <a href="/wiki/Walmart" title="Walmart">Walmart</a> or <a href="/wiki/Costco" title="Costco">Costco</a>. Originally opened with local tax incentives, a growing number of municipalities have been adding zoning bylaws to discourage dollar stores.<sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup> Dollar stores tend to create fewer jobs and lower wage jobs than independent grocery stores.<sup id="cite_ref-61" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup> Additionally, Dollar Generals stifle local competition, hurting the communities in which they're serving.<sup id="cite_ref-62" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Financial_irregularities">Financial irregularities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dollar_General&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Financial irregularities" data-section="19" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>On April 30, 2001, Dollar General Corp was liable for making false statements or failing to disclose adverse facts about the company's financial results,<sup id="cite_ref-63" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup> and paid $162 million for settlement. The company also announced to restate its earnings for the past three fiscal years, due to accounting irregularities including allegations of fraudulent behavior.<sup id="cite_ref-64" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup></p><p>On March 3, 2005, Dollar General announced to restate its results for 2000 through 2003, due to a clarification of lease-accounting matters issued by the SEC.<sup id="cite_ref-65" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="OSHA_fines">OSHA fines</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dollar_General&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: OSHA fines" data-section="20" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>In November 2014, Dollar General was fined $51,700 by the <a href="/wiki/Occupational_Safety_and_Health_Administration" title="Occupational Safety and Health Administration">Occupational Safety and Health Administration</a> (OSHA) following an inspection of a Brooklyn, Mississippi, branch of the store. The statement from OSHA notes that Dollar General has had repeated health and safety violations: "Since 2009, OSHA has conducted 72 inspections of Dollar General nationwide. Of those inspections, 39 have resulted in citations."<sup id="cite_ref-66" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup> In April 2016, OSHA reported that further citations had been given to the store for exposing employees to the risk of electrical hazards due to missing face plates on electrical outlets. The store was fined $107,620.<sup id="cite_ref-67" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup> In December 2016, OSHA has noted that some Dollar General stores continued to block fire exits with merchandise disregarding safety violations resulting in several fines.<sup id="cite_ref-68" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup></p>
