</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(1)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Newell_Brands&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History" data-section="1" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-1 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-1">
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Rubbermaid" title="Rubbermaid">Rubbermaid</a></div>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Newell_Company">Newell Company</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Newell_Brands&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Newell Company" data-section="2" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:203px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Newell_logo_from_the_1900s.jpg" class="image"><noscript><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/2/2a/Newell_logo_from_the_1900s.jpg" decoding="async" width="201" height="89" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="201" data-file-height="89"></noscript><span class="lazy-image-placeholder" style="width: 201px;height: 89px;" data-src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/2/2a/Newell_logo_from_the_1900s.jpg" data-alt="" data-width="201" data-height="89" data-class="thumbimage"> </span></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Newell_logo_from_the_1900s.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>First Newell logo</div></div></div>
<p>The Newell Manufacturing Company was founded by Edgar Newell in <a href="/wiki/Ogdensburg,_New_York" title="Ogdensburg, New York">Ogdensburg, New York</a>, in 1903 as a manufacturer of metal <a href="/wiki/Curtain_rod" title="Curtain rod">curtain rods</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2020)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p><p>In 1908, the company boosted profits by investing in machines that produced curtain rods better and faster than any other manufacturer.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p><p>The <a href="/wiki/F._W._Woolworth_Company" title="F. W. Woolworth Company">F. W. Woolworth</a> <a href="/wiki/Retail_chain" class="mw-redirect" title="Retail chain">retail chain</a> became the first customer for Newell's <a href="/wiki/Bronze" title="Bronze">bronze</a>–<a href="/wiki/Plating" title="Plating">plated</a> curtain rods in 1916, making them the first Newell products to be distributed nationally. This marked the beginning of Newell's mass merchandising strategy. As the business grew, the company built a new 15,000-square-foot facility in Ogdensburg, New York.
</p><p>The company purchased Barnwell Mfg. Co. of Freeport, Illinois in 1921, renaming it Western Newell Manufacturing Company. Freeport's access to railroad lines made it easier to ship products west. Kresge, the department store chain that later became Kmart, was one of Western Newell's larger accounts.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2020)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p><p>During World War II, Western Newell converted factories to assist with the war effort, earning the Army/Navy "E" award for excellence in wartime production.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Daniel_C._Ferguson" title="Daniel C. Ferguson">Daniel C. Ferguson</a> was named president in 1965, and developed a growth-by-acquisition strategy based on his intention to build a strong, multi-product company.
</p><p>The Newell Company <a href="/wiki/Initial_public_offering" title="Initial public offering">went public</a> in 1972, opening on the NASDAQ at $28 per share. In 1974, they acquired EZ Paintr Corporation, then the world's largest maker of <a href="/wiki/Paint" title="Paint">paint</a> <a href="/wiki/Paint_brush" class="mw-redirect" title="Paint brush">applicators</a>. Newell was listed on the <a href="/wiki/New_York_Stock_Exchange" title="New York Stock Exchange">New York Stock Exchange</a> with the ticker symbol NWL in 1979.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2020)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p><p>In 1979, Newell moved into its first corporate headquarters in a historic former bank building in Freeport, Illinois.
</p><p>In 1983, the company entered the cookware market with the purchase of <a href="/wiki/Mirro" class="mw-redirect" title="Mirro">Mirro</a>. In 1987, the company acquired <a href="/wiki/Anchor_Hocking" title="Anchor Hocking">Anchor Hocking</a> Corporation, a specialty manufacturer of glassware, flatware, cookware and other products. The deal included the purchase of <a href="/wiki/Amerock" title="Amerock">Amerock</a>, a maker of cabinet hardware and window components.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2020)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p><p>In 1992, they acquired <a href="/wiki/Sanford_L.P." title="Sanford L.P.">Sanford</a>, a manufacturer and marketer of writing instruments, including the Sharpie and Expo brands. A year later, they acquired Levolor, a manufacturer and marketer of window treatments. Additionally, they entered the beauty and style category by acquiring Goody hair care accessories, which included Ace men's grooming accessories.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2020)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p><p>In 1997, the company acquired Kirsch, a company involved with <a href="/wiki/Drapery" title="Drapery">drapery</a> hardware and custom <a href="/wiki/Window_covering" title="Window covering">window coverings</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2020)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p><p>In 1998, the company expanded cookware family with the purchase of Calphalon Corporation, a manufacturer of cookware marketed primarily to <a href="/wiki/Luxury_good" class="mw-redirect" title="Luxury good">upscale</a> retailers and department stores. The company also purchased Panex, a cookware maker operating in South America.
</p><p>In 2016, Newell moved its corporate headquarters to Hoboken, New Jersey.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(2)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Criticism">Criticism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Newell_Brands&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Criticism" data-section="3" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-2 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-2">
<p>Newell Rubbermaid has been criticized in the UK for closing British factories, including those of Parker Pen (relocating them to Nantes, France, and China)<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> as well as toolmakers Berol, Record and Marples.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p><p>Newell Sistema  products has been criticized for requiring workers in their Auckland, New Zealand factory to work in unsafe conditions during the COVID-19 outbreak and lock down without adequate distances between workers and proper personal protective equipment. After a WorkSafe NZ visit workers were told they would not have to go to work and would be on full pay for the 4 week lock down.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(3)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Mergers">Mergers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Newell_Brands&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Mergers" data-section="4" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-3 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-3">
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Rubbermaid">Rubbermaid</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Newell_Brands&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Rubbermaid" data-section="5" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Newell_Rubbermaid_logo.svg" class="image"><noscript><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d8/Newell_Rubbermaid_logo.svg/200px-Newell_Rubbermaid_logo.svg.png" decoding="async" width="200" height="27" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="1024" data-file-height="136"></noscript><span class="lazy-image-placeholder" style="width: 200px;height: 27px;" data-src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d8/Newell_Rubbermaid_logo.svg/200px-Newell_Rubbermaid_logo.svg.png" data-alt="" data-width="200" data-height="27" data-srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d8/Newell_Rubbermaid_logo.svg/300px-Newell_Rubbermaid_logo.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d8/Newell_Rubbermaid_logo.svg/400px-Newell_Rubbermaid_logo.svg.png 2x" data-class="thumbimage"> </span></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Newell_Rubbermaid_logo.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Newell Rubbermaid logo used from 1999 to 2016</div></div></div>
<p>In 1999, Newell Company acquired the Rubbermaid and Graco brand names in a megamerger deal worth $5.8 billion, and later renamed the combined firm Newell Rubbermaid. This was an acquisition ten times as big as the last biggest acquisition Newell had made before. This nearly doubled the company's size, and significantly increased Newell's <a href="/wiki/Portfolio_(finance)" title="Portfolio (finance)">portfolio</a> of brands. 
</p><p>According to the November 10, 2004 <i><a href="/wiki/Frontline_(U.S._TV_series)" class="mw-redirect" title="Frontline (U.S. TV series)">Frontline</a></i> documentary series' "Is Wal-Mart Good for America?" episode, Newell's chance to buy the Rubbermaid brand resulted from the original Rubbermaid corporation's bankruptcy and <a href="/wiki/Fire_sale" title="Fire sale">fire sale</a>-style liquidation of its remaining assets. The original Rubbermaid had risen to enormous market share and profits by making Wal-Mart the near-sole distributor of its products, shifting away from a previous, years-long policy of diversifying its product distribution by using multiple retailers. After it had become dependent upon Wal-Mart for almost all of its sales, Rubbermaid claimed that it needed to raise the retail price of its products by a small, single-digit percentage. Rubbermaid said that this price increase was needed to keep pace with operational costs and inflation, without sacrificing its legendary product quality.
</p><p>Despite Rubbermaid's insistence that it couldn't afford to stay in business without it, Wal-Mart, citing its strict commitment to its "everyday low price" (EDLP) policy, and language in their contract with Rubbermaid allowing it to control pricing refused Rubbermaid's request. Rubbermaid's business collapsed shortly thereafter. Most of its physical assets had to be sold off at discount prices to satisfy its creditors; its biggest remaining asset was the Rubbermaid brand name.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p><p>However, the merger in 1999 was dubbed as the "merger from hell" by <i><a href="/wiki/Bloomberg_Business" class="mw-redirect" title="Bloomberg Business">Businessweek</a></i> magazine.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Newell <a href="/wiki/Shareholder" title="Shareholder">shareholders</a> lost 50 percent of their value in the two years following the closing and Rubbermaid shareholders lost 35 percent. In 2002, Newell <a href="/wiki/Write-off" title="Write-off">wrote off</a> $500 million in goodwill.
</p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_mergers">Other mergers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Newell_Brands&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Other mergers" data-section="6" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>In 2000, Newell Rubbermaid acquired <a href="/wiki/Gillette_(brand)" class="mw-redirect" title="Gillette (brand)">Gillette</a>'s stationery products business, including the Paper Mate, Parker, Waterman and <a href="/wiki/Liquid_Paper" title="Liquid Paper">Liquid Paper</a> brands.
</p><p>In 2002, they acquired <a href="/wiki/Irwin_Industrial_Tools" title="Irwin Industrial Tools">American Tool Companies</a>, adding the Irwin, <a href="/wiki/Vise-Grip" class="mw-redirect" title="Vise-Grip">Vise-Grip</a>, and Marathon brands to their portfolio.
</p><p>In 2003, Newell Rubbermaid acquired <a href="/wiki/American_Saw_and_Manufacturing_Company" title="American Saw and Manufacturing Company">American Saw and Manufacturing Company</a>, a manufacturer of linear-edge <a href="/wiki/Power_tool" title="Power tool">power tool</a> accessories, hand tools, and band saw blades marketed under the Lenox brand.
</p><p>In 2005, the company acquired <a href="/wiki/DYMO_Corporation" class="mw-redirect" title="DYMO Corporation">DYMO</a>, designing, manufacturing, and marketing on-demand labeling <a href="/wiki/Solution" title="Solution">solutions</a>. The company expanded its presence in this market with the 2006 purchases of CardScan business card scanners and Mimio interactive whiteboard products along with the 2007 acquisition of postage company Endicia and its Picture-it-Postage brand. In 2005, Mark Ketchum was named president and CEO.  The company added the slogan of "Brands That Matter" to their <a href="/wiki/Logo" title="Logo">logo</a> to emphasize the change.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p><p>In February 2008, Newell Rubbermaid acquired Aprica Kassai, a Japanese maker of strollers, car seats, and other children's products and Technical Concepts, in the away-from-home restroom<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (September 2018)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> market. The company created a global headquarters in the <a href="/wiki/Atlanta_metropolitan_area" title="Atlanta metropolitan area">Atlanta metropolitan area</a> to consolidate numerous brands and functions under one roof. In July 2011, <a href="/wiki/Michael_B._Polk" title="Michael B. Polk">Michael B. Polk</a> joined the company as president and CEO.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p><p>On July 21, 2014, Newell Rubbermaid announced a $308 million acquisition of Ignite Holdings, a Chicago-based maker of reusable water bottles and thermal mugs.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p><p>On October 5, 2015, Newell Rubbermaid announced that it would acquire <a href="/wiki/Elmer%27s_Products" title="Elmer's Products">Elmer's Products</a>, the makers of Elmer's <a href="/wiki/Glue" class="mw-redirect" title="Glue">glue</a>, <a href="/wiki/Krazy_Glue" class="mw-redirect" title="Krazy Glue">Krazy Glue</a>, and <a href="/wiki/X-Acto" title="X-Acto">X-Acto</a>, amongst other brands, for $600 million. The company also announced plans to divest its window covering brands Levolor and Kirsch.<sup id="cite_ref-wsj-rubbermaidelmers_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wsj-rubbermaidelmers-12">[12]</a></sup></p><p>On December 14, 2015, Newell Rubbermaid announced that it would acquire <a href="/wiki/Jarden" title="Jarden">Jarden</a> for over $15 billion of cash and stock. The combined company will be known as Newell Brands, and 55% will be owned by Newell's shareholders. The combined company will have an estimated annual sales of $16 billion.<sup id="cite_ref-bloom-newellbrands_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bloom-newellbrands-13">[13]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-wsj-newelljarden_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wsj-newelljarden-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Divestitures">Divestitures</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Newell_Brands&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Divestitures" data-section="7" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>In 2014 Newell Rubbermaid sold Ashland Hardware Systems, Bulldog and Shurline.
</p><p>In 2017, Newell sold <a href="/wiki/K2_Sports" title="K2 Sports">K2 Sports</a>, <a href="/wiki/V%C3%B6lkl" title="Völkl">Völkl</a>, <a href="/wiki/Diamond_Match_Company" title="Diamond Match Company">Diamond Match Company</a>, Levolor and Kirsch.
</p><p>In January 2018, Newell announced that it would sell off several businesses, mostly former Jarden units, as part of a refocusing effort.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> In May 2018, Newell sold Waddington to Novolex.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> In June 2018, Newell sold <a href="/wiki/Rawlings_(company)" title="Rawlings (company)">Rawlings</a> to Seidler Equity Partners.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> In August 2018, Newell sold <a href="/wiki/Goody_(brand)" title="Goody (brand)">Goody</a> to <a href="/wiki/ACON_Investments" title="ACON Investments">ACON Investments</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> In November 2018, Newell sold its Pure Fishing line of business to <a href="/wiki/Sycamore_Partners" title="Sycamore Partners">Sycamore Partners</a> for $1.3 billion and <a href="/wiki/Jostens" title="Jostens">Jostens</a> to <a href="/wiki/Platinum_Equity" title="Platinum Equity">Platinum Equity</a> for $1.3 billion.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p><p>In June 2019, Newell Brands announced the sale of the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Playing_Card_Company" title="United States Playing Card Company">United States Playing Card Company</a> to Belgian card manufacturer <a href="/wiki/Cartamundi" title="Cartamundi">Cartamundi Group</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-newell-pr-2019_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-newell-pr-2019-20">[20]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-cart-pr-2019_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cart-pr-2019-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
