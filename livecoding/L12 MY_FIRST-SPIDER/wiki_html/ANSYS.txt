<p><b>Ansys, Inc.</b> is a global public company based in <a href="/wiki/Canonsburg,_Pennsylvania" title="Canonsburg, Pennsylvania">Canonsburg, Pennsylvania</a>. It develops and markets <a href="/wiki/Multiphysics" title="Multiphysics">multiphysics</a> engineering simulation software for product design, testing and operation. Ansys was founded in 1970 by <a href="/wiki/John_A._Swanson" title="John A. Swanson">John Swanson</a>. Swanson sold his interest in the company to venture capitalists in 1993. Ansys went public on <a href="/wiki/NASDAQ" class="mw-redirect" title="NASDAQ">NASDAQ</a> in 1996. In the 2000s, Ansys made numerous acquisitions of other engineering design companies, acquiring additional technology for fluid dynamics, electronics design, and other physics analysis. Ansys became a component of the NASDAQ-100 index on December 23, 2019.

</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(1)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Corporate_history">Corporate history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ansys&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Corporate history" data-section="1" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-1 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-1">
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Origins">Origins</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ansys&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Origins" data-section="2" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>The idea for Ansys was first conceived by John Swanson while working at the <a href="/wiki/Westinghouse_Astronuclear_Laboratory" title="Westinghouse Astronuclear Laboratory">Westinghouse Astronuclear Laboratory</a> in the 1960s.<sup id="cite_ref-eight_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-eight-3">[3]</a></sup> At the time, engineers performed <a href="/wiki/Finite_element_analysis" class="mw-redirect" title="Finite element analysis">finite element analysis</a> (FEA) by hand.<sup id="cite_ref-eight_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-eight-3">[3]</a></sup> Westinghouse rejected Swanson's idea to automate FEA by developing general purpose engineering software, so Swanson left the company in 1969 to develop the software on his own.<sup id="cite_ref-eight_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-eight-3">[3]</a></sup> He founded Ansys under the name Swanson <b>An</b>alysis <b>Sys</b>tems Inc. (SASI) the next year, working out of his farmhouse in Pittsburgh.<sup id="cite_ref-five_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-five-4">[4]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-seven_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-seven-5">[5]</a></sup></p><p>Swanson developed the initial Ansys software on punch-cards and used a mainframe computer that was rented by the hour.<sup id="cite_ref-eight_3-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-eight-3">[3]</a></sup> Westinghouse hired Swanson as a consultant, under the condition that any code he developed for Westinghouse could also be included in the Ansys product line.<sup id="cite_ref-five_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-five-4">[4]</a></sup> Westinghouse also became the first Ansys user.<sup id="cite_ref-five_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-five-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Growth">Growth</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ansys&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Growth" data-section="3" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>By 1991 SASI had 153 employees and $29 million in annual revenue,<sup id="cite_ref-three_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-three-6">[6]</a></sup> controlling 10 percent of the market for <a href="/wiki/Finite_element_analysis" class="mw-redirect" title="Finite element analysis">finite element analysis</a> software.<sup id="cite_ref-three_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-three-6">[6]</a></sup> According to <i>The Engineering Design Revolution</i>, the company became "well-respected" among engineering circles, but remained small.<sup id="cite_ref-six_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-six-7">[7]</a></sup> In 1992, SASI acquired Compuflo, which marketed and developed fluid dynamics analysis software.<sup id="cite_ref-six_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-six-7">[7]</a></sup></p><p>In 1993, Mr. Swanson sold his majority interest in the company to venture capitalist firm <a href="/wiki/TA_Associates" title="TA Associates">TA Associates</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-five_4-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-five-4">[4]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-three_6-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-three-6">[6]</a></sup> Peter Smith was appointed CEO<sup id="cite_ref-six_7-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-six-7">[7]</a></sup> and SASI was renamed after the software, Ansys, the following year.<sup id="cite_ref-five_4-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-five-4">[4]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-three_6-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-three-6">[6]</a></sup> Ansys went public in 1996, raising about $46 million in an initial public offering.<sup id="cite_ref-six_7-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-six-7">[7]</a></sup> By 1997, Ansys had grown to $50.5 million in annual revenue.<sup id="cite_ref-four_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-four-8">[8]</a></sup> In the late 1990s, Ansys shifted its business model.<sup id="cite_ref-six_7-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-six-7">[7]</a></sup> It focused less on selling software licenses and corresponding revenue declined.<sup id="cite_ref-six_7-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-six-7">[7]</a></sup> However, revenue from services increased more dramatically.<sup id="cite_ref-six_7-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-six-7">[7]</a></sup> From 1996 to 1999, profits at Ansys grew an average of 160 percent per year.<sup id="cite_ref-three_6-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-three-6">[6]</a></sup></p><p>In February 2000, Jim Cashman was appointed CEO.<sup id="cite_ref-six_7-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-six-7">[7]</a></sup> Current CEO Ajei S. Gopal was appointed in early 2017.<sup id="cite_ref-ENGINEERING.com_|_Information_&amp;_Inspiration_for_Engineers_2017_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ENGINEERING.com_%7C_Information_&amp;_Inspiration_for_Engineers_2017-9">[9]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Picker_2016_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Picker_2016-10">[10]</a></sup></p>

<p>Ansys develops and markets engineering simulation software for use across the product life cycle.<sup id="cite_ref-four_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-four-8">[8]</a></sup> Ansys Mechanical <a href="/wiki/Finite_element_analysis" class="mw-redirect" title="Finite element analysis">finite element analysis</a> software is used to simulate computer models of structures, electronics, or machine components for analyzing strength, toughness, elasticity, temperature distribution, electromagnetism, fluid flow, and other attributes.<sup id="cite_ref-four_8-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-four-8">[8]</a></sup> Ansys is used to determine how a product will function with different specifications, without building test products or conducting crash tests.<sup id="cite_ref-three_6-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-three-6">[6]</a></sup> For example, Ansys software may simulate how a bridge will hold up after years of traffic, how to best process salmon in a cannery to reduce waste, or how to design a slide that uses less material without sacrificing safety.<sup id="cite_ref-seven_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-seven-5">[5]</a></sup></p><p>Most Ansys simulations are performed using the Ansys Workbench system,<sup id="cite_ref-nine_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nine-32">[32]</a></sup> which is one of the company's main products.<sup id="cite_ref-three_6-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-three-6">[6]</a></sup> Typically Ansys users break down larger structures into small components that are each modeled and tested individually.<sup id="cite_ref-seven_5-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-seven-5">[5]</a></sup> A user may start by defining the dimensions of an object,<sup id="cite_ref-seventeen_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-seventeen-33">[33]</a></sup> and then adding weight, pressure, temperature and other physical properties.<sup id="cite_ref-seventeen_33-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-seventeen-33">[33]</a></sup> Finally, the Ansys software simulates and analyzes movement, fatigue, fractures, fluid flow, temperature distribution, electromagnetic efficiency and other effects over time.<sup id="cite_ref-seventeen_33-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-seventeen-33">[33]</a></sup></p><p>Ansys also develops software for data management and backup, academic research and teaching.<sup id="cite_ref-three_6-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-three-6">[6]</a></sup> Ansys software is sold on an annual subscription basis.<sup id="cite_ref-three_6-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-three-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ansys&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: History" data-section="6" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>The first commercial version of Ansys software was labeled version 2.0 and released in 1971.<sup id="cite_ref-three_6-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-three-6">[6]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-two_11-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-two-11">[11]</a></sup> At the time, the software was made up of boxes of punch cards, and the program was typically run overnight to get results the following morning.<sup id="cite_ref-five_4-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-five-4">[4]</a></sup> In 1975, non-linear and thermo-electric features were added.<sup id="cite_ref-two_11-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-two-11">[11]</a></sup> The software was exclusively used on mainframes,<sup id="cite_ref-six_7-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-six-7">[7]</a></sup> until version 3.0 (the second release) was introduced for the <a href="/wiki/VAXstation" title="VAXstation">VAXstation</a> in 1979.<sup id="cite_ref-five_4-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-five-4">[4]</a></sup> Version 3 had a command line interface like <a href="/wiki/DOS" title="DOS">DOS</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Lee_2017_p._51_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lee_2017_p._51-34">[34]</a></sup></p><p>In 1980, <a href="/wiki/Apple_II" title="Apple II">Apple II</a> was released, allowing Ansys to convert to a graphical user interface in version 4 later that year.<sup id="cite_ref-Lee_2017_p._51_34-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lee_2017_p._51-34">[34]</a></sup> Version 4 of the Ansys software was easier to use and added features to simulate electromagnetism.<sup id="cite_ref-five_4-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-five-4">[4]</a></sup> In 1989, Ansys began working with Compuflo.<sup id="cite_ref-five_4-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-five-4">[4]</a></sup> Compuflo's Flotran fluid dynamics software was integrated into Ansys by version 5, which was released in 1993.<sup id="cite_ref-five_4-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-five-4">[4]</a></sup> Performance improvements in version 5.1 shortened processing time two to four-fold, and was followed by a series of performance improvements to keep pace with advancements in computing.<sup id="cite_ref-six_7-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-six-7">[7]</a></sup> Ansys also began integrating its software with CAD software, such as <a href="/wiki/Autodesk" title="Autodesk">Autodesk</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-six_7-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-six-7">[7]</a></sup></p><p>In 1996, Ansys released the DesignSpace structural analysis software, the LS-DYNA crash and drop test simulation product, and the Ansys Computational Fluid Dynamics (CFD) simulator.<sup id="cite_ref-two_11-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-two-11">[11]</a></sup> Ansys also added parallel processing support for PCs with multiple processors.<sup id="cite_ref-two_11-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-two-11">[11]</a></sup> The educational product Ansys/ed was introduced in 1998.<sup id="cite_ref-five_4-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-five-4">[4]</a></sup> Version 6.0 of the main Ansys product was released in December 2001.<sup id="cite_ref-five_4-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-five-4">[4]</a></sup> Version 6.0 made large-scale modeling practical for the first time, but many users were frustrated by a new blue user interface.<sup id="cite_ref-five_4-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-five-4">[4]</a></sup> The interface was redone a few months later in 6.1.<sup id="cite_ref-five_4-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-five-4">[4]</a></sup> Version 8.0 introduced the Ansys multi-field solver, which allows users to simulate how multiple physics problems would interact with one another.<sup id="cite_ref-Engineers_2004_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Engineers_2004-35">[35]</a></sup></p><p>Version 8.0 was published in 2005<sup id="cite_ref-two_11-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-two-11">[11]</a></sup> and introduced Ansys' <a href="/wiki/Fluid%E2%80%93structure_interaction" title="Fluid–structure interaction">fluid–structure interaction</a> software,<sup id="cite_ref-two_11-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-two-11">[11]</a></sup> which simulates the effect structures and fluids have on one another. Ansys also released its  Probabilistic Design System and DesignXplorer software products, which both deal with probabilities and randomness of physical elements.<sup id="cite_ref-Reh_Beley_Mukherjee_Khor_2006_pp._17–43_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Reh_Beley_Mukherjee_Khor_2006_pp._17%E2%80%9343-36">[36]</a></sup> In 2009 version 12 was released with an overhauled second version of Workbench.<sup id="cite_ref-two_11-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-two-11">[11]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Dadkhah_Zecher_2009_p._1-PA5_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dadkhah_Zecher_2009_p._1-PA5-37">[37]</a></sup> Ansys also began increasingly consolidating features into the Workbench software.<sup id="cite_ref-nine_32-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nine-32">[32]</a></sup></p><p>Version 15 of Ansys was released in 2014.<sup id="cite_ref-nine_32-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nine-32">[32]</a></sup> It added a new features for composites, bolted connections, and better mesh tools.<sup id="cite_ref-nine_32-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nine-32">[32]</a></sup> In February 2015, version 16 introduced the AIM physics engine and Electronics Desktop, which is for semiconductor design.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> The following year, version 17 introduced a new user interface and performance improvement for computing fluid dynamics problems.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> In January 2017, Ansys released version 18.<sup id="cite_ref-aduhfaekl_40-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-aduhfaekl-40">[40]</a></sup> Version 18 allowed users to collect real-world data from products and then incorporate that data into future simulations.<sup id="cite_ref-aduhfaekl_40-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-aduhfaekl-40">[40]</a></sup> The Ansys Application Builder, which allows engineers to build, use, and sell custom engineering tools, was also introduced with version 18.<sup id="cite_ref-aduhfaekl_40-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-aduhfaekl-40">[40]</a></sup></p><p>Released in January 2020, Ansys R1 2020 updates Ansys’ simulation process and data management (SPDM), materials information and electromagnetics product offerings.<sup id="cite_ref-Engineering_2020_41-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Engineering_2020-41">[41]</a></sup> In early 2020, the Ansys Academic Program surpassed one million student downloads.<sup id="cite_ref-Pulse_2.0_2020_42-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pulse_2.0_2020-42">[42]</a></sup></p>
