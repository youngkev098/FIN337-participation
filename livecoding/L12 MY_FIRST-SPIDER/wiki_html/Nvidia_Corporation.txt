formerly stylized as <b>nVidia</b> on products from the mid 90s to early-mid 2000s), is an American <a href="/wiki/Technology_company" title="Technology company">technology company</a> incorporated in <a href="/wiki/Delaware" title="Delaware">Delaware</a> and based in <a href="/wiki/Santa_Clara,_California" title="Santa Clara, California">Santa Clara</a>, <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> It designs <a href="/wiki/Graphics_processing_unit" title="Graphics processing unit">graphics processing units</a> (GPUs) for the gaming and professional markets, as well as <a href="/wiki/System_on_a_chip" title="System on a chip">system on a chip</a> units (SoCs) for the <a href="/wiki/Mobile_computing" title="Mobile computing">mobile computing</a> and automotive market. Its primary GPU product line, labeled "<a href="/wiki/GeForce" title="GeForce">GeForce</a>", is in direct competition with <a href="/wiki/Advanced_Micro_Devices" title="Advanced Micro Devices">Advanced Micro Devices</a>' (AMD) "<a href="/wiki/Radeon" title="Radeon">Radeon</a>" products. Nvidia expanded its presence in the gaming industry with its handheld <a href="/wiki/Shield_Portable" class="mw-redirect" title="Shield Portable">Shield Portable</a>, <a href="/wiki/Shield_Tablet" title="Shield Tablet">Shield Tablet</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Nvidia_Shield_(set-top_box)" class="mw-redirect" title="Nvidia Shield (set-top box)">Shield Android TV</a>.

</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(1)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Company_history">Company history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nvidia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Company history" data-section="1" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-1 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-1">
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Nvidia_campus_aerial.jpg" class="image"><noscript><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d3/Nvidia_campus_aerial.jpg/220px-Nvidia_campus_aerial.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="273" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="825" data-file-height="1025"></noscript><span class="lazy-image-placeholder" style="width: 220px;height: 273px;" data-src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d3/Nvidia_campus_aerial.jpg/220px-Nvidia_campus_aerial.jpg" data-alt="" data-width="220" data-height="273" data-class="thumbimage"> </span></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Nvidia_campus_aerial.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Aerial view of the new Nvidia headquarters building and surrounding campus and area in <a href="/wiki/Santa_Clara,_California" title="Santa Clara, California">Santa Clara, California</a>, in 2017.  <a href="/wiki/Apple_Park" title="Apple Park">Apple Park</a> is visible in the distance.</div></div></div>
<p>In the early 1990s, the three co-founders hypothesized that the proper direction for the next wave of computing would be accelerated or graphics based. They believed that this model of computing could solve problems that general-purpose computing fundamentally couldn't. They also observed that video games were some of the most computationally challenging problems, but would have incredibly high sales volume. With a capital of $40,000, the company was born. The company initially had no name and the co-founders named all their files NV, as in "next version". The need to incorporate the company prompted the co-founders to review all words with those two letters, leading them to "<a href="/wiki/Invidia" title="Invidia">invidia</a>", the Latin word for "envy".<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> The company went public on January 22, 1999.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Founders_and_initial_investment">Founders and initial investment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nvidia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Founders and initial investment" data-section="2" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>Three people co-founded Nvidia on April 5, 1993:<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Jensen_Huang" title="Jensen Huang">Jensen Huang</a> (CEO as of 2019<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Nvidia&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>), a Taiwanese American, previously director of CoreWare at <a href="/wiki/LSI_Logic" class="mw-redirect" title="LSI Logic">LSI Logic</a> and a <a href="/wiki/Microprocessor" title="Microprocessor">microprocessor</a> designer at <a href="/wiki/Advanced_Micro_Devices" title="Advanced Micro Devices">Advanced Micro Devices</a> (AMD)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Chris_Malachowsky" title="Chris Malachowsky">Chris Malachowsky</a>, an electrical engineer who worked at <a href="/wiki/Sun_Microsystems" title="Sun Microsystems">Sun Microsystems</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Curtis_Priem" title="Curtis Priem">Curtis Priem</a>, previously a senior staff engineer and graphics chip designer at Sun Microsystems</li></ul><p>The company received $20 million of venture capital funding from <a href="/wiki/Sequoia_Capital" title="Sequoia Capital">Sequoia Capital</a> and others.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Major_releases_and_acquisitions">Major releases and acquisitions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nvidia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Major releases and acquisitions" data-section="3" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>The release of the <a href="/wiki/RIVA_TNT" title="RIVA TNT">RIVA TNT</a> in 1998 solidified Nvidia's reputation for developing capable graphics adapters. In late 1999, Nvidia released the <a href="/wiki/GeForce_256" title="GeForce 256">GeForce 256</a> (NV10), most notably introducing on-board <a href="/wiki/Transform,_clipping,_and_lighting" title="Transform, clipping, and lighting">transformation and lighting</a> (T&amp;L) to consumer-level 3D hardware. Running at 120 MHz and featuring four pixel pipelines, it implemented advanced video acceleration, motion compensation and hardware sub-picture alpha blending. The GeForce outperformed existing products by a wide margin.
</p><p>Due to the success of its products, Nvidia won the contract to develop the graphics hardware for <a href="/wiki/Microsoft" title="Microsoft">Microsoft</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Xbox_(console)" title="Xbox (console)">Xbox</a> game console, which earned Nvidia a $200 million advance. However, the project took many of its best engineers away from other projects. In the short term this did not matter, and the <a href="/wiki/GeForce_2_Series" class="mw-redirect" title="GeForce 2 Series">GeForce2 GTS</a> shipped in the summer of 2000. In December 2000, Nvidia reached an agreement to acquire the intellectual assets of its one-time rival <a href="/wiki/3dfx_Interactive" title="3dfx Interactive">3dfx</a>, a pioneer in consumer 3D graphics technology leading the field from mid 1990s until 2000.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> The acquisition process was finalized in April 2002.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p><p>In July 2002, Nvidia acquired Exluna for an undisclosed sum. Exluna made software rendering tools and the personnel were merged into the Cg project.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> In August 2003, Nvidia acquired MediaQ for approximately US$70 million.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> On April 22, 2004, Nvidia acquired iReady, also a provider of high performance TCP/IP and iSCSI offload solutions.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> In December 2004, it was announced that Nvidia would assist <a href="/wiki/Sony" title="Sony">Sony</a> with the design of the graphics processor (<a href="/wiki/RSX_%27Reality_Synthesizer%27" class="mw-redirect" title="RSX 'Reality Synthesizer'">RSX</a>) in the <a href="/wiki/PlayStation_3" title="PlayStation 3">PlayStation 3</a> game console. 
On December 14, 2005, Nvidia acquired <a href="/wiki/Acer_Laboratories_Incorporated" class="mw-redirect" title="Acer Laboratories Incorporated">ULI Electronics</a>, which at the time supplied third-party <a href="/wiki/Southbridge_(computing)" title="Southbridge (computing)">southbridge</a> parts for <a href="/wiki/Chipset" title="Chipset">chipsets</a> to <a href="/wiki/ATI_Technologies" title="ATI Technologies">ATI</a>, Nvidia's competitor.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> In March 2006, Nvidia acquired <a href="/wiki/Hybrid_Graphics" title="Hybrid Graphics">Hybrid Graphics</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> In December 2006, Nvidia, along with its main rival in the graphics industry AMD (which had acquired ATI), received subpoenas from the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Justice" title="United States Department of Justice">U.S. Department of Justice</a> regarding possible <a href="/wiki/United_States_antitrust_law" title="United States antitrust law">antitrust</a> violations in the graphics card industry.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p><p><i><a href="/wiki/Forbes" title="Forbes">Forbes</a></i> named Nvidia its <i>Company of the Year</i> for 2007, citing the accomplishments it made during the said period as well as during the previous five years.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> On January 5, 2007, Nvidia announced that it had completed the acquisition of <a href="/wiki/PortalPlayer" title="PortalPlayer">PortalPlayer</a>, Inc.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> In February 2008, Nvidia acquired <a href="/wiki/Ageia" title="Ageia">Ageia</a>, developer of the <a href="/wiki/PhysX" title="PhysX">PhysX</a> <a href="/wiki/Physics_engine" title="Physics engine">physics engine</a> and <a href="/wiki/Physics_processing_unit" title="Physics processing unit">physics processing unit</a>. Nvidia announced that it planned to integrate the PhysX technology into its future GPU products.<sup id="cite_ref-cnet-nvidiaphysx_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cnet-nvidiaphysx-29">[29]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-ars-physxsse_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ars-physxsse-30">[30]</a></sup></p><p>In November 2011, after initially unveiling it at <a href="/wiki/Mobile_World_Congress" title="Mobile World Congress">Mobile World Congress</a>, Nvidia released its <a href="/wiki/Tegra_3" class="mw-redirect" title="Tegra 3">Tegra 3</a> <a href="/wiki/ARM_Holdings" class="mw-redirect" title="ARM Holdings">ARM</a> <a href="/wiki/System-on-chip" class="mw-redirect" title="System-on-chip">system-on-chip</a> for mobile devices. Nvidia claimed that the chip featured the first-ever quad-core mobile CPU.<sup id="cite_ref-tr-tegra3_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-tr-tegra3-31">[31]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> In May 2011, it was announced that Nvidia had agreed to acquire <a href="/wiki/Icera" title="Icera">Icera</a>, a baseband chip making company in the UK, for $367 million.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> In January 2013, Nvidia unveiled the <a href="/wiki/Tegra_4" class="mw-redirect" title="Tegra 4">Tegra 4</a>, as well as the <a href="/wiki/Shield_Portable" class="mw-redirect" title="Shield Portable">Nvidia Shield</a>, an <a href="/wiki/Android_(operating_system)" title="Android (operating system)">Android</a>-based <a href="/wiki/Handheld_game_console" title="Handheld game console">handheld game console</a> powered by the new system-on-chip.<sup id="cite_ref-verge-shieldunveil_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-verge-shieldunveil-34">[34]</a></sup> On July 29, 2013, Nvidia announced that they acquired <a href="/wiki/The_Portland_Group" title="The Portland Group">PGI</a> from STMicroelectronics.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></p><p>On May 6, 2016, Nvidia unveiled the first <a href="/wiki/GeForce_10_series" title="GeForce 10 series">GeForce 10 series</a> GPUs, the GTX 1080 and 1070, based on the company's new <a href="/wiki/Pascal_microarchitecture" class="mw-redirect" title="Pascal microarchitecture">Pascal microarchitecture</a>. Nvidia claimed that both models outperformed its <a href="/wiki/Maxwell_(microarchitecture)" title="Maxwell (microarchitecture)">Maxwell</a>-based Titan X model; the models incorporate <a href="/wiki/GDDR5" class="mw-redirect" title="GDDR5">GDDR5</a>X and GDDR5 memory respectively, and use a 16 nm manufacturing process. The architecture also supports a new hardware feature known as simultaneous multi-projection (SMP), which is designed to improve the quality of multi-monitor and <a href="/wiki/Virtual_reality" title="Virtual reality">virtual reality</a> rendering.<sup id="cite_ref-verge-1080unveil_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-verge-1080unveil-36">[36]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> Laptops that include these GPUs and are sufficiently thin – as of late 2017, under 0.8 inches (20 mm) – have been designated as meeting Nvidia's "Max-Q" design standard.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup></p><p>In July 2016, Nvidia agreed to a settlement for a <a href="/wiki/False_advertising" title="False advertising">false advertising</a> lawsuit regarding its <a href="/wiki/GTX_970" class="mw-redirect" title="GTX 970">GTX 970</a> model, as the models were unable to use all of their advertised 4 GB of RAM due to limitations brought by the design of its hardware.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> In May 2017, Nvidia announced a partnership with Toyota Motor Corp. Toyota will use Nvidia's <a href="/wiki/Drive_PX-series" class="mw-redirect" title="Drive PX-series">Drive PX-series</a> artificial intelligence platform for its autonomous vehicles.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> In July 2017, Nvidia and Chinese search giant Baidu, Inc. announced a far-reaching AI partnership that includes cloud computing, autonomous driving, consumer devices, and Baidu's open-source AI framework PaddlePaddle. Baidu unveiled that Nvidia 's Drive PX 2 AI will be the foundation of its autonomous-vehicle platform.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup></p><p>Nvidia officially released the NVIDIA TITAN V on December 7, 2017.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup></p><p>Nvidia officially released the Nvidia Quadro GV100 on March 27, 2018.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup></p><p>Nvidia officially released RTX 2080GPUs September 27, 2018.
</p><p>In 2018, <a href="/wiki/Google" title="Google">Google</a> announced that Nvidia's Tesla P4 graphic cards would be integrated into Google Cloud service's artificial intelligence.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup></p><p>On March 11, 2019, Nvidia announced a deal to buy <a href="/wiki/Mellanox_Technologies" title="Mellanox Technologies">Mellanox Technologies</a> for $6.9 billion<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> to substantially expand its footprint in the high-performance computing market.
</p><p>In May 2019, Nvidia announced new RTX Studio laptops. The creators say that the new laptop is going to be seven times faster than a top-end MacBook Pro with a Core i9 and AMD's Radeon Pro Vega 20 graphics in apps like Maya and RedCine-X Pro.<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Class_action_lawsuit">Class action lawsuit</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nvidia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Class action lawsuit" data-section="4" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>In July 2008, Nvidia took a write-down of approximately $200 million on its first-quarter revenue, after reporting that certain mobile chipsets and GPUs produced by the company had "abnormal failure rates" due to manufacturing defects. Nvidia, however, did not reveal the affected products. In September 2008, Nvidia became the subject of a class action lawsuit over the defects, claiming that the faulty GPUs had been incorporated into certain laptop models manufactured by <a href="/wiki/Apple_Inc." title="Apple Inc.">Apple Inc.</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dell" title="Dell">Dell</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Hewlett-Packard" title="Hewlett-Packard">HP</a>. In September 2010, Nvidia reached a settlement, in which it would reimburse owners of the affected laptops for repairs or, in some cases, replacement.<sup id="cite_ref-pcmag-laptopsettlement_49-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pcmag-laptopsettlement-49">[49]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-extremetech-nvidiadefect_50-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-extremetech-nvidiadefect-50">[50]</a></sup>  On January 10, 2011, Nvidia signed a six-year, $1.5 billion cross-licensing agreement with Intel, ending all litigation between the two companies.<sup id="cite_ref-engadget-nvintel_51-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-engadget-nvintel-51">[51]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span id="Apple.2FNvidia_web_driver_controversy"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Apple/Nvidia_web_driver_controversy">Apple/Nvidia web driver controversy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nvidia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Apple/Nvidia web driver controversy" data-section="5" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>In May 2018, on the Nvidia user forum, a thread was started<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup> asking the company to update users when they would release web drivers for its cards installed on legacy <a href="/wiki/Mac_Pro" title="Mac Pro">Mac Pro</a> 'cheesegrater' machines up to <a href="/wiki/Mac_Pro#1st_generation" title="Mac Pro">mid 2012 5,1</a> running the <a href="/wiki/MacOS_Mojave" title="MacOS Mojave">macOS Mojave</a> operating system 10.14.  <a href="/wiki/Device_driver" title="Device driver">Web drivers</a> are required to enable graphics acceleration and multiple <a href="/wiki/Computer_monitor" title="Computer monitor">display monitor</a> capabilities of the <a href="/wiki/Graphics_processing_unit" title="Graphics processing unit">GPU</a>.  On its Mojave update info website, <a href="/wiki/Apple_Computer" class="mw-redirect" title="Apple Computer">Apple</a> stated that macOS Mojave would run on legacy machines with <a href="/wiki/Metal_(API)" title="Metal (API)">'Metal compatible'</a> graphics cards<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup> and listed Metal compatible GPUs, including some manufactured by Nvidia.<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup> However, this list did not include metal compatible cards that currently work in <a href="/wiki/MacOS_High_Sierra" title="MacOS High Sierra">macOS High Sierra</a> using Nvidia developed web drivers.  In September, Nvidia responded, "Apple fully control drivers for Mac OS. But if Apple allows, our engineers are ready and eager to help Apple deliver great drivers for Mac OS 10.14 (Mojave)."<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup>  In October, Nvidia followed this up with another public announcement, "Apple fully controls drivers for Mac OS. Unfortunately, Nvidia currently cannot release a driver unless it is approved by Apple,"<sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup> suggesting a possible rift between the two companies.<sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup>  By January 2019, with still no sign of the enabling web drivers, <a href="/wiki/Apple_community#AppleInsider" title="Apple community">Apple Insider</a> weighed into the controversy with a claim that Apple management "doesn't want Nvidia support in macOS".<sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup>  The following month, Apple Insider followed this up with another claim that Nvidia support was abandoned because of "relational issues in the past",<sup id="cite_ref-59" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup>  and that Apple was developing its own GPU technology.<sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup>  Without Apple approved Nvidia web drivers, Apple users are faced with replacing their Nvidia cards with a competing supported brand, such as <a href="/wiki/AMD_Radeon" class="mw-redirect" title="AMD Radeon">AMD Radeon</a> from the list recommended by Apple.<sup id="cite_ref-61" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup></p>

<p>NVIDIA's <b>GPU Technology Conference</b> (GTC) is a series of technical conferences held around the world.<sup id="cite_ref-GPU_Technology_Conference_63-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GPU_Technology_Conference-63">[63]</a></sup> It originated in 2009 in <a href="/wiki/San_Jose,_California" title="San Jose, California">San Jose, California</a>, with an initial focus on the potential for solving computing challenges through GPUs.<sup id="cite_ref-64" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup> In recent years, the conference focus has shifted to various applications of artificial intelligence and deep learning, including: <a href="/wiki/Autonomous_car" class="mw-redirect" title="Autonomous car">self-driving cars</a>, healthcare, <a href="/wiki/High_Performance_Computing" class="mw-redirect" title="High Performance Computing">high performance computing</a>, and NVIDIA Deep Learning Institute (DLI) training.<sup id="cite_ref-65" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup> GTC 2018 attracted over 8400 attendees.<sup id="cite_ref-GPU_Technology_Conference_63-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GPU_Technology_Conference-63">[63]</a></sup></p>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(4)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Product_families">Product families</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nvidia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Product families" data-section="8" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-4 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-4">
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:NVIDIA_Shield_Tablet_with_Wireless_Controller_(16131034479).jpg" class="image"><noscript><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d1/NVIDIA_Shield_Tablet_with_Wireless_Controller_%2816131034479%29.jpg/220px-NVIDIA_Shield_Tablet_with_Wireless_Controller_%2816131034479%29.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="5184" data-file-height="3456"></noscript><span class="lazy-image-placeholder" style="width: 220px;height: 147px;" data-src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d1/NVIDIA_Shield_Tablet_with_Wireless_Controller_%2816131034479%29.jpg/220px-NVIDIA_Shield_Tablet_with_Wireless_Controller_%2816131034479%29.jpg" data-alt="" data-width="220" data-height="147" data-class="thumbimage"> </span></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:NVIDIA_Shield_Tablet_with_Wireless_Controller_(16131034479).jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>An Nvidia <a href="/wiki/Shield_Tablet" title="Shield Tablet">Shield Tablet</a></div></div></div>
<p>Nvidia's family includes primarily graphics, wireless communication, PC processors and automotive hardware/software. Some families are listed below:
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/GeForce" title="GeForce">GeForce</a>, consumer-oriented graphics processing products</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Quadro" class="mw-redirect" title="Quadro">Quadro</a>, computer-aided design and digital content creation workstation graphics processing products</li>
<li>NVS, multi-display business graphics solution</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Tegra" title="Tegra">Tegra</a>, a <a href="/wiki/System_on_a_chip" title="System on a chip">system on a chip</a> series for mobile devices</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Nvidia_Tesla" title="Nvidia Tesla">Tesla</a>, dedicated general purpose GPU for high-end image generation applications in professional and scientific fields</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/NForce" title="NForce">nForce</a>, a motherboard chipset created by Nvidia for Intel (Celeron, Pentium and Core 2) and AMD (Athlon and Duron) microprocessors</li>
<li>Nvidia Grid, a set of hardware and services by Nvidia for graphics virtualization</li>
<li>Nvidia Shield, a range of gaming hardware including the <a href="/wiki/Shield_Portable" class="mw-redirect" title="Shield Portable">Shield Portable</a>, <a href="/wiki/Shield_Tablet" title="Shield Tablet">Shield Tablet</a> and, most recently, the <a href="/wiki/Shield_Android_TV" class="mw-redirect" title="Shield Android TV">Shield Android TV</a></li>
<li>Nvidia Drive automotive solutions, a range of hardware and software products for assisting car drivers. The <a href="/wiki/Drive_PX-series" class="mw-redirect" title="Drive PX-series">Drive PX-series</a> is a high performance computer platform aimed at autonomous driving through deep learning,<sup id="cite_ref-66" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup> while Driveworks is an operating system for driverless cars.<sup id="cite_ref-67" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup></li></ul></section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(5)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Open-source_software_support">Open-source software support</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nvidia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Open-source software support" data-section="9" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-5 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-5">
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Free_and_open-source_graphics_device_driver" title="Free and open-source graphics device driver">Free and open-source graphics device driver</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mesa_3D" class="mw-redirect" title="Mesa 3D">Mesa 3D</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Nouveau_(software)" title="Nouveau (software)">nouveau (software)</a></div>
<p>Until September 23, 2013, Nvidia had not published any documentation for its hardware,<sup id="cite_ref-68" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup> meaning that programmers could not write <a href="/wiki/Free_and_open-source" class="mw-redirect" title="Free and open-source">free and open-source</a> <a href="/wiki/Device_driver" title="Device driver">device driver</a> for its products without resorting to (<a href="/wiki/Clean_room_design" title="Clean room design">clean room</a>) <a href="/wiki/Reverse_engineering" title="Reverse engineering">reverse engineering</a>.
</p><p>Instead, Nvidia provides its own <a href="/wiki/Binary_file" title="Binary file">binary</a> GeForce graphics drivers for <a href="/wiki/X.Org_Server" title="X.Org Server">X.Org</a> and an open-source library that interfaces with the <a href="/wiki/Linux_kernel" title="Linux kernel">Linux</a>, <a href="/wiki/FreeBSD" title="FreeBSD">FreeBSD</a> or <a href="/wiki/Solaris_(operating_system)" title="Solaris (operating system)">Solaris</a> kernels and the <a href="/wiki/Proprietary_software" title="Proprietary software">proprietary</a> graphics software. Nvidia also provided but stopped supporting an obfuscated open-source driver that only supports two-dimensional hardware acceleration and ships with the X.Org distribution.<sup id="cite_ref-69" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup></p><p>The proprietary nature of Nvidia's drivers has generated dissatisfaction within <a href="/wiki/Free_software" title="Free software">free-software</a> <a href="/wiki/Community" title="Community">communities</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-70" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup> Some Linux and BSD users insist on using only open-source drivers and regard Nvidia's insistence on providing nothing more than a binary-only driver as inadequate, given that competing manufacturers such as <a href="/wiki/Intel" title="Intel">Intel</a> offer support and documentation for <a href="/wiki/Open-source_model" title="Open-source model">open-source</a> developers and that others (like <a href="/wiki/Advanced_Micro_Devices" title="Advanced Micro Devices">AMD</a>) release partial documentation and provide some active development.<sup id="cite_ref-71" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-ubuntugamer_72-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ubuntugamer-72">[72]</a></sup></p><p>Because of the closed nature of the drivers, Nvidia video cards cannot deliver adequate features on some platforms and architectures given that the company only provides x86/x64 and ARMv7-A driver builds.<sup id="cite_ref-73" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-73">[73]</a></sup> As a result, support for 3D graphics acceleration in Linux on <a href="/wiki/PowerPC" title="PowerPC">PowerPC</a> does not exist, nor does support for Linux on the <a href="/wiki/Hypervisor" title="Hypervisor">hypervisor</a>-restricted <a href="/wiki/PlayStation_3" title="PlayStation 3">PlayStation 3</a> console.
</p><p>Some users claim that Nvidia's Linux drivers impose artificial restrictions, like limiting the number of monitors that can be used at the same time, but the company has not commented on these accusations.<sup id="cite_ref-74" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-74">[74]</a></sup></p><p>In 2014, with Maxwell GPUs, Nvidia started to require firmware by them to unlock all features of its graphics cards. Up to now this state did not change and makes writing open source drivers difficult.<sup id="cite_ref-75" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-75">[75]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-76" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-76">[76]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-77" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-77">[77]</a></sup></p>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(6)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Deep_learning">Deep learning</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nvidia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Deep learning" data-section="10" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-6 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-6">
<p>Nvidia GPUs are used in <a href="/wiki/Deep_learning" title="Deep learning">deep learning</a>, <a href="/wiki/Artificial_intelligence" title="Artificial intelligence">artificial intelligence</a>, and accelerated analytics. The company developed GPU-based deep learning in order to use artificial intelligence to approach problems like cancer detection, weather prediction, and self-driving vehicles.<sup id="cite_ref-78" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-78">[78]</a></sup> They are included in all <a href="/wiki/Tesla_Inc." class="mw-redirect" title="Tesla Inc.">Tesla vehicles</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-79" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-79">[79]</a></sup> The purpose is to help networks learn to “think”.<sup id="cite_ref-80" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-80">[80]</a></sup> According to <i><a href="/wiki/TechRepublic" title="TechRepublic">TechRepublic</a></i>, Nvidia GPUs "work well for deep learning tasks because they are designed for parallel computing and do well to handle the vector and matrix operations that are prevalent in deep learning".<sup id="cite_ref-81" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-81">[81]</a></sup> These GPUs are used by researchers, laboratories, tech companies and enterprise companies.<sup id="cite_ref-82" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-82">[82]</a></sup> In 2009, Nvidia was involved in what was called the "big bang" of deep learning, "as deep-learning neural networks were combined with Nvidia graphics processing units (GPUs)".<sup id="cite_ref-83" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-83">[83]</a></sup> That year, the <a href="/wiki/Google_Brain" title="Google Brain">Google Brain</a> used Nvidia GPUs to create Deep Neural Networks capable of machine learning, where <a href="/wiki/Andrew_Ng" title="Andrew Ng">Andrew Ng</a> determined that GPUs could increase the speed of deep-learning systems by about 100 times.<sup id="cite_ref-84" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-84">[84]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="DGX">DGX</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nvidia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: DGX" data-section="11" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>In April 2016, Nvidia produced the <a href="/wiki/DGX-1" class="mw-redirect" title="DGX-1">DGX-1</a> <a href="/wiki/Supercomputer" title="Supercomputer">supercomputer</a> based on an 8 GPU cluster, to improve the ability of users to use deep learning by combining GPUs with integrated deep learning software.<sup id="cite_ref-85" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-85">[85]</a></sup> It also developed Nvidia Tesla K80 and P100 GPU-based virtual machines, which are available through <a href="/wiki/Google_Cloud_Platform" title="Google Cloud Platform">Google Cloud</a>, which Google installed in November 2016.<sup id="cite_ref-86" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-86">[86]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Microsoft" title="Microsoft">Microsoft</a> added GPU servers in a preview offering of its N series based on Nvidia's Tesla K80s, each containing 4992 processing cores. Later that year, AWS's P2 instance was produced using up to 16 Nvidia Tesla K80 GPUs. That month Nvidia also partnered with IBM to create a software kit that boosts the AI capabilities of <a href="/wiki/Watson_(computer)" title="Watson (computer)">Watson</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-87" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-87">[87]</a></sup> called IBM PowerAI.<sup id="cite_ref-88" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-88">[88]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-89" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-89">[89]</a></sup> Nvidia also offers its own NVIDIA Deep Learning software development kit.<sup id="cite_ref-90" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-90">[90]</a></sup> In 2017, the GPUs were also brought online at the <a href="/wiki/Riken" title="Riken">RIKEN</a> Center for Advanced Intelligence Project for <a href="/wiki/Fujitsu" title="Fujitsu">Fujitsu</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-91" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-91">[91]</a></sup> The company's deep learning technology led to a boost in its 2017 earnings.<sup id="cite_ref-92" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-92">[92]</a></sup></p><p>In May 2018, researchers at the artificial intelligence department of Nvidia realized the possibility that a robot can learn to perform a job simply by observing the person doing the same job. They have created a system that, after a short revision and testing, can already be used to control the universal robots of the next generation. In addition to GPU manufacturing, Nvidia provides parallel processing capabilities to researchers and scientists that allow them to efficiently run high-performance applications.<sup id="cite_ref-93" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-93">[93]</a></sup></p>
