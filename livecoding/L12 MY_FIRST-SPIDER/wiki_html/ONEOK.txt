<p>ONEOK's Energy Services operation focuses primarily on marketing natural gas and related services throughout the U.S. Energy Services, which derives more than 84 percent of its earnings from the physical marketing business, showed an operating income increase of $26.5 million. Energy Services’ retail business participates in customer gas choice program in Nebraska and Wyoming.
</p><p>On December 22, 2005,  The ONEOK Foundation said it would donate $1 million this winter to assist residents in Oklahoma, Kansas and Texas with their energy bills.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p><p>In 2007, <i>Fortune</i> magazine named ONEOK the most admired company in the energy industry.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p><p>ONEOK's predecessor, Oklahoma Natural Gas Company (ONG), had been headquartered in an <a href="/wiki/Art_Deco" title="Art Deco">Art Deco</a> <a href="/wiki/Oklahoma_Natural_Gas_Company_Building" title="Oklahoma Natural Gas Company Building">building</a> on the northwest corner of Seventh Street and Boston Avenue in Tulsa since 1928. In 1982, ONEOK chairman, J. E. Tyree, announced plans to demolish the ONG building and replace it with a new 16-story tower. However, this did not happen. Instead, ONEOK bought a new Cities Service Company building still under construction (later renamed <a href="/wiki/Citgo" title="Citgo">Citgo</a>) in August, 1982. The Cities Service Company, undergoing financial difficulties, already had a project underway to build a high-rise headquarters building at Fifth and Boulder. ONEOK realized that it would be more economical to cap the planned structure at 17 stories and move its headquarters there, rather than to proceed with its original plan. It completed the new black granite and glass tower in 1984.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p><p>In 2009, ONEOK sponsored the construction of <a href="/wiki/ONEOK_Field" title="ONEOK Field">ONEOK Field</a>, the new Tulsa Drillers minor league baseball stadium in downtown Tulsa.
</p>

<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:ONGBuildingTulsa.jpg" class="image"><noscript><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/56/ONGBuildingTulsa.jpg/220px-ONGBuildingTulsa.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="363" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="1606" data-file-height="2651"></noscript><span class="lazy-image-placeholder" style="width: 220px;height: 363px;" data-src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/56/ONGBuildingTulsa.jpg/220px-ONGBuildingTulsa.jpg" data-alt="" data-width="220" data-height="363" data-class="thumbimage"> </span></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:ONGBuildingTulsa.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Oklahoma Natural Gas Company headquarters in Tulsa, Oklahoma built in 1928</div></div></div>
<p>Oklahoma Natural Gas Company (ONG) was founded in 1906 by two attorneys, <a href="/wiki/Dennis_T._Flynn" class="mw-redirect" title="Dennis T. Flynn">Dennis T. Flynn</a> and <a href="/w/index.php?title=Charles_B._Ames&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Charles B. Ames (page does not exist)">Charles B. Ames</a>, who planned to develop the natural gas that was a byproduct of the northern Oklahoma oilfields. ONG incorporated on October 9, 1906, under the laws of Oklahoma Territory. Flynn was president and Ames the secretary.  Joined by Chicago entrepreneur, H. M. Byllesby, for whom they worked, they also formed the <a href="/wiki/Oklahoma_Gas_%26_Electric" title="Oklahoma Gas &amp; Electric">Oklahoma Gas and Electric Company</a> (OG&amp;E) to bring natural gas from the Cleveland Oil Field to <a href="/wiki/Oklahoma_City" title="Oklahoma City">Oklahoma City</a>, using it to generate electric power. Byllesby soon resigned, but Glenn T. Braden and Theodore N. Barnsdall joined ONG. Both were already experienced oilmen. Together, they owned the Osage and Oklahoma Gas Company. Braden had been an executive of <a href="/wiki/Standard_Oil_Company" class="mw-redirect" title="Standard Oil Company">Standard Oil Company</a> and Barnsdall an oil producer.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[a]</a></sup> ONG built a natural gas pipeline from the Osage fields to Oklahoma City which went into service December 28, 1907. ONG extended the line to <a href="/wiki/Guthrie,_Oklahoma" title="Guthrie, Oklahoma">Guthrie</a> and <a href="/wiki/Shawnee,_Oklahoma" title="Shawnee, Oklahoma">Shawnee</a>. It also built the first gas pipeline compressor station in the state in 1910.<sup id="cite_ref-EOHC-ONG_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EOHC-ONG-10">[9]</a></sup> Braden followed Flynn as president of the company.
</p><p>ONG continued to build pipelines throughout Oklahoma. By the time Braden retired in 1921, ONG owned over 1,300 miles (2,100 km) of lines and served over a half million customers in 48 of the state's 77 counties. In 1928, it built its noted headquarters building in Tulsa. It reorganized several times, first becoming a Maryland Corporation, then a Delaware Corporation.<sup id="cite_ref-EOHC-ONG_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EOHC-ONG-10">[9]</a></sup></p><p>In December 1980, the Oklahoma Natural Gas board voted to reorganize as a holding company, ONEOK Inc. <a href="/wiki/Oklahoma_Natural_Gas" title="Oklahoma Natural Gas">Oklahoma Natural Gas</a> became ONEOK's leading subsidiary, headquartered in Oklahoma City. The parent company is headquartered in Tulsa.<sup id="cite_ref-EOHC-ONG_10-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EOHC-ONG-10">[9]</a></sup></p><p>In 1996, ONEOK acquired the natural gas operations of Kansas utility Western Resources (now <a href="/wiki/Westar_Energy" class="mw-redirect" title="Westar Energy">Westar Energy</a>) and reorganized them as <a href="/wiki/Kansas_Gas_Service" title="Kansas Gas Service">Kansas Gas Service</a>. In 2003, it acquired the Texas properties of Southern Union Gas, which became another ONEOK operating company, Texas Gas service.
</p><p>In February 2014, ONEOK spun off its natural gas distribution companies–Oklahoma Natural Gas, Kansas Gas Service, and Texas Gas Services–into a separate publicly traded company, <a href="/wiki/ONE_Gas" title="ONE Gas">ONE Gas</a>. For all intents and purposes, ONEOK had spun out the core of the original Oklahoma Natural Gas.
</p>
