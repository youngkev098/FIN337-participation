<p><b>PNC Financial Services Group, Inc.</b> (stylized as <b>PNC</b>) is an American  <a href="/wiki/Bank_holding_company" title="Bank holding company">bank holding company</a> and <a href="/wiki/Financial_services" title="Financial services">financial services</a> corporation based in <a href="/wiki/Pittsburgh,_Pennsylvania" class="mw-redirect" title="Pittsburgh, Pennsylvania">Pittsburgh, Pennsylvania</a>. Its <a href="/wiki/Bank" title="Bank">banking</a> subsidiary, PNC Bank, operates in 19 <a href="/wiki/U.S._state" title="U.S. state">states</a> and the <a href="/wiki/District_of_Columbia" class="mw-redirect" title="District of Columbia">District of Columbia</a> with 2,459 <a href="/wiki/Branch_(banking)" title="Branch (banking)">branches</a> and 9,051 <a href="/wiki/Automated_teller_machine" title="Automated teller machine">ATMs</a>. <h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Retail_banking">Retail banking</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=PNC_Financial_Services&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Retail banking" data-section="2" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>PNC Bank offers consumer and business banking services via 2,459 branches in <a href="/wiki/Alabama" title="Alabama">Alabama</a>, <a href="/wiki/Delaware" title="Delaware">Delaware</a>, the <a href="/wiki/District_of_Columbia" class="mw-redirect" title="District of Columbia">District of Columbia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Florida" title="Florida">Florida</a>, <a href="/wiki/Georgia_(U.S._state)" title="Georgia (U.S. state)">Georgia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kentucky" title="Kentucky">Kentucky</a>, <a href="/wiki/Indiana" title="Indiana">Indiana</a>, <a href="/wiki/Illinois" title="Illinois">Illinois</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kansas" title="Kansas">Kansas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Maryland" title="Maryland">Maryland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Michigan" title="Michigan">Michigan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Missouri" title="Missouri">Missouri</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_Jersey" title="New Jersey">New Jersey</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_York_(state)" title="New York (state)">New York</a>, <a href="/wiki/North_Carolina" title="North Carolina">North Carolina</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ohio" title="Ohio">Ohio</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pennsylvania" title="Pennsylvania">Pennsylvania</a>, <a href="/wiki/South_Carolina" title="South Carolina">South Carolina</a>, <a href="/wiki/Texas" title="Texas">Texas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Virginia" title="Virginia">Virginia</a>, <a href="/wiki/West_Virginia" title="West Virginia">West Virginia</a>, &amp; <a href="/wiki/Wisconsin" title="Wisconsin">Wisconsin</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-10K_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10K-1">[1]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p><p>PNC is one of the largest <a href="/wiki/Small_Business_Administration" title="Small Business Administration">Small Business Administration</a> lenders.<sup id="cite_ref-10K_1-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10K-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h4 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Virtual_Wallet_banking_product">Virtual Wallet banking product</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=PNC_Financial_Services&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Virtual Wallet banking product" data-section="3" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h4>
<p>Virtual Wallet is a combination <a href="/wiki/Checking_account" class="mw-redirect" title="Checking account">checking account</a> and <a href="/wiki/Savings_account" title="Savings account">savings account</a> accessible via <a href="/wiki/Mobile_app" title="Mobile app">mobile app</a>. It features 3 integrated accounts: a checking account called Spend, an interest-bearing checking account called Reserve, and a savings account called Growth. It was launched in July 2008 and was aimed primarily to the tech-savvy members of <a href="/wiki/Generation_Y" class="mw-redirect" title="Generation Y">Generation Y</a>. In June 2012, the product had 1 million users.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="BlackRock">BlackRock</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=PNC_Financial_Services&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: BlackRock" data-section="4" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>PNC owns 22% of <a href="/wiki/BlackRock" title="BlackRock">BlackRock</a>, the largest asset management firm in the world by <a href="/wiki/Assets_under_management" title="Assets under management">assets under management</a>. This <a href="/wiki/Ownership" title="Ownership">ownership</a> interest had a market value of $17.9 billion as of December 31, 2017.<sup id="cite_ref-10K_1-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10K-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Asset_Management_Group">Asset Management Group</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=PNC_Financial_Services&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Asset Management Group" data-section="5" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>PNC Asset Management Group (AMG) provides Institutional Asset Management and <a href="/wiki/Wealth_Management" class="mw-redirect" title="Wealth Management">Wealth Management</a> services to <a href="/wiki/High-net-worth_individual" title="High-net-worth individual">high-net-worth individuals</a> and has approximately $282 billion in assets under administration, of which $151 billion is directly under management.<sup id="cite_ref-10K_1-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10K-1">[1]</a></sup> PNC Wealth Management is the 16th largest wealth management firm with $48.8 billion in private client <a href="/wiki/Assets_under_management" title="Assets under management">assets under management</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-10K_1-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10K-1">[1]</a></sup> Hawthorn Family Wealth provides asset management services for <a href="/wiki/Family_office" title="Family office">family offices</a> with over $20 million in net worth and directly oversaw almost $31 billion as of 2017.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Lending">Lending</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=PNC_Financial_Services&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Lending" data-section="6" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>As of December 31, 2017, PNC had $220 billion in outstanding loans, including $147 billion in commercial loans and $73 billion in consumer loans.<sup id="cite_ref-10K_1-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10K-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h4 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Credit_cards">Credit cards</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=PNC_Financial_Services&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Credit cards" data-section="7" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h4>
<p>As of December 31, 2017, PNC had $5.3 billion in outstanding <a href="/wiki/Credit_card" title="Credit card">credit card</a> loans.<sup id="cite_ref-10K_1-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10K-1">[1]</a></sup> In 2016, PNC ranked as the 12th largest  credit card issuer, with $33 billion in purchase volume.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h4 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="PNC_Business_Credit">PNC Business Credit</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=PNC_Financial_Services&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: PNC Business Credit" data-section="8" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h4>
<p>Operating out of offices in the United States, Canada, and the United Kingdom, PNC Business Credit provides asset-based lending to <a href="/wiki/Private_equity_firm" title="Private equity firm">private equity firms</a> and <a href="/wiki/Middle-market_company" title="Middle-market company">middle market companies</a>.
</p>
<h4 class="in-block"><span id="Corporate_and_Institutional_Banking.2FHarris_Williams_.26_Co"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Corporate_and_Institutional_Banking/Harris_Williams_&amp;_Co">Corporate and Institutional Banking/Harris Williams &amp; Co</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=PNC_Financial_Services&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Corporate and Institutional Banking/Harris Williams &amp; Co" data-section="9" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h4>
<p>PNC operates one of the largest <a href="/wiki/Treasury_management" title="Treasury management">treasury management</a> businesses and the 2nd-largest lead arranger of asset-based <a href="/wiki/Loan_syndication" class="mw-redirect" title="Loan syndication">loan syndications</a> in the United States. <a href="/wiki/Harris_Williams_%26_Co." title="Harris Williams &amp; Co.">Harris Williams &amp; Co.</a>, a subsidiary of the company, is one of the U.S.'s largest <a href="/wiki/Mergers_and_acquisitions" title="Mergers and acquisitions">mergers and acquisitions</a> advisory firms for <a href="/wiki/Middle-market_companies" class="mw-redirect" title="Middle-market companies">middle-market companies</a>.
</p>
<h4 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Residential_mortgages">Residential mortgages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=PNC_Financial_Services&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Residential mortgages" data-section="10" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h4>
<p>PNC Mortgage (formerly National City Mortgage) is credited with the 1st mortgage in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> and has offices across the country. The company had $17.212 billion in mortgage loans outstanding as of December 31, 2017.<sup id="cite_ref-10K_1-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10K-1">[1]</a></sup></p><p>This is the 2nd mortgage division to be named PNC Mortgage. In 2001, PNC sold the original PNC Mortgage to <a href="/wiki/Washington_Mutual" title="Washington Mutual">Washington Mutual</a> due to volatility in the market.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> In 2005, PNC began <a href="/wiki/Outsourcing" title="Outsourcing">outsourcing</a> mortgages to <a href="/wiki/Wells_Fargo" title="Wells Fargo">Wells Fargo</a> until the National City deal.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h4 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Auto_and_student_loans">Auto and student loans</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=PNC_Financial_Services&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Auto and student loans" data-section="11" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h4>
<p>As of December 31, 2017, PNC had $12.9 billion in <a href="/wiki/Car_finance" title="Car finance">car finance</a> loans and $5 billion in <a href="/wiki/Student_loan" title="Student loan">student loans</a> outstanding.<sup id="cite_ref-10K_1-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10K-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h4 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Commercial_real_estate_lending">Commercial real estate lending</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=PNC_Financial_Services&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Commercial real estate lending" data-section="12" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h4>
<p>PNC provides acquisition, development and permanent financing for commercial and real estate clients including term loans and treasury management and capital markets services.
</p><p>PNC is ranked 7th on the list of largest direct lenders by National Real Estate Investor, with $16.9 billion in annual real estate lending.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Midland_Loan_Services">Midland Loan Services</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=PNC_Financial_Services&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Midland Loan Services" data-section="13" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>Midland Loan Services, a division of PNC Real Estate, is a third-party provider of service and technology for the commercial real estate finance industry. It specializes in commercial loan and <a href="/wiki/Commercial_mortgage-backed_security" title="Commercial mortgage-backed security">CMBS</a> portfolio servicing. Founded in 1991, its headquarters are in <a href="/wiki/Overland_Park,_Kansas" title="Overland Park, Kansas">Overland Park, Kansas</a>.
</p><p>PNC/Midland is ranked by <a href="/wiki/Mortgage_Bankers_Association" title="Mortgage Bankers Association">Mortgage Bankers Association</a> as the 2nd largest master and primary servicer of commercial bank and savings institution loans.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(2)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=PNC_Financial_Services&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: History" data-section="14" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-2 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-2">
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:66px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Pittsburghnationalbank.jpg.gif" class="image"><noscript><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/d/df/Pittsburghnationalbank.jpg.gif" decoding="async" width="64" height="52" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="64" data-file-height="52"></noscript><span class="lazy-image-placeholder" style="width: 64px;height: 52px;" data-src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/d/df/Pittsburghnationalbank.jpg.gif" data-alt="" data-width="64" data-height="52" data-class="thumbimage"> </span></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Pittsburghnationalbank.jpg.gif" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>1970s-era Pittsburgh National Bank logo, used until the 1982 merger to form PNC Bank.</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Topoftroy2007.JPG" class="image"><noscript><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8b/Topoftroy2007.JPG/170px-Topoftroy2007.JPG" decoding="async" width="170" height="227" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="1524" data-file-height="2032"></noscript><span class="lazy-image-placeholder" style="width: 170px;height: 227px;" data-src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8b/Topoftroy2007.JPG/170px-Topoftroy2007.JPG" data-alt="" data-width="170" data-height="227" data-class="thumbimage"> </span></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Topoftroy2007.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>PNC's offices in <a href="/wiki/Troy,_Michigan" title="Troy, Michigan">Troy, Michigan</a>.</div></div></div>
<p>PNC Financial Services traces its history to the Pittsburgh Trust and Savings Company which was founded in <a href="/wiki/Pittsburgh" title="Pittsburgh">Pittsburgh</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pennsylvania" title="Pennsylvania">Pennsylvania</a> on April 10, 1845.<sup id="cite_ref-firsthundred_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-firsthundred-11">[11]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Thurston_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Thurston-12">[12]</a></sup> Due to the long recovery from the <a href="/wiki/Great_Fire_of_Pittsburgh" title="Great Fire of Pittsburgh">Great Fire of Pittsburgh</a>, PNC was not fully operational until January 28, 1852.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> It originally opened offices at <a href="/wiki/Liberty_Avenue_(Pittsburgh)" title="Liberty Avenue (Pittsburgh)">Liberty Avenue</a> and 12th Street. The bank was renamed The Pittsburgh Trust Company in 1853.<sup id="cite_ref-White_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-White-14">[14]</a></sup> In 1858, the company located its corporate offices to the corner of <a href="/wiki/Fifth_Avenue_(Pittsburgh)" title="Fifth Avenue (Pittsburgh)">Fifth Avenue</a> and Wood Street in Pittsburgh, where they remain to this day. The bank changed its name to First National Bank of Pittsburgh in 1863, after it became the first bank in the country to apply for a national charter<sup id="cite_ref-firsthundred_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-firsthundred-11">[11]</a></sup> as part of that year's <a href="/wiki/National_Banking_Act" class="mw-redirect" title="National Banking Act">National Banking Act</a>. It received the 48th charter on August 5, 1863, with other later banks receiving charters sooner due to paperwork problems and the fact that the bank was already in business.<sup id="cite_ref-firsthundred_11-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-firsthundred-11">[11]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-White_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-White-14">[14]</a></sup></p><p>In 1946, First National merged with Peoples-Pittsburgh Trust Company, with whom it had worked closely since the 1930s, to form Peoples First National Bank &amp; Trust. In 1959, Peoples First merged with Fidelity Trust Company to form Pittsburgh National Bank. At this time, the bank adopted the first version of its present logo–a stylized triangle representing the <a href="/wiki/Golden_Triangle_(Pittsburgh)" class="mw-redirect" title="Golden Triangle (Pittsburgh)">Golden Triangle</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> In 1969, the bank reorganized as a holding company, Pittsburgh National Corporation. 
</p><p>Another branch of the current bank, the <a href="/wiki/Philadelphia" title="Philadelphia">Philadelphia</a>-based Provident National Corporation, dates back to 1865.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p><p>In 1982, Pittsburgh National Corporation and Provident National Corporation merged into a new entity named PNC Financial Corporation. It was the largest bank merger in American history at the time and created a company with $10.3 billion in assets.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> Between 1991 and 1996, PNC purchased more than ten smaller banks and financial institutions that broadened its market base from <a href="/wiki/Kentucky" title="Kentucky">Kentucky</a> to the <a href="/wiki/New_York_metropolitan_area" title="New York metropolitan area">New York metropolitan area</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2020)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p><p>In 1998, PNC acquired <a href="/wiki/Hilliard_Lyons" title="Hilliard Lyons">Hilliard Lyons</a> for $275 million in cash and stock.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> Hilliard Lyons was sold in 2008.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p><p>In 1998, PNC sold its credit card business to Metris (now <a href="/wiki/HSBC_Finance" title="HSBC Finance">HSBC Finance</a>)<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/MBNA" title="MBNA">MBNA</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p><p>In the year 2000, the company committed to diversify their service, thus they adopted a new brand image and changed their name to PNC Financial Services Group.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p><p>In 2006, PNC got back into the credit card business by marketing and issuing credit cards under the <a href="/wiki/MasterCard" class="mw-redirect" title="MasterCard">MasterCard</a> brand in partnership with <a href="/wiki/U.S._Bancorp" title="U.S. Bancorp">U.S. Bancorp</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> After the National City merger in 2008, the <a href="/wiki/U.S._Bancorp" title="U.S. Bancorp">U.S. Bancorp</a> products were converted to PNC Bank products.
</p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="National_City_acquisition">National City acquisition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=PNC_Financial_Services&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: National City acquisition" data-section="15" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information: <a href="/wiki/National_City_acquisition_by_PNC" title="National City acquisition by PNC">National City acquisition by PNC</a></div>
<p>On October 24, 2008, during the <a href="/wiki/Financial_crisis_of_2007-2008" class="mw-redirect" title="Financial crisis of 2007-2008">financial crisis of 2007-2008</a>, PNC announced that it would acquire <a href="/wiki/Cleveland" title="Cleveland">Cleveland</a>-based <a href="/wiki/National_City_Corp." title="National City Corp.">National City Corp.</a> for $5.2 billion in stock.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> The announcement came hours after PNC sold <a href="/wiki/Preferred_stock" title="Preferred stock">preferred stock</a> to the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Treasury" class="mw-redirect" title="United States Treasury">United States Treasury</a> as part of the <a href="/wiki/Troubled_Asset_Relief_Program" title="Troubled Asset Relief Program">Troubled Asset Relief Program</a> implemented as part of the <a href="/wiki/Emergency_Economic_Stabilization_Act_of_2008" title="Emergency Economic Stabilization Act of 2008">Emergency Economic Stabilization Act of 2008</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> The stock issued to the U.S. Treasury was repurchased in 2010.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p><p>The deal helped PNC double in size and become the 6th largest bank in the United States by deposit and 5th largest by branches. It also made PNC the largest bank in <a href="/wiki/Pennsylvania" title="Pennsylvania">Pennsylvania</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ohio" title="Ohio">Ohio</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Kentucky" title="Kentucky">Kentucky</a>, as well as the second largest bank in <a href="/wiki/Maryland" title="Maryland">Maryland</a> and <a href="/wiki/Indiana" title="Indiana">Indiana</a>. It also greatly expanded PNC's presence in the <a href="/wiki/Midwestern_United_States" title="Midwestern United States">Midwest</a> as well as entering the <a href="/wiki/Florida" title="Florida">Florida</a> market. National City complemented PNC's presence, as Western Pennsylvania, <a href="/wiki/Cincinnati" title="Cincinnati">Cincinnati</a> and <a href="/wiki/Louisville,_Kentucky" title="Louisville, Kentucky">Louisville, Kentucky</a> were among the few markets in which both banks had a major presence.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p><p>PNC then had its footprint stretch from <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a> to <a href="/wiki/St._Louis,_Missouri" class="mw-redirect" title="St. Louis, Missouri">St. Louis, Missouri</a>, with branches as far south as <a href="/wiki/Miami" title="Miami">Miami</a> and as far north as <a href="/wiki/Sault_Ste._Marie,_Michigan" title="Sault Ste. Marie, Michigan">Sault Ste. Marie, Michigan</a> on the <a href="/wiki/Upper_Peninsula_of_Michigan" title="Upper Peninsula of Michigan">Upper Peninsula of Michigan</a>. PNC completed the conversion of the National City branches on June 14, 2010.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_acquisitions">Other acquisitions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=PNC_Financial_Services&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Other acquisitions" data-section="16" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>In 2004, PNC acquired United National Bancorp based in <a href="/wiki/Bridgewater,_New_Jersey" class="mw-redirect" title="Bridgewater, New Jersey">Bridgewater, New Jersey</a> for $321 million in cash and 6.6 million shares of its common stock.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p><p>In 2005, PNC acquired <a href="/wiki/Riggs_Bank" title="Riggs Bank">Riggs National Corporation</a> of <a href="/wiki/Washington,_D._C." class="mw-redirect" title="Washington, D. C.">Washington, D. C.</a> Riggs had been fined after aiding Chilean dictator <a href="/wiki/Augusto_Pinochet" title="Augusto Pinochet">Augusto Pinochet</a> in <a href="/wiki/Money_laundering" title="Money laundering">money laundering</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-acquired_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-acquired-33">[33]</a></sup> At one stroke, PNC became one of the largest banks in the <a href="/wiki/Washington_metropolitan_area" title="Washington metropolitan area">Washington metropolitan area</a>.
</p><p>On March 2, 2007, PNC acquired Maryland-based Mercantile Bankshares, making PNC the 8th largest bank in the United States by deposits.<sup id="cite_ref-acquired_33-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-acquired-33">[33]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Postgazette_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Postgazette-34">[34]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> On October 26, 2007, PNC acquired Yardville National Bancorp, a small commercial bank centered in central New Jersey and eastern Pennsylvania.<sup id="cite_ref-acquired_33-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-acquired-33">[33]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> On April 4, 2008, PNC acquired Sterling Financial Corporation, a commercial and consumer bank with accounts and branches in central Pennsylvania, northeastern Maryland and Delaware.<sup id="cite_ref-acquired_33-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-acquired-33">[33]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></p><p>On September 15, 2007, PNC Bank acquired <a href="/wiki/Citizens_National_Bank_(Laurel,_Maryland)" title="Citizens National Bank (Laurel, Maryland)">Citizens National Bank</a> of <a href="/wiki/Laurel,_Maryland" title="Laurel, Maryland">Laurel, Maryland</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-NIC-citizens_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NIC-citizens-38">[38]</a></sup></p><p>On August 14, 2009, PNC took over Dwelling House Savings &amp; Loan and its only branch location in Pittsburgh's <a href="/wiki/Hill_District" class="mw-redirect" title="Hill District">Hill District</a> after Dwelling House suffered from <a href="/wiki/Bank_failure" title="Bank failure">bank failure</a> and was placed under receivership by the <a href="/wiki/Federal_Deposit_Insurance_Corporation" title="Federal Deposit Insurance Corporation">Federal Deposit Insurance Corporation</a> (FDIC).<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> Dwelling House had been known in Pittsburgh to provide low-income <a href="/wiki/African_American" class="mw-redirect" title="African American">African Americans</a> loans that other banks would deny. The branch was closed and  accounts were transferred to the existing PNC branch in the Hill District.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup></p><p>In 2011, PNC acquired BankAtlantic's <a href="/wiki/Tampa_Bay_Area" class="mw-redirect" title="Tampa Bay Area">Tampa Bay Area</a> branches.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup></p><p>In December 2011, PNC acquired 27 branches in the northern <a href="/wiki/Atlanta" title="Atlanta">Atlanta</a> suburbs with $240 million in deposits and $42 million in book value from <a href="/wiki/Flagstar_Bank" title="Flagstar Bank">Flagstar Bank</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup></p><p>In 2012, PNC acquired <a href="/wiki/RBC_Bank" title="RBC Bank">RBC Bank</a> from <a href="/wiki/Royal_Bank_of_Canada" title="Royal Bank of Canada">Royal Bank of Canada</a> for $3.45 billion.<sup id="cite_ref-RBC_46-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RBC-46">[46]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> RBC Bank had a 426 branches in southern <a href="/wiki/Virginia" title="Virginia">Virginia</a>, <a href="/wiki/North_Carolina" title="North Carolina">North Carolina</a>, <a href="/wiki/South_Carolina" title="South Carolina">South Carolina</a>, <a href="/wiki/Georgia_(U.S._state)" title="Georgia (U.S. state)">Georgia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alabama" title="Alabama">Alabama</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Florida" title="Florida">Florida</a>. Of these regions, PNC had existing branches only in Florida and the acquisition filled a gap in PNC's market footprint between northern Virginia and central Florida, adding about 900,000 customers and 483 ATM locations. It made PNC the 5th largest bank by branches behind <a href="/wiki/Wells_Fargo" title="Wells Fargo">Wells Fargo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bank_of_America" title="Bank of America">Bank of America</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chase_(bank)" class="mw-redirect" title="Chase (bank)">Chase</a>, and <a href="/wiki/U.S._Bancorp" title="U.S. Bancorp">U.S. Bancorp</a> and the 6th largest by total assets behind the aforementioned four banks and <a href="/wiki/Citibank" title="Citibank">Citibank</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-RBC_46-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RBC-46">[46]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span id="Sale_of_PNC_Global_Investment_Servicing_.282010.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Sale_of_PNC_Global_Investment_Servicing_(2010)">Sale of PNC Global Investment Servicing (2010)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=PNC_Financial_Services&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Sale of PNC Global Investment Servicing (2010)" data-section="17" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>Established in 1973, PNC's Global Investment Servicing subsidiary (GIS) was the second largest full-service <a href="/wiki/Mutual_fund" title="Mutual fund">mutual fund</a> transfer agent in the U.S and the second-largest full service accounting and administration provider to U.S. mutual funds. With 4,700 employees, GIS operated from the <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Ireland" title="Republic of Ireland">Republic of Ireland</a>, the United States and the <a href="/wiki/Cayman_Islands" title="Cayman Islands">Cayman Islands</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Luxembourg" title="Luxembourg">Luxembourg</a>. GIS serviced $1.9 trillion in total assets and 58 million shareholder accounts. In 2007, GIS was awarded a license allowing it to expand further into Europe. PNC Global Investment Servicing was known as PFPC until July 2008.<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup></p><p>In 2010, PNC sold GIS to <a href="/wiki/The_Bank_of_New_York_Mellon" title="The Bank of New York Mellon">The Bank of New York Mellon</a> to repay funds from the <a href="/wiki/Troubled_Asset_Relief_Program" title="Troubled Asset Relief Program">Troubled Asset Relief Program</a>, which were used for the <a href="/wiki/National_City_acquisition_by_PNC" title="National City acquisition by PNC">National City acquisition</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Construction_of_The_Tower_at_PNC_Plaza">Construction of The Tower at PNC Plaza</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=PNC_Financial_Services&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Construction of The Tower at PNC Plaza" data-section="18" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Tower_at_PNC_Plaza" title="Tower at PNC Plaza">Tower at PNC Plaza</a></div>
<p>On May 23, 2011, PNC unveiled plans for the <a href="/wiki/Tower_at_PNC_Plaza" title="Tower at PNC Plaza">Tower at PNC Plaza</a>, a new $400 million headquarters building, originally planned to be 40 stories, to be constructed by the company on the corner of Fifth Avenue and Wood Street in Downtown Pittsburgh, catercorner to the former headquarters, <a href="/wiki/One_PNC_Plaza" title="One PNC Plaza">One PNC Plaza</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup> The modern, <a href="/wiki/Environmentally_friendly" title="Environmentally friendly">environmentally friendly</a>, 33-floor, 800,000-square-foot (74,000 m<sup>2</sup>) building designed by <a href="/wiki/Gensler" title="Gensler">Gensler</a> opened on October 2, 2015.<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup> PNC owns the building and occupies all its space, except for street-level storefronts which are leased to retail tenants. It is one of the world's most environmentally friendly skyscrapers. Some of its features include a double glass facade to reduce cooling costs and promote natural airflow into the building, a high-efficiency climate-control system to heat or cool specific zones of the building as needed, and a pair of living rooftops to collect and channel rainwater and reduce heat gain.<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Harris_Teeter_partnership">Harris Teeter partnership</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=PNC_Financial_Services&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Harris Teeter partnership" data-section="19" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>On July 30, 2012, PNC announced plans to put ATMs in 138 <a href="/wiki/Harris_Teeter" title="Harris Teeter">Harris Teeter</a> grocery stores in the <a href="/wiki/Carolinas" title="Carolinas">Carolinas</a>, plus 53 other stores.<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup></p>

<p>In September 2014, PNC acquired Solebury Capital Group, a capital markets advisory firm, for $50 million.<sup id="cite_ref-Tascarella_56-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Tascarella-56">[56]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Tascarella_56-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Tascarella-56">[56]</a></sup></p><p>In April 2017, the company acquired the U.S. equipment finance business of <a href="/wiki/ECN_Capital" title="ECN Capital">ECN Capital</a> for $1.3 billion.<sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup></p><p>In November 2017, the company acquired The Trout Group, an <a href="/wiki/Investor_relations" title="Investor relations">investor relations</a> and strategic advisory firm servicing the <a href="/wiki/Healthcare_industry" title="Healthcare industry">healthcare industry</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-59" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup></p><p>In 2018, the company acquired Fortis Advisors, which provides post-merger shareholder services.<sup id="cite_ref-61" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-62" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup> The company ranked 165th on the 2018 <a href="/wiki/Fortune_500" title="Fortune 500">Fortune 500</a> list of the largest United States corporations by revenue.<sup id="cite_ref-63" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup></p>

<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Overcharging_of_Black_and_Hispanic_borrowers_by_National_City">Overcharging of Black and Hispanic borrowers by National City</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=PNC_Financial_Services&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Overcharging of Black and Hispanic borrowers by National City" data-section="22" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>In December 2013, the Department of Justice and the Consumer Financial Protection Bureau announced that they had reached an agreement with National City Bank to resolve allegations that the bank had charged Black and Hispanic borrowers higher prices for mortgages between 2002 and 2008, before the acquisition by PNC. Regulators claimed that National City had violated the <a href="/wiki/Fair_Housing_Act" class="mw-redirect" title="Fair Housing Act">Fair Housing Act</a> and <a href="/wiki/Equal_Credit_Opportunity_Act" title="Equal Credit Opportunity Act">Equal Credit Opportunity Act</a> by charging more than 75,000 borrowers higher loan rates based on their race or ethnicity rather than their risk level. National City's lack of pricing guidelines resulted in black borrowers being charged an average of $159 more in extra upfront fees or higher interest than white borrowers. Black borrowers also paid an average of $228 more annually over the life of the loan than white borrowers. Hispanics paid $125 more upfront and $154 more annually than white borrowers. Under the terms of the settlement, PNC was required to pay victims $35 million.<sup id="cite_ref-64" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Retailer_breach_forced_PNC_to_reissue_customer_cards">Retailer breach forced PNC to reissue customer cards</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=PNC_Financial_Services&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Retailer breach forced PNC to reissue customer cards" data-section="23" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>In March 2006, PNC and other large banks were forced to reissue hundreds of debit cards to customers after card numbers were disclosed by a breach at an unknown retailer.<sup id="cite_ref-65" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Lawsuit_from_Military_Channel">Lawsuit from Military Channel</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=PNC_Financial_Services&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Lawsuit from Military Channel" data-section="24" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>Also in March 2006, PNC Bank was sued by Paul Bariteau, an investor in the <a href="/wiki/Military_Channel" class="mw-redirect" title="Military Channel">Military Channel</a>. Bariteau claimed PNC let the chairman of the Military Channel make unauthorized withdrawals of millions of dollars from the company's account for personal use.<sup id="cite_ref-66" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Securities_fraud_settlement">Securities fraud settlement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=PNC_Financial_Services&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Securities fraud settlement" data-section="25" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>In June 2003, PNC Bank agreed to pay $115 million to settle federal securities fraud charges after one of its subsidiaries fraudulently transferred $762 million in bad loans and other venture capital investments to an AIG entity in order to conceal them from investors.<sup id="cite_ref-67" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Overtime_claim_by_loan_officers">Overtime claim by loan officers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=PNC_Financial_Services&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Overtime claim by loan officers" data-section="26" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>In 2017, PNC agreed to pay $16 million to settle claims of overtime wages by loan officers under the <a href="/wiki/Fair_Labor_Standards_Act" class="mw-redirect" title="Fair Labor Standards Act">Fair Labor Standards Act</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-68" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="National_Prearranged_Services_Fraud">National Prearranged Services Fraud</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=PNC_Financial_Services&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: National Prearranged Services Fraud" data-section="27" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>According to a lawsuit, funds entrusted to National Prearranged Services (NPS), a St.Louis-based company that sold prepaid funerals, were diverted and embezzled. PNC Bank is the successor to Allegiant Bank, which served as a trustee for NPS from 1998 to 2004. In 2015, a jury ordered PNC to pay $391 million.<sup id="cite_ref-69" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup> In 2017, a federal judge overturned the decision.<sup id="cite_ref-70" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Municipal_Bonds_Disclosure_Violations">Municipal Bonds Disclosure Violations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=PNC_Financial_Services&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: Municipal Bonds Disclosure Violations" data-section="28" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>In 2015, PNC was one of 22 companies that violated disclosure requirements for <a href="/wiki/Municipal_bond" title="Municipal bond">municipal bonds</a> by failing to divulge that the issuers had filed late financial reports. It was fined $500,000.<sup id="cite_ref-71" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup></p>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(4)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Community_initiatives">Community initiatives</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=PNC_Financial_Services&amp;action=edit&amp;section=29" title="Edit section: Community initiatives" data-section="29" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-4 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-4">
<p>PNC has sponsored several initiatives to improve <a href="/wiki/Education" title="Education">education</a>, <a href="/wiki/Health" title="Health">health</a> and human services, <a href="/wiki/Cultural" class="mw-redirect" title="Cultural">cultural</a> activities and <a href="/wiki/The_arts" title="The arts">the arts</a>. These include  "PNC Grow Up Great", a commitment to early childhood development, the "PNC Foundation", and <a href="/wiki/Community_development" title="Community development">community development</a> investments.<sup id="cite_ref-72" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-72">[72]</a></sup></p><p>Since 1984, PNC has compiled the <a href="/wiki/Christmas_Price_Index" title="Christmas Price Index">Christmas Price Index</a>, a humorous <a href="/wiki/Economic_indicator" title="Economic indicator">economic indicator</a> which estimates the prices of the items found in the song "<a href="/wiki/The_Twelve_Days_of_Christmas_(song)" title="The Twelve Days of Christmas (song)">The Twelve Days of Christmas</a>".<sup id="cite_ref-73" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-73">[73]</a></sup></p><p>In 2012, PNC opened the PNC Fairfax Connection, a community center in Cleveland, Ohio.<sup id="cite_ref-74" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-74">[74]</a></sup></p>

<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Mountaintop_Mining">Mountaintop Mining</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=PNC_Financial_Services&amp;action=edit&amp;section=31" title="Edit section: Mountaintop Mining" data-section="31" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>The Earth Quaker Action Team, led by <a href="/wiki/George_Lakey" title="George Lakey">George Lakey</a>, requested that PNC divest in mountaintop mining projects in March 2010.  The <a href="/wiki/Quakers" title="Quakers">Quakers</a> were especially sensitive to PNC's investments, as it was informally referred to as "The Quaker Bank," due to the fact that Provident Life &amp; Trust Company had been founded by Quakers.<sup id="cite_ref-75" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-75">[75]</a></sup> After the bank ignored their request, EQAT began to protest.  In 29 September 2010, EQAT along with some <a href="/wiki/Swarthmore_College" title="Swarthmore College">Swarthmore College</a> students, members of the <a href="/wiki/Rainforest_Action_Network" title="Rainforest Action Network">Rainforest Action Network</a> and <a href="/wiki/Reverend_Billy_and_the_Church_of_Stop_Shopping" title="Reverend Billy and the Church of Stop Shopping">Reverend Billy and the Church of Stop Shopping</a> protested at a PNC bank in Washington, D.C. by building a small mountain made of dirt. Police arrested the leader George Lakey and three others. "In October of 2010, PNC announced that they would stop loaning money to companies that gained over 50% of their coal from mountaintop removal. However, none of the companies with which PNC conducted business produced that much coal via mountaintop removal, and thus the new policy did not represent change. Soon after the bank’s new commitment, PNC gave money to Massey Energy, a company found responsible for poor safety conditions that led to the deaths of thirty-one miners in April 2009. Unsatisfied by PNC’s ineffective policy, EQAT staged another protest on 17 December 2010. Protesters sang Christmas carols while delivering stockings full of coal to a PNC Bank in Media, Pennsylvania." <sup id="cite_ref-76" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-76">[76]</a></sup></p><p>Students at <a href="/wiki/Temple_University" title="Temple University">Temple University</a> and Swarthmore College held sit-ins to protest the bank, and EQAT next protested PNC by conducting Quaker-style worship (sitting in silence until let to speak) during a shareholders meeting in D.C. "At the end of the meeting, the activists gave a gift bag to CEO James Rohr with a framed picture of the Appalachian Mountains and a pamphlet about the dangers of mountaintop removal." <sup id="cite_ref-77" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-77">[77]</a></sup></p><p>Over time, protests became more and more elaborate and creative.  September 22, 2011, EQAT charged PNC with "impersonating a green bank" and held trial in the PNC lobby.  Two weeks later, they held a "die-in" alongside activists from Occupy Philadelphia, where protesters acted dead by laying on the floor of a PNC bank.  December 6, 2011, police arrested five EQAT members for constructing windmills (a clean alternative to coal) inside of the downtown PNC bank.  In February 2012, EQAT convinced various Quaker groups to withdraw nearly two million dollars (later increased to over three million) invested with PNC, and in April of that year, EQAT along with students from various colleges, protested by dancing to bluegrass music next to the University of Pennsylvania’s PNC campus branch.  April 30, 2012, EQAT members began a 200 mile walk from Philadelphia to the PNC Bank headquarters in Pittsburgh. EQAT held a series of protests at PNC branches along the way. <sup id="cite_ref-78" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-78">[78]</a></sup>  In October 2012, activist students began to approach PNC bank managers, asking them to drink well water from affected communities in Appalachia.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2020)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p><p>On March 14, 2013, EQAT members began a collective 40-day fast (not all members fasted the entire time), making their fasts public through social media.  During the fast, members publicly protested dressed in traditional Quaker costumes when the Philadelphia Chamber of Congress awarding PNC's J. William Mills with the William Penn award.  The fast ended on 23 April with a worship session in the middle of a PNC shareholder meeting.  On October 21, 2013, EQAT staged the largest bank branch protest in U.S. history. Activists protested by holding silent worship inside sixteen PNC bank branches, most of which were in Pittsburgh, leading many of the branches to shut down for the day.  Seven protesters were arrested by Pittsburgh police after refusing to leave.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2020)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p><p>By 2014, the movement had spread across the country. Protesters again held Quaker worship inside of the PNC shareholders meeting, despite the fact that the location had been moved to Tampa, Florida.  On December 6, 2014, thirty protests were held in branches across twelve states.<sup id="cite_ref-79" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-79">[79]</a></sup>  Finally in February 2015, after five years and 125 separate protests, PNC Bank announced it would officially stop investing in mountaintop removal coal mining. <sup id="cite_ref-80" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-80">[80]</a></sup></p>
