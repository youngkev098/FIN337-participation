<p><b>The Kellogg Company</b>, doing business as <b>Kellogg's</b>, is an American <a href="/wiki/Multinational_corporation" title="Multinational corporation">multinational</a> food manufacturing company headquartered in <a href="/wiki/Battle_Creek,_Michigan" title="Battle Creek, Michigan">Battle Creek, Michigan</a>, United States. Kellogg's produces <a href="/wiki/Cereal" title="Cereal">cereal</a> and convenience foods, including <a href="/wiki/Cracker_(food)" title="Cracker (food)">crackers</a> and <a href="/wiki/Toaster_pastry" title="Toaster pastry">toaster pastries</a> and markets their products by several well known brands including <a href="/wiki/Corn_flakes" title="Corn flakes">Corn Flakes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Frosted_Flakes" title="Frosted Flakes">Frosted Flakes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pringles" title="Pringles">Pringles</a>, <a href="/wiki/Eggo" title="Eggo">Eggo</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Cheez-It" title="Cheez-It">Cheez-It</a>. </section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(1)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span></h2><section class="mf-section-1 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-1">
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/W._K._Kellogg" class="mw-redirect" title="W. K. Kellogg">W. K. Kellogg</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:209px;"><a href="/wiki/File:CornFlakesPackage1906.jpg" class="image"><noscript><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/2/2c/CornFlakesPackage1906.jpg" decoding="async" width="207" height="287" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="207" data-file-height="287"></noscript><span class="lazy-image-placeholder" style="width: 207px;height: 287px;" data-src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/2/2c/CornFlakesPackage1906.jpg" data-alt="" data-width="207" data-height="287" data-class="thumbimage"> </span></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:CornFlakesPackage1906.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>First Battle Creek Toasted Corn Flake Co. <a href="/wiki/Corn_flakes" title="Corn flakes">corn flakes</a> package (1906), later to become the Kellogg Food Company in 1908</div></div></div>
<p>In 1876, <a href="/wiki/John_Harvey_Kellogg" title="John Harvey Kellogg">John Harvey Kellogg</a> became the superintendent of the <a href="/wiki/Battle_Creek_Sanitarium" title="Battle Creek Sanitarium">Battle Creek Sanitarium</a> (originally the Western Health Reform Institute founded by <a href="/wiki/Ellen_G._White" title="Ellen G. White">Ellen White</a>) and his brother, <a href="/wiki/W._K._Kellogg" class="mw-redirect" title="W. K. Kellogg">W. K. Kellogg</a>, worked as the bookkeeper. This is where corn flakes were accidentally created and led to the eventual formation of the Kellogg Company.
</p><p>For years, W. K. Kellogg assisted his brother in research aimed at improving the vegetarian diet of the Battle Creek Sanitarium's patients, especially in the search for wheat-based granola. In 1894 at the Battle Creek Sanitarium, a combination hospital and health spa for the elite and famous, W.K. Kellogg and his brother, Dr. John Harvey Kellogg were in the process of cooking some wheat for a type of granola when they were called away. When they returned, the wheat had become stale. They decided to force the tempered grain through the rollers anyway, and surprisingly, the grain did not come out in long sheets of dough. Instead, each wheat berry was flattened and came out as a thin flake.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p><p>W. K. Kellogg persuaded his brother to serve the food in flake form. Soon the flaked wheat was being packaged to meet hundreds of mail-order requests from guests after they left the Sanitarium. However, Dr. John Harvey forbade his brother Will from distributing cereal beyond his consumers. As a result, the brothers fell out, and W. K. launched the <b>Battle Creek Toasted Corn Flake Company</b> on February 19, 1906.<sup id="cite_ref-ShurtleffAoyagi2014_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ShurtleffAoyagi2014-8">[8]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Benjamin2013_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Benjamin2013-9">[9]</a></sup> Convincing his brother to relinquish rights to the product, Will's company produced and marketed the hugely successful Kellogg's Toasted Corn Flakes and was renamed the <b>Kellogg Toasted Corn Flake Company</b> in 1909, taking on the current name of the <b>Kellogg Company</b> in 1922.<sup id="cite_ref-ShurtleffAoyagi2014_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ShurtleffAoyagi2014-8">[8]</a></sup></p><p>In 1931, the Kellogg Company announced that most of its factories would shift towards 30-hour work weeks, from the usual 40.  W.K. Kellogg stated that he did this so that an additional shift of workers would be employed in an effort to support people through the depression era. This practice remained until <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, and continued briefly after the war, although some departments and factories remained locked into 30-hour work weeks until 1980.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p><p>From 1969 to 1970, the slogan “Kellogg’s puts more into your day” was used on Sunday morning tv shows. From 1969 to 1977, Kellogg's acquired various small businesses including Salada Foods, Fearn International, <a href="/wiki/Mrs._Smith%27s_Pies" class="mw-redirect" title="Mrs. Smith's Pies">Mrs. Smith's Pies</a>, <a href="/wiki/Eggo" title="Eggo">Eggo</a>, and Pure Packed Foods;<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> however, it was later criticized for not diversifying further like <a href="/wiki/General_Mills" title="General Mills">General Mills</a> and <a href="/wiki/Quaker_Oats" class="mw-redirect" title="Quaker Oats">Quaker Oats</a> were.  After underspending its competition in marketing and product development, Kellogg's U.S. market share hit a low 36.7% in 1983. A prominent <a href="/wiki/Wall_Street" title="Wall Street">Wall Street</a> analyst called it "a fine company that's past its prime" and the cereal market was being regarded as "mature". Such comments stimulated Kellogg chairman William E. LaMothe to improve, which primarily involved approaching the demographic of 80 million <a href="/wiki/Baby_boomer" class="mw-redirect" title="Baby boomer">baby boomers</a> rather than marketing children-oriented cereals. In emphasizing cereal's convenience and nutritional value, Kellogg's helped persuade U.S. consumers age 25 to 49 to eat 26% more cereal than people of that age ate five years prior. The U.S. ready-to-eat cereal market, worth $3.7 billion at retail in 1983, totaled $5.4 billion by 1988 and had expanded three times as fast as the average grocery category. Kellogg's also introduced new products including <a href="/wiki/Crispix" title="Crispix">Crispix</a>, Raisin Squares, and <a href="/wiki/Nutri-Grain" title="Nutri-Grain">Nutri-Grain</a> Biscuits and reached out internationally with <a href="/wiki/Just_Right" title="Just Right">Just Right</a> aimed at <a href="/wiki/Australians" title="Australians">Australians</a> and Genmai Flakes for Japan. During this time, the company maintained success over its top competitors: <a href="/wiki/General_Mills" title="General Mills">General Mills</a>, which largely marketed children's cereals, and <a href="/wiki/Post_Foods" class="mw-redirect" title="Post Foods">Post</a>, which had difficulty in the adult cereal market.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p><p>In 2001, Kellogg's acquired the <a href="/wiki/Keebler_Company" title="Keebler Company">Keebler Company</a> for $3.87 billion.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Over the years, it has also gone on to acquire <a href="/wiki/Morningstar_Farms" title="Morningstar Farms">Morningstar Farms</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kashi_Company" class="mw-redirect" title="Kashi Company">Kashi</a> divisions or subsidiaries. Kellogg's also owns the <a href="/wiki/Bear_Naked" class="mw-redirect" title="Bear Naked">Bear Naked</a>, Natural Touch, <a href="/wiki/Cheez-It" title="Cheez-It">Cheez-It</a>, Murray, Austin cookies and crackers, <a href="/wiki/Famous_Amos" title="Famous Amos">Famous Amos</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gardenburger" title="Gardenburger">Gardenburger</a> (acquired 2007), and Plantation brands. Presently, Kellogg's is a member of the <a href="/wiki/World_Cocoa_Foundation" title="World Cocoa Foundation">World Cocoa Foundation</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2019)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p><p>In 2012, Kellogg's became the world's second-largest snack food company (after <a href="/wiki/PepsiCo" title="PepsiCo">PepsiCo</a>) by acquiring the <a href="/wiki/Potato_crisps" class="mw-redirect" title="Potato crisps">potato crisps</a> brand <a href="/wiki/Pringles" title="Pringles">Pringles</a> from <a href="/wiki/Procter_%26_Gamble" title="Procter &amp; Gamble">Procter &amp; Gamble</a> for $2.7 billion in a cash deal.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p><p>In 2017, Kellogg's acquired <a href="/wiki/Chicago" title="Chicago">Chicago</a>-based food company <a href="/wiki/Rxbar" title="Rxbar">Rxbar</a> for $654 million.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Earlier that year, Kellogg's also opened new corporate office space in Chicago's <a href="/wiki/Merchandise_Mart" title="Merchandise Mart">Merchandise Mart</a> for its global growth and IT departments.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> In the UK, Kellogg's also released the W. K. Kellogg brand of organic, vegan and plant-based cereals (such as granolas, organic wholegrain wheat, and "super grains") with no added sugars.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p><p>In 2018, Kellogg's decided to cease their operations in Venezuela due to the economic crisis the country is facing.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p><p>On April 1, 2019, it was announced that Kellogg's was selling <a href="/wiki/Famous_Amos" title="Famous Amos">Famous Amos</a>, Murray's, <a href="/wiki/Keebler_Company" title="Keebler Company">Keebler</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mother%27s_Cookies" title="Mother's Cookies">Mother's</a> and Little Brownie Bakers (one of the producers of the <a href="/wiki/Girl_Scout_Cookies" title="Girl Scout Cookies">cookies for the Girl Scouts of the USA</a>) to <a href="/wiki/Ferrero_SpA" title="Ferrero SpA">Ferrero SpA</a> for $1.4 billion.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> On July 29, 2019 that sale was completed.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>

<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Sample_Kellogg%27s_Sans_typeface.png" class="image"><noscript><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/14/Sample_Kellogg%27s_Sans_typeface.png/220px-Sample_Kellogg%27s_Sans_typeface.png" decoding="async" width="220" height="286" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="800" data-file-height="1040"></noscript><span class="lazy-image-placeholder" style="width: 220px;height: 286px;" data-src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/14/Sample_Kellogg%27s_Sans_typeface.png/220px-Sample_Kellogg%27s_Sans_typeface.png" data-alt="" data-width="220" data-height="286" data-class="thumbimage"> </span></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Sample_Kellogg%27s_Sans_typeface.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Kellogg's Sans (typeface used by Kellogg's)</div></div></div>
<p>Various methods have been used in the company's history to promote the company and its brands. Foremost among these is the design of the Kellogg's logo by Ferris Crane under the art direction of famed type guru Y. Ames. Another was the well-remembered jingle "K E double-L, O double-good, Kellogg's best to you!"<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Naming_conventions_(geographic_names)" title="Wikipedia:Naming conventions (geographic names)"><span title="The geographic scope near this tag is ambiguous. (January 2020)">where?</span></a></i>]</sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Chronological_items" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers"><span title="The time period mentioned near this tag is ambiguous. (January 2020)">when?</span></a></i>]</sup></p><p>With the rising popularity of patent medicine in early 20th century advertising, <a href="/wiki/The_Kellogg_Company" class="mw-redirect" title="The Kellogg Company">The Kellogg Company</a> of Canada published a book named <i>A New Way of Living</i> that showed readers "how to achieve a new way of living; how to preserve vitality; how to maintain enthusiasm and energy; how to get the most out of life because of a physical ability to enjoy it". <h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Premiums_and_prizes">Premiums and prizes</span></h3>
<p>W.K. Kellogg was the first to introduce <a href="/wiki/Prize_(marketing)" title="Prize (marketing)">prizes</a> in boxes of cereal.  The marketing strategy that he established has produced thousands of different <a href="/wiki/Cereal_box_prize" title="Cereal box prize">cereal box prizes</a> that have been distributed by the tens of billions.<sup id="cite_ref-promomagazine.com_43-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-promomagazine.com-43">[43]</a></sup></p>
<h4 class="in-block"><span id="Children.27s_premiums"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Children's_premiums">Children's premiums</span></h4>
<p><a href="/wiki/Corn_flakes" title="Corn flakes">Kellogg's Corn Flakes</a> had the first cereal <a href="/wiki/Premium_(marketing)" title="Premium (marketing)">premium</a> with <i>The Funny Jungleland Moving Pictures Book.</i> The book was originally available as a <a href="/wiki/Prize_(marketing)" title="Prize (marketing)">prize</a> that was given to the customer in the store with the purchase of two packages of the cereal.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> But in 1909, Kellogg's changed the book giveaway to a premium mail-in offer for the cost of a <a href="/wiki/Dime_(United_States_coin)" title="Dime (United States coin)">dime</a>. Over 2.5 million copies of the book were distributed in different editions over a period of 23 years.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup></p>
<h4 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Cereal_box_prizes">Cereal box prizes</span></h4>
<p>In 1945, Kellogg's inserted a <a href="/wiki/Cereal_box_prize" title="Cereal box prize">prize</a> in the form of <a href="/wiki/Pin-back_button" title="Pin-back button">pin-back buttons</a> into each box of Pep cereal. Pep pins have included <a href="/wiki/U.S._Army" class="mw-redirect" title="U.S. Army">U.S. Army</a> squadrons as well as characters from newspaper comics and were available through 1947. There were five series of comic characters and 18 different buttons in each set, with a total of 90 in the collection.<sup id="cite_ref-promomagazine.com_43-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-promomagazine.com-43">[43]</a></sup>  Other manufacturers of major brands of cereal, including <a href="/wiki/General_Mills" title="General Mills">General Mills</a>, <a href="/wiki/Malt-O-Meal_Company" class="mw-redirect" title="Malt-O-Meal Company">Malt-O-Meal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nestl%C3%A9" title="Nestlé">Nestlé</a>, <a href="/wiki/Post_Foods" class="mw-redirect" title="Post Foods">Post Foods</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Quaker_Oats_Company" title="Quaker Oats Company">Quaker Oats</a>, followed suit and inserted prizes into boxes of cereal to promote sales and brand loyalty.
</p>

<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Merchandising">Merchandising</span></h3>
<p>Kellogg's has used some merchandising for their products. Kellogg's once released <i>Mission Nutrition</i>, a <a href="/wiki/Video_game" title="Video game">PC game</a> that came free with special packs of cereal. It played in a similar fashion as <i><a href="/wiki/Donkey_Kong_Country" title="Donkey Kong Country">Donkey Kong Country</a></i>; users could play as Tony the Tiger, Coco the Monkey, or Snap, Crackle, and Pop. Kellogg's has also released "Talking" games. The two current versions are Talking Tony and Talking Sam. In these games, a microphone is used to play games and create voice commands for their computers. In Talking Tony, Tony the Tiger, one of Kellogg's most famous mascots, would be the main and only character in the game. In Talking Sam, Toucan Sam, another famous mascot, would be in the game, instead. Some [toy cars] have the Kellogg's logo on them, and occasionally their mascots.
</p><p>There was also a Talking Snap Crackle and Pop software.

<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Questionable_nutritional_value">Questionable nutritional value</span></h3>
<p>Some of Kellogg's marketing has been questioned in the press, prompted by an increase in consumer awareness of the mismatch between the marketing messages and the products themselves.<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup></p><p>Food bloggers are also questioning the marketing methods used by cereal manufacturing companies such as Kellogg's, due to their high sugar content and use of ingredients such as <a href="/wiki/High-fructose_corn_syrup" title="High-fructose corn syrup">high-fructose corn syrup</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="2010_cereal_recall">2010 cereal recall</span></h3>
<p>On June 25, the company voluntarily began to recall about 28 million boxes of <a href="/wiki/Apple_Jacks" title="Apple Jacks">Apple Jacks</a>, <a href="/wiki/Corn_Pops" title="Corn Pops">Corn Pops</a>, <a href="/wiki/Froot_Loops" title="Froot Loops">Froot Loops</a> and <a href="/wiki/Honey_Smacks" title="Honey Smacks">Honey Smacks</a> because of an unusual smell and flavor from the packages' liners that could make people ill. Kellogg's said about 20 people complained about the cereals, including five who reported nausea and vomiting. Consumers reported the cereal smelled or tasted waxy or like metal or soap. Company spokeswoman J. Adaire Putnam said some described it as tasting stale. However, no serious health problems had been reported.<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup></p><p>The suspected chemical that caused the illnesses was <a href="/wiki/2-methylnaphthalene" class="mw-redirect" title="2-methylnaphthalene">2-methylnaphthalene</a>, used in the cereal packaging process. Little is known about 2-methylnaphthalene's impact on human health as the <a href="/wiki/Food_and_Drug_Administration" title="Food and Drug Administration">Food and Drug Administration</a> has no scientific data on its impact on humans, and the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Environmental_Protection_Agency" title="United States Environmental Protection Agency">United States Environmental Protection Agency</a> (EPA) also does not have health and safety data.  This is despite the EPA having sought information on it from the chemical industry for 16 years.  2-Methylnaphthalene is a component of crude oil and is "structurally related to <a href="/wiki/Naphthalene" title="Naphthalene">naphthalene</a>, an ingredient in mothballs and toilet-deodorant blocks" that the EPA considers a possible human carcinogen.<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup></p><p><i>Kellogg's</i> offered consumers refunds in the meantime.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Only products with the letters "KN" following the use-by date were included in the recall. The products were distributed throughout the U.S. and began arriving in stores in late March 2010. Products in Canada were not affected.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="2012_cereal_recall">2012 cereal recall</span></h3>
<p>Kellogg's issued a voluntary recall of some of its "Frosted Mini-Wheats Bite Size Original" and "Mini-Wheats Unfrosted Bite Size" products due to the possibility of flexible metal mesh fragments in the food. The affected products varied in size from single-serving bowls to large 70-ounce cartons. Use-by dates printed on the recalled packages ranged from April 1, 2013, to September 21, 2013, and were accompanied by the letters KB, AP or FK.<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup></p>

<p>On June 3, 2010, Kellogg's was found to be making unsubstantiated and <a href="/wiki/False_advertising" title="False advertising">misleading claims</a> in advertising their cereal products by the <a href="/wiki/Federal_Trade_Commission" title="Federal Trade Commission">Federal Trade Commission</a> (FTC).<sup id="cite_ref-FTC1_54-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FTC1-54">[54]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FTC_commission_finding_55-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FTC_commission_finding-55">[55]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-nyt1_56-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nyt1-56">[56]</a></sup></p><p>Kellogg's responded by stating "We stand behind the validity of our product claims and research, so we agreed to an order that covers those claims. In July 2012, the UK banned a "Special K" advertisement due to its citing caloric values that did not take into account the caloric value of milk consumed with the cereal.<sup id="cite_ref-59" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup> In 2016 an ad telling UK consumers that Special K is “full of goodness” and “nutritious” was banned.<sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Human_right_violations_of_palm_oil_in_2016">Human right violations of palm oil in 2016</span></h3>
<p>According to <a href="/wiki/Amnesty_International" title="Amnesty International">Amnesty International</a> in 2016, Kellogg's palm oil provider <a href="/wiki/Wilmar_International" title="Wilmar International">Wilmar International</a> profited from 8 to 14-year-old <a href="/wiki/Child_labor" class="mw-redirect" title="Child labor">child labor</a> and <a href="/wiki/Forced_labor" class="mw-redirect" title="Forced labor">forced labor</a>. Some workers were extorted, threatened or not paid for work. Some workers suffered severe injuries from chemicals such as Paraquat.<sup id="cite_ref-61" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-62" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup> Kellogg's alleged not being aware of the child abuses due to traceability; Amnesty's human rights director replied that "Using mealy-mouthed excuses about 'traceability' is a total cop-out."<sup id="cite_ref-63" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Expropriation_of_the_brand_in_Venezuela">Expropriation of the brand in Venezuela</span></h3>
<p>In 2018 Kellogg's ceased operations in <a href="/wiki/Venezuela" title="Venezuela">Venezuela</a>. The factories were taken by Venezuelan state under <a href="/wiki/Nicol%C3%A1s_Maduro" title="Nicolás Maduro">Nicolás Maduro</a> administration. Mid 2019, Venezuelan Kellogg's cereal boxes, portraying the Venezuelan flag and a motto from Maduro "Together, everything is possible" (<a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">Spanish</a>: <i lang="es">Juntos todo es posible</i>) alongside Kellogg's logo and mascots, were sold all over the country. The Kellogg's Company considers it as an illicit use and the company will take legal action.<sup id="cite_ref-64" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup></p>
