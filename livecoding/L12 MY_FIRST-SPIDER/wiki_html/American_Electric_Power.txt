</p><p>AEP's utility units operate as AEP Ohio, AEP Texas, Appalachian Power (in Virginia, West Virginia, and Tennessee), Indiana Michigan Power, Kentucky Power, Public Service Company of Oklahoma, and Southwestern Electric Power Company (in Arkansas, Louisiana and east Texas). AEP's headquarters are in Columbus, Ohio.
</p><p>American Electric Power was the first utility to utilize 345 kV transmission lines which took place in 1953.
The company ranked 185th on the 2018 <a href="/wiki/Fortune_500" title="Fortune 500">Fortune 500</a> of the largest United States corporations by revenue.<sup id="cite_ref-Fortune_500_Companies_2018_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fortune_500_Companies_2018-5">[5]</a></sup></p>

</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(1)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Subsidiaries">Subsidiaries</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=American_Electric_Power&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Subsidiaries" data-section="1" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-1 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-1">
<p>The company is divided into seven major geographic local operating companies:
</p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="AEP_Ohio">AEP Ohio</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=American_Electric_Power&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: AEP Ohio" data-section="2" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>AEP Ohio serves 1.5 million customers in central, southern and northwestern Ohio.  For years, it consisted of two operating companies, Ohio Power and Columbus Southern Power.  However, in 2014, Columbus Southern was merged into Ohio Power, leaving Ohio Power as the legal operating company for regulatory purposes.
</p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="AEP_Texas">AEP Texas</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=American_Electric_Power&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: AEP Texas" data-section="3" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>AEP Texas was formed from a merger of various predecessor utilities, and joined AEP as part of its acquisition of Central and South West Corporation in 1997. It consists of AEP Texas North Company (formerly West Texas Utilities), which operates in west Texas, and AEP Texas Central Company (formerly Central Power and Light), which operates in south Texas.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Appalachian_Power">Appalachian Power</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=American_Electric_Power&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Appalachian Power" data-section="4" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>Appalachian Power (APCO) is based in <a href="/wiki/Charleston,_West_Virginia" title="Charleston, West Virginia">Charleston, West Virginia</a> and owns the naming rights to <a href="/wiki/Appalachian_Power_Park" title="Appalachian Power Park">Appalachian Power Park</a> in Charleston. APCO serves about one million customers in central and <a href="/wiki/Southern_West_Virginia" title="Southern West Virginia">Southern West Virginia</a>, the West Virginia Panhandle, <a href="/wiki/Southwest_Virginia" title="Southwest Virginia">Southwest Virginia</a> and parts of <a href="/wiki/Tri-Cities,_Tennessee" title="Tri-Cities, Tennessee">Northeast Tennessee</a>, specifically <a href="/wiki/Kingsport,_Tennessee" title="Kingsport, Tennessee">Kingsport</a>.  Cities in the Appalachian Power service territory include <a href="/wiki/Wheeling,_West_Virginia" title="Wheeling, West Virginia">Wheeling</a>, Charleston and <a href="/wiki/Huntington,_West_Virginia" title="Huntington, West Virginia">Huntington, West Virginia</a>; <a href="/wiki/Roanoke,_Virginia" title="Roanoke, Virginia">Roanoke, Virginia</a> and Kingsport.
</p><p>Until the 21st century, AEP's operations in Tennessee were part of a separate operating company, Kingsport Power Company.  However, since the turn of the millennium, Kingsport Power's operations have been almost completely merged with those of Appalachian Power. While Kingsport Power still legally exists, the Kingsport Power name is almost never used anymore except for regulatory formality. AEP considers Appalachian Power to be the operating company in Kingsport.
</p><p>Wheeling Power, based in Wheeling, was long treated as a <i>de facto</i> part of Ohio Power, but is now treated as part of Appalachian Power.
</p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Indiana_Michigan_Power">Indiana Michigan Power</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=American_Electric_Power&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Indiana Michigan Power" data-section="5" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>Indiana Michigan Power (I&amp;M) serves northeastern and east central Indiana, including <a href="/wiki/Muncie,_Indiana" title="Muncie, Indiana">Muncie</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fort_Wayne" class="mw-redirect" title="Fort Wayne">Fort Wayne</a>; and parts of north central Indiana and southwest Michigan, including <a href="/wiki/South_Bend" class="mw-redirect" title="South Bend">South Bend</a>, <a href="/wiki/St._Joseph,_Michigan" title="St. Joseph, Michigan">St. Joseph</a>, <a href="/wiki/Benton_Harbor" class="mw-redirect" title="Benton Harbor">Benton Harbor</a> and <a href="/wiki/Three_Rivers,_Michigan" title="Three Rivers, Michigan">Three Rivers</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Donald_C._Cook_Nuclear_Generating_Station" class="mw-redirect" title="Donald C. Cook Nuclear Generating Station">Donald C. Cook</a> <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_power_plant" title="Nuclear power plant">nuclear power plant</a> is located in I&amp;M's territory.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Kentucky_Power">Kentucky Power</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=American_Electric_Power&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Kentucky Power" data-section="6" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>Kentucky Power serves most of <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Kentucky" class="mw-redirect" title="Eastern Kentucky">Eastern Kentucky</a>, the area abutting the Appalachian Power service area, including communities of <a href="/wiki/Pikeville,_Kentucky" title="Pikeville, Kentucky">Pikeville</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hazard,_Kentucky" title="Hazard, Kentucky">Hazard</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ashland,_Kentucky" title="Ashland, Kentucky">Ashland</a>. Kentucky Power headquarters is in Ashland and they maintain a government relations office in <a href="/wiki/Frankfort,_Kentucky" title="Frankfort, Kentucky">Frankfort, Kentucky</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span id="Public_Service_Company_of_Oklahoma_.28PSO.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Public_Service_Company_of_Oklahoma_(PSO)">Public Service Company of Oklahoma (PSO)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=American_Electric_Power&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Public Service Company of Oklahoma (PSO)" data-section="7" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>PSO was one of the four CSW operating companies when CSW merged with AEP.<sup id="cite_ref-sec-csa_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sec-csa-11">[11]</a></sup> Incorporated in 1913, PSO serves approximately 540,000 customers in eastern and southwestern Oklahoma. Its headquarters are in Tulsa. PSO has 4,269 megawatts of generating capacity and provides electricity to 232 cities and towns across a service area encompassing 30,000 square miles.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p><p>In April 2014, Oklahoma Governor Mary Fallin signed into law an AEP-backed bill that would add a tax onto anyone in the state who adopted rooftop solar.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> This anti-solar legislation was headed by Rep. <a href="/wiki/Mike_Turner_(Oklahoma_politician)" title="Mike Turner (Oklahoma politician)">Mike Turner (Oklahoma politician)</a> (R. Edmond), known for both his support of ending rooftop solar and legislation he introduced to end all marriage in the state. After partnering with AEP to pass the legislation, Rep. Turner suffered a failed federal congressional race.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Southwestern_Electric_Power_Company">Southwestern Electric Power Company</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=American_Electric_Power&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Southwestern Electric Power Company" data-section="8" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>Often called SWEPCO, the Southwestern Electric Power Company serves most of western <a href="/wiki/Arkansas" title="Arkansas">Arkansas</a>, northwestern <a href="/wiki/Louisiana" title="Louisiana">Louisiana</a>, and northeastern <a href="/wiki/Texas" title="Texas">Texas</a>. Like PSO, it was one of the four CSW Operating Companies.<sup id="cite_ref-sec-csa_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sec-csa-11">[11]</a></sup></p><p>Between 2013 and 2014, AEP, under subsidiary SWEPCO, tried to eliminate a fundamental solar policy, net metering, at the LPSC several times, and failed each time. SWEPCO also backed openly anti-solar candidate Eric Skrmetta, who has been widely criticized for receiving 2/3 of his campaign contributions from entities he regulates.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p><p>In Arkansas, SWEPCO is expected in 2015 to ask regulators to allow them to pass along the costs of building and running the <a href="/wiki/John_W._Turk_Jr._Coal_Plant" title="John W. Turk Jr. Coal Plant">John W. Turk Jr. Coal Plant</a>, the most expensive project in state history, to Arkansas ratepayers. This process is expected to elicit similar controversy to what has plagued the plant since construction.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_subsidiaries">Other subsidiaries</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=American_Electric_Power&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Other subsidiaries" data-section="9" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>AEP also bought much of the town of <a href="/wiki/Cheshire,_Ohio" title="Cheshire, Ohio">Cheshire, Ohio</a>, where the <a href="/wiki/Gavin_Power_Plant" title="Gavin Power Plant">Gavin Power Plant</a> is located, due to pollution issues.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup>
In 2004, AEP announced their plans to build one, or more, <a href="/wiki/Integrated_gasification_combined_cycle" title="Integrated gasification combined cycle">integrated gasification combined cycle</a> (IGCC) coal-fired power plant which is expected to reduce emissions while providing additional electricity capacity to the customers served by AEP.<sup id="cite_ref-American-Electric-Power-Aug-2004-8-K_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-American-Electric-Power-Aug-2004-8-K-18">[18]</a></sup></p><p>The company also operates its own inland <a href="/wiki/Barge" title="Barge">barge</a> line, AEP River Operations (formerly MEMCO Barge Line), and owns major tracts of land throughout its service areas.
</p><p>In August 2008, AEP has formed a joint venture company with <a href="/wiki/Duke_Energy" title="Duke Energy">Duke Energy</a> to build and own new electric transmission assets.<sup id="cite_ref-AEP_And_Duke_To_Form_Joint_Venture_To_Build_Transmission_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AEP_And_Duke_To_Form_Joint_Venture_To_Build_Transmission-19">[19]</a></sup></p><p>It is the largest shareholder in the <a href="/wiki/Ohio_Valley_Electric_Corporation" title="Ohio Valley Electric Corporation">Ohio Valley Electric Corporation</a> (OVEC). OVEC is an <a href="/wiki/Associate_company" title="Associate company">associate company</a> of AEP, not a subsidiary, because AEP owns less than half of it.
</p>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(2)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Nuclear">Nuclear</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=American_Electric_Power&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Nuclear" data-section="10" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-2 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-2">
<p>AEP owns and operates the <a href="/wiki/Donald_C._Cook_Nuclear_Generating_Station" class="mw-redirect" title="Donald C. Cook Nuclear Generating Station">Donald C. Cook</a> <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_power_plant" title="Nuclear power plant">nuclear power plant</a>.  This accounts for 6% of the generation portfolio.
</p>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(3)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Hybrid_technology">Hybrid technology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=American_Electric_Power&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Hybrid technology" data-section="11" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-3 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-3">
<p>AEP is expanding its green efforts to include 18 more <a href="/wiki/International_DuraStar" title="International DuraStar">International DuraStar</a> hybrid diesel trucks.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> AEP is also teaming with <a href="/wiki/Ford" class="mw-redirect" title="Ford">Ford</a> for the integration of a Vehicle-To-Power grid communication system, which allows hybrid vehicles to communicate with power companies to determine where, how long, and what it would cost to re-charge a hybrid during travel.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(4)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Solar_and_wind_energy">Solar and wind energy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=American_Electric_Power&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Solar and wind energy" data-section="12" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-4 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-4">
<p>In 2009, AEP signed a deal with Wyandot Solar LLC to purchase power from one of the largest solar fields in the eastern United States of America, based in <a href="/wiki/Upper_Sandusky,_Ohio" title="Upper Sandusky, Ohio">Upper Sandusky, Ohio</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p><p>AEP owns and operates the <a href="/wiki/Desert_Sky_Wind_Farm" title="Desert Sky Wind Farm">Desert Sky Wind Farm</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Trent_Wind_Farm" title="Trent Wind Farm">Trent Wind Farm</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p><p>In 2009, AEP partnered with other energy companies in commissioning a study of how to transmit wind energy generated in the Upper Midwest to consumers in the East.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p><p>In 2017, Invenergy and GE announced plans for the $4.5 billion, 2,000 MW Wind Catcher (a/k/a Windcatcher) project on a 300,000-acre site in <a href="/wiki/Cimarron_County,_Oklahoma" title="Cimarron County, Oklahoma">Cimarron</a> and <a href="/wiki/Texas_County,_Oklahoma" title="Texas County, Oklahoma">Texas</a> counties in the <a href="/wiki/Oklahoma_Panhandle" title="Oklahoma Panhandle">Oklahoma Panhandle</a>, which would have been among the world's largest wind farms when completed in 2020.<sup id="cite_ref-GE_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GE-25">[25]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Windcatcher_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Windcatcher-26">[26]</a></sup>  AEP utility subsidiaries PSO and SWEPCO asked utility regulators in Louisiana, Arkansas, Texas and Oklahoma to approve plans to purchase the wind farm from Invenergy upon completion of construction.<sup id="cite_ref-GE_25-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GE-25">[25]</a></sup>  However, the project ran into opposition and was finally cancelled in July 2018.<sup id="cite_ref-Windcatcher_26-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Windcatcher-26">[26]</a></sup></p><p>PSO was approved in early 2020 by regulators in Oklahoma as well as Arkansas to own a 45.5% share of a massive 1,485 megawatt wind project known as the North Central Energy Facilities, with SWEPCO owning the rest.<sup id="cite_ref-NorthCen_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NorthCen-27">[27]</a></sup>  The project includes three wind farms covering areas in <a href="/wiki/Alfalfa_County,_Oklahoma" title="Alfalfa County, Oklahoma">Alfalfa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Blaine_County,_Oklahoma" title="Blaine County, Oklahoma">Blaine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Custer_County,_Oklahoma" title="Custer County, Oklahoma">Custer</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kingfisher_County,_Oklahoma" title="Kingfisher County, Oklahoma">Kingfisher</a>, <a href="/wiki/Garfield_County,_Oklahoma" title="Garfield County, Oklahoma">Garfield</a>, <a href="/wiki/Major_County,_Oklahoma" title="Major County, Oklahoma">Major</a> and <a href="/wiki/Woods_County,_Oklahoma" title="Woods County, Oklahoma">Woods</a> counties of Oklahoma.<sup id="cite_ref-NorthCen_27-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NorthCen-27">[27]</a></sup>  Hearings still need to be held in Texas and Louisiana.<sup id="cite_ref-NorthCen_27-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NorthCen-27">[27]</a></sup>  While the abandoned Wind Catcher plan envisioned a new 765-kilovolt transmission line, which would have run hundreds of miles in Oklahoma, the North Central facilities are near an existing PSO/SWEPCO transmission system.<sup id="cite_ref-E&amp;E_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-E&amp;E-28">[28]</a></sup>   Also, the project is said to be scalable, so that states which approve the project would have the ability to increase the number of megawatts allocated to them should another state reject the proposal, as long as a minimum of 810 MW is committed.<sup id="cite_ref-E&amp;E_28-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-E&amp;E-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(5)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="PATH_Proposal">PATH Proposal</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=American_Electric_Power&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: PATH Proposal" data-section="13" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-5 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-5">
<p>In 2007, AEP teamed with <a href="/wiki/Allegheny_Energy" title="Allegheny Energy">Allegheny Energy</a> to propose the US$1.8 billion<sup id="cite_ref-American-Electric-Power-Nov-2007-10-Q_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-American-Electric-Power-Nov-2007-10-Q-29">[29]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Allegheny-Energy-Feb-2011-10-K_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Allegheny-Energy-Feb-2011-10-K-30">[30]</a></sup> (changed to US$2.1 billion in 2011<sup id="cite_ref-American-Electric-Power-Mar-2011-DEF-14A_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-American-Electric-Power-Mar-2011-DEF-14A-31">[31]</a></sup>) <a href="/wiki/Potomac-Appalachian_Transmission_Highline" title="Potomac-Appalachian Transmission Highline">Potomac-Appalachian Transmission Highline</a> (PATH), a 290-mile (470 km), 765  kilovolt transmission line that would run through West Virginia, Virginia, and Maryland. According to Joe Denault, a volunteer spokesperson for the proposal, the PATH proposal would incorporate new technology to reduce carbon dioxide emissions by 380,000 short tons (340,000 t) a year; allow for the transmission of renewable energy sources, such as wind, solar, and hydroelectric; and generate 5,700 jobs, with $420 million in employee compensation annually.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> However, many of these claims are disputed<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> and the proposal must pass several legal hurdles before moving forward.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> On August 24, 2012, PJM Interconnection officially removed the PATH project from its long-range expansion plans, citing a slow economy for reducing the projected growth in electricity use.<sup id="cite_ref-Jersey-Central-Power-Light-Dec-2012-10-Q_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jersey-Central-Power-Light-Dec-2012-10-Q-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(6)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=American_Electric_Power&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: History" data-section="14" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-6 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-6">
<p>The <b>American Gas and Electric Company</b>, which would be renamed American Electric Power in 1958, was incorporated in 1906.<sup id="cite_ref-history_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-history-36">[36]</a></sup> It replaced Electric Company of America, a holding company that had existed since 1899. It built "the first plant in the world to reheat steam to do double duty in the process of generating electricity"<sup id="cite_ref-history_36-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-history-36">[36]</a></sup> at the <a href="/wiki/Philo_Power_Plant" title="Philo Power Plant">Philo Power Plant</a> in Philo, Ohio in 1923. Several of its holdings were divested following the passage of the Public Utility Holding Company Act in 1935; these holdings would include Atlantic City Electric (now a subsidiary of <a href="/wiki/Exelon" title="Exelon">Exelon</a>) and Scranton Electric (since absorbed by <a href="/wiki/PPL_Corporation" title="PPL Corporation">PPL</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> However, it retained its Central System, which ran between Michigan and Virginia. It moved its headquarters from New York City to Columbus, Ohio in the 1980s. On May 1, 1999, The North American electric power industry tested and cleared 75 percent of the U.S. electricity system for compliance with the <a href="/wiki/Year_2000_problem" title="Year 2000 problem">Year 2000 computer glitch</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> On August 31, 2004,  American Electric Power Company's $10 billion acquisition of the Central and South West Corporation was approved.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Acquisitions">Acquisitions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=American_Electric_Power&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Acquisitions" data-section="15" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>

<li>1924 American Electric Power Company, a Philadelphia holding company</li>
<li>1925 Appalachian Power Company</li>
<li>1948 Indiana Service Corporation of Fort Wayne</li>
<li>1980 Columbus and Southern Ohio Electric Company</li>
<li>1997 Central and South West Corporation (completed in 2000)<sup id="cite_ref-American-Electric-Power-Jun-2000-8-K_40-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-American-Electric-Power-Jun-2000-8-K-40">[40]</a></sup></li></ul></section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(7)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Ownership">Ownership</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=American_Electric_Power&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Ownership" data-section="16" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-7 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-7">
<p>As of 2017 American Electric Power shares are mainly held by institutional investors, and large <a href="/w/index.php?title=Single_Family_Offices&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Single Family Offices (page does not exist)">Single Family Offices</a> (<a href="/wiki/Vanguard_group" class="mw-redirect" title="Vanguard group">Vanguard group</a>, <a href="/wiki/BlackRock" title="BlackRock">BlackRock</a>, <a href="/wiki/State_Street_Corporation" title="State Street Corporation">State Street Corporation</a>, and others<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup>)
</p>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(8)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Environmental_record">Environmental record</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=American_Electric_Power&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Environmental record" data-section="17" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-8 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-8">
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Political_Economy_Research_Institute" title="Political Economy Research Institute">Political Economy Research Institute</a> ranks American Electric Power 45th among corporations emitting airborne pollutants in the United States. The ranking is based on the quantity (91,000,000 pounds (41,000 t) in 2005) and toxicity of the emissions.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> Major pollutants include <a href="/wiki/Sulfuric_acid" title="Sulfuric acid">sulfuric</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hydrochloric_acid" title="Hydrochloric acid">hydrochloric</a> acids, and <a href="/wiki/Chromium" title="Chromium">chromium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Manganese" title="Manganese">manganese</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nickel" title="Nickel">nickel</a> compounds.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> Overall, electric power plants, such as those operated by AEP, account for almost "70 percent of sulfur dioxide emissions each year and 30 percent of nitrogen oxides emissions." Individually, these pollutants cause serious respiratory damage and other illnesses; when combined, they create what's known as <a href="/wiki/Acid_rain" title="Acid rain">acid rain</a>, which causes long term damage to the environment and deterioration of natural and man-made structures.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/United_States_Environmental_Protection_Agency" title="United States Environmental Protection Agency">Environmental Protection Agency</a> has named American Electric a potentially responsible party at the Green River Disposal Inc. <a href="/wiki/Superfund" title="Superfund">Superfund</a> toxic waste site.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup></p><p>In February 2018, AEP announced plans to reduce carbon emissions 80 percent by 2050 through increased use of renewables, natural gas and energy efficiency.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup></p><p>In August 2018, AEP started a program in Ohio that incentivizes the installation of electric vehicle infrastructure by investing $10 million for the installation of up to 375 electric vehicle charging stations. According to Utility DIVE, "The program is part of a wave of EV charging rollouts utilities are spearheading in an effort to spur EV adoption."<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Justice_Department_lawsuit">Justice Department lawsuit</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=American_Electric_Power&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Justice Department lawsuit" data-section="18" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>The United States Justice Department filed a lawsuit on November 3, 1999, against AEP and six other companies for violating the <a href="/wiki/Clean_Air_Act_(United_States)" class="mw-redirect" title="Clean Air Act (United States)">Clean Air Act</a>. On October 8, 2007, AEP agreed to install US$4.6 billion in equipment to reduce emission, as well as pay a US$15 million civil fine and provide US$36 million for environmental projects and $24 million for environmental mitigation.<sup id="cite_ref-American-Electric-Power-Oct-2007-8-K_48-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-American-Electric-Power-Oct-2007-8-K-48">[48]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup>
The company will cut 813,000 short tons (738,000 t) of air pollutants annually once all of the controls are installed.<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> According to the press release, the agreement imposes caps on emissions of pollutants from 16 plants located in five states.  The facilities are located in <a href="/wiki/Moundsville,_West_Virginia" title="Moundsville, West Virginia">Moundsville</a> (two facilities), <a href="/wiki/St._Albans,_West_Virginia" title="St. Albans, West Virginia">St. Albans</a>, <a href="/wiki/Glasgow,_West_Virginia" title="Glasgow, West Virginia">Glasgow</a>, and <a href="/wiki/New_Haven,_West_Virginia" title="New Haven, West Virginia">New Haven</a> (two facilities), <a href="/wiki/West_Virginia" title="West Virginia">West Virginia</a>; <a href="/wiki/Louisa,_Kentucky" title="Louisa, Kentucky">Louisa, Kentucky</a>;  <a href="/wiki/Glen_Lyn,_Virginia" title="Glen Lyn, Virginia">Glen Lyn</a> and Carbo, <a href="/wiki/Virginia" title="Virginia">Virginia</a>; <a href="/wiki/Brilliant,_Ohio" title="Brilliant, Ohio">Brilliant</a>, <a href="/wiki/Conesville,_Ohio" title="Conesville, Ohio">Conesville</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cheshire,_Ohio" title="Cheshire, Ohio">Cheshire</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lockbourne,_Ohio" title="Lockbourne, Ohio">Lockbourne</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Beverly,_Ohio" title="Beverly, Ohio">Beverly</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ohio" title="Ohio">Ohio</a>; and <a href="/wiki/Rockport,_Indiana" title="Rockport, Indiana">Rockport</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lawrenceburg,_Indiana" title="Lawrenceburg, Indiana">Lawrenceburg</a>, <a href="/wiki/Indiana" title="Indiana">Indiana</a>.
</p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Attempt_to_Halt_Solar_Energy">Attempt to Halt Solar Energy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=American_Electric_Power&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Attempt to Halt Solar Energy" data-section="19" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>AEP has come under criticism in many of the states they operate in for attacking rooftop solar. They have specifically attempted to halt distributed solar in Louisiana, Arkansas, Oklahoma, West Virginia, Indiana, Kentucky, and Ohio.<sup id="cite_ref-Morgan_County_USA_51-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Morgan_County_USA-51">[51]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-CleanTechnica_52-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CleanTechnica-52">[52]</a></sup></p>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(9)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Political_influence">Political influence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=American_Electric_Power&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Political influence" data-section="20" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-9 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-9">
<p>AEP's political action committee, the American Electric Power Committee for Responsible Government, has increased spending since the 1998 election cycle, reaching $1.4 million in contributions in 2007–2008.<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup> Also in 2008, American Electric Power significantly increased lobbying expenditures from less than $2 million a year to over $11 million, as climate legislation became a key issue in Washington. As of December 2011, lobbying expenses remained between $6 million and $10 million per year.<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup></p><p>In 2009, AEP CEO <a href="/wiki/Michael_G._Morris" title="Michael G. Morris">Michael G. Morris</a> contributed $100,000 to Newt Gingrich's <a href="/wiki/American_Solutions_for_Winning_the_Future" title="American Solutions for Winning the Future">American Solutions for Winning the Future</a>, which supports increased oil drilling and opposes mandatory limits on greenhouse gas pollution.<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup> American Electric Power is also a member of the <a href="/wiki/American_Coalition_for_Clean_Coal_Electricity" title="American Coalition for Clean Coal Electricity">American Coalition for Clean Coal Electricity</a>, a lobbying and marketing organization which opposes former President Obama's climate and clean energy legislation.<sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup></p><p>Paul Loeffelman, AEP director of environmental public policy, served as co-chair of the <a href="/wiki/ALEC" class="mw-redirect" title="ALEC">ALEC</a> Energy, Environment, and Agriculture task force, which in 2012 adopted model legislation entitled the "Electricity Freedom Act", designed to repeal state-level mandates requiring electric utilities to produce some of their electricity from renewable sources.<sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup> In 2013, ALEC-affiliated legislators introduced bills in 13 states designed to weaken or repeal renewable energy mandates. None of the bills passed in 2013.<sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup></p>
