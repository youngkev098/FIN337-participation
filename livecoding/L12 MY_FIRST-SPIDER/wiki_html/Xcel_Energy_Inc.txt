</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(1)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Xcel_Energy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History" data-section="1" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-1 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-1">
<p>Xcel Energy was built on three companies: Minneapolis-based Northern States Power Company (NSP), Denver-based Public Service Company of Colorado (PSCo), and Amarillo-based Southwestern Public Service (SPS). Southwestern Public Service Co. (SPS) dates its origins to 1904 and the Pecos Valley in New Mexico when Maynard Gunsell received an electricity franchise for the city of <a href="/wiki/Roswell,_New_Mexico" title="Roswell, New Mexico">Roswell, New Mexico</a> and its 2,000 residents. The financial strain of creating this new enterprise soon overwhelmed him and he sold the franchise to W.H. Gillenwater, who named his utility the Roswell Electric Light Co. He later sold the company to an investment firm in Cleveland, Ohio, which already owned the Roswell Gas Co.<sup id="cite_ref-Southwestern_Public_Service_Company_History_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Southwestern_Public_Service_Company_History-6">[6]</a></sup></p><p>Northern States Power Company's timeline begins with the organization of the Washington County Light &amp; Power Co. in 1909. When H. M. Byllesby began building his utility holding company across the Northwestern region of the US, he renamed it the Consumers Power Co. in 1910 and which was renamed the Northern States Power Co. in 1916.<sup id="cite_ref-Minnesota_Historical_Society_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Minnesota_Historical_Society-7">[7]</a></sup> While the bulk of NSP's territory grew across central and southern Minnesota (starting from the Twin Cities), it acquired territory in North Dakota (centering on Fargo, Grand Forks, and Minot) and extended southwest into South Dakota (centering on Sioux Falls). NSP's system also extended east into Wisconsin, but because of utility ownership laws in that state, it was operated as an entity separate from the rest of the company.
</p><p>Public Service Company of Colorado (PSCo) was formed in 1923 to provide an electric generating station for the Denver area.  By 1924, it had acquired most of the electric companies in northern and central Colorado. Originally a subsidiary of Cities Service Company, it became an independent autonomous operation in November 1943.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>  By this time, it served 80 percent of Colorado’s gas and electricity needs. As demand for energy continued to grow, so did PSCo.  Eventually, the company merged with SPS to form <b>New Century Energies (NCE)</b> in 1995.<sup id="cite_ref-New-Century-Energies-Dec-1995-S-4_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-New-Century-Energies-Dec-1995-S-4-9">[9]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p><p>Northern States Power and <a href="/wiki/Wisconsin_Energy_Corporation" class="mw-redirect" title="Wisconsin Energy Corporation">Wisconsin Energy Corporation</a> had planned to merge into a new outfit that was to be called Primergy<sup id="cite_ref-Northern-States-Power-Company-May-1995-8-K_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Northern-States-Power-Company-May-1995-8-K-11">[11]</a></sup> - but in 1997, the merger fell through because of the time it was taking to gain the required approvals from state and federal agencies.<sup id="cite_ref-Northern-States-Power-Company-May-1997-8-K_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Northern-States-Power-Company-May-1997-8-K-12">[12]</a></sup> After the failed Primergy merger, NSP (both the Minnesota and Wisconsin companies) merged with New Century Energies to form Xcel Energy.<sup id="cite_ref-Xcel-Energy-Jun-1999-8-K_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Xcel-Energy-Jun-1999-8-K-13">[13]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup>  In 2005, Xcel sold Cheyenne Light, Fuel and Power to <a href="/wiki/Black_Hills_Corporation" title="Black Hills Corporation">Black Hills Corporation</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Xcel-Energy-Feb-2005-35-CERT_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Xcel-Energy-Feb-2005-35-CERT-15">[15]</a></sup>  Cheyenne Light, Fuel and Power had been a subsidiary of PSCo since the 1920s, and had become an operating company of NCE after the merger with SPS.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Xcel_Energy_Cabin_Creek_Fire" title="Xcel Energy Cabin Creek Fire">The Cabin Creek Fire</a> occurred on October 2, 2007 at Xcel Energy’s <a href="/wiki/Hydropower" title="Hydropower">Hydropower</a> Generation plant in <a href="/wiki/Georgetown,_Colorado" title="Georgetown, Colorado">Georgetown, Colorado</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Xcel-Energy-Oct-2007-10-Q_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Xcel-Energy-Oct-2007-10-Q-16">[16]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Investigators_Trace_Fatal_Colorado_Fire_to_Sealant_Device_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Investigators_Trace_Fatal_Colorado_Fire_to_Sealant_Device-17">[17]</a></sup> On June 1, 2011, Federal prosecutors opened their charges that Xcel Energy was criminally liable for the deaths of the five RPI workers. On June 28, the jury found Xcel Energy <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.huffingtonpost.com/2011/06/29/xcel-energy-acquitted-of-_n_886723.html">not guilty</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Northern-States-Power-Company-Jul-2011-10-Q_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Northern-States-Power-Company-Jul-2011-10-Q-18">[18]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Xcel_Energy_Acquitted_Of_Criminal_Charges_For_Five_Deaths_Of_Workers_At_Cabin_Creek_Hydroelectric_Plant_Fire_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Xcel_Energy_Acquitted_Of_Criminal_Charges_For_Five_Deaths_Of_Workers_At_Cabin_Creek_Hydroelectric_Plant_Fire-19">[19]</a></sup> On December 19, 2011, RPI Coating pleaded <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.denverpost.com/news/ci_19582334?IADID">guilty</a> to workplace safety violations and paid $1.55 million in a cash settlement. The company took responsibility for the deaths of five workers and the injuries to three.<sup id="cite_ref-RPI_to_pay_$1.55_million_in_2007_Colorado_power_plant_fire_that_killed_5_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RPI_to_pay_%241.55_million_in_2007_Colorado_power_plant_fire_that_killed_5-20">[20]</a></sup></p><p>In December 2018, Xcel Energy became the first major US utility to pledge to go <a href="/wiki/Renewable_energy" title="Renewable energy">carbon-free</a>, aiming for 80% <a href="/wiki/Carbon_neutrality" title="Carbon neutrality">carbon reduction</a> by 2030, and 100% reduction by 2050.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-21">[21]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p><p>Utility industry magazine Utility Dive awarded Xcel Energy its 2018 "Utility of the Year" award for its plans for add 12 <a href="/wiki/Wind_farms" class="mw-redirect" title="Wind farms">wind farms</a>, its project with <a href="/wiki/Google" title="Google">Google</a> to develop new ways for customers to personalize <a href="/wiki/Energy_management" title="Energy management">energy management</a>, and its plan to retire 50 percent of its coal-powered capacity by 2026 (and replacing it with a combination of renewable energy, efficiency, and natural gas).<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p><p>On May 20, 2019, Xcel Energy announced its intent to close all of its remaining coal-fired plants in Minnesota by 2030 while compensating by increasing solar production capacity by 1,400%. It also declared its plans to continue operating its <a href="/wiki/Monticello_Nuclear_Generating_Plant" title="Monticello Nuclear Generating Plant">Monticello nuclear plant</a> near <a href="/wiki/Monticello,_Minnesota" title="Monticello, Minnesota">Monticello, Minnesota</a> until at least 2040.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(2)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Generation_portfolio">Generation portfolio</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Xcel_Energy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Generation portfolio" data-section="2" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-2 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-2">
<p>Xcel Energy currently has 13 coal plants with a capacity of 7,697 MW. Seven of those plants are operated in Colorado.<sup id="cite_ref-Xcel’s_Coal_Plants_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Xcel%E2%80%99s_Coal_Plants-26">[26]</a></sup> Xcel Energy owns and operates three wind farms. In October 2011, Xcel Energy set a world record for electricity from wind power, with an hourly penetration of 55.6% of production from wind.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p><p>Xcel Energy generates over 500 megawatts of <a href="/wiki/Hydroelectric_power" class="mw-redirect" title="Hydroelectric power">hydroelectric power</a> from 27 plants in Wisconsin, Minnesota, and Colorado. This accounts for only four percent of their electricity generation. They also purchase large amounts of hydro-generated electricity from <a href="/wiki/Manitoba_Hydro" title="Manitoba Hydro">Manitoba Hydro</a>.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Xcel_Energy_Bay_Front_Power_Plant_(a).jpg" class="image"><noscript><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cc/Xcel_Energy_Bay_Front_Power_Plant_%28a%29.jpg/250px-Xcel_Energy_Bay_Front_Power_Plant_%28a%29.jpg" decoding="async" width="250" height="188" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="3648" data-file-height="2736"></noscript><span class="lazy-image-placeholder" style="width: 250px;height: 188px;" data-src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cc/Xcel_Energy_Bay_Front_Power_Plant_%28a%29.jpg/250px-Xcel_Energy_Bay_Front_Power_Plant_%28a%29.jpg" data-alt="" data-width="250" data-height="188" data-class="thumbimage"> </span></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Xcel_Energy_Bay_Front_Power_Plant_(a).jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The Bay Front Power Plant in Wisconsin.</div></div></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Biomass" title="Biomass">Biomass</a> electricity comes from organic fuel sources. Xcel Energy has contracts for about 110 megawatts of electricity from biomass generators. Two in northern Minnesota are fueled by forest harvest residue, such as treetops and limbs. A third facility, brought on line in 2007 in western Minnesota, generates power using turkey litter.<sup id="cite_ref-xcelenergy.com_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-xcelenergy.com-28">[28]</a></sup></p><p>Xcel Energy's Bay Front plant in <a href="/wiki/Ashland,_Wisconsin" title="Ashland, Wisconsin">Ashland, Wisconsin</a>, is a three-unit generating station that has become a model for the creative use of fuels: coal, waste wood, railroad ties, discarded tires, <a href="/wiki/Natural_gas" title="Natural gas">natural gas</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Petroleum_coke" title="Petroleum coke">petroleum coke</a>.  Two of the three Bay Front operating units already use biomass as their primary fuel.  Xcel Energy recently proposed a plan to install biomass gasification technology at Bay Front. The <a href="/wiki/Waste-to-energy" title="Waste-to-energy">waste-to-energy</a> facilities use waste that would otherwise end up in <a href="/wiki/Landfills" class="mw-redirect" title="Landfills">landfills</a>.
The Wisconsin waste-to-energy plant burns wood waste in combination with <a href="/wiki/Refuse-derived_fuel" title="Refuse-derived fuel">refuse-derived fuel</a> (RDF).
</p><p>Xcel Energy owns and operates two <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_power_plant" title="Nuclear power plant">nuclear power plants</a>: 
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Monticello_Nuclear_Generating_Plant" title="Monticello Nuclear Generating Plant">Monticello Nuclear Generating Plant</a> near <a href="/wiki/Monticello,_Minnesota" title="Monticello, Minnesota">Monticello, Minnesota</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Prairie_Island_Nuclear_Generating_Plant" class="mw-redirect" title="Prairie Island Nuclear Generating Plant">Prairie Island Nuclear Generating Plant</a> near <a href="/wiki/Red_Wing,_Minnesota" title="Red Wing, Minnesota">Red Wing, Minnesota</a></li></ul><p>and stores the spent fuel from these nuclear plants on site in independent spent fuel storage installations. (ISFSIs).<sup id="cite_ref-nuclear_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nuclear-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(3)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="System_information_and_transmission">System information and transmission</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Xcel_Energy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: System information and transmission" data-section="3" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-3 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-3">
<p>Xcel Energy operates the fourth largest transmission system in the United States, spanning 10 states. In 2011, Xcel Energy’s transmission system was worth $3.3 billion.<sup id="cite_ref-Transmission_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Transmission-30">[30]</a></sup></p><p>The transmission system is operated on a non-discriminatory basis under the open access requirements of the <a href="/wiki/Federal_government_of_the_United_States" title="Federal government of the United States">Federal Government</a>. This means that all wholesale buyers and sellers of electricity can use the transmission system under the same terms and conditions used to serve Xcel Energy’s own retail customers.<sup id="cite_ref-xcelenergy.com_28-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-xcelenergy.com-28">[28]</a></sup>  The transmission lines are utilized to carry 115,000 volts, 230,000 volts, and 345,000 volts.  There is also a 500,000 volt transmission line that runs from the Dorsey Substation outside of <a href="/wiki/Winnipeg,_Manitoba" class="mw-redirect" title="Winnipeg, Manitoba">Winnipeg, Manitoba</a> in <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a> to the Chisago Substation located in <a href="/wiki/Chisago_County" class="mw-redirect" title="Chisago County">Chisago County</a> just north of <a href="/wiki/St._Paul,_Minnesota" class="mw-redirect" title="St. Paul, Minnesota">St. Paul, Minnesota</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-xcelenergy.com_28-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-xcelenergy.com-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(4)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Grid_security">Grid security</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Xcel_Energy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Grid security" data-section="4" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-4 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-4">
<p>In 2017, Xcel Energy partnered with the Financial Services Information Sharing and Analysis Center to create a new "threat information sharing community" intended to share cyber and physical security intelligence with the energy sector. The new community is called the Energy Analytic Security Exchange (EASE). It is run by the FS-ISAC Sector Services team; FS-ISAC is an organization that gathers cyber and physical risk intelligence for the financial services industry. Additionally, the <a href="/wiki/North_American_Electric_Reliability_Corporation" title="North American Electric Reliability Corporation">North American Electric Reliability Council</a> (NERC) manages the Electricity Information Sharing and Analysis Center, which is another resource that the energy sector uses to gather threat intelligence.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Advanced_Grid_in_Colorado">Advanced Grid in Colorado</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Xcel_Energy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Advanced Grid in Colorado" data-section="5" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>In 2016, Xcel Energy announced the Advanced Grid Intelligence and Security (AGIS) initiative, a long-term effort related to power reliability, distributed generation, and information sharing with customers. Through the initiative, Xcel would build an "intelligent grid" in Colorado in order to improve grid security. The company filed a request for permission with the <a href="/wiki/Colorado_Public_Utilities_Commission" title="Colorado Public Utilities Commission">Colorado Public Utilities Commission</a> for the program, which would cost $500 million.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(5)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Programs">Programs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Xcel_Energy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Programs" data-section="6" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-5 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-5">
<p>Since 1998, Xcel Energy's Windsource program has allowed customers to designate that part or all of their electricity comes from a <a href="/wiki/Renewable_energy" title="Renewable energy">renewable energy</a> source. In 2015, about 96,000 people were enrolled in Windsource.<sup id="cite_ref-Xcel-Energy-Inc-Feb-2016-10-K_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Xcel-Energy-Inc-Feb-2016-10-K-33">[33]</a></sup> In 2011, more than 2.3 million electric and 261,800 natural gas customers took part in Xcel Energy’s energy efficiency programs for homes and businesses.<sup id="cite_ref-2011_Energy_Efficiency_Program_Results_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2011_Energy_Efficiency_Program_Results-34">[34]</a></sup></p><p>Xcel Energy also offers customers incentives to install solar panels. At the end of 2011, more than 10,600 photovoltaic systems had been installed, with a capacity of about 121 megawatts (DC). In early 2011, Xcel Energy suspended the solar rebate program before reaching a settlement a month later with representatives of solar power companies to restore the solar incentive program until it is fully reviewed by the Public Utilities Commission.<sup id="cite_ref-Colorado_PUC_OKs_Xcel_solar_incentives_settlement_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Colorado_PUC_OKs_Xcel_solar_incentives_settlement-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(6)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Controversies">Controversies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Xcel_Energy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Controversies" data-section="7" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-6 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-6">
<p>On August 1, 2002, Xcel Energy Inc. was sued because of engaging in "round-trip" energy trades that provided no economic benefit for the company, and because the company lacked the necessary internal controls to adequately monitor the trading of its power.<sup id="cite_ref-Northern-States-Power-Company-Nov-2002-8-K_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Northern-States-Power-Company-Nov-2002-8-K-36">[36]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> Xcel paid $80,000,000 in a settlement.<sup id="cite_ref-Northern-States-Power-Company-Jun-2007-11-K_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Northern-States-Power-Company-Jun-2007-11-K-38">[38]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Northern-States-Power-Company-Jan-2005-8-K_39-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Northern-States-Power-Company-Jan-2005-8-K-39">[39]</a></sup></p><p>In early October 2007, a <a href="/wiki/Xcel_Energy_Cabin_Creek_Fire" title="Xcel Energy Cabin Creek Fire">flash fire</a> within a penstock at Xcel Energy's hydroelectric plant at Cabin Creek occurred which resulted in the death of 5 contract workers.<sup id="cite_ref-Northern-States-Power-Company-Dec-2007-10-Q/A_40-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Northern-States-Power-Company-Dec-2007-10-Q/A-40">[40]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/U.S._Chemical_Safety_and_Hazard_Investigation_Board" title="U.S. Chemical Safety and Hazard Investigation Board">U.S. Chemical Safety and Hazard Investigation Board</a> conducted an investigation of the incident. Its report can be found on the CSB website. It is considered one of the worst unmitigated incidents to occur in a permit required confined space.
</p><p>In 2011, Xcel Energy threatened to cut solar and efficiency rebates in <a href="/wiki/Boulder,_Colorado" title="Boulder, Colorado">Boulder, Colorado</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_21-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-21">[21]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup></p>
