<p class="mw-empty-elt">
</p>
<p><b>Consolidated Edison, Inc.</b>, commonly known as <b>Con Edison</b> (stylized as <b>conEdison</b>) or <b>ConEd</b>, is one of the largest <a href="/wiki/Investor-owned_utility" title="Investor-owned utility">investor-owned</a> <a href="/wiki/Energy_company" class="mw-redirect" title="Energy company">energy companies</a> in the United States, with approximately $12 billion in annual revenues as of 2017, and over $48 billion in assets.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> The company provides a wide range of energy-related products and services to its customers through its subsidiaries:
</p><ul><li><b>Consolidated Edison Company of New York, Inc.</b>, <b>(CECONY)</b>, a <a href="/wiki/Public_utilities_commission" title="Public utilities commission">regulated utility</a> providing electric and gas service in <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a> and <a href="/wiki/Westchester_County,_New_York" title="Westchester County, New York">Westchester County, New York</a>, and steam service in the borough of <a href="/wiki/Manhattan" title="Manhattan">Manhattan</a>;</li>
<li><b>Orange and Rockland Utilities, Inc.</b>, a regulated utility serving customers in a 1,300-square-mile (3,400 km<sup>2</sup>) area in southeastern <a href="/wiki/New_York_(state)" title="New York (state)">New York</a> and northern <a href="/wiki/New_Jersey" title="New Jersey">New Jersey</a>;</li>
<li><b>Con Edison Solutions</b>, an energy services company;</li>
<li><b>Con Edison Energy</b>, a wholesale energy services company;</li>
<li><b>Con Edison Development</b>, a company that owns and operates renewable and energy infrastructure projects, and,</li>

<p>In 2015, electric revenues accounted for 70.35% of consolidated sales (70.55% in 2014); gas revenues 13.61% (14.96% in 2014); steam revenues 5.01% (4.86% in 2014); and non-utility revenues of 11.02% (9.63 in 2014%).<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(1)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Consolidated_Edison&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History" data-section="1" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-1 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-1">
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:169px;"><a href="/wiki/File:PearlStreetStation.jpg" class="image"><noscript><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/58/PearlStreetStation.jpg/167px-PearlStreetStation.jpg" decoding="async" width="167" height="256" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="250" data-file-height="383"></noscript><span class="lazy-image-placeholder" style="width: 167px;height: 256px;" data-src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/58/PearlStreetStation.jpg/167px-PearlStreetStation.jpg" data-alt="" data-width="167" data-height="256" data-class="thumbimage"> </span></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:PearlStreetStation.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A sketch of an early power plant on <a href="/wiki/Pearl_Street_(Manhattan)" title="Pearl Street (Manhattan)">Pearl Street</a></div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:169px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Steam_Rising_from_New_York_City_Streets.JPG" class="image"><noscript><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/36/Steam_Rising_from_New_York_City_Streets.JPG/167px-Steam_Rising_from_New_York_City_Streets.JPG" decoding="async" width="167" height="223" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="1920" data-file-height="2560"></noscript><span class="lazy-image-placeholder" style="width: 167px;height: 223px;" data-src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/36/Steam_Rising_from_New_York_City_Streets.JPG/167px-Steam_Rising_from_New_York_City_Streets.JPG" data-alt="" data-width="167" data-height="223" data-class="thumbimage"> </span></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Steam_Rising_from_New_York_City_Streets.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>As well as gas and electricity, Con Ed supplies steam to New York City</div></div></div>
<p>In 1823, Con Edison's earliest corporate predecessor, the New York Gas Light Company, was founded by a consortium of New York City investors. A year later, it was listed on the <a href="/wiki/New_York_Stock_Exchange" title="New York Stock Exchange">New York Stock Exchange</a>. Due to the Board of Aldermen's authority to grant franchises in the City of New York in the early to mid 1800s, interaction with <a href="/wiki/Tammany_Hall" title="Tammany Hall">Tammany Hall</a> was required to expand business. By <a href="/wiki/William_M._Tweed" title="William M. Tweed">William M. Tweed's</a> reign in the late 1860s as the Boss of Tammany Hall, the power to authorize franchises lay with the County Board of Supervisors, of which Tweed had been a member. By 1871, Tweed was a member of the board of the Harlem Gas Light Company, a precursor to the Consolidated Edison Company.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> In 1884, six gas companies combined into the Consolidated Gas Company.
</p><p>The New York Steam Company began providing service in lower <a href="/wiki/Manhattan" title="Manhattan">Manhattan</a> in 1882. Today, Con Edison operates the <a href="/wiki/New_York_City_steam_system" title="New York City steam system">largest commercial steam system</a> in the world, providing steam service to nearly 1,600 commercial and residential establishments in Manhattan from <a href="/wiki/Battery_Park" class="mw-redirect" title="Battery Park">Battery Park</a> to 96th Street.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p><p>Con Edison's electric business also dates back to 1882, when <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Edison" title="Thomas Edison">Thomas Edison</a>’s <a href="/wiki/Edison_Illuminating_Company" title="Edison Illuminating Company">Edison Illuminating Company</a> of New York began supplying electricity to 59 customers in a square-mile area in lower Manhattan. After the "<a href="/wiki/War_of_currents" class="mw-redirect" title="War of currents">war of currents</a>", there were more than 30 companies generating and distributing electricity in New York City and Westchester County. But by 1920 there were far fewer, and the New York Edison Company (then part of Consolidated Gas) was clearly the leader.
</p><p>In 1936, with electric sales far outstripping gas sales, the company incorporated and the name was changed to Consolidated Edison Company of New York, Inc. The years that followed brought further amalgamations as Consolidated Edison acquired or merged with more than a dozen companies between 1936 and 1960. Con Edison today is the result of <a href="/wiki/Mergers_and_acquisitions" title="Mergers and acquisitions">acquisitions</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dissolution_(law)" title="Dissolution (law)">dissolutions</a> and <a href="/wiki/Merger" class="mw-redirect" title="Merger">mergers</a> of more than 170 individual <a href="/wiki/Electric" class="mw-redirect" title="Electric">electric</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gas" title="Gas">gas</a> and <a href="/wiki/Steam" title="Steam">steam</a> companies.
</p><p>Consolidated Edison acquired land on the Hudson River in Buchanan, NY, in 1954 for the <a href="/wiki/Indian_Point_Energy_Center" title="Indian Point Energy Center">Indian Point nuclear power plant.</a> The first reactor (Indian Point 1) began generating power on September 16, 1962. The reactor was shut down on October 31, 1974, because the emergency core cooling system did not meet regulatory requirements. The company built two more reactors at Indian Point during the 1970s: Indian Point 2 and 3. Indian Point 3 was sold to the <a href="/wiki/New_York_Power_Authority" title="New York Power Authority">New York Power Authority</a> in 1975.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Entergy" title="Entergy">Entergy</a> acquired Indian Point 2 in November 2000,<sup id="cite_ref-Archibold_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Archibold-7">[7]</a></sup> nine months after a steam generator leak.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> With the sale of Indian Point 2, the last power plant it owned, Consolidated Edison, Inc. became primarily an energy distributor.<sup id="cite_ref-Archibold_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Archibold-7">[7]</a></sup></p><p>On January 1, 1998, following the <a href="/wiki/Deregulation" title="Deregulation">deregulation</a> of the utility industry in New York state, a holding company, Consolidated Edison, Inc., was formed. It is one of the nation's largest investor-owned energy companies, with approximately $13 billion in annual revenues and $47 billion in assets. The company provides a wide range of energy-related products and services to its customers through two regulated utility subsidiaries and three competitive energy businesses. Under a number of corporate names, the company has been traded on the NYSE without interruption since 1824—longer than any other NYSE stock. Its largest subsidiary, Consolidated Edison Company of New York, Inc. provides electric, gas and steam service to more than 3 million customers in New York City and Westchester County, New York, an area of 660 square miles (1,700 km<sup>2</sup>) with a population of nearly 9 million. Also in 1998, Consolidated Edison, Inc. acquired Orange &amp; Rockland Utilities, which is operated separately.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(2)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Systems">Systems</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Consolidated_Edison&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Systems" data-section="2" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-2 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-2">
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Clean_energy">Clean energy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Consolidated_Edison&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Clean energy" data-section="3" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>To date, Con Edison has invested $3 billion in solar and wind projects. In September 2017 it was announced that the company would invest $1.25 billion in “renewable energy production facilities over the next three years.”<sup id="cite_ref-:0_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-10">[10]</a></sup></p><p>The company's “renewable portfolio” contains more than 1.5 gigawatts of operating capacity. Seventy-five percent of that capacity comes from solar energy. Clean energy accounts for around eight percent of the company's earnings, as of fall 2017.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-10">[10]</a></sup></p><p>To support electric vehicles, Con Edison partnered with the company FleetCarma to provide $500 in rewards to owners of electric vehicles in New York City and Westchester County, New York. Through this program, Con Edison pays customers to charge their vehicles when energy demand is low.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Electrical">Electrical</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Consolidated_Edison&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Electrical" data-section="4" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>The Con Edison electrical transmission system utilizes voltages of 138 kilovolts (kV), 345 kV, and 500 kV. The company has two 345 kV interconnections with upstate New York that enable it to import power from <a href="/wiki/Hydro-Qu%C3%A9bec" title="Hydro-Québec">Hydro-Québec</a> in <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a> and one 345 kV interconnection each with <a href="/wiki/Public_Service_Electric_and_Gas" class="mw-redirect" title="Public Service Electric and Gas">Public Service Electric and Gas</a> in New Jersey and <a href="/wiki/Long_Island_Power_Authority" title="Long Island Power Authority">LIPA</a> on <a href="/wiki/Long_Island" title="Long Island">Long Island</a>. Con Edison is also interconnected with <a href="/wiki/Public_Service_Electric_and_Gas" class="mw-redirect" title="Public Service Electric and Gas">Public Service Electric and Gas</a> via the Branchburg-Ramapo 500 kV line. Con Ed's distribution voltages are 33 kV, 27 kV, 13 kV, and 4 kV.
</p><p>The 93,000 miles (150,000 km) of underground cable in the Con Edison system could wrap around the Earth 3.6 times. Nearly 36,000 miles (58,000 km) of overhead electric wires complement the underground system—enough cable to stretch between New York and Los Angeles 13 times.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Gas">Gas</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Consolidated_Edison&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Gas" data-section="5" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>The Con Edison gas system has nearly 7,200 miles (11,600 km) of pipes—if laid end to end, long enough to reach Paris and back to New York City, and serves Westchester County, the Bronx, Manhattan and parts of Queens. Gas service in Brooklyn, Staten Island and the rest of Queens is provided by <a href="/wiki/National_Grid_USA" class="mw-redirect" title="National Grid USA">National Grid USA</a>'s New York City operations, with the exception of the Rockaway peninsula, which is serviced by National Grid's Long Island operations. The average volume of gas that travels through Con Edison's gas system annually could fill the Empire State Building nearly 6,100 times.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Steam">Steam</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Consolidated_Edison&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Steam" data-section="6" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/New_York_City_steam_system" title="New York City steam system">New York City steam system</a></div>
<p>Con Edison produces 30 billion pounds of steam each year through its seven power plants which boil water to 1,000 °F (538 °C) before distributing it to hundreds of buildings in the <a href="/wiki/New_York_City_steam_system" title="New York City steam system">New York City steam system</a>, which is the biggest <a href="/wiki/District_heating" title="District heating">district steam system</a> in the world.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Steam traveling through the system is used to heat and cool some of New York's most famous addresses, including the <a href="/wiki/Headquarters_of_the_United_Nations" title="Headquarters of the United Nations">United Nations complex</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Empire_State_Building" title="Empire State Building">Empire State Building</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Metropolitan_Museum_of_Art" title="Metropolitan Museum of Art">Metropolitan Museum of Art</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>

</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(6)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Major_accidents_and_incidents">Major accidents and incidents</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Consolidated_Edison&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Major accidents and incidents" data-section="10" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-6 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-6">
<ul><li><b>1977:</b> All of New York City, with the exception of the <a href="/wiki/Rockaways" class="mw-redirect" title="Rockaways">Rockaways</a> – which get their power from the <a href="/wiki/Long_Island_Lighting_Company" title="Long Island Lighting Company">Long Island Lighting Company</a> (LILCo) – was <a href="/wiki/New_York_City_blackout_of_1977" title="New York City blackout of 1977">blacked out overnight</a> on July 13 and 14, due to lightning strikes on a number of sub-stations and the resulting failures of interconnects in the power grid.</li>
<li><b>1989:</b> A <a href="/wiki/Gramercy_Park_asbestos_steam_explosion" title="Gramercy Park asbestos steam explosion">steam pipe explosion in Gramercy Park</a> killed three, injured 24, and required the evacuation of a damaged apartment building due to high levels of asbestos in the air.  Workers had failed to drain water from the pipe before turning the steam on. The utility also eventually pleaded guilty to lying about the absence of asbestos contamination, and paid a $2 million fine.<sup id="cite_ref-NYT_1989-08-24_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NYT_1989-08-24-21">[21]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>2004:</b> In Manhattan, <a href="/wiki/Stray_voltage" title="Stray voltage">stray voltage</a> killed a woman walking her dog in the <a href="/wiki/East_Village,_Manhattan" title="East Village, Manhattan">East Village</a> when she stepped on an electrified metal plate.<sup id="cite_ref-NYT_2006-03-04_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NYT_2006-03-04-22">[22]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>2006:</b> After the <a href="/wiki/2006_Queens_blackout" title="2006 Queens blackout">blackout in Queens</a>, the company was criticized by public officials for a poor record in the restoration of service to its customers.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>2007:</b> On July 18, <a href="/wiki/2007_New_York_City_steam_explosion" title="2007 New York City steam explosion">an explosion occurred in midtown Manhattan</a> near <a href="/wiki/Grand_Central_Terminal" title="Grand Central Terminal">Grand Central Terminal</a> when an 83-year-old Con Edison steam pipe failed, resulting in one death, over 40 injuries, as well as subway and surface disruptions.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>2007:</b> The day before Thanksgiving, an explosion critically burned Queens resident Kunta Oza when an 80-year-old cast iron gas main ruptured. Oza died on Thanksgiving Day, and her family later settled with Con Edison for $3.75 million.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>2009:</b> Another <a href="/wiki/Gas_explosion" title="Gas explosion">gas explosion</a> claimed a life in Queens while Con Edison personnel were on the scene. There was a leak in a manhole and a fault in an electrical feeder at the same time. The fault in the feeder caused the explosion due to the sparks being generated. When the mechanic opened the manhole more oxygen entered and the explosion took place.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Due to that event, Con Edison has changed its procedure on outside gas leak calls.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>2012:</b>
<ul><li>On October 29, flooding from <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Sandy" title="Hurricane Sandy">Hurricane Sandy</a> caused a transformer explosion at a Con-Ed plant on New York City's East Side.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-CBSLocal_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CBSLocal-28">[28]</a></sup></li>
<li>During the storm, Con Edison used social media to get outage and restoration information out to customers. The company's Twitter account gained an extra 16,000 followers during the storm.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></li>
<li>Con Edison's subsidiary, Orange &amp; Rockland Utilities, was criticized for its response to <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Sandy" title="Hurricane Sandy">Hurricane Sandy</a>. Some customers experienced a loss of electrical power for 11 days.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></li></ul></li>
<li><b>2014:</b> On March 12, <a href="/wiki/2014_East_Harlem_gas_explosion" title="2014 East Harlem gas explosion">two apartment buildings exploded</a> in <a href="/wiki/East_Harlem" title="East Harlem">East Harlem</a> after a reported Con Edison gas leak. Eight people were killed in the massive explosion that reduced the conjoining buildings to rubble.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>2018:</b> After 9 p.m. on December 27, a transformer short-circuit<sup id="cite_ref-atlantic_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-atlantic-34">[34]</a></sup> at a ConEd power plant in <a href="/wiki/Astoria,_Queens" title="Astoria, Queens">Astoria, Queens</a> shut down <a href="/wiki/La_Guardia_Airport" class="mw-redirect" title="La Guardia Airport">La Guardia Airport</a> for several hours – until it switched to back-up generators – caused extensive delays on the <a href="/wiki/IRT_Flushing_Line" title="IRT Flushing Line">#7 subway line</a>, and an outage on <a href="/wiki/Rikers_Island" title="Rikers Island">Rikers Island</a>, until it, too, reverted to back-up equipment.<sup id="cite_ref-abc_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-abc-35">[35]</a></sup> The incident caused a large portion of the sky in the surrounding area to be lit up by blue light<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> that was caused by <a href="/wiki/Arc_flash" title="Arc flash">arc flashes</a>, in which light-emitting atoms of excited gas, called <a href="/wiki/Plasma_(physics)" title="Plasma (physics)">plasma</a>, are projected into the air.  The arc flashes probably lasted only a few minutes, but because of meteorological conditions which caused them to be <a href="/wiki/Refraction" title="Refraction">refracted</a>, they were seen across a large portion of the New York City metropolitan area.<sup id="cite_ref-atlantic_34-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-atlantic-34">[34]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-abc_35-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-abc-35">[35]</a></sup> There was no explosion or fire connected to the electrical surge,<sup id="cite_ref-atlantic_34-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-atlantic-34">[34]</a></sup> and no reported injuries.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/New_York_Police_Department" class="mw-redirect" title="New York Police Department">New York Police Department</a> reported that 911 calls increased from 500 in the half-hour before the event to over 3,200 in the 30 minutes afterwards.ConEd is investigating the cause of the surge in equipment that was intended to monitor voltage in the electrical sub-station, but suspects that the problem was a malfunctioning of its relay system.<sup id="cite_ref-abc_35-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-abc-35">[35]</a></sup> The lights were nicknamed the "Astoria Borealis" on <a href="/wiki/Twitter" title="Twitter">Twitter</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>2019:</b> On the night of July 13 a significant portion of Manhattan saw a blackout due to a Consolidated Edison cable that burnt out in a transformer on <a href="/wiki/West_End_Avenue" class="mw-redirect" title="West End Avenue">West End Avenue</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> The blackout, which lasted for about three hours, shut down a number of subway stations, much of the West Side from the 40s to 72nd Street, parts of <a href="/wiki/Times_Square" title="Times Square">Times Square</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rockefeller_Center" title="Rockefeller Center">Rockefeller Center</a>, and other areas, resulting in an estimated 73,000 customers losing power.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> The outage fell on the anniversary of the <a href="/wiki/New_York_City_blackout_of_1977" title="New York City blackout of 1977">1977</a> blackout which most of the city lost power.</li></ul></section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(7)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Bribery_prosecution">Bribery prosecution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Consolidated_Edison&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Bribery prosecution" data-section="11" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-7 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-7">
<p>On January 14, 2009, eleven Con Edison supervisors were arrested for demanding more than $1 million in kickbacks related to work done by a construction company that was repairing the midtown steam pipe eruption of 2007. According to federal prosecutors, the employees had approved payment for work that was unnecessary or not performed, and promised faster payment for some work performed by the construction company in exchange for the <a href="/wiki/Bribery" title="Bribery">bribes</a>. The FBI had two retired Con Edison employees and the president of the construction company wear recording devices that recorded the suspects demanding bribes of between $1000 to $5000.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> Later that year Con Edison sued Brendan Maher, one of the construction supervisors who was arrested and later admitted taking bribes that the utility company claimed amounted to $10,000.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup></p><p>In April 2016, Con Edison agreed to pay over $171 million, about 1.5% of its annual revenue, back to its customers in compensation for harm resulting from the bribery. The <a href="/wiki/New_York_State_Public_Service_Commission" class="mw-redirect" title="New York State Public Service Commission">Public Service Commission</a> had found that Con Edison failed to supervise the employees. Con Edison admitted no wrongdoing.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup></p>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(8)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Honors_and_criticism">Honors and criticism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Consolidated_Edison&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Honors and criticism" data-section="12" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-8 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-8">
<p>In March 2002, <i><a href="/wiki/Fortune_(magazine)" title="Fortune (magazine)">Fortune</a></i> magazine named the company as one of "America's Most Admired Companies" in the publication's newest corporate ranking survey. In 2003, Con Edison ranked second on the top ten list for electric and gas utilities.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup></p><p>In December 2011, the non-partisan organization <a href="/wiki/Public_Campaign#Reports" class="mw-redirect" title="Public Campaign">Public Campaign</a> released a report criticizing ConEd for spending $1.8 million on <a href="/wiki/Lobbying" title="Lobbying">lobbying</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tax_avoidance" title="Tax avoidance">not paying any taxes</a> during 2008–2010, instead getting $127 million in tax rebates, despite making a profit of $4.2 billion, and increasing executive pay by 82% to $17.4 million in 2010 for its top five executives.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup></p><p>In 2014, Con Edison was named the #1 utility and #16 overall among corporations, in <i><a href="/wiki/Newsweek" title="Newsweek">Newsweek</a><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s</i> Green Rankings, and one of the 50 best companies for Latinas by <i>Latina Style Magazine</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> In its "Best of the Best" issue in 2015, <i>Hispanic Network Magazine</i> named the company a top employer among energy, gas, and oil companies.<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> Con Edison was also selected as one of the top regional utilities by <i><a class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> In 2016, the company was listed among America's best large employers by <i><a href="/wiki/Forbes" title="Forbes">Forbes</a></i>.<sup
