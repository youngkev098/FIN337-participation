</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(1)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=General_Dynamics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History" data-section="1" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-1 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-1">
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:General_Dynmamics_1960.jpg" class="image"><noscript><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/6/6a/General_Dynmamics_1960.jpg/220px-General_Dynmamics_1960.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="320" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="244" data-file-height="355"></noscript><span class="lazy-image-placeholder" style="width: 220px;height: 320px;" data-src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/6/6a/General_Dynmamics_1960.jpg/220px-General_Dynmamics_1960.jpg" data-alt="" data-width="220" data-height="320" data-class="thumbimage"> </span></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:General_Dynmamics_1960.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Poster by <a href="/wiki/Erik_Nitsche" title="Erik Nitsche">Erik Nitsche</a> from 1960</div></div></div>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Electric_Boat">Electric Boat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=General_Dynamics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Electric Boat" data-section="2" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/General_Dynamics_Electric_Boat" title="General Dynamics Electric Boat">General Dynamics Electric Boat</a></div>
<p>General Dynamics traces its ancestry to <a href="/wiki/John_Philip_Holland" title="John Philip Holland">John Philip Holland</a>'s Holland Torpedo Boat Company. This company was responsible for developing the <a href="/wiki/U.S._Navy" class="mw-redirect" title="U.S. Navy">U.S. Navy</a>'s first modern <a href="/wiki/Submarine" title="Submarine">submarines</a>, built at <a href="/wiki/Lewis_Nixon_(naval_architect)" title="Lewis Nixon (naval architect)">Lewis Nixon</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Crescent_Shipyard" title="Crescent Shipyard">Crescent Shipyard</a> in <a href="/wiki/Elizabethport,_New_Jersey" class="mw-redirect" title="Elizabethport, New Jersey">Elizabethport, New Jersey</a>. The then revolutionary submarine torpedo boat <a href="/wiki/USS_Holland_(SS-1)" title="USS Holland (SS-1)"><i>Holland VI</i></a> was built there, its keel being laid down in 1896.
</p><p>In 1906 Electric Boat subcontracted submarine construction to the <a href="/wiki/Fore_River_Shipyard" title="Fore River Shipyard">Fore River Shipyard</a> in <a href="/wiki/Quincy,_Massachusetts" title="Quincy, Massachusetts">Quincy, Massachusetts</a> to build the submarines they had designed and won contracts for.  In 1933 Electric Boat acquired ownership of a shipyard in <a href="/wiki/Groton,_Connecticut" title="Groton, Connecticut">Groton, Connecticut</a> to build submarines.  The first submarine built in Groton to be delivered to the U.S. Navy was the <a href="/wiki/USS_Cuttlefish_(SS-171)" title="USS Cuttlefish (SS-171)">USS <i>Cuttlefish</i></a> in 1934.
</p><p>Electric Boat was cash-flush but lacking in work following <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, during which it produced 80 submarines for the Navy, with its workforce shrinking from 13,000 to 4,000 by 1946.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> President and chief executive officer <a href="/wiki/John_Jay_Hopkins" title="John Jay Hopkins">John Jay Hopkins</a> started looking for companies that would fit into Electric Boat's market in hopes of diversifying.
</p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Canadair_purchase">Canadair purchase</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=General_Dynamics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Canadair purchase" data-section="3" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Canadair" title="Canadair">Canadair</a> was owned by the Canadian government and was suffering from the same post-war malaise as Electric Boat. It was up for sale, and Hopkins bought the company for $10 million in 1946. The factory alone was worth more than $22 million, according to the Canadian government's calculations,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> excluding the value of the remaining contracts for planes or spare parts. However, Canadair's production line and inventory systems were in disorder when Electric Boat purchased the company. Hopkins hired Canadian-born mass-production specialist H. Oliver West to take over the president's role and return Canadair to profitability. Shortly after the takeover, Canadair began delivering its new <a href="/wiki/Canadair_North_Star" title="Canadair North Star">Canadair North Star</a> (a version of the <a href="/wiki/Douglas_DC-4" title="Douglas DC-4">Douglas DC-4</a>) and was able to deliver aircraft to <a href="/wiki/Trans-Canada_Airlines" class="mw-redirect" title="Trans-Canada Airlines">Trans-Canada Airlines</a>, <a href="/wiki/Canadian_Pacific_Airlines" class="mw-redirect" title="Canadian Pacific Airlines">Canadian Pacific Airlines</a>, and <a href="/wiki/British_Overseas_Airways_Corporation" title="British Overseas Airways Corporation">British Overseas Airways Corporation</a> (BOAC) well in advance of their contracted delivery times.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p><p>Defense spending increased with the onset of the Cold War, and Canadair went on to win many Canadian military contracts for the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Canadian_Air_Force" title="Royal Canadian Air Force">Royal Canadian Air Force</a> and became a major aerospace company. These included <a href="/wiki/Canadair_CT-133_Silver_Star" title="Canadair CT-133 Silver Star">Canadair CT-133 Silver Star</a> trainer, the <a href="/wiki/Canadair_Argus" class="mw-redirect" title="Canadair Argus">Canadair Argus</a> long-range maritime reconnaissance and <a href="/wiki/Military_transport_aircraft" title="Military transport aircraft">transport aircraft</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Canadair_F-86" class="mw-redirect" title="Canadair F-86">Canadair F-86</a> <i>Sabre</i>. Between 1950 and 1958, 1,815 Sabres were built.  Canadair also produced 200 <a href="/wiki/CF-104_Starfighter" class="mw-redirect" title="CF-104 Starfighter">CF-104 Starfighter</a> supersonic fighter aircraft, a license-built version of the <a href="/wiki/Lockheed_F-104_Starfighter" title="Lockheed F-104 Starfighter">Lockheed F-104</a>.
</p><p>In 1976, General Dynamics sold Canadair to the Canadian Government for $38 million. Canadair was acquired by <a href="/wiki/Bombardier_Inc." title="Bombardier Inc.">Bombardier Inc.</a> in 1986.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="General_Dynamics_emerges">General Dynamics emerges</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=General_Dynamics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: General Dynamics emerges" data-section="4" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>Aircraft production became increasingly important at Canadair, and Hopkins argued that the name "Electric Boat" was no longer appropriate—so Electric Boat was reorganized as General Dynamics on 24 April 1952.<sup id="cite_ref-centennial_GD_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-centennial_GD-13">[13]</a></sup></p><p>General Dynamics purchased <a href="/wiki/Convair" title="Convair">Convair</a> from the Atlas Group in March 1953.<sup id="cite_ref-centennial_GD_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-centennial_GD-13">[13]</a></sup> The sale was approved by government oversight with the provision that GD would continue to operate out of <a href="/wiki/Air_Force_Plant_4" class="mw-redirect" title="Air Force Plant 4">Air Force Plant 4</a> in <a href="/wiki/Fort_Worth,_Texas" title="Fort Worth, Texas">Fort Worth, Texas</a>. This factory had been set up in order to spread out strategic aircraft production and rented to Convair during the war to produce <a href="/wiki/B-24_Liberator" class="mw-redirect" title="B-24 Liberator">B-24 Liberator</a> bombers. Over time, the Fort Worth plant became Convair's major production center.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p><p>General Dynamics purchased Liquid Carbonic Corporation in September 1957 and controlled it as a wholly owned subsidiary until being forced by a Federal antitrust ruling to spin it off to shareholders in January 1969.  Liquid Carbonic was then bought  that same month by the Houston Natural Gas Company.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p><p>Convair worked as an independent division under the General Dynamics umbrella. Over the next decade, the company introduced the <a href="/wiki/F-106_Delta_Dart" class="mw-redirect" title="F-106 Delta Dart">F-106 Delta Dart</a> <a href="/wiki/Interceptor_aircraft" title="Interceptor aircraft">Interceptor</a>, the <a href="/wiki/B-58_Hustler" class="mw-redirect" title="B-58 Hustler">B-58 Hustler</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Convair_880" title="Convair 880">Convair 880</a> and <a href="/wiki/Convair_990" class="mw-redirect" title="Convair 990">990</a> <a href="/wiki/Airliner" title="Airliner">airliners</a>. Convair also introduced the <a href="/wiki/Atlas_missile" class="mw-redirect" title="Atlas missile">Atlas missile</a> platform, the first operational <a href="/wiki/Intercontinental_ballistic_missile" title="Intercontinental ballistic missile">intercontinental ballistic missile</a>.
</p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Management_churn">Management churn</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=General_Dynamics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Management churn" data-section="5" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>Hopkins fell seriously ill during 1957 and was eventually replaced by Frank Pace later that year.<sup id="cite_ref-centennial_GD_13-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-centennial_GD-13">[13]</a></sup> Meanwhile, John Naish succeeded Joseph McNarney as president of Convair. Chicago industrialist <a href="/wiki/Henry_Crown" title="Henry Crown">Henry Crown</a> became the company's largest shareholder and merged his <a href="/w/index.php?title=Material_Service_Corporation&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Material Service Corporation (page does not exist)">Material Service Corporation</a> with GD in 1959.
</p><p>Naish left in May 1961, taking most of Convair's top people with him.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>  GD subsequently reorganized into Eastern Group in <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a> and Western Group in <a href="/wiki/San_Diego" title="San Diego">San Diego</a>, <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a>, with the latter taking over all of the aerospace activities and dropping the Convair brand name from its aircraft in the process.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p><p>Frank Pace retired under pressure in 1962 and Roger Lewis, former Assistant Secretary of the Air Force and <a href="/wiki/Pan_American_Airways" class="mw-redirect" title="Pan American Airways">Pan American Airways</a> CEO, was brought in as CEO.  The company recovered, then fell back into the same struggles.  In 1970, the board brought in <a href="/wiki/McDonnell_Douglas" title="McDonnell Douglas">McDonnell Douglas</a> president <a href="/wiki/David_S._Lewis,_Jr." class="mw-redirect" title="David S. Lewis, Jr.">Dave Lewis</a> (no relation) as chairman and CEO, who served until retiring in 1985.
</p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Aviation_powerhouse">Aviation powerhouse</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=General_Dynamics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Aviation powerhouse" data-section="6" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>During the early 1960s the company bid on the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Air_Force" title="United States Air Force">United States Air Force</a>'s <a href="/wiki/TFX_Program" title="TFX Program">Tactical Fighter, Experimental</a> (TFX) project for a new low-level "penetrator". <a href="/wiki/Robert_McNamara" title="Robert McNamara">Robert McNamara</a>, newly installed as the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Secretary_of_Defense" title="United States Secretary of Defense">Secretary of Defense</a>, forced a merger of the TFX with <a href="/wiki/United_States_Navy" title="United States Navy">U.S. Navy</a> plans for a new long-range "fleet defender" aircraft. In order to bid on a naval version successfully, GD partnered with <a href="/wiki/Grumman" title="Grumman">Grumman</a>, which would build a customized version for <a href="/wiki/Aircraft_carrier" title="Aircraft carrier">aircraft carrier</a> duties. After four rounds of bids and changes, the GD/Grumman team finally won the contract over a <a href="/wiki/Boeing" title="Boeing">Boeing</a> submission.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/General_Dynamics_F-111" class="mw-redirect" title="General Dynamics F-111">F-111</a> that resulted first flew in December 1964. The F-111B flew in May 1965, but the navy said that it was too heavy for use on aircraft carriers.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>   With an unacceptable navy version, estimates for 2,400 F-111s, including exports, were sharply reduced, but GD still managed to make a $300-million profit on the project.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Grumman went on to use many of the innovations of the F-111 in the highly successful <a href="/wiki/F-14_Tomcat" class="mw-redirect" title="F-14 Tomcat">F-14 Tomcat</a>,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2020)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> an aircraft designed solely as a carrier-borne fighter.
</p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Reorganization">Reorganization</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=General_Dynamics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Reorganization" data-section="7" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>In May 1965, GD reorganized into 12 operating divisions based on product lines. The board decided to build all future planes in Fort Worth, ending plane production at Convair's original plant in San Diego but continuing with space and missile development there. In October 1970, Roger Lewis left and David S. Lewis from <a href="/wiki/McDonnell_Douglas" title="McDonnell Douglas">McDonnell Douglas</a> was named CEO. Lewis required that the company headquarters move to <a href="/wiki/St._Louis,_Missouri" class="mw-redirect" title="St. Louis, Missouri">St. Louis, Missouri</a>, which occurred in February 1971.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="F-16_success">F-16 success</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=General_Dynamics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: F-16 success" data-section="8" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>In 1972, GD bid on the USAF's <a href="/wiki/Lightweight_Fighter" class="mw-redirect" title="Lightweight Fighter">Lightweight Fighter</a> (LWF) project. GD and <a href="/wiki/Northrop_Corporation" title="Northrop Corporation">Northrop</a> were awarded prototype contracts. GD's F-111 program was winding down, and the company desperately needed a new aircraft contract. It organized its own version of <a href="/wiki/Lockheed_Corporation" title="Lockheed Corporation">Lockheed</a>'s famed "<a href="/wiki/Skunk_Works" title="Skunk Works">Skunk Works</a>", the Advanced Concepts Laboratory, and responded with a new aircraft design incorporating advanced technologies.
</p><p>GD's <a href="/wiki/F-16_Fighting_Falcon" class="mw-redirect" title="F-16 Fighting Falcon">YF-16</a> first flew in January 1974 and proved to have slightly better performance than the <a href="/wiki/Northrop_YF-17" title="Northrop YF-17">YF-17</a> in head-to-head testing. It entered production as the F-16 in January 1975 with an initial order of 650 and a total order of 1,388. The F-16 also won contracts worldwide, beating the F-17 in foreign competition as well. GD built an aircraft production factory in Fort Worth, Texas.  F-16 orders eventually totaled more than 4,000, making it the largest and most successful program for the company, and one of the most successful western military projects since World War II.
</p>

<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Land_Systems_and_Marine_Systems_focus">Land Systems and Marine Systems focus</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=General_Dynamics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Land Systems and Marine Systems focus" data-section="9" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/General_Dynamics_Land_Systems" title="General Dynamics Land Systems">General Dynamics Land Systems</a></div>
<p>In 1976, General Dynamics sold the struggling Canadair back to the Canadian government for $38 million. By 1984, General Dynamics had four divisions: Convair in San Diego, General Dynamics-Fort Worth, General Dynamics-Pomona, and General Dynamics-Electronics. In 1985 a further reorganization created the Space Systems Division from the Convair Space division. In 1985, GD also acquired <a href="/wiki/Cessna" title="Cessna">Cessna</a>.  In 1986 the Pomona division (which mainly produced the Standard Missile and the Phalanx CIWS for the navy) was split up,  creating the Valley Systems Division.  Valley Systems produced the Stinger surface-to-air missile and the Rolling Airframe Missile (RAM).  Both units were recombined into one entity in 1992.
</p><p>Henry Crown, still GD's largest shareholder, died on 15 August 1990. Following this, the company started to rapidly divest its under-performing divisions under CEO <a href="/wiki/William_Anders" class="mw-redirect" title="William Anders">William Anders</a>. Cessna was re-sold to <a href="/wiki/Textron" title="Textron">Textron</a> in January 1992, the San Diego and Pomona missile production units to <a href="/wiki/General_Motors" title="General Motors">General Motors</a>-<a href="/wiki/Hughes_Aerospace" class="mw-redirect" title="Hughes Aerospace">Hughes Aerospace</a> in May 1992, the Fort Worth aircraft production to <a href="/wiki/Lockheed_Corporation" title="Lockheed Corporation">Lockheed</a> in March 1993 (a nearby electronics production facility was separately sold to Israeli-based <a href="/wiki/Elbit_Systems" title="Elbit Systems">Elbit Systems</a>, marking that company's entry into the United States market), and its Space Systems Division to <a href="/wiki/Martin_Marietta" title="Martin Marietta">Martin Marietta</a> in 1994. The remaining Convair Aircraft Structure unit was sold to McDonnell Douglas in 1994. The remains of the Convair Division were simply closed in 1996. GD's exit from the aviation world was short-lived, and in 1999 the company acquired <a href="/wiki/Gulfstream_Aerospace" title="Gulfstream Aerospace">Gulfstream Aerospace</a>.  The Pomona operation was closed shortly after its sale to Hughes Aircraft.
</p><p>In 1995, General Dynamics purchased the privately held <a href="/wiki/Bath_Iron_Works" title="Bath Iron Works">Bath Iron Works</a> shipyard in <a href="/wiki/Bath,_Maine" title="Bath, Maine">Bath, Maine</a>, for $300 million, diversifying its shipbuilding portfolio to include U.S. Navy surface ships such as <a href="/wiki/Guided_missile_destroyer" title="Guided missile destroyer">guided-missile destroyers</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> In 1998, the company acquired NASSCO, formerly <a href="/wiki/National_Steel_and_Shipbuilding_Company" title="National Steel and Shipbuilding Company">National Steel and Shipbuilding Company</a>, for $415 million. The San Diego shipyard produces U.S. Navy auxiliary and support ships as well as commercial ships that are eligible to be U.S.-flagged under the <a href="/wiki/Merchant_Marine_Act_of_1920" title="Merchant Marine Act of 1920">Jones Act</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p><p>Having divested itself of its aviation holdings, GD concentrated on land and sea products. GD purchased <a href="/wiki/Chrysler" title="Chrysler">Chrysler</a>'s defense divisions in 1982, renaming them General Dynamics Land Systems. In 2003, it purchased the defense divisions of <a href="/wiki/General_Motors" title="General Motors">General Motors</a> as well. It is now a major supplier of armored vehicles of all types, including the <a href="/wiki/M1_Abrams" title="M1 Abrams">M1 Abrams</a>, <a href="/wiki/LAV_25" class="mw-redirect" title="LAV 25">LAV 25</a>, <a href="/wiki/Stryker" title="Stryker">Stryker</a>, and a wide variety of vehicles based on these chassis.  <a href="/wiki/Force_Protection_Inc" title="Force Protection Inc">Force Protection, Inc.</a> was acquired by General Dynamics Land Systems in November 2011 for $350 mil.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/General_Dynamics_Land_Systems" title="General Dynamics Land Systems">General Dynamics Land Systems</a> (GDLS) was hurt by the cancellation of the US Army's <a href="/wiki/Future_Combat_Systems" title="Future Combat Systems">Future Combat Systems</a> program and the loss in the <a href="/wiki/Joint_Light_Tactical_Vehicle" title="Joint Light Tactical Vehicle">Joint Light Tactical Vehicle</a> MRAP replacement competition.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Recent_history">Recent history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=General_Dynamics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Recent history" data-section="10" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>On August 19, 2008, GD agreed to pay $4 million to settle a lawsuit brought by the US Government claiming that a GD unit fraudulently billed the government for defectively manufactured parts used in US military aircraft and submarines.  The US alleged that GD defectively manufactured or failed to test parts used in US military aircraft from September 2001 to August 2003, such as for the <a href="/wiki/C-141_Starlifter" class="mw-redirect" title="C-141 Starlifter">C-141 Starlifter</a> transport plane. The GD unit involved, based in <a href="/wiki/Glen_Cove,_New_York" title="Glen Cove, New York">Glen Cove, New York</a>, closed in 2004.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p><p>In 2014, the government of Canada announced it had selected the General Dynamics Land Systems subsidiary in <a href="/wiki/London,_Ontario" title="London, Ontario">London, Ontario</a>, to produce <a href="/wiki/LAV-25" title="LAV-25">Light Armoured Vehicles</a> for <a href="/wiki/Saudi_Arabia" title="Saudi Arabia">Saudi Arabia</a> as part of a $10 billion deal with the <a href="/wiki/Canadian_Commercial_Corporation" title="Canadian Commercial Corporation">Canadian Commercial Corporation</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup>  The sale has been criticized by political opponents because of the <a href="/wiki/Saudi_Arabian-led_intervention_in_Yemen" title="Saudi Arabian-led intervention in Yemen">Saudi Arabian-led intervention in Yemen</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Post_union_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Post_union-21">[21]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-gma_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gma-22">[22]</a></sup> In December 2018, after Prime Minister <a href="/wiki/Justin_Trudeau" title="Justin Trudeau">Justin Trudeau</a> suggested Canada might href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup>  Trudeau has since said that while he is critical of Saudi conduct, he cannot simply scrap the deal because "Canada as a country of the rule of law needs to respect its contracts."<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup>  On 30 January 2019, CEO <a href="/wiki/Phebe_Novakovic" title="Phebe Novakovic">Phebe Novakovic</a> warned investors that the matter had "significantly impacted" the company's cash flow because Saudi Arabia was nearly $2 billion in arrears on its payments.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p><p>In 2018, General Dynamics acquired information technology services giant <a href="/wiki/CSRA_(IT_services_company)" class="mw-redirect" title="CSRA (IT services company)">CSRA</a> for $9.7 billion, and merged it with GDIT.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p><p>General Dynamics has been accused by groups such as <a href="/wiki/Code_Pink" title="Code title="United States Department of Health and Human Services">Department of Health and Human Services</a>' <a href="/wiki/Office_of_Refugee_Resettlement" title="Office of Refugee Resettlement">Office of Refugee Resettlement</a>, the government agency that operates shelters for unaccompanied children to include those separated from their families as part of the <a detention centers, and that its contracts to provide training an technical services began in 2000 and have spanned across four presidential administrations.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p><p>It was announced in September 2018 that the U.S. Navy  awarded contracts for 10 new Arleigh Burke-class destroyers from General Dynamics Bath Iron Works and Huntington Ingalls Industries.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p><p>Former <a href="/wiki/United_States_Secretary_of_Defense" title="United States Secretary of Defense">U.S. Secretary of Defense</a> Gen. <a href="/wiki/Jim_Mattis" title="Jim Mattis">Jim Mattis</a> re-joined the company's <a href="/wiki/Board_of_directors" title="Board of He had previously served on the board, but resigned and divested before becoming secretary of defense.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></p>

<p>Current members of the <a href="/wiki/Board_of_directors" title="Board of directors">board of directors</a> of General Dynamics are: Catherine Reynolds, Nicholas Chabraja, James Crown, William Fricks, Paul Kaminski, John Keane, Lester Lyles, Phebe Novakovic, William A. Osborn, Laura J. Schumacher and Robert Walmsley.<sup id="cite_ref-66" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Financials">Financials</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=General_Dynamics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Financials" data-section="25" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>General Dynamics has $30.9 billion in sales as of 2017<sup id="cite_ref-67" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup> primarily military, but also civilian with its Gulfstream Aerospace unit and conventional shipbuilding and repair with its National Steel and Shipbuilding subsidiary.
</p><p>In 2004, General Dynamics bid for the UK company <a href="/wiki/Alvis_plc" title="Alvis plc">Alvis plc</a>, the leading British manufacturer of armored vehicles. In March the board of Alvis Vickers voted in favor of the £309m takeover. However at the last minute <a href="/wiki/BAE_Systems" title="BAE Systems">BAE Systems</a> offered £355m for the company.  This deal was finalized in June 2004.<sup id="cite_ref-68" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup></p><p>The corporation's subsidiaries are donors to the <a href="/wiki/Canadian_Defence_and_Foreign_Affairs_Institute" class="mw-redirect" title="Canadian Defence and Foreign Affairs Institute">Canadian Defence and Foreign Affairs Institute</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-69" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:What_Wikipedia_is_not#Encyclopedic_content" title="Wikipedia:What Wikipedia is not"><span title="The material preceding this tag may lack sufficient importance.">importance?</span></a></i>]</sup></p><p>10% of General Dynamic stock is owned by the Crown family, which includes Nancy Carrington Crown and Steven Crown. This is the family's largest single asset.<sup id="cite_ref-70" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup></p>
