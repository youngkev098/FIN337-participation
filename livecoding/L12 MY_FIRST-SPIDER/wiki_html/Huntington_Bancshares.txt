<p><b>Huntington Bancshares Incorporated</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Bank_holding_company" title="Bank holding company">bank holding company</a> headquartered in <a href="/wiki/Columbus,_Ohio" title="Columbus, Ohio">Columbus, Ohio</a>. The company is ranked 610th on the <a href="/wiki/Fortune_500" title="Fortune 500">Fortune 500</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> and is among the <a href="/wiki/List_of_largest_banks_in_the_United_States" title="List of largest banks in the United States">largest banks in the United States</a>.

<p>The company's banking subsidiary, <b>The Huntington National Bank</b>, operates 920 banking offices, primarily in the <a href="/wiki/Midwest" class="mw-redirect" title="Midwest">Midwest</a>: 459 in <a href="/wiki/Ohio" title="Ohio">Ohio</a>, 290 in <a href="/wiki/Michigan" title="Michigan">Michigan</a>, 51 in <a href="/wiki/Pennsylvania" title="Pennsylvania">Pennsylvania</a>, 45 in <a href="/wiki/Indiana" title="Indiana">Indiana</a>, 35 in <a href="/wiki/Illinois" title="Illinois">Illinois</a>, 29 in <a href="/wiki/West_Virginia" title="West Virginia">West Virginia</a>, and 10 in <a href="/wiki/Kentucky" title="Kentucky">Kentucky</a>.
</p><p>The company is the second-largest originator of <a href="/wiki/Small_Business_Administration" title="Small Business Administration">Small Business Administration</a> loans.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(1)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Huntington_Bancshares&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History" data-section="1" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-1 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-1">
<p>P. W. Huntington formed P. W. Huntington &amp; Company in 1866.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHeer1914312_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHeer1914312-4">[4]</a></sup> Huntington built its first 5-story building in 1878. Four out of five sons of P. W. would become partners during the 1890s and early 1900s. The bank was incorporated in 1905 as The Huntington National Bank of Columbus.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMcNally191460_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMcNally191460-5">[5]</a></sup> Huntington died in 1918 shortly after turning the bank over to his sons.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p><p>Francis Huntington became president and provided active leadership for 14 years.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHerringshaw1906869_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHerringshaw1906869-7">[7]</a></sup> In 1915, the bank received limited trust powers. In 1922, the bank received full trust powers from the <a href="/wiki/Federal_Reserve_System" class="mw-redirect" title="Federal Reserve System">Federal Reserve System</a>. In 1923, Huntington purchased Columbus-based the State Savings Bank &amp; Trust Company and the Hayden-Clinton National Bank of Columbus, swelling its capital base.<sup id="cite_ref-timeline_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-timeline-8">[8]</a></sup></p><p>In 1958, Huntington acquired the Columbus-based The Market Exchange Bank Company. In 1962, Huntington acquired both First National Bank of Grove City and The People's Bank of Canal Winchester. In 1963, Huntington acquired both The Columbus Savings Bank and the Columbus-based The Northern Savings Bank. In 1966, Huntington Bancshares Incorporated (HBI) was established as a bank holding company.
</p><p>In 1967, Huntington Bancshares acquired the <a href="/wiki/Washington_Court_House,_Ohio" title="Washington Court House, Ohio">Washington Court House</a>-based The Washington Savings Bank. In 1969, it acquired the <a href="/wiki/Ashland,_Ohio" title="Ashland, Ohio">Ashland</a>-based Farmers Bank. In 1970, it also acquired the <a href="/wiki/Bowling_Green,_Ohio" title="Bowling Green, Ohio">Bowling Green</a>-based The Bank of Wood County Company, the <a href="/wiki/Toledo,_Ohio" title="Toledo, Ohio">Toledo</a>-based The Lucas County State Bank, and Lagonda National Bank of Springfield. In 1971, Huntington Bancshares acquired First National Bank &amp; Trust Company of Lima, The Woodville State Bank, and the <a href="/wiki/Kent,_Ohio" title="Kent, Ohio">Kent</a>-based The Portage National Bank. In 1972, it acquired The First National Bank of Wadsworth and The First National Bank of Kenton, also establishing the first 24-hour, fully automated banking office.
</p><p>In 1973, Alger Savings Bank merged into an affiliate in <a href="/wiki/Kenton,_Ohio" title="Kenton, Ohio">Kenton, Ohio</a>. In 1976, The Huntington Mortgage Company formed as a subsidiary of Huntington Bancshares with The Pickerington Bank being merged into the bank. In 1977, Huntington Bancshares acquired The Bellefontaine National Bank, The Central National Bank of London, and Columbus-based The Franklin National Bank. In 1979, a loan production office opened in <a href="/wiki/Dayton,_Ohio" title="Dayton, Ohio">Dayton, Ohio</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-timeline_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-timeline-8">[8]</a></sup></p><p>In 1975, the company changed its logo to its current "honeycomb" logo.
</p><p>In 1980, Farmers &amp; Merchants Bank, Milford Center and The First National Bank of Burton were merged with Huntington Bancshares.<sup id="cite_ref-timeline_8-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-timeline-8">[8]</a></sup></p><p>In 1981, the bank acquired Alexandria Bank Company and renamed it The Huntington State Bank, with a loan production office opening in <a href="/wiki/Cincinnati,_Ohio" class="mw-redirect" title="Cincinnati, Ohio">Cincinnati, Ohio</a>.
</p><p>In 1982, the bank merged with the Reeves Banking and Trust Company.<sup id="cite_ref-timeline_8-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-timeline-8">[8]</a></sup> Huntington acquired the tiny Savings Bank of <a href="/wiki/Chillicothe,_Ohio" title="Chillicothe, Ohio">Chillicothe, Ohio</a> in the early 1980s, which gained some fame in 2011 when 100-year-old June Gregg revealed to Huntington officials that her father had opened a <a href="/wiki/Savings_account" title="Savings account">savings account</a> for her as a baby with Savings Bank in 1913 and that she had subsequently kept the account open. Huntington officials later confirmed it and gave her account a temporary increase in her <a href="/wiki/Interest_rate" title="Interest rate">interest rate</a> to 5% as a <a href="/wiki/Centenarian" title="Centenarian">centenarian</a> present for her 98-year loyalty to Huntington and the Chillicothe branch's predecessor, Savings Bank.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p><p>In 1983, the bank acquired <a href="/wiki/Cleveland" title="Cleveland">Cleveland</a>-based Union Commerce Bank.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p><p>In 1997, the bank acquired First Michigan Bank Corporation of <a href="/wiki/Holland,_Michigan" title="Holland, Michigan">Holland, Michigan</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p><p>In 2002, the company sold its branches in Florida to <a href="/wiki/SunTrust_Banks" title="SunTrust Banks">SunTrust Banks</a> for $705 million.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p><p>In March 2006, the company acquired Unizan Financial.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p><p>In July 2007, the company acquired <a href="/wiki/Sky_Financial_Group_Inc." class="mw-redirect" title="Sky Financial Group Inc.">Sky Financial Group Inc.</a> based in <a href="/wiki/Bowling_Green,_Ohio" title="Bowling Green, Ohio">Bowling Green, Ohio</a>, which increased its presence in Indiana and Ohio and expanded it into <a href="/wiki/Western_Pennsylvania" title="Western Pennsylvania">Western Pennsylvania</a> for the first time.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p><p>In November 2008, the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_the_Treasury" title="United States Department of the Treasury">United States Department of the Treasury</a> invested $1.4 billion in the company as part of the <a href="/wiki/Troubled_Asset_Relief_Program" title="Troubled Asset Relief Program">Troubled Asset Relief Program</a> and in December 2010, the company repaid the Treasury. The U.S. government made a profit of over $144 million from its investment in the company.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p><p>In 2009, Huntington bid against rival <a href="/wiki/Fifth_Third_Bank" title="Fifth Third Bank">Fifth Third Bank</a> to acquire <a href="/wiki/National_City_Corp." title="National City Corp.">National City Corp.</a> branches in the <a href="/wiki/Pittsburgh" title="Pittsburgh">Pittsburgh</a> region from <a href="/wiki/PNC_Financial_Services" title="PNC Financial Services">PNC Financial Services</a>. The <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Justice" title="United States Department of Justice">United States Department of Justice</a> ordered PNC to sell the branches to comply with <a href="/wiki/United_States_antitrust_law" title="United States antitrust law">United States antitrust law</a> concerns after the <a href="/wiki/National_City_acquisition_by_PNC" title="National City acquisition by PNC">National City acquisition by PNC</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-sabatini_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sabatini-28">[28]</a></sup> Ultimately, PNC sold the overlapping branches to <a href="/wiki/First_Niagara_Bank" title="First Niagara Bank">First Niagara Bank</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p><p>On October 3, 2009, the <a href="/wiki/Federal_Deposit_Insurance_Corporation" title="Federal Deposit Insurance Corporation">Federal Deposit Insurance Corporation</a> named Huntington as <a href="/wiki/Receivership" title="Receivership">receiver</a> of a $400 million deposit portfolio from the <a href="/wiki/Bank_failure" title="Bank failure">bank failure</a> of Warren Bank in <a href="/wiki/Warren,_Michigan" title="Warren, Michigan">Warren, Michigan</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-three_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-three-31">[31]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p><p>On December 18, 2009, Huntington signed a 45-day lease with the FDIC to run a <a href="/wiki/Bridge_bank_(United_States)" class="mw-redirect" title="Bridge bank (United States)">bridge bank</a> for the failed Citizens State Bank in <a href="/wiki/New_Baltimore,_Michigan" title="New Baltimore, Michigan">New Baltimore, Michigan</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-shutter_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-shutter-35">[35]</a></sup></p><p>In March 2012, the bank acquired Dearborn-based Fidelity Bank.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup></p><p>In 2012, Huntington was in merger discussions with <a href="/wiki/Flint,_Michigan" title="Flint, Michigan">Flint, Michigan</a>-based <a href="/wiki/Citizens_Republic_Bancorp" title="Citizens Republic Bancorp">Citizens Republic Bancorp</a>. Discussions stalled and <a href="/wiki/FirstMerit" class="mw-redirect" title="FirstMerit">FirstMerit</a>, which was itself acquired by Huntington in 2016, purchased Citizens Republic in September 2012.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup></p><p>In the first quarter of 2013, Huntington changed its ATMs to new ones that allow customers to make deposits by inserting cash and checks directly into the ATM.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> The bank started in 2014 offering ATM deposits from mobile phones and through online transfers until 11:59 p.m. and post them that day.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup></p><p>In March 2014, the company acquired Ohio-based <a href="/wiki/Camco_Financial" title="Camco Financial">Camco Financial</a>, holding company for <b>Advantage Bank</b>, for $97 million in stock.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup></p><p>In September 2014, the company acquired 24 offices of <a href="/wiki/Bank_of_America" title="Bank of America">Bank of America</a> in <a href="/wiki/Central_Michigan" title="Central Michigan">Central Michigan</a>, including the <a href="/wiki/Port_Huron,_Michigan" title="Port Huron, Michigan">Port Huron</a>, <a href="/wiki/Flint,_Michigan" title="Flint, Michigan">Flint</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Saginaw,_Michigan" title="Saginaw, Michigan">Saginaw</a> markets. This raised the number of Huntington branches in Michigan to 173, including over 40 locations housed in <a href="/wiki/Meijer" title="Meijer">Meijer</a> stores.<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup></p><p>In March 2015, the company acquired <a href="/wiki/Michigan" title="Michigan">Michigan</a>-based Macquarie Equipment Finance, Inc. from <a href="/wiki/Sydney,_Australia" class="mw-redirect" title="Sydney, Australia">Sydney, Australia</a>-based <a href="/wiki/Macquarie_Group" title="Macquarie Group">Macquarie Group</a> for $458 million.<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup></p><p>In January 2016, Huntington announced it would purchase <a href="/wiki/Akron,_Ohio" title="Akron, Ohio">Akron</a>-based <a href="/wiki/FirstMerit_Corporation" title="FirstMerit Corporation">FirstMerit Corporation</a> for $3.4 billion, making it one of the largest banks in Ohio.<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup> Due to <a href="/wiki/Sherman_Antitrust_Act" class="mw-redirect" title="Sherman Antitrust Act">Sherman Antitrust Act</a> concerns by the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Justice" title="United States Department of Justice">United States Department of Justice</a>, it sold 11 branches in Canton and two in <a href="/wiki/Ashtabula,_Ohio" title="Ashtabula, Ohio">Ashtabula</a> to <a href="/wiki/First_Commonwealth_Bank" title="First Commonwealth Bank">First Commonwealth Bank</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup> Additionally, 107 branches located within 2.5 miles of other Huntington / FirstMerit branches were closed.<sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Historic_checks">Historic checks</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Huntington_Bancshares&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Historic checks" data-section="2" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>In 2012, Huntington started displaying old checks that were written by famous people, including 24 former <a href="/wiki/President_of_the_United_States" title="President of the United States">U.S. Presidents</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Abraham_Lincoln" title="Abraham Lincoln">Abraham Lincoln</a>, <a href="/wiki/George_Washington" title="George Washington">George Washington</a>, <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Jefferson" title="Thomas Jefferson">Thomas Jefferson</a>, <a href="/wiki/Andrew_Jackson" title="Andrew Jackson">Andrew Jackson</a>, <a href="/wiki/Theodore_Roosevelt" title="Theodore Roosevelt">Theodore Roosevelt</a>, <a href="/wiki/Franklin_Roosevelt" class="mw-redirect" title="Franklin Roosevelt">Franklin Roosevelt</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Niles,_Ohio" title="Niles, Ohio">Niles, Ohio</a> native <a href="/wiki/William_McKinley" title="William McKinley">William McKinley</a>. Other checks were signed by <a href="/wiki/Charles_Dickens" title="Charles Dickens">Charles Dickens</a>, <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Edison" title="Thomas Edison">Thomas Edison</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ernest_Hemingway" title="Ernest Hemingway">Ernest Hemingway</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Susan_B._Anthony" title="Susan B. Anthony">Susan B. Anthony</a>, among others.<sup id="cite_ref-59" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup> The most notable check was one written by Lincoln to "self" for $800 dated April 13, 1865, the day before his <a href="/wiki/Assassination_of_Abraham_Lincoln" title="Assassination of Abraham Lincoln">assassination</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup> The checks are estimated to be worth over $75,000 today. Huntington acquired the checks in 1983 when it purchased Union Commerce Bank and received several boxes of old documents, but weren't discovered until 2011 when a Huntington employee was looking through the documents.<sup id="cite_ref-WSJ_61-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WSJ-61">[61]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Huntington_Preferred_Capital">Huntington Preferred Capital</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Huntington_Bancshares&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Huntington Preferred Capital" data-section="3" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>Huntington Bancshares also operates Huntington Preferred Capital, Inc., a <a href="/wiki/Real_estate_investment_trust" title="Real estate investment trust">real estate investment trust</a> (REIT). It was organized under Ohio law in 1992 and designated as a REIT in 1998. Four related parties own HPCI's <a href="/wiki/Common_stock" title="Common stock">common stock</a>: Huntington Capital Financing LLC; Huntington Preferred Capital II, Inc.; Huntington Preferred Capital Holdings, Inc.; and Huntington Bancshares Incorporated. All these entities are tied via ownership and/or interlocking directorships to Huntington Bancshares, either directly or through Huntington National Bank.
</p><p>In addition to the common stock, Huntington Preferred Capital also issued 2 million shares of <a href="/wiki/Preferred_stock" title="Preferred stock">preferred stock</a>, paying a quarterly cash dividend of $0.4925 per share. This stock is largely held by the same companies as the common stock, but a small fraction of the available shares are sold on the open market. Huntington Preferred Capital had one subsidiary, HPCLI, Inc., a taxable REIT subsidiary formed in March 2001 for the purpose of holding certain assets (primarily leasehold improvements). On December 31, 2007, Huntington Preferred Capital paid common stock dividends consisting of cash and the stock of HPCLI to its common stock shareholders. After the stock dividend was paid, HPCLI became a <a href="/wiki/Wholly_owned_subsidiary" class="mw-redirect" title="Wholly owned subsidiary">wholly owned subsidiary</a> of Huntington Preferred Capital Holdings, which holds all the shares of HPCLI.<sup id="cite_ref-62" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup></p><p>Steve Steinour is president, CEO, and chairman of the bank.<sup id="cite_ref-dispatch_63-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dispatch-63">[63]</a></sup></p>
