<p class="mw-empty-elt">
</p>
<p><b>Corning Incorporated</b> is an American <a href="/wiki/Multinational_corporation" title="Multinational corporation">multinational</a> <a href="/wiki/Technology_company" title="Technology company">technology company</a> that specializes in specialty <a href="/wiki/Glass" title="Glass">glass</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ceramic" title="Ceramic">ceramics</a>, and related materials and technologies including advanced <a href="/wiki/Optics" title="Optics">optics</a>, primarily for industrial and scientific applications. The company was named <b>Corning Glass Works</b> until 1989.<sup id="cite_ref-Corning-Jan-1994-S-3/A_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Corning-Jan-1994-S-3/A-3">[3]</a></sup> Corning divested its consumer product lines (including <a href="/wiki/CorningWare" title="CorningWare">CorningWare</a> and <a href="/wiki/Visions_(cookware)" title="Visions (cookware)">Visions</a> <a href="/wiki/Pyroceram" title="Pyroceram">Pyroceram</a>-based cookware, <a href="/wiki/Corelle" title="Corelle">Corelle</a> Vitrelle tableware, and <a href="/wiki/Pyrex" title="Pyrex">Pyrex</a> glass bakeware) in 1998 by selling the Corning Consumer Products Company subsidiary (now known as <a href="/wiki/Corelle_Brands" title="Corelle Brands">Corelle Brands</a>) to <a href="/wiki/Borden_Food_Corporation" class="mw-redirect" title="Borden Food Corporation">Borden</a>, but still holds an interest of about 8 percent.

Corning is involved in two <a href="/wiki/Joint_venture" title="Joint venture">joint ventures</a>: <a href="/wiki/Dow_Corning" title="Dow Corning">Dow Corning</a> and Pittsburgh Corning. <a href="/wiki/Quest_Diagnostics" title="Quest Diagnostics">Quest Diagnostics</a> and <a href="/wiki/Covance" title="Covance">Covance</a> were <a href="/wiki/Corporate_spin-off" title="Corporate spin-off">spun off</a> from Corning in 1996.<sup id="cite_ref-Corning-Jan-1997-8-K_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Corning-Jan-1997-8-K-4">[4]</a></sup> Corning is one of the main suppliers to <a href="/wiki/Apple_Inc." title="Apple Inc.">Apple Inc.</a> since working with <a href="/wiki/Steve_Jobs" title="Steve Jobs">Steve Jobs</a> in 2007 to develop the <a href="/wiki/IPhone" title="IPhone">iPhone</a>;<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Corning develops and manufactures <a href="/wiki/Gorilla_Glass" title="Gorilla Glass">Gorilla Glass</a>, which is used by many smartphone makers. It is one of the world's biggest glassmakers.<sup id="cite_ref-veconomist_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-veconomist-6">[6]</a></sup> Corning won the <a href="/wiki/National_Medal_of_Technology_and_Innovation" title="National Medal of Technology and Innovation">National Medal of Technology and Innovation</a> four times for its product and process innovations.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>

</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(1)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Corning_Inc.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History" data-section="1" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-1 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-1">
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Corning_Conaphore_y.jpg" class="image"><noscript><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cc/Corning_Conaphore_y.jpg/200px-Corning_Conaphore_y.jpg" decoding="async" width="200" height="150" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="800" data-file-height="600"></noscript><span class="lazy-image-placeholder" style="width: 200px;height: 150px;" data-src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cc/Corning_Conaphore_y.jpg/200px-Corning_Conaphore_y.jpg" data-alt="" data-width="200" data-height="150" data-class="thumbimage"> </span></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Corning_Conaphore_y.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>One of the first optic <a href="/wiki/Headlamp" title="Headlamp">headlamp</a> lenses, the Corning Conaphore. <a href="/wiki/Selective_yellow" title="Selective yellow">Selective yellow</a> "Noviol" glass version shown.</div></div></div>
<p>Corning Glass Works was founded in 1851 by Amory Houghton, in <a href="/wiki/Somerville,_Massachusetts" title="Somerville, Massachusetts">Somerville</a>, <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts" title="Massachusetts">Massachusetts</a>, originally as the Bay State Glass Co. It later moved to <a href="/wiki/Williamsburg,_Brooklyn" title="Williamsburg, Brooklyn">Williamsburg</a>, <a href="/wiki/Brooklyn" title="Brooklyn">Brooklyn</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_York_(state)" title="New York (state)">New York</a>, and operated as the Brooklyn Flint Glass Works. The company moved again to its ultimate home and namesake, the city of <a href="/wiki/Corning_(city),_New_York" title="Corning (city), New York">Corning</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_York_(state)" title="New York (state)">New York</a>, in 1868 under leadership of the founder's son, Amory Houghton, Jr.
</p><p>Over 147 years later, Corning continues to maintain its world headquarters at Corning, N.Y. The firm also established one of the first industrial research labs there in 1908. It continues to expand the nearby <a href="/wiki/Research_and_development" title="Research and development">research and development</a> facility, as well as operations associated with <a href="/wiki/Catalytic_converters" class="mw-redirect" title="Catalytic converters">catalytic converters</a> and <a href="/wiki/Diesel_particulate_filter" title="Diesel particulate filter">diesel engine filter</a> product lines. Corning has a long history of <a href="/wiki/Community_development" title="Community development">community development</a> and has assured community leaders that it intends to remain headquartered in its small upstate New York hometown.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p><p>The <a href="/wiki/California_Institute_of_Technology" title="California Institute of Technology">California Institute of Technology</a>'s 200-inch (5.1 m) telescope mirror at <a href="/wiki/Palomar_Observatory" title="Palomar Observatory">Palomar Observatory</a> was cast by Corning during 1934–1936 out of low expansion borosilicate glass.<sup id="cite_ref-Caltec_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Caltec-12">[12]</a></sup>  In 1932, <a href="/wiki/George_Ellery_Hale" title="George Ellery Hale">George Ellery Hale</a> approached <a href="/wiki/Corning_Incorporated" class="mw-redirect" title="Corning Incorporated">Corning</a> with the challenge of fabricating the required optic for his Palomar project.  A previous effort to fabricate the optic from <a href="/wiki/Fused_quartz" title="Fused quartz">fused quartz</a> had failed.  Corning's first attempt was a failure, the cast blank having voids.  Using lessons learned, Corning was successful in the casting of the second blank.  After a year of cooling, during which it was almost lost to a flood, in 1935 the blank was completed.  The first blank now resides in <a href="/wiki/Corning_Museum_of_Glass" title="Corning Museum of Glass">Corning's Museum of Glass.</a>
</p><p>In 1935, Corning formed a partnership with bottle maker <a href="/wiki/Owens-Illinois" class="mw-redirect" title="Owens-Illinois">Owens-Illinois</a>, which formed the company known today as <a href="/wiki/Owens_Corning" title="Owens Corning">Owens Corning</a>.  Owens Corning was spun off as a separate company in 1938.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Corning_Conaphore.jpg" class="image"><noscript><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a2/Corning_Conaphore.jpg/200px-Corning_Conaphore.jpg" decoding="async" width="200" height="290" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="1500" data-file-height="2175"></noscript><span class="lazy-image-placeholder" style="width: 200px;height: 290px;" data-src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a2/Corning_Conaphore.jpg/200px-Corning_Conaphore.jpg" data-alt="" data-width="200" data-height="290" data-class="thumbimage"> </span></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Corning_Conaphore.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>1917 advertisement for the Corning Conaphore headlamp lens shown above.</div></div></div>
<p>The company had a history of science-based innovations following <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a> and the strategy by management was research and "disruptive" and "on demand" product innovation.<sup id="cite_ref-clarke_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-clarke-13">[13]</a></sup></p><p>In 1962 Corning developed Chemcor, a new toughened automobile <a href="/wiki/Windshield" title="Windshield">windshield</a> designed to be thinner and lighter than existing windshields, which reduced danger of personal injury by shattering into small granules when smashed.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> This <a href="/wiki/Toughened_glass" class="mw-redirect" title="Toughened glass">toughened glass</a> had a chemically hardened outer layer, and its manufacture incorporated an ion exchange and a "fusion process" in special furnaces that Corning built in its <a href="/wiki/Blacksburg,_Virginia" title="Blacksburg, Virginia">Blacksburg, Virginia</a> facility.<sup id="cite_ref-clarke_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-clarke-13">[13]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-dyer_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dyer-15">[15]</a></sup> Corning developed it as an alternative to <a href="/wiki/Laminated_glass" title="Laminated glass">laminated</a> windshields with the intention of becoming an <a href="/wiki/Automotive_industry" title="Automotive industry">automotive industry</a> supplier.<sup id="cite_ref-clarke_13-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-clarke-13">[13]</a></sup> After being installed as side glass in a limited run of 1968 Plymouth Barracudas and Dodge Darts, Chemcor windshields debuted on the 1970 model year <a href="/wiki/AMC_Javelin" title="AMC Javelin">Javelins</a> and <a href="/wiki/AMC_AMX" title="AMC AMX">AMXs</a> built by <a href="/wiki/American_Motors" class="mw-redirect" title="American Motors">American Motors Corporation (AMC)</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-dyer_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dyer-15">[15]</a></sup> As there were no mandatory safety standards for motor vehicle windshields, the larger automakers had no financial incentive to change from the cheaper existing products.<sup id="cite_ref-clarke_13-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-clarke-13">[13]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-dyer_15-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dyer-15">[15]</a></sup> Corning terminated its windshield project in 1971, after it turned out to be one of the company's "biggest and most expensive failures."<sup id="cite_ref-dyer_15-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dyer-15">[15]</a></sup>  However, like many Corning innovations, the unique process to manufacture this automotive glass was resurrected and is today the basis of their very profitable LCD glass business.
</p><p>In the fall of 1970, the company announced that researchers Robert D. Maurer, Donald Keck, Peter C. Schultz, and Frank Zimar had demonstrated an <a href="/wiki/Optical_fiber_cable" class="mw-redirect" title="Optical fiber cable">optical fiber</a> with a low optical attenuation of 17 dB per kilometer by <a href="/wiki/Doping_(semiconductor)" title="Doping (semiconductor)">doping</a> <a href="/wiki/Silica_glass" class="mw-redirect" title="Silica glass">silica glass</a> with <a href="/wiki/Titanium" title="Titanium">titanium</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> A few years later they produced a fiber with only 4 dB/km, using <a href="/wiki/Germanium_oxide" title="Germanium oxide">germanium oxide</a> as the core <a href="/wiki/Dopant" title="Dopant">dopant</a>. Such low attenuations made <a href="/wiki/Fiber_optics" class="mw-redirect" title="Fiber optics">fiber optics</a> practical for <a href="/wiki/Telecommunications" class="mw-redirect" title="Telecommunications">telecommunications</a> and <a href="/wiki/Computer_network" title="Computer network">networking</a>. Corning became the world's leading manufacturer of optical fiber.
</p><p>In 1977, considerable attention was given to Corning's Z Glass project. Z Glass was a product used in television picture tubes. Due to a number of factors, the exact nature of which are subject to dispute, this project was considered a steep loss in profit and productivity. The following year the project made a partial recovery. This incident has been cited as a case study by the Harvard School of Business.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p><p>Company profits soared in the late 1990s during the <a href="/wiki/Dot-com_company" title="Dot-com company">dot-com</a> boom, and Corning expanded its fiber operations significantly with several new plants.  The company also entered the <a href="/wiki/Photonics" title="Photonics">photonics</a> market, investing heavily with the intent of becoming the leading provider of complete fiber-optic systems.  Failure to succeed in photonics and the <a href="/wiki/Dot-com_bubble#The_bubble_bursts" title="Dot-com bubble">collapse in 2000 of the dot-com market</a> had a major impact on the company, and Corning stock plummeted to $1 per share. However, as of 2007<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Corning_Inc.&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup> the company had posted five straight years of improving financial performance.
</p>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(2)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Current_technologies">Current technologies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Corning_Inc.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Current technologies" data-section="2" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-2 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-2">

Later, other companies also adopted its <a href="/wiki/Gorilla_glass" class="mw-redirect" title="Gorilla glass">Gorilla glass</a> screen. In 2019, rumours also came that Corning was making folding glass for <a href="/wiki/Apple_Inc." title="Apple Inc.">Apple</a> for its folding <a href="/wiki/IPad" title="IPad">iPad</a> and <a href="/wiki/IPhone" title="IPhone">iPhone</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup>
In 2011 Corning announced the expansion of existing facilities and the construction of a Gen 10 facility co-located with the <a href="/wiki/Sharp_Corporation" title="Sharp Corporation">Sharp Corporation</a> manufacturing complex in <a href="/wiki/Sakai,_Osaka" class="mw-redirect" title="Sakai, Osaka">Sakai, Osaka</a>, Japan.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> The LCD glass substrate is produced without heavy metals. Corning is a leading manufacturer of the glass used in <a href="/wiki/Liquid_crystal_display" class="mw-redirect" title="Liquid crystal display">liquid crystal displays</a><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>.
</p><p>The company continues to produce optical fiber and cable for the communications industry at its <a href="/wiki/Wilmington,_North_Carolina" title="Wilmington, North Carolina">Wilmington</a> and <a href="/wiki/Concord,_North_Carolina" title="Concord, North Carolina">Concord</a> plants in North Carolina. It is also a major manufacturer of ceramic emission control devices for <a href="/wiki/Catalytic_converter" title="Catalytic converter">catalytic converters</a> in cars and light trucks that use gasoline engines. The company is also investing in the production of ceramic emission control products for diesel engines as a result of tighter emission standards for those engines both in the U.S. and abroad.
</p><p>In 2007 Corning introduced an optic fiber, <a href="/wiki/ClearCurve" title="ClearCurve">ClearCurve</a>, which uses nanostructure technology to facilitate the small radius bending found in <a href="/wiki/FTTX" class="mw-redirect" title="FTTX">FTTX</a> installations.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Nokia_N8_gorilla_glass_screen_(5083750412).jpg" class="image"><noscript><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/05/Nokia_N8_gorilla_glass_screen_%285083750412%29.jpg/220px-Nokia_N8_gorilla_glass_screen_%285083750412%29.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="3648" data-file-height="2736"></noscript><span class="lazy-image-placeholder" style="width: 220px;height: 165px;" data-src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/05/Nokia_N8_gorilla_glass_screen_%285083750412%29.jpg/220px-Nokia_N8_gorilla_glass_screen_%285083750412%29.jpg" data-alt="" data-width="220" data-height="165" data-class="thumbimage"> </span></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Nokia_N8_gorilla_glass_screen_(5083750412).jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Corning's <a href="/wiki/Gorilla_Glass" title="Gorilla Glass">Gorilla Glass</a> is used on the <a href="/wiki/Nokia_N8" title="Nokia N8">Nokia N8</a>'s screen</div></div></div>
<p>Gorilla Glass, an outgrowth of the 1960s Chemcor project, is a high-strength alkali-<a href="/wiki/Aluminosilicate" title="Aluminosilicate">aluminosilicate</a> thin <a href="/wiki/Sheet_glass" class="mw-redirect" title="Sheet glass">sheet glass</a> used as a protective cover glass offering scratch resistance and durability in many touchscreens.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> According to the book <i>Steve Jobs</i> by Walter Isaacson, Gorilla Glass was used in the first iPhone released in 2007.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p><p>On October 25, 2011 Corning unveiled <a href="/wiki/Lotus_Glass" class="mw-redirect" title="Lotus Glass">Lotus Glass</a>, an environmentally friendly and high-performance glass developed for <a href="/wiki/OLED" title="OLED">OLED</a> and <a href="/wiki/LCD" class="mw-redirect" title="LCD">LCD</a> displays.<sup id="cite_ref-Corning-Feb-2012-10-K_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Corning-Feb-2012-10-K-22">[22]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p><p>Corning invests about 10% of revenue in <a href="/wiki/Research_and_development" title="Research and development">research and development</a>, and has allocated <a href="/wiki/US%24" class="mw-redirect" title="US$">US$</a>300 million towards further expansion of its Sullivan Park research facility near headquarters in Corning, New York.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p><p>Corning Incorporated manufactures a high-purity fused silica employed in microlithography systems, a low expansion glass utilized in the construction of reflective mirror blanks, windows for U.S. <a href="/wiki/Space_shuttle" class="mw-redirect" title="Space shuttle">space shuttles</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Steuben_art_glass" class="mw-redirect" title="Steuben art glass">Steuben art glass</a>. The number of Corning facilities employing the traditional tanks of molten glass has declined over the years, but it maintains the capacity to supply bulk or finished glass of many types.
</p><p>Corning is engaged in research and development on green <a href="/wiki/Lasers" class="mw-redirect" title="Lasers">lasers</a>, mercury abatement, <a href="/wiki/Microreactors" class="mw-redirect" title="Microreactors">microreactors</a>, photovoltaics, and silicon on glass. Through its Life Sciences division, the company offers products to support life science research, including <a href="/wiki/Stem_cell" title="Stem cell">stem-cell</a> culture products.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p><p>In September 2019, Apple announced that it would invest $250 million to Corning, in an effort to develop and manufacture the glass needed for many of its products, including the iPhone, Apple Watch, and iPad. Though not confirmed by either company, the investment could be used to develop new products in the future. Apple has already invested $200 million to Corning back in 2017.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(3)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_activities">Other activities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Corning_Inc.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Other activities" data-section="3" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-3 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-3">
<p>Corning employs roughly 51,500 people worldwide and had sales of $11.29 billion in 2018.<sup id="cite_ref-s22.q4cdn.com_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-s22.q4cdn.com-2">[2]</a></sup> The company has been listed for many years among <a href="/wiki/Fortune_magazine" class="mw-redirect" title="Fortune magazine"><i>Fortune</i> magazine's</a> 500 largest companies, and was ranked #297 in 2015.
</p><p>Although the company has long been publicly owned, <a href="/wiki/James_R._Houghton" title="James R. Houghton">James R. Houghton</a>, great-great-grandson of the founder, served as chairman of the board of directors from 2001 to 2007. Over the years Houghton family ownership has declined to about 2%. <a href="/wiki/Wendell_P._Weeks" class="mw-redirect" title="Wendell P. Weeks">Wendell P. Weeks</a> has been with the company since 1983 and as of March 2013<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Corning_Inc.&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup> was chairman, chief executive officer, and president.<sup id="cite_ref-Corning-Mar-2013-DEF-14A_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Corning-Mar-2013-DEF-14A-27">[27]</a></sup></p><p>Over its 160-year history Corning invented a process for rapid and inexpensive production of <a href="/wiki/Light_bulb" class="mw-redirect" title="Light bulb">light bulbs</a>, including developing the glass for <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Edison" title="Thomas Edison">Thomas Edison</a>'s light bulb. Corning was the glass supplier for lightbulbs for <a href="/wiki/General_Electric" title="General Electric">General Electric</a> after <a href="/wiki/Edison_General_Electric" class="mw-redirect" title="Edison General Electric">Edison General Electric</a> merged with <a href="/wiki/Thomson-Houston_Electric_Company" title="Thomson-Houston Electric Company">Thomson-Houston Electric Company</a> in 1892.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> It was an early major manufacturer of glass panels and funnels for television tubes, invented and produced <a href="/wiki/Vycor" title="Vycor">Vycor</a> (high temperature glass with high thermal shock resistance). Corning invented and produced <a href="/wiki/Pyrex" title="Pyrex">Pyrex</a>, <a href="/wiki/CorningWare" title="CorningWare">CorningWare</a> <a href="/wiki/Pyroceram" title="Pyroceram">Pyroceram</a> glass-ceramic cookware, and <a href="/wiki/Corelle" title="Corelle">Corelle</a> durable glass dinnerware. Corning manufactured the windows for US manned <a href="/wiki/Space_vehicle" title="Space vehicle">space vehicles</a>, and supplied the glass blank for the primary mirror in the <a href="/wiki/Hubble_Space_Telescope" title="Hubble Space Telescope">Hubble Space Telescope</a>.
</p><p>In July 2008 Corning announced the sale of <a href="/wiki/Steuben_Glass_Works" title="Steuben Glass Works">Steuben Glass Works</a> to Steuben Glass LLC, an affiliate of the <a href="/wiki/Private_equity_firm" title="Private equity firm">private equity firm</a> <a href="/wiki/Schottenstein_Stores_Corp." class="mw-redirect" title="Schottenstein Stores Corp.">Schottenstein Stores Corporation</a>.  Steuben Glass had been unprofitable for more than a decade, losing 30 million dollars over the previous five years.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p><p>In February 2011, Corning acquired MobileAccess Networks, an <a href="/wiki/Israel" title="Israel">Israeli</a> company that develops <a href="/wiki/Distributed_antenna_system" title="Distributed antenna system">Distributed antenna systems</a>, which are often used by universities, stadiums and airports to ensure seamless wireless coverage throughout a facility. MobileAccess Networks became part of Corning's telecommunications business unit.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> In July 2017, Corning acquired <a href="/wiki/SpiderCloud_Wireless" title="SpiderCloud Wireless">SpiderCloud Wireless</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
