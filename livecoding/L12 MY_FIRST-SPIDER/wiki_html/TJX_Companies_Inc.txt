</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(1)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=TJX_Companies&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History" data-section="1" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-1 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-1">
<p>Stanley Feldberg was one of the 1956 founders of Zayre Corp. He served as president of the company until 1978, and afterwards remained on the Board of Directors, until he retired in 1989.<sup id="cite_ref-2004rep_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2004rep-8">[8]</a></sup>  Once the company had sold off the "Zayre" name, the company consisted of its one core remaining store brand, T.J.Maxx.<sup id="cite_ref-2004rep_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2004rep-8">[8]</a></sup>  The next year, in 1990, TJX expanded into an additional store brand division, and at the same time it first went international, as it entered the Canadian market by acquiring the five-store Winners chain.<sup id="cite_ref-2004rep_8-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2004rep-8">[8]</a></sup> Two years later, it launched its third brand, HomeGoods, in the United States.<sup id="cite_ref-2004rep_8-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2004rep-8">[8]</a></sup>  TJX's expansion beyond North America came in 1994, when the fourth brand division, T.K. Maxx, was founded in the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>, and then expanded into <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2004rep_8-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2004rep-8">[8]</a></sup></p><p>In 1995, TJX doubled in size when it acquired Marshalls, its fifth brand. T.J.Maxx and Marshalls later became consolidated as two brands under a single division, The Marmaxx Group.<sup id="cite_ref-2004rep_8-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2004rep-8">[8]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> The following year, TJX Companies Inc. was added to the <a href="/wiki/Standard_%26_Poor" class="mw-redirect" title="Standard &amp; Poor">Standard &amp; Poor</a>'s <a href="/wiki/S%26P_500" class="mw-redirect" title="S&amp;P 500">S&amp;P 500</a> Composite Index, which consists of 500 of the largest companies in the United States.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p><p>TJX launched a sixth brand, A.J. Wright, in 1998 in the eastern U.S. The brand went national in 2004 when it opened its first stores in California on the west coast.<sup id="cite_ref-2004rep_8-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2004rep-8">[8]</a></sup> The company's seventh brand division, HomeSense, formed in 2001, was a Canadian brand modeled after the existing US brand, HomeGoods.<sup id="cite_ref-2004rep_8-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2004rep-8">[8]</a></sup></p><p>In 2002, TJX revenue reached almost $12 billion.<sup id="cite_ref-2004rep_8-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2004rep-8">[8]</a></sup> In mid-2003, TJX acquired an eighth brand division, Bob's Stores, concentrated in <a href="/wiki/New_England" title="New England">New England</a>. In Canada, TJX began to configure some Winners and HomeSense stores side-by-side as superstores. The superstores feature open passageways between them, with dual branding. TJX's revenue in 2003 reached over $13 billion.<sup id="cite_ref-2004rep_8-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2004rep-8">[8]</a></sup> TJX began to test the side-by-side superstore model in the United States in 2004, combining some of each of the two Marmaxx brand stores with HomeGoods.  The company reached 141st position in the 2004 <a href="/wiki/Fortune_500" title="Fortune 500">Fortune 500</a> rankings, with almost $15 billion in revenue. That year was also marked by the death of retired Zayre founder Stanley Feldberg.<sup id="cite_ref-2004rep_8-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2004rep-8">[8]</a></sup></p><p>In April 2008, TJX launched the <a href="/wiki/HomeSense" title="HomeSense">HomeSense</a> brand in the UK, with six stores opening throughout May. The brand is more upmarket than its Canadian namesake. Later that year, in August, TJX sold <a href="/wiki/Bob%27s_Stores" title="Bob's Stores">Bob's Stores</a> to Versa Capital Management and Crystal Capital.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p><p>In December 2010, TJX announced that the A.J. Wright stores would be closed, cutting about 4,400 jobs, and that more than half of them would reopen under other company brands.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p><p>In July 2015, TJX acquired the Trade Secret and Home Secret off-price retail businesses from Australian company Gazal Corporation Limited. The deal was completed in December.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> In October, Ernie Herrman was named CEO of the company, replacing <a href="/wiki/Carol_Meyrowitz" title="Carol Meyrowitz">Carol Meyrowitz</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-14">[14]</a></sup> He took over in January 2016.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-14">[14]</a></sup></p><p>In November 2019, TJX purchased a 25% stake in Russian retailer Familia.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Computer_systems_intrusion">Computer systems intrusion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=TJX_Companies&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Computer systems intrusion" data-section="2" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>On January 17, 2007, TJX announced that it was the victim of an <a href="/wiki/Hacker_(computer_security)" class="mw-redirect" title="Hacker (computer security)">unauthorized computer systems intrusion</a>. It discovered in mid-December 2006 that its computer systems were compromised and <a href="/wiki/Identity_theft" title="Identity theft">customer data was stolen</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> The hackers accessed a system that stores data on <a href="/wiki/Credit_card" title="Credit card">credit card</a>, <a href="/wiki/Debit_card" title="Debit card">debit card</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cheque" title="Cheque">check</a>, and merchandise return transactions.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> The intrusion was kept confidential as requested by law enforcement. TJX said that it is working with <a href="/wiki/General_Dynamics" title="General Dynamics">General Dynamics</a>, <a href="/wiki/IBM" title="IBM">IBM</a> and <a href="/wiki/Deloitte" title="Deloitte">Deloitte</a> to upgrade computer security.
</p><p>By the end of March 2007, the number of affected customers had reached 45.7 million<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> and has prompted <a href="/wiki/Credit_bureau" title="Credit bureau">credit bureaus</a> to seek legislation requiring retailers to be responsible for compromised customer information saved in their systems. In addition to credit card numbers, personal information such as <a href="/wiki/Social_security_number" class="mw-redirect" title="Social security number">social security numbers</a> and <a href="/wiki/Driver%27s_license" title="Driver's license">driver's license</a> numbers from 451,000 customers were downloaded by the intruders. The breach was possible due to a non-secure <a href="/wiki/Wireless_network" title="Wireless network">wireless network</a> in one of the stores.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p><p>Eleven men have been charged in the theft, and one (Damon Patrick Toey) has pleaded guilty to numerous charges related to the breach.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> One man, <a href="/wiki/Jonathan_James" title="Jonathan James">Jonathan James</a>, professed his innocence and later committed suicide, apparently out of the belief that he was going to be indicted.<sup id="cite_ref-wired1_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wired1-21">[21]</a></sup></p><p>The alleged ringleader <a href="/wiki/Albert_Gonzalez" title="Albert Gonzalez">Albert Gonzalez</a> was indicted in August 2009 with attacking <a href="/wiki/Heartland_Payment_Systems" title="Heartland Payment Systems">Heartland Payment Systems</a> in which 130 million records were compromised.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
