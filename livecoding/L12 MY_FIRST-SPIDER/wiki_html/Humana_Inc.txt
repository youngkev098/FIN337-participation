title="Medicare (United States)">Medicare</a> recipients that the <a href="/wiki/AARP" title="AARP">AARP</a> characterized as deceptive. The company's managed care model has also been criticized for ethical lapses and limitations.
</p><p>The health insurer <a href="/wiki/Aetna" title="Aetna">Aetna</a> said on July 3, 2015, that it had agreed to acquire its smaller rival Humana for $37 billion in cash and stock<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> but walked away from the deal after a court ruling that the merger would be anti-competitive.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>

</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(1)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Humana&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History" data-section="1" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-1 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-1">
<h3 class="in-block"><span id="1961.E2.80.931983:_Nursing_homes_and_hospitals"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="1961–1983:_Nursing_homes_and_hospitals">1961–1983: Nursing homes and hospitals</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Humana&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: 1961–1983: Nursing homes and hospitals" data-section="2" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>Lawyers David A. Jones Sr. and <a href="/wiki/Wendell_Cherry" title="Wendell Cherry">Wendell Cherry</a> founded a <a href="/wiki/Nursing_home" class="mw-redirect" title="Nursing home">nursing home</a> company in 1961. The company, known in 1968 as Extendicare Inc., became the largest nursing home company in the United States. In 1972, Jones and Cherry sold the nursing home chain, to purchase <a href="/wiki/Hospitals" class="mw-redirect" title="Hospitals">hospitals</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-biz_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-biz-9">[9]</a></sup></p><p>In 1974, the partners changed the corporate name to Humana Inc.<sup id="cite_ref-biz_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-biz-9">[9]</a></sup> The name was meant to change public perception from 'warehousing' or indifferently treating people to providing a higher-level of human care and, by extension, more humane care.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> It grew in the following years, both by business and in 1978 through the takeover of American Medicorp Inc.,<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> which doubled the company's size, and growing into the world's largest hospital company in the 1980s.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> During this period, Humana developed the double corridor model for hospital construction. This design minimized the distance between patients and nurses by placing nursing support services in the interior of the building with patient rooms surrounding the perimeter.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span id="1984.E2.80.93present"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="1984–present">1984–present</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Humana&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: 1984–present" data-section="3" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>As the <a href="/wiki/Health_care_in_the_United_States" title="Health care in the United States">American health care system</a> changed in the 1980s, "one of its hospitals in Arizona lost a contract with the largest health-maintenance organization in the area [and] Humana created its own <a href="/wiki/Health_insurance" title="Health insurance">health insurance</a> plan."<sup id="cite_ref-biz_9-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-biz-9">[9]</a></sup></p><p>In 1993, Humana had become the largest hospital operator in the country, owning 77 hospitals. Humana executives spun off hospital operations from health insurance operations to create Galen Health Care. The following year they sold the 73 hospitals of Galen Health Care Inc. to Nashville-based Columbia <a href="/wiki/Hospital_Corporation_of_America" class="mw-redirect" title="Hospital Corporation of America">Hospital Corporation of America</a> for $3.4 billion.<sup id="cite_ref-biz_9-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-biz-9">[9]</a></sup></p><p>In 1998, one year after Jones had stepped aside as CEO, <a href="/wiki/UnitedHealth_Group" title="UnitedHealth Group">United Healthcare</a> made an unsuccessful attempt to acquire Humana.<sup id="cite_ref-biz_9-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-biz-9">[9]</a></sup> Humana pulled out of the acquisition after United stock dropped $2.9 billion in value.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> In 2001, Humana was a cofounder of Availity.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p><p>In 2005, Humana entered into a <a href="/wiki/Partnership" title="Partnership">business partnership</a> with <a href="/wiki/Virgin_Group" title="Virgin Group">Virgin Group</a>, offering financial incentives to members for healthy behavior, such as regular exercise.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p><p>On November 16, 2006, the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC) and Humana Inc. partnered to expand on traditional private-sector approaches to population health management.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p><p>In 2006, Humana launched an education campaign to market <a href="/wiki/Medicare_(United_States)#Part_C:_Medicare_Advantage_plans" title="Medicare (United States)">Medicare Advantage</a> (MA) and <a href="/wiki/Medicare_Part_D" title="Medicare Part D">Prescription Drug Plans</a> (PDP) nationwide to Medicare-eligible consumers, following the passage of the <a href="/wiki/Medicare_modernization_act" class="mw-redirect" title="Medicare modernization act">Medicare modernization act</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
The same year, Humana launched RightSource, a national <a href="/wiki/Mail-order" class="mw-redirect" title="Mail-order">mail-order</a> retail <a href="/wiki/Pharmacy" title="Pharmacy">pharmacy</a> business.<sup id="cite_ref-biz_9-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-biz-9">[9]</a></sup>
In its March 2008 issue, <i><a href="/wiki/Fortune_Magazine" class="mw-redirect" title="Fortune Magazine">Fortune Magazine</a></i> named Humana one of the Top 5 Most Admired Healthcare Companies in the United States.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p><p>In 2010, Humana bought Texas-based <a href="/wiki/Concentra" title="Concentra">Concentra</a> Inc., which owns urgent-care and physical therapy centers, for $790 million, effectively returning to healthcare services.<sup id="cite_ref-biz_9-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-biz-9">[9]</a></sup> In May 2011, Humana announced it would be using <a href="/wiki/MobileStorm" title="MobileStorm">mobileStorm</a> to transmit protected health information to patients.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p><p>In March 2015, Humana announced the sale of Concentra to private equity firm <a href="/wiki/Welsh,_Carson,_Anderson_%26_Stowe" title="Welsh, Carson, Anderson &amp; Stowe">Welsh, Carson, Anderson &amp; Stowe</a> and <a href="/wiki/Select_Medical_Holdings_Corporation" class="mw-redirect" title="Select Medical Holdings Corporation">Select Medical Holdings Corporation</a> for about $1 billion, with proceeds to fund a "$2 billion share buyback program and other corporate spending."<sup id="cite_ref-reu_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-reu-19">[19]</a></sup></p><p>In July 2015, <a href="/wiki/Aetna" title="Aetna">Aetna</a> announced that it would acquire Humana for $37 billion in cash and stock (approximately $230 a share at that time). Aetna and Humana shareholders would own 74% and 26% of the new combined company, however the merger was blocked by a federal judge in January 2017.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> In February 2017, <a href="/wiki/Aetna_Inc." class="mw-redirect" title="Aetna Inc.">Aetna Inc.</a> and Humana Inc. quashed a $34 billion merger agreement after judges ruled against the merger for a second time.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p><p>In July 2018, Humana joined two <a href="/wiki/Private_equity_firm" title="Private equity firm">private equity firms</a> in the acquisition of <a href="/wiki/Kindred_Healthcare" title="Kindred Healthcare">Kindred Healthcare</a>. The deal provided Humana with a 40% stake in the company's home health, hospice and community care businesses, called "Kindred at Home," for approximately $800 million.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p><p>In December 2019, the company announced it would acquire Enclara Healthcare from Consonance Capital Partners and Enclara management.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(2)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Corporate_affairs">Corporate affairs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Humana&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Corporate affairs" data-section="4" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-2 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-2">
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Sponsorship">Sponsorship</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Humana&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Sponsorship" data-section="5" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/LPGA" title="LPGA">LPGA</a> player <a href="/wiki/Nancy_Scranton" title="Nancy Scranton">Nancy Scranton</a> is a spokesperson for Humana.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> In the past, PGA Tour player <a href="/wiki/David_Toms" title="David Toms">David Toms</a>' David Toms Foundation has partnered with the Humana Foundation to provide grants to several children's charities in New Orleans.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> Humana is the official health benefits provider of the <a href="/wiki/PGA_Tour" title="PGA Tour">PGA Tour</a> and <a href="/wiki/Champions_Tour" class="mw-redirect" title="Champions Tour">Champions Tour</a>.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Humana_Distaff_Handicap" class="mw-redirect" title="Humana Distaff Handicap">Humana Distaff Handicap</a> is a <a href="/wiki/Group_races" title="Group races">Grade 1</a> race for <a href="/wiki/Thoroughbred" title="Thoroughbred">thoroughbred</a> <a href="/wiki/Fillies" class="mw-redirect" title="Fillies">fillies</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mare_(horse)" class="mw-redirect" title="Mare (horse)">mares</a>, four-years-old and up. The race is run each spring on <a href="/wiki/Kentucky_Derby" title="Kentucky Derby">Kentucky Derby</a> day at <a href="/wiki/Churchill_Downs" title="Churchill Downs">Churchill Downs</a> and set at a distance of 7 furlongs for a purse of $250,000.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p><p>Humana is the presenting sponsor of the <i><a href="/wiki/Grand_Ole_Opry" title="Grand Ole Opry">Grand Ole Opry</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p><p>Since 1979, Humana has been a principal sponsor of the annual <a href="/wiki/Humana_Festival_of_New_American_Plays" title="Humana Festival of New American Plays">Humana Festival of New American Plays</a> in Louisville, Kentucky.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Humana_Military_Healthcare_Services">Humana Military Healthcare Services</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Humana&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Humana Military Healthcare Services" data-section="6" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>In 1993, Humana founded Humana Military Healthcare Services (HMHS) as a wholly owned subsidiary.<sup id="cite_ref-biz_9-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-biz-9">[9]</a></sup></p><p>From 2004 to 2009, HMHS was the managed care contractor for the Department of Defense <a href="/wiki/Military_Health_System" title="Military Health System">Military Health System</a> <a href="/wiki/TRICARE" class="mw-redirect" title="TRICARE">TRICARE</a> South Region.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
In 2009, HMHS' Managed Care Support Contract was awarded to United Military and Veterans Services, a subsidiary of <a href="/wiki/UnitedHealth_Group" title="UnitedHealth Group">UnitedHealth Group</a>. HMHS protested that decision and the <a href="/wiki/Government_Accountability_Office" title="Government Accountability Office">Government Accountability Office</a> upheld the protest in late 2009.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
In 2011, HMHS regained the five-year contract to administer medical benefits to military members and families in the South region, a contract worth $23.5 billion.<sup id="cite_ref-biz_9-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-biz-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(3)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Controversy">Controversy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Humana&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Controversy" data-section="7" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-3 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-3">
<p>In 1987, Humana sued <a href="/wiki/NBC" title="NBC">NBC</a> over a story line in the television <a href="/wiki/Medical_drama" title="Medical drama">medical drama</a> <i><a href="/wiki/St._Elsewhere" title="St. Elsewhere">St. Elsewhere</a></i> in which the hospital was to be sold to a <a href="/wiki/Business" title="Business">for-profit</a> medical corporation and renamed "Ecumena", with subsequent changes to the hospital, both positive and negative, emanating from that change. Humana was successful at forcing NBC into showing a disclaimer at the beginning of the September 30 episode saying that the drama had no connection whatsoever with Humana.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p><p>On May 30, 1996, <a href="/wiki/Linda_Peeno" title="Linda Peeno">Linda Peeno</a>, a physician who was contracted to work for Humana for nine months, testified before <a href="/wiki/United_States_Congress" title="United States Congress">Congress</a> as to the downside of <a href="/wiki/Managed_care" title="Managed care">managed care</a>. Peeno said she was effectively rewarded by her employer for causing the death of a patient, because it saved the company a half-million dollars. Peeno stated that she felt the "managed care" model was inherently unethical.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p><p>In 1999, season one of Michael Moore's TV series <i><a href="/wiki/The_Awful_Truth_(TV_series)" title="The Awful Truth (TV series)">The Awful Truth</a></i> reported on Humana refusing to pay for a diabetic patient with <a href="/wiki/Pancreatic_failure" class="mw-redirect" title="Pancreatic failure">pancreatic failure</a> needing a transplant.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> The man's policy stated it covered all of his <a href="/wiki/Diabetes" title="Diabetes">diabetes</a>-related expenses, but another section of the policy stated that it did not cover organ transplants. Moore conducted a fake funeral on the front steps of the Humana building, and three days later Humana changed its policy and authorized the man's treatment..<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p><p><a href="/wiki/Michael_Moore" title="Michael Moore">Michael Moore</a>'s 2007 documentary <i><a href="/wiki/Sicko" title="Sicko">Sicko</a></i> used the video of Linda Peeno's testimony. On June 28, 2007, Humana declared that Peeno was never a Humana "associate" (permanent, full-time employee), but rather a "part-time contractor." Humana disputed portions of her Congressional testimony by saying that because the patient's healthcare plan did not cover heart transplants, denial of coverage was valid.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p><p>On September 21, 2009 the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Health_and_Human_Services" title="United States Department of Health and Human Services">U.S. Department of Health and Human Services</a> opened an investigation into Humana mass mailings to elderly Medicare recipients.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> The mail was made to appear to contain official information about <a href="/wiki/Medicare_Advantage" title="Medicare Advantage">Medicare Advantage</a> and prescription drug benefit information, but instead alleged that core Medicare benefits could be cut by the <a href="/wiki/Obama_administration" class="mw-redirect" title="Obama administration">Obama administration</a>'s healthcare reform,<sup id="cite_ref-Teo_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Teo-35">[35]</a></sup> a claim refuted by John Rother, <a href="/wiki/AARP" title="AARP">AARP</a>'s executive vice president.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> Douglas Elmendorf, the head of the <a href="/wiki/Congressional_Budget_Office" title="Congressional Budget Office">Congressional Budget Office</a> (CBO), supported the claim that Medicare benefits could be cut,<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> but his comments were in reference to just one of several congressional bills. CBO estimates of another healthcare reform bill found that changes to premiums would vary.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Centers_for_Medicare_and_Medicaid_Services" class="mw-redirect" title="Centers for Medicare and Medicaid Services">Centers for Medicare and Medicaid Services</a> instructed Humana to cease all such mailings to Medicare plan members pending an investigation.<sup id="cite_ref-Teo_35-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Teo-35">[35]</a></sup> HHS Secretary Kathleen Sebelius, in a letter to the insurance industry, threatened that bad actors may be excluded from new health insurance markets that were to open in 2014. Senate Republicans pointed out in a letter to Sebelius that a 1997 directive from the Centers for Medicare and Medicaid Services explicitly allowed HMOs to tell members about legislation and urge them to express opinions.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
