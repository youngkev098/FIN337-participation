</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(1)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=NetApp&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History" data-section="1" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-1 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-1">
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:NetApp_Headquarters_Sunnyvale.jpg" class="image"><noscript><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/05/NetApp_Headquarters_Sunnyvale.jpg/170px-NetApp_Headquarters_Sunnyvale.jpg" decoding="async" width="170" height="255" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="2608" data-file-height="3912"></noscript><span class="lazy-image-placeholder" style="width: 170px;height: 255px;" data-src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/05/NetApp_Headquarters_Sunnyvale.jpg/170px-NetApp_Headquarters_Sunnyvale.jpg" data-alt="" data-width="170" data-height="255" data-class="thumbimage"> </span></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:NetApp_Headquarters_Sunnyvale.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>NetApp headquarters in <a href="/wiki/Sunnyvale,_California" title="Sunnyvale, California">Sunnyvale, California</a></div></div></div>
<p>NetApp was founded in 1992 by <a href="/wiki/David_Hitz" title="David Hitz">David Hitz</a>, James Lau,<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> and Michael Malcolm<sup id="cite_ref-corpbrief_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-corpbrief-4">[4]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> as <b>Network Appliance, Inc.</b><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> At the time, its major competitor was <a href="/wiki/Auspex_Systems" title="Auspex Systems">Auspex Systems</a>. In 1994, NetApp received venture capital funding from <a href="/wiki/Sequoia_Capital" title="Sequoia Capital">Sequoia Capital</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> It had its <a href="/wiki/Initial_public_offering" title="Initial public offering">initial public offering</a> in 1995. NetApp thrived in the <a href="/wiki/Internet_bubble" class="mw-redirect" title="Internet bubble">internet bubble</a> years of the mid 1990s to 2001, during which the company grew to $1 billion in annual revenue. After the bubble burst, NetApp's revenues quickly declined to $800 million in its fiscal year 2002. Since then, the company's revenue has steadily climbed.
</p><p>In 2006, NetApp sold the NetCache product line to <a href="/wiki/Blue_Coat_Systems" title="Blue Coat Systems">Blue Coat Systems</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p><p>In 2008, Network Appliance officially changed its legal name to NetApp, Inc. reflecting the nickname by which it was already well-known.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p><p>On June 1, 2015, Tom Georgens stepped down as CEO and was replaced by George Kurian.<sup id="cite_ref-newceo_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-newceo-12">[12]</a></sup></p><p>In May 2018 NetApp announced its first End to End NVMe array called All Flash FAS A800 with release of ONTAP 9.4 software.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> NetApp claims over 1.3 million IOPS at 500 microseconds per high-availability pair; Read throughput of up to 300GBps (Giga Byte per second) per all-flash 24 node cluster and 50% higher IOPS and up to 34% lower latency by upgrading previous model A700 with ONTAP 9.4.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup>
In January 2019 Dave Hitz announced retirement from NetApp.
</p>

<li>2004 - <i>Spinnaker Networks</i> acquired for $300 million. Technologies from Spinnaker integrated into Data ONTAP GX and first released in 2006, later Data ONTAP GX become Clustered Data <a href="/wiki/ONTAP" title="ONTAP">ONTAP</a></li>
<li>2005 - <i>Alacritus</i> acquired for $11 million. The tape virtualization technology Alacritus brought to NetApp was integrated into the NetApp NearStore Virtual Tape Library (VTL) product line, introduced in 2006.</li>

<li>2006 - <i>Topio</i> acquired for $160 million. Software that helped replicate, recover, and protect data over any distance regardless of the underlying server or storage infrastructure. This technology became known as ReplicatorX (Open System SnapVault), and has since been abandoned.</li>
<li>2008 - <i>Onaro</i> acquired for $120 million. Storage service management software which helps customers manage storage more efficiently with guaranteed service levels for availability and performance. Onaro's SANscreen technology launched as such and probably later influencing NetApp OnCommand Insight.</li>
<li>2010 - <i>Bycast</i> acquired for between $20 million and $50 million. Technologies from Bycast gave start for <a href="/wiki/NetApp#StorageGRID" title="NetApp">StorageGRID</a> product</li>
<li>2011 - <i>Akorri</i> acquired for $60 million. Cross-domain analysis and advanced analytics to help customers manage, optimize, and plan performance and utilization across their data center infrastructure.</li>
<li>2011 - <i>Engenio</i> (LSI) acquired for $480 million. Engenio external storage systems business unit of LSI Corporation. Launched as NetApp <a href="/wiki/NetApp#E-Series" title="NetApp">NetApp E-Series</a> product line for</li>
<li>2012 - <i>Bycast</i>: Development of software for storage with the purpose of control on the petabyte level; global collections of images, videos, and records</li>
<li>2012 - <i>Cache IQ</i>: Development of NAS cache systems</li>
<li>2013 - <i>IonGrid</i>: A technology developer that allows iOS devices to access users and internal business applications through a secure connection</li>
<li>2014 - <i>SteelStore</i>: NetApp acquired <a href="/wiki/Riverbed_Technology" title="Riverbed Technology">Riverbed Technology</a>'s SteelStore line of data backup and protection products,<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> which it later renamed as <a href="/wiki/NetApp#AltaVault" title="NetApp">AltaVault</a><sup id="cite_ref-AltaVault_ComputerWeekly_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AltaVault_ComputerWeekly-17">[17]</a></sup> and then to Cloud Backup</li>
<li>2015 - <i>SolidFire</i>: In December 2015 (closing in January 2016), NetApp acquired founded in 2009 <a href="/wiki/Flash_storage" class="mw-redirect" title="Flash storage">flash storage</a> vendor <a href="/wiki/SolidFire" class="mw-redirect" title="SolidFire">SolidFire</a> for $870 million.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> with its Active IQ software available for end users as web-based GUI service for monitoring and prediction of storage systems performance and availability</li>
<li>2017 - <i>Plexistor</i>: NetApp first announced the acquisition of a company and technology called Plexistor in May 2017. Technologies from Plexistor gave start for <a href="/wiki/NetApp#Memory_Accelerated_Data" title="NetApp">MAX Data</a> product</li>
<li>2017 - <i>Greenqloud</i> with its Qstack product. A private startup company that created cloud services, orchestration and management platform for hybrid cloud and multi-cloud environments</li>
<li>2017 - <i>Immersive Partner Solutions</i>, a Littleton, Colo.-based developer of software to validate multiple converged infrastructures through their lifecycles</li>
<li>2018 - <i>StackPointCloud</i>: NetApp acquired StackPointCloud, a project for multi-cloud Kubernetes as-a-service and a contributor to the Kubernetes which gave start for <a href="/wiki/NetApp#Kubernetes_Service" title="NetApp">Kubernetes Service</a> product</li>
<li>2019 - <i>Cognigo</i>: Israeli AI-driven data compliance and security supplier<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></li>
<li>2020 - Talon - Cloud Data Storage company enabling data consolidation and security for enterprises.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></li></ul></section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(2)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Competition">Competition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=NetApp&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Competition" data-section="3" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-2 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-2">
<p>NetApp competes in the <a href="/wiki/Computer_data_storage" title="Computer data storage">computer data storage</a> hardware industry.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> In 2009, NetApp ranked second in <a href="/wiki/Market_capitalization" title="Market capitalization">market capitalization</a> in its industry behind <a href="/wiki/EMC_Corporation" class="mw-redirect" title="EMC Corporation">EMC Corporation</a>, now Dell EMC, and ahead of <a href="/wiki/Seagate_Technology" title="Seagate Technology">Seagate Technology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Western_Digital" title="Western Digital">Western Digital</a>, <a href="/wiki/Brocade_Communications_Systems" title="Brocade Communications Systems">Brocade</a>, <a href="/wiki/Imation" class="mw-redirect" title="Imation">Imation</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Quantum_Corporation" title="Quantum Corporation">Quantum</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> In total revenue of 2009, NetApp ranked behind EMC, Seagate, Western Digital, and ahead of Imation, Brocade, <a href="/wiki/Xyratex" class="mw-redirect" title="Xyratex">Xyratex</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Hutchinson_Technology" title="Hutchinson Technology">Hutchinson Technology</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> According to a 2014 IDC report, NetApp ranked second in the network storage industry "Big 5's list", behind EMC(DELL), and ahead of IBM, HP and Hitachi.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> According to Gartner's 2018 Magic Quadrant for Solid-State Arrays NetApp named a leader, behind only <a href="/wiki/Pure_Storage" title="Pure Storage">Pure Storage</a> Systems. In 2019, Gartner named NetApp as #1 in Primary Storage.
</p>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(3)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Products">Products</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=NetApp&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Products" data-section="4" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-3 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-3">
<p>NetApp's OnCommand management software controls and automates data-storage.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> ActiveIQ comes to NetApp with the acquisition of SolidFire. ActiveIQ is SaaS portal with built-in monitoring, prediction, recommendations for optimizing configurations and performance for NetApp storage systems based on machine-learning capabilities and artificial intelligence. Later ONTAP Analytics and Telemetry Service (OATS) product, which can be installed in AWS cloud and on-premise, was renamed to Active IQ Performance Analytics Services (ActiveIQ PAS).
</p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="NetApp_ONTAP-based_Hardware_Appliances">NetApp ONTAP-based Hardware Appliances</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=NetApp&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: NetApp ONTAP-based Hardware Appliances" data-section="5" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/NetApp_FAS" title="NetApp FAS">NetApp FAS</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Wikimedia_Foundation_Servers-8055_01.jpg" class="image"><noscript><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/41/Wikimedia_Foundation_Servers-8055_01.jpg/220px-Wikimedia_Foundation_Servers-8055_01.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="330" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="3456" data-file-height="5184"></noscript><span class="lazy-image-placeholder" style="width: 220px;height: 330px;" data-src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/41/Wikimedia_Foundation_Servers-8055_01.jpg/220px-Wikimedia_Foundation_Servers-8055_01.jpg" data-alt="" data-width="220" data-height="330" data-class="thumbimage"> </span></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Wikimedia_Foundation_Servers-8055_01.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>NetApp FAS3240 (second from bottom) with three DS4243 shelves on top</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:NetApp_AFF_A800.jpg" class="image"><noscript><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d0/NetApp_AFF_A800.jpg/220px-NetApp_AFF_A800.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="271" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="1039" data-file-height="1280"></noscript><span class="lazy-image-placeholder" style="width: 220px;height: 271px;" data-src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d0/NetApp_AFF_A800.jpg/220px-NetApp_AFF_A800.jpg" data-alt="" data-width="220" data-height="271" data-class="thumbimage"> </span></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:NetApp_AFF_A800.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>NetApp AFF A800 with 48 NVMe SSD drives</div></div></div>
<p>NetApp's FAS (Fabric-Attached Storage), AFF (All-Flash FAS), and ASA (All SAN Array) storage systems serve as the company's flagship products. Such products are made up of storage controllers, and one or more enclosures of hard disks, known as shelves. In entry-level systems, the drives may be physically located in the storage controller itself.
</p><p>In the early 1990s, NetApp's storage systems initially offered <a href="/wiki/Network_File_System_(protocol)" class="mw-redirect" title="Network File System (protocol)">NFS</a> and <a href="/wiki/Server_Message_Block" title="Server Message Block">SMB</a> protocols based on standard <a href="/wiki/Local_area_network" title="Local area network">local area networks</a> (LANs), whereas block storage consolidation required <a href="/wiki/Storage_area_network" title="Storage area network">storage area networks</a> (SANs) implemented with the <a href="/wiki/Fibre_Channel" title="Fibre Channel">Fibre Channel</a> (FC) protocol.
</p><p>In 2002, in an attempt to increase market share, NetApp added block-storage access as well, supporting the Fiber Channel and <a href="/wiki/ISCSI" title="ISCSI">iSCSI</a> protocols. As of 2016<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=NetApp&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup> NetApp systems support Fibre Channel, <a href="/wiki/ISCSI" title="ISCSI">iSCSI</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fibre_Channel_over_Ethernet" title="Fibre Channel over Ethernet">Fibre Channel over Ethernet</a> (FCoE) and the FC-NVMe protocol.
</p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="ONTAP">ONTAP</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=NetApp&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: ONTAP" data-section="6" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/ONTAP" title="ONTAP">ONTAP</a></div>
<p>NetApp AFF, FAS, ONTAP Select, Cloud Volumes ONTAP and Cloud Volume Services, Lenovo DM storage system series use NetApp's proprietary operating system called <a href="/wiki/ONTAP" title="ONTAP">ONTAP</a> which includes code from Berkeley Net/2 <a href="/wiki/BSD_Unix" class="mw-redirect" title="BSD Unix">BSD Unix</a>, Spinnaker Networks technology and other operating systems.<sup id="cite_ref-Is_Data_ONTAP_Based_On_UNIX_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Is_Data_ONTAP_Based_On_UNIX-27">[27]</a></sup> There are three <a href="/wiki/ONTAP#Platforms" title="ONTAP">ONTAP platforms</a>: FAS/AFF systems, software on commodity servers (<a href="/wiki/ONTAP#Select" title="ONTAP">ONTAP Select</a>) as virtual machine or in the cloud (<a href="/wiki/ONTAP#Cloud_Volumes_ONTAP" title="ONTAP">Cloud Volumes ONTAP</a>). All ONTAP systems using <a href="/wiki/Write_Anywhere_File_Layout" title="Write Anywhere File Layout">WAFL</a> file system which provide basis for snapshots and other snapshot-based and data protection technologies.
</p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Cloud_Backup">Cloud Backup</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=NetApp&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Cloud Backup" data-section="7" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>Previously known as Riverbed SteelStore after acquired by NetApp renamed to AltaVault and then to Cloud Backup. Cloud Backup was initially available in three forms: as a hardware appliance, virtual appliance, and cloud appliance. Later NetApp announced the end of sale for hardware and virtual appliances. Data placed on NAS share on Cloud Backup deduplicated, compressed, encrypted and transferred with Object Protocols to object storage systems like <a href="/wiki/Amazon_S3" title="Amazon S3">Amazon S3</a>, Azure Blob Storage or StorageGRID; thus Cloud Backup appears as a transparent gateway for archiving data to a private or public cloud. There are two modes of Cloud Backup operation: with local caching or without. Cloud Backup systems could accept SnapMirror replication from ONTAP systems.
</p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="HCI">HCI</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=NetApp&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: HCI" data-section="8" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:NetApp_HCI.png" class="image"><noscript><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/38/NetApp_HCI.png/220px-NetApp_HCI.png" decoding="async" width="220" height="99" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="1751" data-file-height="789"></noscript><span class="lazy-image-placeholder" style="width: 220px;height: 99px;" data-src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/38/NetApp_HCI.png/220px-NetApp_HCI.png" data-alt="" data-width="220" data-height="99" data-class="thumbimage"> </span></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:NetApp_HCI.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>NetApp HCI: two 2U HCI Chassis with four half-width blade servers at the bottom and one 1U storage node at the top</div></div></div>
<p>NetApp <a href="/wiki/Hyper-converged_infrastructure" title="Hyper-converged infrastructure">Hyper-converged infrastructure</a> (HCI) or sometimes referred by NetApp as Hybrid Cloud Infrastructure. NetApp HCI is based on commodity blade and rack servers, NetApp Element software and <a href="/wiki/VMware_vSphere" title="VMware vSphere">VMware vSphere</a>. NetApp HCI includes web-based GUI with installation wizard called NetApp Deployment Engine (NDE) for configuring vCenter, IP addresses, login &amp; password, and storage nodes, install NetApp Element plugin in vCenter to manage storage and configure NetApp HCI in about 30 minutes according to NetApp.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> NetApp HCI is different than conventional HCI designs because it has dedicated storage nodes, while other HCI systems like <a href="/wiki/Nutanix" title="Nutanix">Nutanix</a> or <a href="/wiki/Dell_EMC" title="Dell EMC">Dell EMC</a> VxRail or <a href="/wiki/VSAN" title="VSAN">vSAN</a> do not have dedicated storage nodes and utilize disk drives installed in each server. Dedicated storage nodes allow the cluster to grow or decrease storage capacity and performance separately from compute nodes. Minimum NetApp HCI configuration requires two compute blade server nodes and additionally, Element software requires a minimum of 4 storage nodes but is available to customers as four physical storage nodes or two physical storage nodes and two nodes as a virtual machine playing witness role on compute nodes.
</p>
<h4 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="2U_HCI_Chassis_with_four_half-width_blade_servers">2U HCI Chassis with four half-width blade servers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=NetApp&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: 2U HCI Chassis with four half-width blade servers" data-section="9" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:NetApp_HCI.jpg" class="image"><noscript><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2d/NetApp_HCI.jpg/220px-NetApp_HCI.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="98" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="1280" data-file-height="572"></noscript><span class="lazy-image-placeholder" style="width: 220px;height: 98px;" data-src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2d/NetApp_HCI.jpg/220px-NetApp_HCI.jpg" data-alt="" data-width="220" data-height="98" data-class="thumbimage"> </span></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:NetApp_HCI.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>NetApp HCI, two 2U HCI Chassis</div></div></div>
<p>Each storage node drive set consists of 6 SSD drives directly connected to a dedicated storage node and installed in front of the blade chassis, all drives in a set must be the same capacity. Each node either storage or compute server is one rack unit half-width, so minimum NetApp HCI configuration with storage nodes and servers consumes four rack units with six blade server slots used and two empty slots for expansion. Each storage and compute blade nodes have <a href="/wiki/25_Gigabit_Ethernet" title="25 Gigabit Ethernet">25 Gigabit Ethernet</a> ports which could be used as 10Gbit/s ports as well as dedicated 1Gb ports for management purposes. There are three types of compute nodes differentiated from each other by CPU power and memory capacity. Also, there are three types of storage nodes, differentiated from each other by SSD capacity and overall performance. It is possible to intermix any compute and storage nodes in a NetApp HCI installation, though with storage nodes it is required to have a minimum of 2 with the same capacity. It is also possible to use NetApp HCI with software other than VMware vSphere. Network switches were not included, and in NetApp HCI with Element software release 11 NetApp announced H-Series Switch as part of HCI solution, so all hardware components must be bought from NetApp. <a href="/wiki/ONTAP#ONTAP_Select" title="ONTAP">ONTAP Select</a> available as <a href="/wiki/Software-defined_storage" title="Software-defined storage">SDS</a> on NetApp HCI for customers interested in NAS protocols. The self-service portal allows automating common provisioning and management tasks without involving the IT team. NetApp Kuberneties Service will support NetApp HCI with the acquisition of Stackpoint. NetApp SolidFire storage and NetApp HCI can be expanded &amp; mixed in a single cluster. At the NetApp Insight 2018 conference in Las Vegas NetApp presented two new compute nodes: H410C and H610C, where H610C includes additional GPU cards which can be used in VDI environments. Starting with Element OS version 11, automatically detected and enabled by default with the upgrade, HCI has <i>Protection Domains</i> functionality to provide resiliency into HCI chassis. In the case of maintenance or chassis failure, helix algorithm spans data blocks workload will automatically fail-over to another operational chassis.
</p>

<p>SolidFire storage system uses OS called NetApp Element Software (formally SolidFire Element OS) based on <a href="/wiki/Linux" title="Linux">Linux</a> and designed for SSDs and scale-out architecture with the ability to expand up to 100 nodes and provide access to data through SAN protocols <a href="/wiki/ISCSI" title="ISCSI">iSCSI</a> natively and <a href="/wiki/Fiber_Channel" class="mw-redirect" title="Fiber Channel">Fiber Channel</a> with two gateway nodes. Element OS provides a REST-based API for storage automation, configuration, management, and consumption. SF node H610S has 12 2.5" NVMe SSD drives and can install only Element version 10.4, while previous models have 10 SSD drives. Element SW version 11, will not support FC. SolidFire uses iSCSI login redirection to distribute reads and writes across the cluster using helix algorithm.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> This architecture does not have disk shelves like traditional storage systems and expands with adding nodes to the cluster. Each node has pre-installed SSD drives. Each node can have only one type of SSD drives with the same capacity. Each SolidFire cluster can have a mix of different node models &amp; generations. Element X uses the replication factor of 2, where blocks of data spread across the cluster which has no performance impact but require more space in contrary to Erasure Coding technology. Such architecture allows users to expand performance and capacity separately as needed. Also, SolidFire has the ability to set three types of QoS for its <a href="/wiki/Logical_unit_number" title="Logical unit number">LUNs</a>: minimum, maximum and burst. Burst is used as credits which were not used by the LUN while it was not received its maximums. Element X available as software-only on commodity servers. SolidFire systems using <a href="/wiki/Amazon_S3" title="Amazon S3">S3</a> protocol could backup data to an <a href="/wiki/Object_storage" title="Object storage">Object storage</a> systems like StorageGRID. SolidFire could replicate data with SnapMirror protocol to ONTAP systems and starting with Element OS 11 to Cloud Volumes ONTAP. VEEAM backup &amp; Replication 9.5 Update 4 will implement seamless integration with NetApp HCI and Solidfire provide application consistent storage snapshot capabilities, Instant VM Recovery, and Single Item Restore for some applications.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> CommVault Simpana also provides application-consistent storage snapshot capability for NetApp HCI and Solidfire. All HCI configurations require at list 4 10/25 Gbit/s ports for connections until Element OS 11, where two ports are enough.
</p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="StorageGRID">StorageGRID</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=NetApp&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: StorageGRID" data-section="11" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:NetApp_SG6060_StorageGRID_Webscale.jpg" class="image"><noscript><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/72/NetApp_SG6060_StorageGRID_Webscale.jpg/220px-NetApp_SG6060_StorageGRID_Webscale.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="4032" data-file-height="3024"></noscript><span class="lazy-image-placeholder" style="width: 220px;height: 165px;" data-src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/72/NetApp_SG6060_StorageGRID_Webscale.jpg/220px-NetApp_SG6060_StorageGRID_Webscale.jpg" data-alt="" data-width="220" data-height="165" data-class="thumbimage"> </span></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:NetApp_SG6060_StorageGRID_Webscale.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>NetApp SG6060 StorageGRID Webscale</div></div></div>
<p>StorageGRID is a software-defined storage system which provides access to data via object IP-based protocols like <a href="/wiki/Amazon_S3" title="Amazon S3">S3</a> and <a href="/wiki/OpenStack_Swift" class="mw-redirect" title="OpenStack Swift">OpenStack Swift</a>. Available in form of hardware or as software. A node in a StorageGRID cluster is an appliance, virtual machine or docker container. StorageGRID is a geo-dispersed namespace clustered storage system, also known as "the grid", with an ability to make and store multiple copies (replicas) of objects (also known as Replication Factor) or in <a href="/wiki/Erasure_code" title="Erasure code">Erasure Coding</a> (EC) manner among cluster storage nodes with object granularity based on configured policies for data availability and durability purposes. StorageGRID stores metadata separately from the objects and allows users to configure data Life Cycle Management (ILM) policies on a per-object level to automatically satisfy and confirm changes in the cluster once changes introduced to the cluster like the cost of network usage, storage media usage changes a node was added or removed, etc. ONTAP, Cloud Backup, SANtricity, and Element X can replicate data to StorageGRID systems. SG6060 is optimized for high transactional throughput, MA, AI, and FabricPool.
</p>

</p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="E-Series">E-Series</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=NetApp&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: E-Series" data-section="13" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:NetApp_E5700.jpg" class="image"><noscript><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b4/NetApp_E5700.jpg/220px-NetApp_E5700.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="124" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="1280" data-file-height="720"></noscript><span class="lazy-image-placeholder" style="width: 220px;height: 124px;" data-src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b4/NetApp_E5700.jpg/220px-NetApp_E5700.jpg" data-alt="" data-width="220" data-height="124" data-class="thumbimage"> </span></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:NetApp_E5700.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>E5700 with 60 disk drives enclosure</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:DDP_vs_RAID6.png" class="image"><noscript><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a8/DDP_vs_RAID6.png/220px-DDP_vs_RAID6.png" decoding="async" width="220" height="160" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="1122" data-file-height="817"></noscript><span class="lazy-image-placeholder" style="width: 220px;height: 160px;" data-src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a8/DDP_vs_RAID6.png/220px-DDP_vs_RAID6.png" data-alt="" data-width="220" data-height="160" data-class="thumbimage"> </span></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:DDP_vs_RAID6.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>RAID comparison with DDP</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:DDP_D-Stripe_and_D-Piece.png" class="image"><noscript><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/82/DDP_D-Stripe_and_D-Piece.png/220px-DDP_D-Stripe_and_D-Piece.png" decoding="async" width="220" height="82" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="1814" data-file-height="679"></noscript><span class="lazy-image-placeholder" style="width: 220px;height: 82px;" data-src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/82/DDP_D-Stripe_and_D-Piece.png/220px-DDP_D-Stripe_and_D-Piece.png" data-alt="" data-width="220" data-height="82" data-class="thumbimage"> </span></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:DDP_D-Stripe_and_D-Piece.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>DDP components and data reconstruction process</div></div></div>
<p>Previously known as LSI Engenio <b>RDAC</b> after NetApp acquisition the product renamed to NetApp E-Series. It is a general-purpose enterprise storage system with two controllers for SAN protocols such as <a href="/wiki/Fibre_Channel" title="Fibre Channel">Fibre Channel</a>, <a href="/wiki/ISCSI" title="ISCSI">iSCSI</a>, <a href="/wiki/Serial_Attached_SCSI" title="Serial Attached SCSI">SAS</a> and <a href="/wiki/InfiniBand" title="InfiniBand">InfiniBand</a> (includes SRP, iSER, and NVMe over Fabrics protocol). NetApp E-Series platform uses proprietary OS SANtricity and proprietary <a href="/wiki/RAID" title="RAID">RAID</a> called Dynamic Disk Pool (DDP) alongside with traditional RAIDs like RAID 10, RAID 6, RAID 5, etc. In DDP pool each D-Stripe works similar to traditional <a href="/wiki/RAID#RAID-4" title="RAID">RAID-4</a> and <a href="/wiki/RAID#RAID-6" title="RAID">RAID-6</a> but on block level instead of entire disk level, therefore, have no dedicated parity drives. DDP compare to traditional RAID groups restores data from lost disk drive to multiple drives which provide a few times faster reconstruction time<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> while traditional RAIDs restores lost disk drive to a dedicated parity drive. Starting with SANtricity 11.50 E-Series systems EF570 and E5700 support <a href="/wiki/NVMe" class="mw-redirect" title="NVMe">NVMe</a> over Ethernet (<a href="/wiki/RDMA_over_Converged_Ethernet" title="RDMA over Converged Ethernet">RoCEv2</a>) with 100Gbit/s Ethernet ports and NVMe over InfiniBand. Starting with EF600 systems are end-to-end NVMe and capable of NVMe/FC in addition to NVMe/RoCE &amp; NVMe/InfiniBand. Sync and async mirroring are supported with SANtricity 11.50. SANtricity Unified Manager is a web-based manager that supports up to 500 EF/E-Series arrays and supports LDAP, RBAC, CA &amp; SSL for authorization &amp; authentication. In August 2019 NetApp announced E600 with support for NVMe/IB, NVMe/RoCE, NVMe/FC protocols, up to 44GBps of bandwidth and full-function embedded REST API.
</p>

<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Memory_Accelerated_Data">Memory Accelerated Data</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=NetApp&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Memory Accelerated Data" data-section="19" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>NetApp MAX Data for short. MAX Data is a proprietary <a href="/wiki/Linux" title="Linux">Linux</a> <a href="/wiki/File_system" title="File system">file system</a> with auto-tiering from PMEM to SSD and some enterprise data protection features. NetApp officially announced MAX Data product availability at NetApp Insight 2018 in October, supported with RHEL Server 7.5, CentOS 7.5, CentOS 7.6, RHEL Server 7.6 and RHEL Server 7.7; With version 1.2 MAX Data supports in-guest VM configuration for VMware vShpere starting with 6.7 U2 and in-guest iSCSI connection. Also, NetApp did some KVM testing while containers are in the long-term roadmap. MAX Data also can be run in the cloud, but currently, not supported. Starting with MAX Data 1.5 supported FAS &amp; ONTAP Select storages, also snapshots on server-only configurations supported. MAX Data came from the acquisition of Plexistor company in May 2017, Plexistor was founded in 2013 and positioned for Artificial Intelligence (Deep Learning &amp; Machine Learning, Fraud Prevention/Banking), Real-Time Analytics/Trading Platforms, Data Wherehouse, IoT workloads and applications like MongoDB, Cassandra DB, Couchbase, Oracle DB, JDE type databases, Spark, etc. but can run any applications which can use MAX Data file system. SAP HANA is currently qualifying whether MAX Data is suitable for the application but not supported yet.
</p>
<h4 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Technology_explanation">Technology explanation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=NetApp&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Technology explanation" data-section="20" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h4>
<p>MAX Data consists of two tiers: Tier 1 and Tier 2, where cold data destaged to Tier 2 from Tier 1 or promoted from Tier 2 to Tier 1 when accessed, by MAX Data tiering algorithm, transparently to the applications. Currently, NetApp has recommended ratio for MAX Data as 1 to 25 for Tier 1 and Tier 2 respectively. MAX Data according to NetApp<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> will have two modes: to use MAX Data as a POSIX-compatible (the internal name is M1FS) file system or as API memory extension. Usage of MAX Data as POSIX FS does not require application modifications while API memory extension requires applications to be modified in order to utilize this functionality. MAX Data installed on Linux hosts to utilize ultra-low latency with persistent memory such as the Optane DC persistent memory (Optane DCPMM), NVDIMM or DRAM (when persistence not needed, for example for testing purposes) memory for Tier 1 and a NetApp AFF storage system for Tier 2. Optane DCPMM is the Intel brand name of products that use 3D XPoint technology and supported starting with MAX Data version 1.3. MAX FS is a Persistent Memory-based Filesystem (PM-based FS) that doesn't require application modification but also can be a Direct Access enabled File system (DAX-enabled FS) for applications with optimization for Persistent Memory using SPDK. DAX is the mechanism that enables direct access to files stored in persistent memory arrays without the need to copy the data through the page cache.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> Optane DCPMM requires to have the second generation of <a href="/wiki/Intel_Xeon" class="mw-redirect" title="Intel Xeon">Intel Xeon</a> Scalable CPU. MAX FS based on ZUFS (Zero-copy User-mode File System) interface for the user-space file system and does not need a dedicated kernel module, in comparison to filesystems like NTFS based on FUSE, or XFS &amp; EXT4 directly connected to vfs, and therefore each FS require its kernel module. ZUFS operating in Linux userspace and consisting out of two modules called zuf (Zu Feeder) &amp; zus (Zu Server) available as OpenSource; zuf is a kernel to userspace bridge. MAX Data used as Memory Extension is NUMA aware, byte-addressable near-memory latency (3-10 microseconds), optimized for random I/O, persistent memory and as FUSE-based filesystems do not have a page caching. MAX Data is integrated with NetApp <a href="/wiki/ONTAP" title="ONTAP">ONTAP</a> as part of <a href="/wiki/NetApp#Data_Fabric" title="NetApp">Data Fabric</a> vision. Currently, MAX Data works over SAN (FC &amp; iSCSI) with AFF systems on the back end or SSD drives installed in the same server (starting with MAX Data 1.4), but NetApp considering to add: FC-NVMe protocol already available in ONTAP, iNVMe (NVMe over TCP) which is currently not supported by ONTAP, and NAS protocols which not supported yet but were tested on MAX Data with SDS instances of ONTAP, i.e., ONTAP Select and NetApp Cloud Volumes ONTAP according to Tech ONTAP podcast 154 on SoundCloud. NetApp is also looking into integration MAX Data with ONTAP FlexCache functionality for NFS caching in the long run. Also, NetApp tested MAX Data with Element OS but currently needs some server-side modifications and not supported. MAX Data provides persistent memory capabilities and include data protection capabilities like snapshots (MAX Snap) and SVM SnapMirror, Cloning, Data Tiering, and MAX Recovery found as a key functionality by enterprise companies running <a href="/wiki/In-memory_processing" title="In-memory processing">In-memory processing</a> applications. MAX Recovery is technology copying data from memory of one (primary) server over RoCE network to memory of another (recovery) server, which allows recovering back to the original primary server data in seconds compared to hours if it were done from SSD media. For cluster connection for MAX Recovery, NetApp recommends using a dedicated RDMA over Ethernet (RoCEv2) 100Gbit/s network (also referred to as Persistent Memory over Fabrics, PMoF) which takes additionally 2-3 microseconds compare to single-node configurations. MAX Snap is a snapshot on MAX Data (Tier 1) level which can call another MAX Data service called Sync Snap to synchronize snapshot on Tier 2 so snapshot on storage system will capture and merge data into coherent, consistent snapshot on the storage system which later can be replicated with SnapMirror. MAX Data have APIs for application-consistent snapshots and plans to add functionality to integrate it with SnapCenter, but currently, doesn't have data protection software integrations. MAX Data have a 3-month release cadence. MAX Data Memory API allows new applications to integrate with MAX Data for application-based and potentially more intelligent memory management, therefore it needs application modification while MAX Data with data tiering algorithm does not need any application modifications if used as the POSIX file system. MAX Data have Pin functionality which can pin a file or a certain range within a file so it will not be going to destaged into the second tier. According to the Yahoo, Cloud Serving Benchmark showed on Tech Field Day on October 24, 2018, MAX Data reaching 3.7 more IOPS and 4-5 times less latency, compared to Linux XFS on NVMe flash in AWS EC2 instance and 64GB memory (with 32GB cache size for MongoDB) with 50% read modify write workload.
</p><p>MAX data have a per-server license and do not depend on CPU, Memory or storage capacity. MAX Data has two different licensing tiers: Basic and Advanced. Advanced licensing includes enterprise capabilities like snapshots, MAX Recovery, while Basic used only if performance with tiering functionality needed.
</p>

NetApp allows upgrading to the newest Kubernetes with about a 5-day delay after release.
</p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="SaaS_Backup">SaaS Backup</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=NetApp&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: SaaS Backup" data-section="27" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>NetApp SaaS Backup (Previously Cloud Control) is back up and recovery service for <a href="/wiki/SaaS" class="mw-redirect" title="SaaS">SaaS</a> Microsoft <a href="/wiki/Office_365" title="Office 365">Office 365</a> and <a href="/wiki/Salesforce.com" class="mw-redirect" title="Salesforce.com">Salesforce</a> which provide extended, granular and custom retention capabilities of backup and recovery process compare to native cloud backup. NetApp planning to extend SaaS Backup and recovery service for <a href="/wiki/G_Suite" title="G Suite">Google Apps (G Suite)</a>, <a href="/wiki/Slack_(software)" title="Slack (software)">Slack</a> and <a href="/wiki/ServiceNow" title="ServiceNow">ServiceNow</a>.
</p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Cloud_Sync">Cloud Sync</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=NetApp&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: Cloud Sync" data-section="28" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>Cloud Sync is service for synchronizing any NAS storage system with another NAS storage, an Object Storage like Amazon S3 or NetApp Storage GRID using an object protocol.
</p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Cloud_Insights">Cloud Insights</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=NetApp&amp;action=edit&amp;section=29" title="Edit section: Cloud Insights" data-section="29" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>SaaS application for monitoring infrastructure application stack for customers consuming cloud resources and also build for the dynamic nature of microservices and web-scale infrastructures. Cloud Insights uses similar to OnCommand Insight front-end API but different technology on the back-end. Cloud Insights available as a preview and will have three editions: Free, Standard and Pro. The free edition will be able to monitor NetApp storage &amp; services using NetApp Active IQ at no cost.
</p>
<h4 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Cloud_Secure">Cloud Secure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=NetApp&amp;action=edit&amp;section=30" title="Edit section: Cloud Secure" data-section="30" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h4>
<p>Cloud Secure is a SaaS security tool that identifies malicious data access and compromised users, in other words, user behavior analytics. It provides centralized visibility and control of all corporate data access across hybrid cloud environments for security and compliance goals. Cloud Secure using data collectors to identify security breaches not solvable by file access control where the user has legitimate access to files and require post-login detection to understand what is appropriate and what is bad behavior. Cloud Secure uses machine learning algorithms to identify the working community, abnormal user behavior and unusual patterns compare to other users. Cloud Secure can identify users been infected with ransomware generating abnormal change rates and prevent them from encrypting the files. Cloud Secure can take customizable actions to users identified with abnormal behavior, like quarantining the user.
Currently supported data repositories: NetApp Cloud Volumes, NetApp ONTAP, NetApp StorageGRID, OneDrive, AWS, Google Suite, HPE, DELLEMC Isilon, Dropbox, Box, @workspace and Office 365. Currently available as a preview only.
</p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="NDAS">NDAS</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=NetApp&amp;action=edit&amp;section=31" title="Edit section: NDAS" data-section="31" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>NetApp Data Availability Services with data protection in the cloud GUI. Cloud service currently located only in AWS but architecturally is cloud-agnostic and ready to be copied to other clouds like Google Cloud &amp; Azure. NDAS is a target for backup, data protection and disaster recovery purposes from ONTAP storage. ONTAP systems starting with ONTAP 9.5 have a built-in proxy application that converting NetApp snapshots with WAFL data &amp; metadata into the S3 format unlike FabricPool technology that stores only data in the object storage. Once data converted to S3 and delivered to the cloud, NDAS will discover newly transferred snapshots and create a searchable catalog of files that can be restored from the NDAS back to the original or an alternate location. Currently, NDAS has only one working backup schema: primary ONTAP system with ONTAP 9.x transferring data to secondary ONTAP 9.5 with SnapMirror; the secondary system has NDAS proxy which converting snapshots and transferring them to S3 into the cloud. NetApp is planning to add NDAS to primary storage as well later on with a newer ONTAP version. NDAS is one of the Data Fabric manifestations.
</p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Cloud_Compliance">Cloud Compliance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=NetApp&amp;action=edit&amp;section=32" title="Edit section: Cloud Compliance" data-section="32" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Fabric_Orchestrator">Fabric Orchestrator</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=NetApp&amp;action=edit&amp;section=33" title="Edit section: Fabric Orchestrator" data-section="33" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<h4 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Fabric_Flows">Fabric Flows</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=NetApp&amp;action=edit&amp;section=34" title="Edit section: Fabric Flows" data-section="34" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h4>
<h4 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Fabric_Advisor">Fabric Advisor</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=NetApp&amp;action=edit&amp;section=35" title="Edit section: Fabric Advisor" data-section="35" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h4>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(5)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Data_Fabric">Data Fabric</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=NetApp&amp;action=edit&amp;section=36" title="Edit section: Data Fabric" data-section="36" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-5 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-5">
<p>Often referred as "Data Fabric Story." Variety of integrations between NetApp's products and ways of data mobility considered by NetApp as <i>Data Fabric vision</i> for the future of data management. Data Fabric defines the NetApp technology architecture for hybrid cloud and provides the next features: SnapMirror replication from SolidFire to ONTAP; SnapMirror replication from ONTAP to Cloud Backup; FabricPool tiering feature for de-staging cold data from ONTAP to StorageGRID, Amazon S3 or Azure Blob; Volume Encryption with FabricPool provide secure data storage and secure over the wire transfer of enterprise data in a cloud provider; SnapMirror between FAS, AFF, ONTAP Select and Cloud Volumes ONTAP; Archiving and DR to public cloud; CloudMirror feature in StorageGRID replicates from on-premise object storage to Amazon S3 storage and triggers some actions in AWS Cloud; SolidFire backup to StorageGRID or Amazon S3; Cloud Backup archiving to variety of object storage systems (including StorageGRID) or many cloud providers; CloudSync is replication of NAS data to object format and back; replication to Cloud Volumes Service; Data backup to on-premise storage from SaaS Backup; SANtricity Cloud Connector for block-based backup, copy, and restore of E-Series volumes to an S3, NetApp Data Availability Services for data protection from ONTAP to cloud S3 storage with backup, DR &amp; data mining capabilities, etc. Other features as part of Data Fabric vision approach committed by NetApp to be implemented in future, like SnapMirror between ONTAP and E-Series; SnapCenter capabilities to expand to include SolidFire storage endpoints, NetApp MAX Data host filesystem tiering to and from ONTAP storage systems connected with NVMeoF, etc.
</p>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(6)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Software_Integrations">Software Integrations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=NetApp&amp;action=edit&amp;section=37" title="Edit section: Software Integrations" data-section="37" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-6 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-6">
<p>NetApp products could be integrated with a variety of software products which gives some additional flexibility, features and built-in provisioning and self-service storage capabilities. Most of the integrations are done for ONTAP systems.
</p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Automation">Automation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=NetApp&amp;action=edit&amp;section=38" title="Edit section: Automation" data-section="38" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>NetApp provides a variety of automation services directly to its products with HTTP protocol or through middle-ware software.
</p>
<h4 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Docker">Docker</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=NetApp&amp;action=edit&amp;section=39" title="Edit section: Docker" data-section="39" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h4>
<p>NetApp Trident software provides a persistent volume plugin for Docker containers with both orchestrators Kubernetes and Swarm and supports ONTAP, SolidFire, E-Series, Azure NetApp Files (ANF), Cloud Volumes and NetApp Kubernetes Service in Cloud. Also, NetApp with Cisco sells CI architectures which incorporate Trident plugin: FlexPod Datacenter with Docker Enterprise Edition and ONTAP AI.
</p>
<h4 class="in-block"><span id="CI.2FCD"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="CI/CD">CI/CD</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=NetApp&amp;action=edit&amp;section=40" title="Edit section: CI/CD" data-section="40" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h4>
<p>NetApp Jenkins Framework provides integration with ONTAP storage for DevOps, accelerating development with automation operations like provisioning and data-set cloning for test and development and leverage ONTAP for version control, create and delete checkpoints etc. Jenkins also integrate with NetApp Service Level Manager software which provides RESTful API for guarantee level of storage performance. Apprenda and CloudBees integrate and accelerate DevOps through Docker persistent volume plugin and Jenkins Framework integration. Apprenda could be integrated with OpenStack running on top of FlexPod.
</p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Backup_and_Recovery">Backup and Recovery</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=NetApp&amp;action=edit&amp;section=41" title="Edit section: Backup and Recovery" data-section="41" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>CommVault, Veeam and Veritas have integrations with ONTAP, SolidFire, Cloud Backup and E-Series leveraging storage capabilities like snapshots and cloning capabilities for testing backup copies and SnapMirror for Backup and Recovery (B&amp;R), Disaster Recovery (DR) and Data Archiving for improving restore time and number of recovery points (see <a href="/wiki/Recovery_point_objective" class="mw-redirect" title="Recovery point objective">RPO/RTO</a>). Cloud Backup integrates with nearly all B&amp;R products for archiving capabilities since it is represented as ordinary NAS share for B&amp;R software. Backup and recovery software from competitor vendors like IBM Spectrum Protect, EMC NetWorker, HP Data Protector, Dell vRanger, Acronis Backup etc. also have some level of integrations with NetApp storage systems.
</p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Enterprise_Applications">Enterprise Applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=NetApp&amp;action=edit&amp;section=42" title="Edit section: Enterprise Applications" data-section="42" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>NetApp systems can integrate with enterprise applications for backup purposes, cloning, provisioning, and other self-service storage features. Oracle DB can be connected using Direct NFS (dNFS) client build inside database app which will provide network performance, resiliency, load balancing for NFS protocol with ONTAP systems. Oracle DB, Microsoft SQL, IBM DB2, MySQL, Mongo DB, SAP HANA, MS Exchange, VMware vSphere, Citrix Xen, KVM integrate with NetApp systems for provisioning, cloning and additional backup and recovery build in capabilities like SnapShots, SnapVault and SnapMirror with variety of B&amp;R software including NetApp's SnapCenter and SnapCreator. Integration with such applications provides the ability to the instant cloning of data sets for test and development and self-service restore capabilities for application administrators and for end users. NetApp ONTAP systems could be integrated with VMware vSphere using NFS plugin for ESXi hosts which offloads some of the storage operations on the storage system. With VASA protocol NetApp systems can integrate for VMware VVol technology. Similarly to NFS plugin for ESXi, ONTAP systems support ODX functionality with Windows systems with file and block-based protocols for offloading data copying or moving processes to the storage system.
</p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="OpenStack">OpenStack</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=NetApp&amp;action=edit&amp;section=43" title="Edit section: OpenStack" data-section="43" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>NetApp systems have integration with such open source projects as OpenStack Cinder for Block storage (SolidFire, ONTAP, E-Series, OnCommand Insight, Cloud Backup), OpenStack Manila for Shared file system (ONTAP, OnCommand Insight), Docker persistent volumes through Trident plugin (SolidFire, ONTAP, E-Series) and others.
</p>

September 13, 2018, Lenovo and NetApp announced its technology partnership, so Lenovo OEM Netapp products under its own name: Lenovo ThinkSystem DE (using NetApp's EF &amp; E-Series array technology), and ThinkSystem DM uses ONTAP software with Lenovo servers and supports FC-NVMe (analog for NetApp FAS &amp; AFF systems).

<h4 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Syrian_surveillance">Syrian surveillance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=NetApp&amp;action=edit&amp;section=47" title="Edit section: Syrian surveillance" data-section="47" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h4>
<p>In November 2011, during the <a href="/wiki/2011_Syrian_uprising" class="mw-redirect" title="2011 Syrian uprising">2011 Syrian uprising</a>, NetApp was named as one of several companies whose products were being used in the Syrian government crackdown. The equipment was allegedly sold to the Syrians by an authorized NetApp reseller.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></p><p>On April 7, 2014, NetApp was notified by the US Department of Commerce "that it had completed its review of this matter and determined that NetApp had not violated the U.S. export laws", and that the file on the matter had been closed.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<h4 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Legal_dispute_with_Sun_Microsystems">Legal dispute with Sun Microsystems</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=NetApp&amp;action=edit&amp;section=48" title="Edit section: Legal dispute with Sun Microsystems" data-section="48" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h4>
<p>In September 2007, NetApp started proceedings against <a href="/wiki/Sun_Microsystems" title="Sun Microsystems">Sun Microsystems</a>, claiming that the <a href="/wiki/ZFS" title="ZFS">ZFS</a> File System developed by Sun infringed its patents.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> The following month, Sun announced plans to countersue based on alleged misuse by NetApp of Sun's own patented technology.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> Several of NetApp's patent claims were rejected on the basis of <a href="/wiki/Prior_art" title="Prior art">prior art</a> after re-examination by the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Patent_and_Trademark_Office" title="United States Patent and Trademark Office">United States Patent and Trademark Office</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> On September 9, 2010, NetApp announced an agreement with <a href="/wiki/Oracle_Corporation" title="Oracle Corporation">Oracle Corporation</a> (the new owner of Sun Microsystems) to dismiss the suits.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup></p>
