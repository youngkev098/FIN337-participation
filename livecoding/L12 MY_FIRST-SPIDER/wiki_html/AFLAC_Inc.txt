</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(1)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aflac&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History" data-section="1" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-1 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-1">
<p>The company was founded by brothers John, Paul (died 2014), and William Amos in <a href="/wiki/Columbus,_Georgia" title="Columbus, Georgia">Columbus, Georgia</a>, in 1955, as American Family Life Insurance Company of Columbus. In 1964, the company name was changed to American Family Life Assurance Company of Columbus. The company, in 1990, adopted the Aflac <a href="/wiki/Acronym" title="Acronym">acronym</a>, although the official name of the underwriting subsidiary remains American Family Life Assurance Company of Columbus. Aflac announced the appointment of Frederick J. Crawford as Chief Financial Officer, as well as Executive Vice President in June 2015.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p><p>The company signed 6,426 policyholders in its first year.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Aflac pioneered <a href="/wiki/Cancer_insurance" title="Cancer insurance">cancer insurance</a> in 1958. Beginning in 1964, the company decided to focus sales on worksite settings, eventually through policies sponsored by employers and funded through payroll deductions. By 2003, more than 98% of Aflac policies in the United States were issued on a payroll deduction basis, making the company a leader in that approach to policy distribution.
</p>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(2)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Business">Business</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aflac&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Business" data-section="2" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-2 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-2">
<p>Aflac operates in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> and <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a>, and has its worldwide headquarters and corporate offices in an eighteen-story tower just east of <a href="/wiki/Downtown_Columbus,_Georgia" title="Downtown Columbus, Georgia">Downtown Columbus, Georgia</a>, in an area known as <a href="/wiki/MidTown_(Columbus,_Georgia)" title="MidTown (Columbus, Georgia)">MidTown</a>. The Aflac tower is <a href="/wiki/Downtown_Columbus,_Georgia#Buildings" title="Downtown Columbus, Georgia">the tallest building in the city</a>. As of June 30, 2015<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Aflac&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, the corporation's total assets were more than $103 billion, and the company insured more than 50 million people worldwide.<sup id="cite_ref-Aflac-Oct-2012-8-K_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Aflac-Oct-2012-8-K-10">[10]</a></sup></p><p>Aflac is the largest provider of guaranteed-renewable insurance in the United States and the largest insurance company overall in Japan, when measured by individual insurance policies in force.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p><p>The company now offers several types of insurance policies in the United States, including the following:
</p>
<ul><li>Accident</li>
<li>Cancer/Specified Disease</li>
<li>Dental</li>
<li>Hospital Confinement Indemnity</li>
<li>Hospital Confinement Sickness Indemnity</li>
<li>Hospital Intensive Care</li>
<li>Life</li>
<li>Lump Sum Cancer</li>
<li>Lump Sum Cancer Critical Illness</li>
<li>Specified Health Event</li>
<li>Short Term Disability</li>
<li>Vision</li></ul><p>Aflac also offers un-reimbursed medical, dependent day-care, and transportation <a href="/wiki/Flexible_spending_account" title="Flexible spending account">flexible spending accounts</a>. The company additionally offers <a href="/wiki/Human_resources" title="Human resources">human resources</a> services for <a href="/wiki/Health_Insurance_Portability_and_Accountability_Act" title="Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act">HIPAA</a> and <a href="/wiki/Consolidated_Omnibus_Budget_Reconciliation_Act_of_1985" title="Consolidated Omnibus Budget Reconciliation Act of 1985">COBRA</a>. From 1979 to 1997, the company owned several television stations, most of them in small and medium markets. It sold the broadcasting division (including flagship station <a href="/wiki/WTVM" title="WTVM">WTVM</a>) to what became <a href="/wiki/Raycom_Media" title="Raycom Media">Raycom Media</a> in 1997.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p><p>Aflac is ranked 137th in the <a href="/wiki/Fortune_500" title="Fortune 500">Fortune 500</a> list of largest United States corporations by revenue.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(3)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Critics_of_cancer_policies">Critics of cancer policies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aflac&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Critics of cancer policies" data-section="3" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-3 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-3">
<p>Consumer groups and some government officials say that cancer insurance returns fewer premium dollars to policyholders than standard insurance. A United States <a href="/wiki/General_Accounting_Office" class="mw-redirect" title="General Accounting Office">General Accounting Office</a> study found that the policies paid back as little as 35% of premiums (Aflac said its cancer insurance paid back 62.4%). In comparison, <a href="/wiki/New_York_State" class="mw-redirect" title="New York State">New York State</a> requires most major-medical policies to pay back 82% and group policies to pay back 75%. New York State does not allow stand-alone cancer policies. 
</p><p>In 1997, AFLAC spent $175,000 on lobbyists and campaign contributions to change the law.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> New York State lifted its ban in 1998, for purchasers who already have basic coverage. <a href="/wiki/Consumer_Reports" title="Consumer Reports">Consumer Reports</a> recommended that policyholders use the money instead to buy lower-deductible insurance.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p><p>Insurance claims by line appear on the Annual Statement filed with each state. Loss claims are a percent of total premium before commissions.
</p>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(4)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Aflac_Duck">The Aflac Duck</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aflac&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: The Aflac Duck" data-section="4" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-4 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-4">
<p>Since December 1999, the company's identity and brand has become more widely recognized in the United States as the result of TV commercials featuring the Aflac Duck, who frustratedly quacks the company's name to unsuspecting prospective policy holders. The duck concept and all of the commercials to date have been created by <a href="/wiki/Kaplan_Thaler_Group" class="mw-redirect" title="Kaplan Thaler Group">Kaplan Thaler Group</a>, an advertising agency based in New York City. Metzer Farms, a <a href="/wiki/Gonzales,_California" title="Gonzales, California">Gonzales, California</a>, waterfowl and gamebird hatchery, supplied them with the initial ducklings that each grew into the famous duck. Struggling to come up with a concept to make the big but relatively obscure insurance company's name memorable, one of the agency's art directors stumbled upon the duck idea while walking around <a href="/wiki/Central_Park" title="Central Park">Central Park</a> at lunchtime uttering, "Aflac, Aflac." He soon realized how much the company's name sounded like a duck's quack. The Aflac Duck character has now starred in more than 30 commercials. In many of these commercials, character actor <a href="/wiki/Earl_Billings" title="Earl Billings">Earl Billings</a> also appears. The Aflac Duck is enshrined on Madison Avenue's Walk of Fame as one of America's Favorite Advertising Icons.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p><p>In April 2009, Aflac introduced a new marketing campaign called "Get the Aflacts", designed to educate consumers about the specific benefits of the insurance products the company sells. The <a href="/wiki/Aflacts" title="Aflacts">Aflacts</a> campaign gave the Aflac Duck "a more prominent role", designed to "help potential customers learn the Aflacts, er, facts about policies and other products", according to <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times" title="The New York Times">The New York Times</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p><p>Celebrities have starred in the Aflac ads, including <a href="/wiki/Chevy_Chase" title="Chevy Chase">Chevy Chase</a> (2003); <a href="/wiki/Yogi_Berra" title="Yogi Berra">Yogi Berra</a>; <a href="/wiki/Yao_Ming" title="Yao Ming">Yao Ming</a>; <a href="/wiki/Donald_Trump" title="Donald Trump">Donald Trump</a>'s wife and current First Lady, <a href="/wiki/Melania_Trump" title="Melania Trump">Melania Trump</a> (2005); <a href="/wiki/Monster_Energy_NASCAR_Cup_Series" class="mw-redirect" title="Monster Energy NASCAR Cup Series">NASCAR Cup Series</a> driver <a href="/wiki/Carl_Edwards" title="Carl Edwards">Carl Edwards</a> (2008–2014); the United States <a href="/wiki/Summer_Olympic_Games" title="Summer Olympic Games">Olympic</a> <a href="/wiki/Synchronized_swimming" class="mw-redirect" title="Synchronized swimming">synchronized swimming</a> team (2004); and <a href="/wiki/Wayne_Newton" title="Wayne Newton">Wayne Newton</a> playing at <a href="/wiki/The_Stardust" class="mw-redirect" title="The Stardust">Stardust Hotel and Casino</a> for the 2003 commercial. The duck also appeared with <a href="/wiki/Bugs_Bunny" title="Bugs Bunny">Bugs Bunny</a>, <a href="/wiki/Daffy_Duck" title="Daffy Duck">Daffy Duck</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wile_E._Coyote_and_the_Road_Runner" title="Wile E. Coyote and the Road Runner">Wile E. Coyote and the Road Runner</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Rudolph_the_Red-Nosed_Reindeer" title="Rudolph the Red-Nosed Reindeer">Rudolph the Red-Nosed Reindeer</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> In 2005, the company logo was changed to incorporate the duck. The first commercial using the new logo featured <a href="/wiki/Gilbert_Gottfried" title="Gilbert Gottfried">Gilbert Gottfried</a> at a pet store because the duck kept saying, "Aflac!" and he had to trade in the duck for a parrot, saying, "If you're hurt and can't work".<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p><p>The duck was originally <a href="/wiki/Voice_over" class="mw-redirect" title="Voice over">voiced</a> by comedian <a href="/wiki/Gilbert_Gottfried" title="Gilbert Gottfried">Gilbert Gottfried</a>, who also voiced Digit on the <a href="/wiki/PBS_Kids" title="PBS Kids">PBS Kids</a> series <i><a href="/wiki/Cyberchase" title="Cyberchase">Cyberchase</a></i> and <a href="/wiki/Iago_(Disney_character)" class="mw-redirect" title="Iago (Disney character)">Iago</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Disney" class="mw-redirect" title="Disney">Disney</a> film <i><a href="/wiki/Aladdin_(1992_Disney_film)" title="Aladdin (1992 Disney film)">Aladdin</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> After 11 years as the voice of the Aflac duck, Gottfried was dismissed on March 14, 2011, due to jokes on Gottfried's <a href="/wiki/Twitter" title="Twitter">Twitter</a> account referencing the <a href="/wiki/2011_T%C5%8Dhoku_earthquake_and_tsunami" title="2011 Tōhoku earthquake and tsunami">Tōhoku earthquake and tsunami</a>. The company's chief marketing officer stated that "Gilbert's recent comments about the crisis in Japan were lacking in humor, and certainly do not represent the thoughts and feelings of anyone at Aflac."<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p><p>On March 23, 2011, Aflac announced that the company was taking applications for the new voice of the Aflac Duck through QuackAflac.com until April 1. Commercials requesting the submissions, first aired in 2006 but updated, resemble a <a href="/wiki/Silent_movie" class="mw-redirect" title="Silent movie">silent movie</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> On April 26, 2011, it was announced that Daniel McKeague, a television advertising sales manager from <a href="/wiki/Hugo,_Minnesota" title="Hugo, Minnesota">Hugo, Minnesota</a>, would be the new voice of the Aflac duck.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> The first Aflac commercial featuring the duck's new voice aired on May 1, 2011.
</p><p>In 2018, an Aflac ad inserted an animated Aflac duck into the opening credits of the <a href="/wiki/DuckTales_(2017_TV_series)" title="DuckTales (2017 TV series)"><i>DuckTales</i> reboot</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup>
In 2020, an Aflac ad inserted an animated Aflac duck into the opening credits of <i><a href="/wiki/Scoob!" title="Scoob!">Scoob!</a></i>.
</p>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(5)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Corporate_philanthropy_and_social_responsibility">Corporate philanthropy and social responsibility</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aflac&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Corporate philanthropy and social responsibility" data-section="5" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-5 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-5">
<p>The company states that through a partnership with <a href="/wiki/Children%27s_Healthcare_of_Atlanta" title="Children's Healthcare of Atlanta">Children's Healthcare of Atlanta</a>, Aflac has contributed more than $100 million to childhood cancer research and treatment.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p><p>Aflac employees are formally involved in an array of charitable organizations such as <a href="/wiki/Habitat_for_Humanity_International" class="mw-redirect" title="Habitat for Humanity International">Habitat for Humanity International</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Easter_Seals_(US)" class="mw-redirect" title="Easter Seals (US)">Easter Seals</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/United_Way_of_America" title="United Way of America">United Way</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p><p>Aflac's stated objectives include the decrease of its environmental impact, for which the company is into a partnership with the <a href="/wiki/The_Clean_Air_Campaign" title="The Clean Air Campaign">Clean Air Campaign</a> to encourage employees to engage with greater frequency in alternate commuting methods.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(6)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span id="Aflac_and_Macy.27s_Partnership"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Aflac_and_Macy's_Partnership">Aflac and Macy's Partnership</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aflac&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Aflac and Macy's Partnership" data-section="6" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-6 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-6">
<p>Aflac and <a href="/wiki/Macy%27s" title="Macy's">Macy’s</a> have partnered since 2001 to sell the annual Aflac holiday plush duck in select Macy’s stores nationwide.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> Net proceeds from each duck sold are donated to the participating children’s cancer facility nearest to where it is purchased. Since 2001, more than $3 million has been raised from the sale of the ducks.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p><p>Aflac has participated in the <a href="/wiki/Macy%E2%80%99s_Thanksgiving_Day_Parade" class="mw-redirect" title="Macy’s Thanksgiving Day Parade">Macy’s Thanksgiving Day Parade</a> for the past six years. A new balloonicle was unveiled in 2014, featuring the Aflac Duck wearing ice skates.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>

<p>Aflac has been the recipient of <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.aflac.com/aboutaflac/citizenship/national_recognition.aspx">several awards</a>:
</p>
<ul><li>Aflac has appeared on <i><a href="/wiki/Fortune_magazine" class="mw-redirect" title="Fortune magazine">Fortune</a>'</i>s <a href="/wiki/100_Best_Companies_to_Work_For" title="100 Best Companies to Work For">100 Best Companies to Work For</a> list for 20 consecutive years.</li>
<li>Aflac has appeared on <i>Fortune'</i>s <a href="/wiki/America%27s_Most_Admired_Companies" class="mw-redirect" title="America's Most Admired Companies">America's Most Admired Companies</a> list for 17 years.</li>
<li>Aflac has been recognized by <a href="/wiki/Ethisphere" class="mw-redirect" title="Ethisphere">Ethisphere</a> magazine as a World's Most Ethical Companies for 12 consecutive years, and is the only insurance company to do so.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></li>
<li>Aflac has been named to the <a href="/wiki/Dow_Jones_Sustainability_Index" class="mw-redirect" title="Dow Jones Sustainability Index">Dow Jones Sustainability Index</a> North America for six consecutive years.</li>
<li>In August 2016, Latina Style magazine placed Aflac on the list of the 50 Best Companies for Latinas to work for in the United States. Aflac has been on this annual list for 17 years.</li>
