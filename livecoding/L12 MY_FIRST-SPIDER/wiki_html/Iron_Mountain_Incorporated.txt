</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(1)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Iron_Mountain_(company)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History" data-section="1" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-1 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-1">
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Iron_Mountain_Truck.jpg" class="image"><noscript><img alt="Iron Mountain Truck.jpg" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/58/Iron_Mountain_Truck.jpg/220px-Iron_Mountain_Truck.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="2304" data-file-height="1728"></noscript><span class="lazy-image-placeholder" style="width: 220px;height: 165px;" data-src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/58/Iron_Mountain_Truck.jpg/220px-Iron_Mountain_Truck.jpg" data-alt="Iron Mountain Truck.jpg" data-width="220" data-height="165" data-class="thumbimage"> </span></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Iron_Mountain_Truck.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div></div></div></div>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Background">Background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Iron_Mountain_(company)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Background" data-section="2" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>The company was started by Herman Knaust, who had made his fortune growing and marketing mushrooms.<sup id="cite_ref-BBJ_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BBJ-4">[4]</a></sup> He purchased a depleted iron ore mine and 100 acres (0.40 km<sup>2</sup>) of land in <a href="/wiki/Livingston,_New_York" title="Livingston, New York">Livingston, New York</a> for $9,000 in 1936, needing more space to grow his product.<sup id="cite_ref-BBJ_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BBJ-4">[4]</a></sup> By 1950, the mushroom market had shifted, and Knaust was looking for alternative uses for his mine, which he had named "Iron Mountain."
</p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span id="Founding_and_early_years_.281951.E2.80.931970.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Founding_and_early_years_(1951–1970)">Founding and early years (1951–1970)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Iron_Mountain_(company)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Founding and early years (1951–1970)" data-section="3" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>Knaust saw a business opportunity, amidst widespread <a href="/wiki/Cold_War" title="Cold War">Cold War</a> fears, in protecting corporate information from <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_warfare" title="Nuclear warfare">nuclear attack</a> and other disasters.
</p><p>The company was originally founded in 1951 as <b>Iron Mountain Atomic Storage Corporation</b>;<sup id="cite_ref-music-burned-nyt_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-music-burned-nyt-5">[5]</a></sup> it opened its first underground "vaults" in 1951 and its first sales office in the <a href="/wiki/Empire_State_Building" title="Empire State Building">Empire State Building</a>, about 125 miles (201 km) south.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Iron Mountain's first customer was East River Savings Bank, who brought microfilm copies of deposit records and duplicate signature cards in armored cars for storage in the mountain facility. In 1978, the company opened its first above-ground records-storage facility.
</p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span id="Middle_years_.281970.E2.80.932000.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Middle_years_(1970–2000)">Middle years (1970–2000)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Iron_Mountain_(company)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Middle years (1970–2000)" data-section="4" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>This first iteration of Iron Mountain was bankrupt by the early 1970s and was wholly acquired by Vincent J. Ryan through his holdings in Schooner Capital Corporation, <a href="/wiki/Boston" title="Boston">Boston</a>, Massachusetts. At the time, it consisted of the original facility in Livingston, New York and IMAR (Iron Mountain at Rosendale), a former limestone mine and mushroom cave outside of Kingston, New York. In 1980, it expanded to Rhode Island through the purchase of a former Industrial National Bank (a precursor to FleetBoston) cold site and data tape repository in <a href="/wiki/Gloucester,_Rhode_Island" class="mw-redirect" title="Gloucester, Rhode Island">Glocester, Rhode Island</a>. It had many Fortune 500 clients at the time, and its revenue was in the $6 million range in the early 1980s.
</p><p>Its breakthrough came in the mid-1980s when it convinced Manufacturer's Hanover Bank to move all its paper records out of Manhattan to an above-ground facility, a former strip mall in <a href="/wiki/Port_Ewen,_New_York" title="Port Ewen, New York">Port Ewen, New York</a>. This was the first time that <a href="/wiki/Bar_code" class="mw-redirect" title="Bar code">bar codes</a> were used by a records management company to allow real time access to shipped boxes and the documents inside. 
</p><p>During the 1980s, the company expanded beyond New York, opening facilities in New York and throughout New England. In 1988, Iron Mountain extended its reach into 12 more U.S. markets by acquiring Bell &amp; Howell Records Management, Inc.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p><p>The firm went public on January 31, 1996. In 1997, Iron Mountain became a leading <a href="/w/index.php?title=Software_escrow&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Software escrow (page does not exist)">software escrow</a> company with the acquisition of <a href="/wiki/Data_Securities_International" title="Data Securities International">Data Securities International</a> (DSI).
</p><p>In 1998, Iron Mountain made its first overseas acquisition by buying British Data Management, Ltd. The company reported a $423 million revenues by the end of the same fiscal year of its acquisition.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span id="Expansion_and_consolidation_.282000.E2.80.93present.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Expansion_and_consolidation_(2000–present)">Expansion and consolidation (2000–present)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Iron_Mountain_(company)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Expansion and consolidation (2000–present)" data-section="5" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>Since 1980, Iron Mountain grew through acquisitions. Revenue over this period increased from $3 million in 1981 to $2.7 billion at the end of 2007.
</p><p>In February 2000, Iron Mountain Incorporated announced the completion of its acquisition of Pierce Leahy Corp. (NYSE:PLH) in a stock-for-stock merger valued at approximately $1.1 billion.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p><p>In 2004, Iron Mountain formed a digital assets division called "Iron Mountain Digital", following the acquisition of Connected Corporation, a maker of online PC backup software.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> <sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> 
A year later, Iron Mountain Digital bought LiveVault, a provider of online backup software for server data.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> In 2007 Iron Mountain acquired Stratify Inc.,<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> one of the larger <a href="/wiki/E-discovery" class="mw-redirect" title="E-discovery">e-discovery</a> service providers at the production end of the Electronic Discovery Reference Model (EDRM).<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> The acquired businesses of LiveVault and Stratify Inc. were consolidated into Iron Mountain Digital.
</p><p>Richard Reese became the company's CEO in 1981, and he concurrently assumed the position of chairman in 1995. He remained in the former position until June 2008, when he was replaced by Bob Brennan, but he remained in the chairman's seat.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup>  However, the company announced Brennan's departure in April 2011, and Reese resumed his former title.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p><p>In February 2010, Iron Mountain acquired a California-based eDiscovery and content archiving software provider, Mimosa Systems. The acquisition too was absorbed into Iron Mountain Digital.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p><p>On May 16, 2011, Iron Mountain decided to divest Iron Mountain Digital, which was acquired by the British <a href="/wiki/Enterprise_search" title="Enterprise search">enterprise search</a> and <a href="/wiki/Knowledge_management" title="Knowledge management">knowledge management</a> firm <a href="/wiki/Autonomy_corporation" class="mw-redirect" title="Autonomy corporation">Autonomy corporation</a> for $380 million.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup>  Shortly thereafter, in August 2011, Hewlett-Packard acquired the <a href="/wiki/Cambridge" title="Cambridge">Cambridge</a> based Autonomy, and amalgamated the operations of Autonomy (which included Iron Mountain Digital) into <a href="/wiki/HP_Software_Division" class="mw-redirect" title="HP Software Division">HP's enterprise software division</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p><p>On May 8, 2012, Iron Mountain expanded its high-security storage facility business through the acquisition of three records storage firms—File House Offsite Record Storage in <a href="/wiki/Fredericksburg,_Virginia" title="Fredericksburg, Virginia">Fredericksburg</a>, Virginia, and Document Systems Inc. in Columbia, South Carolina and First National Safe Deposit in <a href="/wiki/Philadelphia" title="Philadelphia">Philadelphia</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p><p>On November 5, 2013, Iron Mountain announced it would be shutting down its <a href="/wiki/Saint_John,_New_Brunswick" title="Saint John, New Brunswick">Saint John, New Brunswick</a> contact center in 2014. Many of the jobs were transitioned to <a href="/wiki/Convergys" title="Convergys">Convergys</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p><p>As of January 1, 2014, Iron Mountain successfully converted to a real estate investment trust after approval of private letter ruling requests by the IRS to classify steel racking structures as qualified real estate assets.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p><p>At the end of April 2015, Iron Mountain announced it would acquire <a href="/wiki/Australian" class="mw-redirect" title="Australian">Australian</a> data protection services provider Recall Holdings for around $2.2 billion in cash and stock.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> At the end of March 2016 the Australian Competition &amp; Consumer Commission released a statement saying it would not block the acquisition of Recall pursuant to Iron Mountain's agreement to divest most of its Australian business.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> At the same time, the Canadian Competition Bureau announced it entered an agreement with Iron Mountain to allow the acquisition as long as Iron Mountain divested records management assets in the markets in which it found the acquisition would limit effective remaining competitors: Toronto, Montreal, Ottawa, Calgary, Edmonton and Vancouver.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup>  The US Department of Justice also agreed to allow the acquisition provided Iron Mountain divested records management assets in the 15 markets where Iron Mountain and Recall were two of the top three competitors – these markets include Detroit; Kansas City, Missouri; Charlotte, North Carolina; Durham, North Carolina; Raleigh, North Carolina; Buffalo, New York; Tulsa, Oklahoma; Pittsburgh; Greenville/Spartanburg, South Carolina; Nashville, Tennessee; San Antonio, Texas; Richmond, Virginia; San Diego;  Atlanta; and Seattle.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> Finally, the UK's Competition and Markets Authority approved the acquisition pending an investigation into the acquisition's effect on competition in the UK. In June 2016, the Competition and Markets Authority determined the acquisition would create a "substantial lessening of competition" in Aberdeen and Dundee. In response to this report, Iron Mountain and the Competition and Markets Authority reached an agreement whereby Iron Mountain would sell Recall's existing operations in Aberdeen and Dundee (known as C21 Data Services).<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> On May 2, 2016 Iron Mountain announced the completion of the merger for $2 billion (US), mostly in stock.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p><p>In 2016 Fortune magazine listed Iron Mountain at number 729 on its list of the largest 1000 public companies in the United States.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p><p>In September 2016, the unpublished recordings of musician <a href="/wiki/Prince_(musician)" title="Prince (musician)">Prince</a>, consisting of over $200 million worth of recorded music and film, was moved to an Iron Mountain facility in Los Angeles, due to water damage and poor storage conditions in Prince's Paisley Park recording facility, in <a href="/wiki/Chanhassen" class="mw-redirect" title="Chanhassen">Chanhassen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Minnesota" title="Minnesota">Minnesota</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p><p>In December 2017 the company purchased IO Data Centers' US division for $1.3 billion, which includes four colocation data centers in New Jersey, Ohio and two in Arizona.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(2)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Facilities_and_holdings">Facilities and holdings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Iron_Mountain_(company)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Facilities and holdings" data-section="6" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-2 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-2">
<p>Iron Mountain has underground storage facilities in the United States and the rest of the world, but most of the company's over 1,500 storage locations<sup id="cite_ref-music-burned-nyt_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-music-burned-nyt-5">[5]</a></sup> are in above-ground leased warehouse space located near customers.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-35">[35]</a></sup> Its storage location in <a href="/wiki/Dighton,_Massachusetts" title="Dighton, Massachusetts">Dighton, Massachusetts</a> was once a missile storage battery during the <a href="/wiki/Cold_War" title="Cold War">Cold War</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></p><p>The best-known Iron Mountain storage facility is a 1.7 million sq. ft.<sup id="cite_ref-music-burned-nyt_5-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-music-burned-nyt-5">[5]</a></sup> high-security storage facility in a former <a href="/wiki/Limestone" title="Limestone">limestone</a> mine at <a href="/wiki/Boyers,_Pennsylvania" title="Boyers, Pennsylvania">Boyers, Pennsylvania</a>, near the city of <a href="/wiki/Butler,_Pennsylvania" title="Butler, Pennsylvania">Butler</a> in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> (<span class="plainlinks nourlexpansion"><a class="external text" href="//tools.wmflabs.org/geohack/geohack.php?pagename=Iron_Mountain_(company)&amp;params=41.093_N_79.911_W_"><span class="geo-nondefault"><span class="geo-dms" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location"><span class="latitude">41°05′35″N</span> <span class="longitude">79°54′40″W</span></span></span><span class="geo-multi-punct">﻿ / ﻿</span><span class="geo-default"><span class="geo-dec" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location">41.093°N 79.911°W</span><span style="display:none">﻿ / <span class="geo">41.093; -79.911</span></span></span></a></span>). The facility features climate controlled storage areas protected by armed guards.<sup id="cite_ref-music-burned-nyt_5-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-music-burned-nyt-5">[5]</a></sup> It began storing records in 1954 and was purchased by Iron Mountain in 1998. It is here that <a href="/wiki/Bill_Gates" title="Bill Gates">Bill Gates</a> stores his <a href="/wiki/Branded_Entertainment_Network" title="Branded Entertainment Network">Corbis</a> photographic collection in a refrigerated cave 220 feet (67 m) underground,<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> as well as where <a href="/wiki/Universal_Music_Group" title="Universal Music Group">Universal Music Group</a> stores its United States <a href="/wiki/Mastering_(audio)" title="Mastering (audio)">masters</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-music-burned-nyt_5-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-music-burned-nyt-5">[5]</a></sup> Nearby, the U.S. <a href="/wiki/Office_of_Personnel_Management" class="mw-redirect" title="Office of Personnel Management">Office of Personnel Management</a> leases another cavern to store, and process government employee retirement papers.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></p><p>The company stores the wills of <a href="/wiki/Princess_Diana" class="mw-redirect" title="Princess Diana">Princess Diana</a>, <a href="/wiki/Charles_Dickens" title="Charles Dickens">Charles Dickens</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Charles_Darwin" title="Charles Darwin">Charles Darwin</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Master_recordings">Master recordings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Iron_Mountain_(company)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Master recordings" data-section="7" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>It also houses the original recordings of <a href="/wiki/Frank_Sinatra" title="Frank Sinatra">Frank Sinatra</a>, as well as master recordings from <a href="/wiki/Sony_Music_Entertainment" class="mw-redirect" title="Sony Music Entertainment">Sony Music Entertainment</a> (over a million recordings; reportedly in <a href="/wiki/Rosendale,_New_York" title="Rosendale, New York">Rosendale, New York</a>), <a href="/wiki/Universal_Music_Group" title="Universal Music Group">Universal Music Group</a> (in Butler), and <a href="/wiki/Warner_Music_Group" title="Warner Music Group">Warner Music Group</a> (in southern California).<sup id="cite_ref-music-burned-nyt_5-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-music-burned-nyt-5">[5]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:0_35-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-35">[35]</a></sup> In order to protect the masters, Iron Mountain equips some of its music-holding facilities with professional studios so the masters never have to leave the premises.<sup id="cite_ref-music-burned-nyt_5-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-music-burned-nyt-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Archives">Archives</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Iron_Mountain_(company)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Archives" data-section="8" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>Over 1,800 cans of unclaimed nitrate film formerly held by Iron Mountain now make up the Iron Mountain Collection at the <a href="/wiki/Academy_Film_Archive" title="Academy Film Archive">Academy Film Archive</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup></p>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(3)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Recognition">Recognition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Iron_Mountain_(company)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Recognition" data-section="9" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-3 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-3">
<p><i>Security Magazine</i> named Iron Mountain in "Security 500" of 2008, an annual ranking of the United States' 500 most secure companies. Iron Mountain was its industry’s sole representative in the category of business services. Published in the magazine’s November issue, the Security 500<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> ranks companies using several metrics such as the percentage of a company’s revenue spent on security. The survey tracks 16 vertical markets to serve as a benchmarking tool for companies.
</p><p><i>Fortune</i> magazine has had Iron Mountain on its list of the "World’s Most Admired Companies" every year from 2006 to 2011.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> In its category, "diversified outsourcing services", it has every year come in second behind <a href="/wiki/Aramark" title="Aramark">Aramark</a>. The only exception was 2006, when it also ranked below <a href="/wiki/Convergys" title="Convergys">Convergys</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup> The industry rankings reflect feedback from executives, directors, and analysts who rated Iron Mountain and industry peers on nine attributes of reputation, from investment value to quality of management.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p><p>In April 2009, Iron Mountain's Digital Record Center for Images was recognized as a "Product of the Year" by the Massachusetts Network Communications Council in the "<a href="/wiki/Cloud_computing" title="Cloud computing">Cloud Computing</a>, Virtualization and Data Warehousing/Storage category".<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup></p>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(4)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Data_losses">Data losses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Iron_Mountain_(company)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Data losses" data-section="10" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-4 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-4">
<p>The company has received media attention for losing or misplacing customer files and data, particularly tapes containing private information such as home addresses and <a href="/wiki/Social_Security_number" title="Social Security number">Social Security numbers</a>. 
</p><p>In 1997 a mysterious fire destroyed a warehouse just off the New Jersey Turnpike (USA) at Exit 8, full of corporate documents. This was two days after a smaller blaze damaged another warehouse several hundred feet away.<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup> Both buildings are owned by Iron Mountain Inc.
</p><p>In May 2005, <a href="/wiki/Time_Warner" class="mw-redirect" title="Time Warner">Time Warner</a> disclosed that a container of 40 <a href="/wiki/Encrypt" class="mw-redirect" title="Encrypt">unencrypted</a> backup tapes containing the personal information of 600,000 current and former employees had disappeared while being transported in an Iron Mountain van that made 18 other stops in Manhattan that day. After the loss, Time Warner began encrypting its tapes, and Iron Mountain advised its other clients to do the same.<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup></p><p>In April 2006, tapes containing personal information for about 17,000 <a href="/wiki/Long_Island_Rail_Road" title="Long Island Rail Road">Long Island Rail Road</a> employees were lost while in transit to the railroad's office, along with tapes belonging to the <a href="/wiki/U.S._Department_of_Veterans_Affairs" class="mw-redirect" title="U.S. Department of Veterans Affairs">U.S. Department of Veterans Affairs</a> being shipped in the same vehicle.<sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup></p><p>In July 2006, a fire completely destroyed a leased six-story company warehouse in <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">London</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup> The paper records of 600 customers,<sup id="cite_ref-NHS_59-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NHS-59">[59]</a></sup> including client files stored by several prominent London law firms, were lost.<sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup> Also destroyed were the medical records of up to 240,000 patients of  <a href="/wiki/Chelsea_and_Westminster_Hospital" title="Chelsea and Westminster Hospital">Chelsea and Westminster Hospital</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-NHS_59-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NHS-59">[59]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/London_Fire_Brigade" title="London Fire Brigade">London Fire Brigade</a> later concluded that the fire was caused by <a href="/wiki/Arson" title="Arson">arson</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-61" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup></p><p>Also in July 2006, a small fire believed to have been caused by contractors making roof repairs damaged a company warehouse in <a href="/wiki/Ottawa" title="Ottawa">Ottawa</a>, Ontario, Canada.<sup id="cite_ref-62" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup></p><p>In August 2007, the company began retrofitting its unmarked vans and trucks with a new security and alarm system using <a href="/wiki/Chain_of_custody" title="Chain of custody">chain of custody</a> technology to reduce the exposure of customer data to possible loss. Among other security features, the system uses radio frequency authentication and real-time tracking capabilities to help prevent "mysterious disappearances" of tapes, or their actual removal from the vehicle, during transit.<sup id="cite_ref-CW_63-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CW-63">[63]</a></sup></p><p>On November 4, 2011, a large fire struck Iron Mountain’s document storage warehouse and headquarters in Aprilia, Italy.  According to news reports, the entire building was enveloped in flames causing substantial damage to the building and, presumably, to the documents and digital content stored there. Approximately 40 employees worked in the facility but nobody was injured.<sup id="cite_ref-64" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup></p><p>On February 5, 2014, an intentional fire<sup id="cite_ref-65" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup> completely burned down the company's Buenos Aires warehouse. At least nine firefighters died in the incident while seven others were severely injured.<sup id="cite_ref-66" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup></p>
