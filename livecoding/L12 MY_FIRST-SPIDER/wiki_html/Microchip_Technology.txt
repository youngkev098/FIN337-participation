Its products include <a href="/wiki/Microcontroller" title="Microcontroller">microcontrollers</a> (<a href="/wiki/PIC_microcontroller" class="mw-redirect" title="PIC microcontroller">PIC</a>, <a href="/wiki/PIC24#PIC24_and_dsPIC_16-bit_microcontrollers" class="mw-redirect" title="PIC24">dsPIC</a>, <a href="/wiki/Atmel_AVR" class="mw-redirect" title="Atmel AVR">AVR</a> and <a href="/wiki/Atmel_ARM-based_processors" title="Atmel ARM-based processors">SAM</a>), Serial <a href="/wiki/EEPROM" title="EEPROM">EEPROM</a> devices, Serial <a href="/wiki/Static_random_access_memory" class="mw-redirect" title="Static random access memory">SRAM</a> devices, embedded security devices, <a href="/wiki/Radio_frequency" title="Radio frequency">radio frequency</a> (RF) devices, thermal, power and battery management analog devices, as well as linear, interface and wireless solutions.

<p>Corporate headquarters are located in <a href="/wiki/Chandler,_Arizona" title="Chandler, Arizona">Chandler, Arizona</a>, with wafer fabs in <a href="/wiki/Tempe,_Arizona" title="Tempe, Arizona">Tempe, Arizona</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gresham,_Oregon" title="Gresham, Oregon">Gresham, Oregon</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Colorado_Springs" class="mw-redirect" title="Colorado Springs">Colorado Springs, Colorado</a>, assembly/test facilities in <a href="/wiki/Chachoengsao" title="Chachoengsao">Chachoengsao, Thailand</a>, <a href="/wiki/Calamba,_Laguna" title="Calamba, Laguna">Calamba</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cabuyao,_Laguna" class="mw-redirect" title="Cabuyao, Laguna">Cabuyao</a>, Philippines. Sales for the fiscal year ending on March 31, 2019 were $5.35 billion.<sup id="cite_ref-10K_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10K-3">[3]</a></sup></p><p>Microchip Technology offers support and resources to educators, researchers and students in an effort to increase awareness and knowledge of embedded applications.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Support includes access to labs, curricula and course materials, One-on-one consultations, online resources (e.g., code examples, textbook recommendations), training at regional training centers, silicon donations, assistance finding low cost development tools, free versions of Microchip programming tools and product discounts.
</p>

</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(1)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Microchip_Technology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History" data-section="1" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-1 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-1">
<p>Microchip Technology was founded in 1987 when <a href="/wiki/General_Instrument" title="General Instrument">General Instrument</a> spun off its microelectronics division as a wholly owned subsidiary.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>
The newly formed company was a supplier of programmable <a href="/wiki/Non-volatile_memory" title="Non-volatile memory">non-volatile memory</a>, <a href="/wiki/Microcontroller" title="Microcontroller">microcontrollers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Digital_signal_processor" title="Digital signal processor">digital signal processors</a>, card <a href="/wiki/Chip_on_board" title="Chip on board">chip on board</a>, and consumer <a href="/wiki/Integrated_circuit" title="Integrated circuit">integrated circuits</a>.
An initial public offering later in the year was canceled because of the <a href="/wiki/Black_Monday_(1987)" title="Black Monday (1987)">October 1987 stock market crash</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>
Microchip Technology became an independent company in 1989 when it was acquired by a group of venture capitalists led by <a href="/wiki/Sequoia_Capital" title="Sequoia Capital">Sequoia Capital</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>
In the same year, Microchip Technology announced the release of small, inexpensive 8-bit <a href="/wiki/Reduced_instruction_set_computer" title="Reduced instruction set computer">reduced instruction set computing</a> (RISC) microcontrollers for $2.40 apiece whereas most RISC microcontrollers were 32-bit devices selling for hundreds of dollars.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p><p>In 1990, 60% of Microchip Technology's sales were from the disc drive industry and the product portfolio relied heavily on commodity <a href="/wiki/EEPROM" title="EEPROM">EEPROM</a> products.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>
The company was losing $2.5 million per quarter, had less than 6 months of cash in reserve, had exhausted lines of credit, and was failing to control expenses.
Early in the year, the venture capital investors accepted an offer to sell Microchip Technology to <a href="/wiki/Winbond" title="Winbond">Winbond Electronics Corporation</a> of <a href="/wiki/Taiwan" title="Taiwan">Taiwan</a> for $15 million. Winbond Electronics backed out of the deal after the Taiwanese stock market decrease in May 1990. Vice President of Operations, <a href="/wiki/Steve_Sanghi" title="Steve Sanghi">Steve Sanghi</a>, was named president and chief operating officer of Microchip Technology in 1990.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>
After several quarters of losses, Sanghi oversaw Microchip Technology's transition from selling commodity-based products to specialized chips, such as the RISC technology.
</p><p>Microchip Technology conducted an IPO in 1993, which <a href="/wiki/Fortune_(magazine)" title="Fortune (magazine)">Fortune magazine</a> cited as the best performing IPO of the year with a stock appreciation of 500% and over $1 billion in <a href="/wiki/Market_capitalization" title="Market capitalization">market capitalization</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>
At the end of 2015, Microchip Technology posted its 100th consecutive quarter of profitability.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> 
In-line with the general consolidation of the semiconductor industry, Microchip Technology purchased 17 semiconductor manufacturers from 2007 through 2017.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(2)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Products">Products</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Microchip_Technology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Products" data-section="2" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-2 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-2">
<p>Microchip Technology offers thousands of products including 8-bit microcontrollers, 16-bit PIC Microcontrollers, dsPIC® digital signal controllers, 32-bit microcontrollers, 32-bit Arm® Microprocessors, analog and interface products, security and authentication products, timing/communication/real-time clock and calendar products, real-time clock and calendar devices, memory products, wireless products, high-throughput USB and Ethernet interface solutions, MOST® technology, embedded controllers and super I/O devices, touch, multi-touch and 3D gesture control solutions, power over Ethernet systems and ICs, and field programmable gate arrays (FPGAs).<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="8-bit_Microcontrollers">8-bit Microcontrollers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Microchip_Technology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: 8-bit Microcontrollers" data-section="3" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>Microchip Technology's 8-bit portfolio consists of over 1,200 devices constructed under two architectures: <a href="/wiki/PIC_microcontroller" class="mw-redirect" title="PIC microcontroller">PIC microcontrollers</a> or <a href="/wiki/AVR_microcontrollers" title="AVR microcontrollers">AVR microcontrollers</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> Key features of the 8-bit microcontrollers are Core Independent Peripherals, low-power performance with picoPower and eXtreme Low Power (XLP) technology and EMI/EMC performance.
</p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="16-bit_PIC_Microcontrollers">16-bit PIC Microcontrollers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Microchip_Technology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: 16-bit PIC Microcontrollers" data-section="4" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>The 16-bit microcontrollers, such as the <a href="/wiki/PIC_microcontroller#PIC24_and_dsPIC" class="mw-redirect" title="PIC microcontroller">PIC24</a>, offer an upgrade over the 8-bit devices in features and peripherals (e.g., more memory, additional pins).<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> The 16-bit microcontrollers are constructed under the PIC microcontroller architecture. 
</p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="dsPIC_Digital_Signal_Controllers">dsPIC Digital Signal Controllers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Microchip_Technology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: dsPIC Digital Signal Controllers" data-section="5" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/PIC_microcontroller#PIC24_and_dsPIC" class="mw-redirect" title="PIC microcontroller">dsPIC</a> product family of <a href="/wiki/Digital_signal_controller" title="Digital signal controller">digital signal controllers</a> includes a <a href="/wiki/Digital_signal_processor" title="Digital signal processor">digital signal processor</a> engine with up to 100 MIPS of motor control that offers variable speeds, constant torque PI control and field oriented control.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> The dsPIC family also offers dual cores equipped with <a href="/wiki/Pulse-width_modulation" title="Pulse-width modulation">pulse-width modulation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Analog-to-digital_converter" title="Analog-to-digital converter">analog-to-digital converters</a>, programmable gate arrays and the capability for live firmware updates and provides permanent-magnet motors (see also <a href="/wiki/Synchronous_motor#Permanent-magnet_motors" title="Synchronous motor">Synchronous motor#Permanent-magnet motors</a>), alternating current induction motor (see also <a href="/wiki/Induction_motor" title="Induction motor">induction motor</a>) and brushless DC (see also <a href="/wiki/Brushless_DC_electric_motor" title="Brushless DC electric motor">Brushless DC electric motor</a>) motor control in industrial, medical, automotive and consumer applications.
</p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="32-bit_PIC_Microcontrollers">32-bit PIC Microcontrollers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Microchip_Technology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: 32-bit PIC Microcontrollers" data-section="6" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>Microchip Technology's 32-bit product portfolio run at up to 600 DMIPs with up to 2048 KB Flash and 512 KB RAM with 32 MB integrated DDR2 <a href="/wiki/Dynamic_random-access_memory" title="Dynamic random-access memory">dynamic random-access memory</a> (DRAM) or 128 MB externally addressable options.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> The 32-bit portfolio addresses advanced graphics and <a href="/wiki/Internet_of_Things" class="mw-redirect" title="Internet of Things">Internet of Things</a> (IoT) applications.
</p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="32-bit_Arm_Microprocessors">32-bit Arm Microprocessors</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Microchip_Technology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: 32-bit Arm Microprocessors" data-section="7" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Atmel_ARM-based_processors" title="Atmel ARM-based processors">32-bit Arm microprocessors</a> were designed for applications beyond the 32-bit microcontrollers with 600 MHz (942 DMIPS) operation, support for up to 512 MD of external DDR2 or DDR3 DRAM and power down to 0.3 mW sleep.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> Available peripherals and users interfaces include gigabit <a href="/wiki/Ethernet" title="Ethernet">Ethernet</a> <a href="/wiki/MAC_addresses" class="mw-redirect" title="MAC addresses">MAC addresses</a>, <a href="/wiki/USB" title="USB">USBs</a>, hardware video decoding, <a href="/wiki/Capacitive_sensing" title="Capacitive sensing">capacitive sensing</a>, 12-bit <a href="/wiki/Active-pixel_sensor#CMOS_sensor" title="Active-pixel sensor">CMOS sensors</a>, <a href="/wiki/I%C2%B2S" title="I²S">I²S</a> audio interfaces and 24-bit graphic LCD controllers with overlays.
</p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Programming_Tools">Programming Tools</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Microchip_Technology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Programming Tools" data-section="8" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>Microchip Technology offers a variety of <a href="/wiki/Programming_tool" title="Programming tool">programming tools</a> to support the use of their microcontrollers, digital signal controllers, and microprocessors.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/MPLAB" title="MPLAB">MPLAB</a> and Atmel Studio ecosystems include <a href="/wiki/Integrated_development_environment" title="Integrated development environment">integrated development environments</a>, <a href="/wiki/Compiler" title="Compiler">compilers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Configurator" title="Configurator">configurators</a>, <a href="/wiki/Programmer_(hardware)" title="Programmer (hardware)">programmers</a> (e.g., <a href="/wiki/PICkit" title="PICkit">MPLAB PICkit™</a>), in-circuit <a href="/wiki/Emulator" title="Emulator">emulators</a> (e.g., <a href="/wiki/MPLAB_devices" title="MPLAB devices">MPLAB devices</a>), and <a href="/wiki/Debugger" title="Debugger">debuggers</a>.
</p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Analog_and_Interface_Products">Analog and Interface Products</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Microchip_Technology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Analog and Interface Products" data-section="9" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>Microchip Technology offers a broad portfolio of analog products that address <a href="/wiki/Thermal_management_(electronics)" title="Thermal management (electronics)">thermal management</a>, <a href="/wiki/Power_management" title="Power management">power management</a>, battery management, mixed-signal, linear, interface, safety and security needs<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup>. The product portfolio includes stand-alone analog and interface devices in highly integrated solutions that combine various analog functions, save space, support a variety of <a href="/wiki/Bus_(computing)" title="Bus (computing)">bus</a> interfaces and enhance analog features on microcontrollers, digital signal controllers, microprocessors and FPGAs. The power solutions include Silicon <a href="/wiki/Diode" title="Diode">diodes</a>, <a href="/wiki/MOSFET" title="MOSFET">MOSFETs</a>, <a href="/wiki/Insulated-gate_bipolar_transistor" title="Insulated-gate bipolar transistor">insulated-gate bipolar transistors</a>, Silicon carbide MOSFETs and <a href="/wiki/Schottky_diode" title="Schottky diode">Schottky diodes</a>.
</p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Security_and_Authentication_Products">Security and Authentication Products</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Microchip_Technology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Security and Authentication Products" data-section="10" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>Microchip Technology offers crypto element devices that provide <a href="/wiki/Authentication" title="Authentication">authentication</a>, <a href="/wiki/Data_integrity" title="Data integrity">data integrity</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Confidentiality" title="Confidentiality">confidentiality</a> in a variety of applications, such as disposables, accessories and nodes.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> The crypto element devices use ultra-secure, hardware-based cryptographic countermeasures including <a href="/wiki/Tamper-evident_technology" title="Tamper-evident technology">tamper detection</a>.
</p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span id="Timing.2C_Communication_and_Real-Time_Clock_and_Calendar_Products"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Timing,_Communication_and_Real-Time_Clock_and_Calendar_Products">Timing, Communication and Real-Time Clock and Calendar Products</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Microchip_Technology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Timing, Communication and Real-Time Clock and Calendar Products" data-section="11" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>Microchip Technology offers <a href="/wiki/Oscillation" title="Oscillation">oscillators</a>, <a href="/wiki/Clock_generator" title="Clock generator">clock generators</a>, clock and data distribution products and <a href="/wiki/Real-time_clock" title="Real-time clock">real-time clock and calendar</a> devices.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> The oscillator product line offer low <a href="/wiki/Jitter" title="Jitter">jitter</a> and low power online-configurable products with quartz-based or <a href="/wiki/Microelectromechanical_systems" title="Microelectromechanical systems">MEMS</a> silicon-based <a href="/wiki/Resonator" title="Resonator">resonator</a> options. The clock generation product line offer online-configurable, single chip, multiple-frequency clock tree options. The clock and data distribution product line offers buffers, logic translators and <a href="/wiki/Multiplexer" title="Multiplexer">multiplexers</a>. The packet network synchronization product line includes ITU-T/IEEE® standards-compliant digital <a href="/wiki/Phase-locked_loop" title="Phase-locked loop">phase-locked loops</a> for <a href="/wiki/Synchronous_Ethernet" title="Synchronous Ethernet">synchronous Ethernet</a> as well as IEEE 1588 based applications. The real-time clock and calendar devices offer a battery back-up capability, digital timing, and on-board <a href="/wiki/EEPROM" title="EEPROM">EEPROM</a> and <a href="/wiki/SRAM" class="mw-disambig" title="SRAM">SRAM</a> memory.
</p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Memory_Products">Memory Products</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Microchip_Technology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Memory Products" data-section="12" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>Microchip Technology offers a wide-range of memory products that includes serial <a href="/wiki/EEPROM" title="EEPROM">EEPROM</a>, serial <a href="/wiki/SRAM" class="mw-disambig" title="SRAM">SRAM</a>, <a href="/wiki/Flash_memory" title="Flash memory">serial flash</a>, serial <a href="/wiki/NvSRAM" title="NvSRAM">NvSRAM</a>, serial EERAM, parallel EEPROM, parallel one-time programmable flash,  parallel flash and CryptoMemory devices.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Wireless_Products">Wireless Products</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Microchip_Technology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Wireless Products" data-section="13" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>

<p><b>Silicon Storage Technology, Inc.</b> (SST) was a <a href="/wiki/Sunnyvale,_California" title="Sunnyvale, California">Sunnyvale</a>, <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a>, United States, technology company producing <a href="/wiki/Non-volatile_memory" title="Non-volatile memory">non-volatile memory</a> devices and related products.<sup id="cite_ref-TID_48-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TID-48">[48]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-SST2009Q2_49-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SST2009Q2-49">[49]</a></sup>
SST supplied NOR flash and other <a href="/wiki/Integrated_circuit" title="Integrated circuit">integrated circuits</a> for high-volume applications.<sup id="cite_ref-Profile_50-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Profile-50">[50]</a></sup><a href="/wiki/Bing_Yeh" title="Bing Yeh">Bing Yeh</a> co-founded SST in August 1989, and served as its chief executive.<sup id="cite_ref-SVIE_51-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SVIE-51">[51]</a></sup>
Starting in February 2010, private equity firm <a href="/wiki/Cerberus_Capital_Management" title="Cerberus Capital Management">Cerberus Capital Management</a> and public company Microchip Technology both made offers to acquire SST.<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup>

Microchip sold several SST flash memory assets to <a href="/wiki/Greenliant_Systems" title="Greenliant Systems">Greenliant Systems</a> (founded by Yeh) in May of that year.<sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup></p>

</p>

<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="ISSC_Technologies">ISSC Technologies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Microchip_Technology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: ISSC Technologies" data-section="24" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p><b>ISSC Technologies</b> (ISSC) was a <a href="/wiki/Taiwan" title="Taiwan">Taiwan</a> based developer of <a href="/wiki/Bluetooth" title="Bluetooth">Bluetooth</a> <a href="/wiki/System_on_chip" class="mw-redirect" title="System on chip">system on chip</a> solutions for wireless <a href="/wiki/Headset" class="mw-disambig" title="Headset">headset</a>, <a href="/wiki/Loudspeaker" title="Loudspeaker">speaker</a>, connectivity and <a href="/wiki/Human_interface_device" title="Human interface device">human interface device</a> products.<sup id="cite_ref-70" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup> Microchip Technology announced the signing of a definitive agreement to acquire ISSC on May 22, 2014. The terms of the agreement stipulated that Microchip Technology would acquire all outstanding shares of ISSC for approximately $4.74 per share at a total equirety value of about $328.5 million and a total enterprise value of about $34.2 million after accounting for ISSC's cash and investments. The acqusition was expected to strengthen Microchip Technology's expertise in Bluetooth and wireless technologies and position in consumer markets including applications in the wireless and IoT areas. Microchip Technology announced the completion of its tend offer to acquire the outstanding shares of ISSC on July 14, 2014.<sup id="cite_ref-71" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup> The ISSC acquisition represents the first major overseas acquisition completed by Microchip Technology and was funded with the use of a portion of Microchip Technology's foreign cash.
</p>

<p><b>Supertex</b> was a mixed signal semiconductor manufacturer that focused on high voltage analog and mixed signal products for use in the following industries: medical, LED lighting, display, industrial and telecommunications.<sup id="cite_ref-72" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-72">[72]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-73" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-73">[73]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-74" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-74">[74]</a></sup> Microchip Technology announced the execution of a definitive agreement to acquire Supertex, Inc. on February 10, 2014 for $33 per share in cash. The total equity value was $394 million and the total enterprise value after excluding Supertex's cash and investments was approximately $148 million. The acquisition was expected to expand Microchip Technology's expertise in high voltage analog and mixed signal technologies and reach into the medical, industrial and lighting industries. Microchip Technology announced the completion of the acquisition on April 1, 2014 with 98.4% of the Supertex shares that voted approving the merger.<sup id="cite_ref-75" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-75">[75]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Micrel">Micrel</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Microchip_Technology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Micrel" data-section="26" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p><b>Micrel</b> was a global manufacturer of <a href="/wiki/Integrated_circuit" title="Integrated circuit">IC</a> solutions focusing on the networking and consumer markets.<sup id="cite_ref-76" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-76">[76]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-77" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-77">[77]</a></sup> Microchip Technology announced the signing of a definitive agreement to acquire Micrel for $14.00 a share on May 7, 2015. The price per share represented a 3% premium over Micrel's closing stock price on May 6, 2015 and a 30% premium at the close on August 7, 2014. The total equity value was about $839 million. The completion of the acquisition was announced on August 3, 2015 with 98.5% of Micrel shares that voted approving the merger.<sup id="cite_ref-78" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-78">[78]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-79" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-79">[79]</a></sup></p>

<p><b>Atmel</b> was a designer and manufacturer of semiconductors focusing on selling microcontrollers. <h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Microsemi">Microsemi</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Microchip_Technology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: Microsemi" data-section="28" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p><b>Microsemi</b> manufactured semiconductors and system solutions for several markets including aerospace &amp; defense, communications, data centers, and industrial markets. In March 2018, Microchip acquired <a href="/wiki/Microsemi_Corporation" class="mw-redirect" title="Microsemi Corporation">Microsemi Corporation</a>. The acquisition price represents a total equity value of about $8.35 billion, and a total enterprise value of about $10.15 billion, after accounting for Microsemi’s cash and investments, net of debt, on its balance sheet at December 31, 2017.<sup id="cite_ref-84" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-84">[84]</a></sup></p>
