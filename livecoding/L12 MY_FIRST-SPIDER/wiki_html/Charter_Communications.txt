</p>
<p><b>Charter Communications, Inc.</b>, is an American <a href="/wiki/Telecommunications" class="mw-redirect" title="Telecommunications">telecommunications</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mass_media" title="Mass media">mass media</a> company that offers its services to consumers and businesses under the branding of <a href="/wiki/Spectrum_(cable_service)" class="mw-redirect" title="Spectrum (cable service)">Spectrum</a>. Providing services to over 26 million customers in 41 states,<sup id="cite_ref-CharterProfile_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CharterProfile-1">[1]</a></sup> it is the second-largest <a href="/wiki/Cable_television" title="Cable television">cable</a> operator in the United States by subscribers, just behind <a href="/wiki/Comcast" title="Comcast">Comcast</a>, and third largest pay TV operator behind Comcast and <a href="/wiki/AT%26T" title="AT&amp;T">AT&amp;T</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>  It is the fifth largest telephone provider based upon residential subscriber line count.

</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(1)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Charter_Communications&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History" data-section="1" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-1 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-1">
<h3 class="in-block"><span id="1980.E2.80.931993.E2.80.931998:_Beginnings"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="1980–1993–1998:_Beginnings">1980–1993–1998: Beginnings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Charter_Communications&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: 1980–1993–1998: Beginnings" data-section="2" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>Charter Communications CATV systems was founded in 1980 by Charles H. Leonard in Barry County, Michigan.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2019)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> The original Charter system headend and offices were located at 1001 Payne Lake Road, Yankee Springs Township, Michigan.  Mr. Leonard entered into a corporate partnership with Gary Wilcox and Gerry Kazma, both from Naperville, Illinois. During this time, 1981-1983, Spectrum Communications  (Kazma) merged with the Charter Systems.
</p><p>Through continued mergers and acquisition Charter was consolidated  in 1993 by Barry Babcock, Jerald Kent and Howard Wood, who had been former executives at <a href="/wiki/Cencom_Cable_Associates" title="Cencom Cable Associates">Cencom Cable Television</a> in St. Louis, Missouri. It was also incorporated in the state of Missouri in 1993.<sup id="cite_ref-history_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-history-12">[12]</a></sup></p><p>In 1995, Charter paid about $300 million for a <a href="/wiki/Controlling_interest" title="Controlling interest">controlling interest</a> in <a href="/wiki/Crown_Media_Holdings" title="Crown Media Holdings">Crown Media Holdings</a> and acquired Cable South.<sup id="cite_ref-history_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-history-12">[12]</a></sup></p><p>In 1997, Charter and <a href="/wiki/EarthLink" title="EarthLink">EarthLink</a> joined forces to deliver <a href="/wiki/Internet_access" title="Internet access">high-speed Internet</a> access through cable modems to Charter's customers in <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles" title="Los Angeles">Los Angeles</a> and <a href="/wiki/Riverside,_California" title="Riverside, California">Riverside, California</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-history_12-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-history-12">[12]</a></sup></p><p>In 1998, <a href="/wiki/Paul_Allen" title="Paul Allen">Paul Allen</a> bought a controlling interest. The company paid $2.8 billion to acquire Dallas-based cable company <a href="/wiki/Marcus_Cable" title="Marcus Cable">Marcus Cable</a>. Charter Communications had 1 million customers in 1998.<sup id="cite_ref-history_12-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-history-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span id="1999.E2.80.932008:_NASDAQ_listing_and_acquisitions"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="1999–2008:_NASDAQ_listing_and_acquisitions">1999–2008: NASDAQ listing and acquisitions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Charter_Communications&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: 1999–2008: NASDAQ listing and acquisitions" data-section="3" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>In November 1999, the company went public, trading on the <a href="/wiki/NASDAQ" class="mw-redirect" title="NASDAQ">NASDAQ</a> stock exchange.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> At the time, it had 3.9 million customers.
</p><p>Charter completed more than 10 major acquisitions in 1999 when it:<sup id="cite_ref-history_12-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-history-12">[12]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<ul><li>Added 68,000 subscribers in Southern California with the purchase of four cable systems from American Cable Entertainment of Stamford, Connecticut.</li>
<li>Acquired 400,000 <a href="/wiki/InterMedia_Partners" title="InterMedia Partners">InterMedia Partners</a> subscribers, primarily in the Southeast. As part of the deal Charter would turn over about 140,000 of its subscribers to <a href="/wiki/Tele-Communications_Inc." title="Tele-Communications Inc.">TCI</a> in cable system swap.</li>
<li>Merged with <a href="/wiki/Marcus_Cable" title="Marcus Cable">Marcus Cable</a></li>
<li>Acquired cable systems serving 460,000 subscribers from Rifkin Acquisition Partners and InterLink Communications.</li>
<li>Acquired 173,000 subscribers, mostly in central Massachusetts, from New Jersey-based Greater Media Inc.</li>
<li>Acquired Renaissance Media Group, a New York partnership serving 130,000 customers near <a href="/wiki/New_Orleans" title="New Orleans">New Orleans</a>, western Mississippi, and <a href="/wiki/Jackson,_Tennessee" title="Jackson, Tennessee">Jackson, Tennessee</a>.</li>
<li>Acquired New Jersey-based Helicon Cable Communications. The systems served about 171,000 customers in eight states in the Southeast and Northeast.</li>
<li>Acquired Avalon Cable TV, adding 260,000 subscribers primarily in <a href="/wiki/Michigan" title="Michigan">Michigan</a> and <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts" title="Massachusetts">Massachusetts</a>.</li>
<li>Acquired Vista Broadband Communications in <a href="/wiki/Smyrna,_Georgia" title="Smyrna, Georgia">Smyrna</a>, Georgia, adding 30,000 more customers.</li>
<li>Acquired Falcon Cable TV of <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles" title="Los Angeles">Los Angeles</a>. Falcon was the eighth-largest cable operator in the United States with about one million subscribers in 27 states in primarily non-urban areas.</li>
<li>Acquired Fanch Communications Inc. of <a href="/wiki/Denver" title="Denver">Denver</a>. Fanch had 547,000 subscribers in <a href="/wiki/West_Virginia" title="West Virginia">West Virginia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pennsylvania" title="Pennsylvania">Pennsylvania</a>, <a href="/wiki/Michigan" title="Michigan">Michigan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Indiana" title="Indiana">Indiana</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kentucky" title="Kentucky">Kentucky</a>, <a href="/wiki/Louisiana" title="Louisiana">Louisiana</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Wisconsin" title="Wisconsin">Wisconsin</a>.</li></ul><p>Charter also began swapping customers with other systems to improve the geographic clustering of its systems. In December 1999, it signed a letter of intent with <a href="/wiki/AT%26T_Corporation" title="AT&amp;T Corporation">AT&amp;T Corporation</a> to swap 1.3 million cable subscribers in St. Louis as well as in <a href="/wiki/Alabama" title="Alabama">Alabama</a>, <a href="/wiki/Georgia_(U.S._state)" title="Georgia (U.S. state)">Georgia</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Missouri" title="Missouri">Missouri</a>. In 2000, Charter Communications bought select AT&amp;T cable markets, including <a href="/wiki/Reno,_Nevada" title="Reno, Nevada">Reno, Nevada</a>, and the City of St. Louis.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p><p>In 2001, <a href="/wiki/MSN" title="MSN">MSN</a> and Charter signed an agreement to offer MSN content and services to Charter's broadband customers. In the same year, Charter received awards, including the Outstanding Corporate Growth Award from the Association for Corporate Growth, the <a href="/wiki/Ted_Turner" title="Ted Turner">R.E. "Ted" Turner</a> Innovator of the Year Award from the Southern Cable Telecommunications Association,<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> and the Fast 50 Award for Growth from the St. Louis Regional Chamber and Growth Association.
</p><p>In 2008, Charter stock failed to meet <a href="/wiki/NASDAQ" class="mw-redirect" title="NASDAQ">NASDAQ</a> standards and was given warning to comply by October 13 or request an extension.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p><p>Also in 2008, it acquired the cable-television franchise and service for the <a href="/wiki/Cerritos,_California" title="Cerritos, California">Cerritos</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ventura,_California" title="Ventura, California">Ventura</a>, California, areas from <a href="/wiki/Wave_Broadband" title="Wave Broadband">Wave Broadband</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="2009:_Bankruptcy_and_emergence">2009: Bankruptcy and emergence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Charter_Communications&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: 2009: Bankruptcy and emergence" data-section="4" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>In February 2009, Charter Communications announced that it planned to file for <a href="/wiki/Chapter_11,_Title_11,_United_States_Code" title="Chapter 11, Title 11, United States Code">Chapter 11</a> of the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Bankruptcy_Code" class="mw-redirect" title="United States Bankruptcy Code">United States Bankruptcy Code</a> on or before April 1, 2009. The action would allow Charter to pay its debt obligations, and cancel its obligations to shareholders.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> <a href="/wiki/Private_equity" title="Private equity">Private equity</a> firm <a href="/wiki/Apollo_Management" class="mw-redirect" title="Apollo Management">Apollo Management</a> expected to own most of Charter's shares after the bankruptcy.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> Charter filed for a prearranged bankruptcy on March 28, 2009. The company expected the financial <a href="/wiki/Restructuring" title="Restructuring">restructuring</a> to reduce its debt by $8 billion, as well as adding $3 billion of new investment, and refinancing other debt.
</p><p>On November 30, 2009, its bankruptcy plan was approved, which extinguished its stock and cut approximately $8 billion in debt.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> That day, Charter emerged from bankruptcy despite many of its creditors' objections over its bankruptcy plan.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span id="2010.E2.80.932012:_NASDAQ_re-listing.3B_leadership_change"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="2010–2012:_NASDAQ_re-listing;_leadership_change">2010–2012: NASDAQ re-listing; leadership change</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Charter_Communications&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: 2010–2012: NASDAQ re-listing; leadership change" data-section="5" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:CharterPillHillRochesterMN.jpg" class="image"><noscript><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/60/CharterPillHillRochesterMN.jpg/220px-CharterPillHillRochesterMN.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="132" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="3785" data-file-height="2272"></noscript><span class="lazy-image-placeholder" style="width: 220px;height: 132px;" data-src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/60/CharterPillHillRochesterMN.jpg/220px-CharterPillHillRochesterMN.jpg" data-alt="" data-width="220" data-height="132" data-class="thumbimage"> </span></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:CharterPillHillRochesterMN.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Charter Communications service truck in 2012</div></div></div>
<p>On September 14, 2010, Charter Class A common stock was re-listed on NASDAQ under the symbol "CHTR".<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p><p>In 2011, <a href="/wiki/Microsoft" title="Microsoft">Microsoft</a> co-founder <a href="/wiki/Paul_Allen" title="Paul Allen">Paul Allen</a> stepped down as chairman and from the board of directors' seat, but at the time remained the largest single shareholder. Also in that year, Charter signed a multi-year deal with <a href="/wiki/TiVo" title="TiVo">TiVo</a> to deliver content via its platform.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p><p>Thomas M. Rutledge was appointed as a director and president and chief executive officer effective February 13, 2012.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p><p>The same year, Charter priced $1.25 billion senior debt, offering to pay down short- and long-term debt.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span id="2013.E2.80.932014:_Purchase_of_Optimum_West.3B_Liberty_Media_investment"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="2013–2014:_Purchase_of_Optimum_West;_Liberty_Media_investment">2013–2014: Purchase of Optimum West; Liberty Media investment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Charter_Communications&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: 2013–2014: Purchase of Optimum West; Liberty Media investment" data-section="6" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>On February 8, 2013, Charter announced an agreement to acquire some former <a href="/wiki/Optimum_West" class="mw-redirect" title="Optimum West">Bresnan Communications</a> systems from <a href="/wiki/Cablevision" title="Cablevision">Cablevision</a> in a transaction worth US$1.63 billion.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> The deal brought Charter cable systems to 375,000 customers in Colorado's mountains and Western Slope, as well as in Utah, Wyoming and Montana.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p><p>Approximately one month later, on March 19, 2013, Charter announced that <a href="/wiki/Liberty_Media" title="Liberty Media">Liberty Media</a>, a company controlled by former <a href="/wiki/Tele-Communications_Inc." title="Tele-Communications Inc.">TCI</a> CEO <a href="/wiki/John_C._Malone" title="John C. Malone">John C. Malone</a>, would be acquiring a 27.3% ownership interest in the company, making it the company's largest single shareholder, largely through the purchase of interests held by investment funds following Charter's 2009 restructuring.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> In November 2014, Liberty's holdings in Charter as well as a small minority interest in Time Warner Cable were spun off as a separate holding company named Liberty Broadband Corporation,<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> which as of early 2015 was 47.1% controlled by Malone.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span id="2014.E2.80.932017:_Acquisition_of_Time_Warner_Cable_and_Bright_House_Networks"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="2014–2017:_Acquisition_of_Time_Warner_Cable_and_Bright_House_Networks">2014–2017: Acquisition of Time Warner Cable and Bright House Networks</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Charter_Communications&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: 2014–2017: Acquisition of Time Warner Cable and Bright House Networks" data-section="7" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>On January 13, 2014, Charter Communications said it was interested in buying its larger rival <a href="/wiki/Time_Warner_Cable" title="Time Warner Cable">Time Warner Cable</a>. After three previous attempts to buy and merge with the company, all of which failed, Charter's chief executive officer Thomas Rutledge wrote in an open letter to Time Warner Cable's chief executive officer Robert Marcus stating, "I believe we have a significant opportunity to put our companies together in a way that will create maximum, long-term value for shareholders and employees of both companies".<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> The $132.50 per share offer, just above TWC's closing price at $132.40 on January 13, was rejected.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p><p>On February 13, 2014, Time Warner Cable accepted an offer of $158.82 per share from <a href="/wiki/Comcast" title="Comcast">Comcast</a>, avoiding a <a href="/wiki/Hostile_takeover" class="mw-redirect" title="Hostile takeover">hostile takeover</a> situation from Charter.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></p><p>On April 28, 2014, Comcast and Charter announced that, assuming Comcast's merger with Time Warner Cable was successful, Charter would acquire 1.4 million Comcast/Time Warner Cable customers, bringing Charter's subscriber total to 30 million and making Charter, by its own count, the second-largest cable operator in the country.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> In addition to the 1.4 million divested subscribers, Comcast also agreed to swap 1.6 million subscribers with Charter in an even, tax-efficient exchange whose intent is to improve the geographic spread of both companies. In a third part of the agreement, Comcast would spin off 2.5 million subscribers into a new publicly traded company in which Charter would hold a 33% stake – with an option to eventually own the whole company – and former Time Warner Cable shareholders would hold a 67% stake.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></p><p>In late March 2015, Charter announced plans to purchase <a href="/wiki/Bright_House_Networks" title="Bright House Networks">Bright House Networks</a> from <a href="/wiki/Advance_Publications" title="Advance Publications">Advance/Newhouse</a> for $10.4 billion in a combination of cash and equities convertible to Charter stock. The deal was contingent on, among other approvals, the completion of Charter's transactions with Comcast, and the expiration of Time Warner Cable's <a href="/wiki/Right_of_first_refusal" title="Right of first refusal">right of first offer</a> to buy Bright House itself (which was not expected to be exercised in light of the merger with Comcast).<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> However, facing potential difficulties in reaching regulatory approval, Comcast called off its merger with Time Warner Cable in April 2015.<sup id="cite_ref-nyt-calledoff_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nyt-calledoff-38">[38]</a></sup></p><p>On May 26, 2015, Charter and Time Warner Cable announced that they had entered into a definitive agreement for Charter to merge with Time Warner Cable in a deal valued at $78.7 billion.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> Charter also confirmed that it would continue with its proposed acquisition of Bright House Networks under slightly modified terms. The deal was subject to regulatory approval, although the deal was expected to face less scrutiny from the FCC than the Comcast/TWC deal, as the companies were relatively smaller, and their media holdings are not as extensive as those of Comcast. The TWC and Bright House systems were to be migrated to Charter's Spectrum brand following the conclusion of the merger.<sup id="cite_ref-nyt-merger_40-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nyt-merger-40">[40]</a></sup></p><p>Liberty Broadband will invest a further $5 billion in Charter and will ultimately hold about 20% ownership in the combined entity. Advance/Newhouse will own about 14%, and other current Time Warner Cable shareholders are expected to hold a combined 44% stake.<sup id="cite_ref-nyt-merger_40-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nyt-merger-40">[40]</a></sup> The merger was approved by the Department of Justice and FCC on April 25, 2016; it is subject to conditions, including a requirement that Charter must not implement usage-based billing, nor use its dominant position in the market to impact the online video industry – which includes a prohibition on charging for interconnections.<sup id="cite_ref-ars-okayedtwc_41-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ars-okayedtwc-41">[41]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-nyt-approvedtwc_42-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nyt-approvedtwc-42">[42]</a></sup> Charter was also required to expand its services to 2 million new households, with at least 1 million being in markets where competing providers operate.<sup id="cite_ref-verge-nooverbuilding_43-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-verge-nooverbuilding-43">[43]</a></sup></p><p>The merger was completed on May 18, 2016.<sup id="cite_ref-latimes-chartercompletespurchase_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-latimes-chartercompletespurchase-6">[6]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-bloomberg-solongtwc_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bloomberg-solongtwc-7">[7]</a></sup> The purchase made Charter the third-largest pay television company in the United States, behind <a href="/wiki/AT%26T" title="AT&amp;T">AT&amp;T</a> and <a href="/wiki/Comcast" title="Comcast">Comcast</a> (the former having completed its merger with <a href="/wiki/DirecTV" title="DirecTV">DirecTV</a> in mid-2015).<sup id="cite_ref-lat-chartertwc_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-lat-chartertwc-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span id="2017.E2.80.93present:_Post-TWC_acquisition"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="2017–present:_Post-TWC_acquisition">2017–present: Post-TWC acquisition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Charter_Communications&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: 2017–present: Post-TWC acquisition" data-section="8" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Charter_Communications_branded_Versalift_Ford_F-450_Super_Duty_bucket_truck_in_Gillette,_Wyoming.jpg" class="image"><noscript><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bd/Charter_Communications_branded_Versalift_Ford_F-450_Super_Duty_bucket_truck_in_Gillette%2C_Wyoming.jpg/220px-Charter_Communications_branded_Versalift_Ford_F-450_Super_Duty_bucket_truck_in_Gillette%2C_Wyoming.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="5184" data-file-height="3456"></noscript><span class="lazy-image-placeholder" style="width: 220px;height: 147px;" data-src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bd/Charter_Communications_branded_Versalift_Ford_F-450_Super_Duty_bucket_truck_in_Gillette%2C_Wyoming.jpg/220px-Charter_Communications_branded_Versalift_Ford_F-450_Super_Duty_bucket_truck_in_Gillette%2C_Wyoming.jpg" data-alt="" data-width="220" data-height="147" data-class="thumbimage"> </span></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Charter_Communications_branded_Versalift_Ford_F-450_Super_Duty_bucket_truck_in_Gillette,_Wyoming.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A Charter Communications-branded Versalift <a href="/wiki/Ford_Super_Duty" title="Ford Super Duty">Ford F-450 Super Duty</a> bucket truck in <a href="/wiki/Gillette,_Wyoming" title="Gillette, Wyoming">Gillette, Wyoming</a></div></div></div>
<p>On January 26, 2017, it was reported that <a href="/wiki/Verizon_Communications" title="Verizon Communications">Verizon Communications</a> was in talks with Charter to discuss a possible buyout.<sup id="cite_ref-verizon1_44-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-verizon1-44">[44]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-verizon2_45-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-verizon2-45">[45]</a></sup> President and CEO of <a href="/wiki/Liberty_Media" title="Liberty Media">Liberty Media</a>, <a href="/wiki/Greg_Maffei" title="Greg Maffei">Greg Maffei</a> said that they were not interested in the deal.<sup id="cite_ref-verizon3_46-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-verizon3-46">[46]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-verizon4_47-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-verizon4-47">[47]</a></sup> The deal was rejected around the end of May 2017.<sup id="cite_ref-verizon5_48-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-verizon5-48">[48]</a></sup> Charter claimed that the deal was too low for them to accept, and Charter's largest shareholder Liberty Media stated that they weren't ready to sell.
</p><p>In March 2017 under new FCC leadership, Charter's regulatory conditions were changed to require that Charter expand its services to 2 million households that are not currently served by any broadband provider, as opposed to requiring one million of these households to be in areas served by a competitor. The decision was made under goals by new chairman <a href="/wiki/Ajit_Pai" title="Ajit Pai">Ajit Pai</a> to increase the availability of broadband in rural areas not served by high-speed Internet, but was criticized for maintaining oligopolies rather than encouraging wider competition.<sup id="cite_ref-verge-nooverbuilding_43-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-verge-nooverbuilding-43">[43]</a></sup></p><p>In May 2017, it was reported that Charter and Comcast had entered into an agreement to "explore working together in a number of potential operational areas in the wireless space" in respect to <a href="/wiki/Mobile_virtual_network_operator" title="Mobile virtual network operator">mobile virtual network operators</a> (MVNOs); both providers have agreements with <a href="/wiki/Verizon_Wireless" title="Verizon Wireless">Verizon Wireless</a> to re-sell its services, and Comcast announced that it would begin to do so under the brand <a href="/wiki/Xfinity#Cellular_service" title="Xfinity">Xfinity Mobile</a> later in the year. The agreement includes a provision, lasting for one year, that requires the companies to receive consent from each other before performing wireless-related acquisitions or mergers.<sup id="cite_ref-ars-noncompetemobile_49-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ars-noncompetemobile-49">[49]</a></sup></p><p>On June 21, 2017, it was reported that Charter was in talks to buy <a href="/wiki/Cox_Communications" title="Cox Communications">Cox Communications</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-CharterCox1_50-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CharterCox1-50">[50]</a></sup></p><p>On March 28, 2017, <a href="/wiki/International_Brotherhood_of_Electrical_Workers" title="International Brotherhood of Electrical Workers">IBEW Local 3</a> went on strike, representing 1,800 employees. The company has proposed moving independently managed health and pension benefits to its own company plans, which union members consider would include drastic cuts for them and their families and loss of job security.<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup> No significant progress has been made, and the strike continues as of late December 2018.<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup></p><p>On March 12, 2018, it was reported that <a href="/wiki/Softbank" class="mw-redirect" title="Softbank">Softbank</a> had purchased 5% of Charter's stock from the open market.<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup></p>
<h4 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Threatened_revocation_of_New_York_cable_franchises">Threatened revocation of New York cable franchises</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Charter_Communications&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Threatened revocation of New York cable franchises" data-section="9" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h4>
<p>In June 2018, the <a href="/wiki/New_York_Public_Service_Commission" title="New York Public Service Commission">New York Public Service Commission</a> fined Charter $2 million for failing to meet obligations it agreed to as conditions of its acquisition of Time Warner Cable. Charter was required to expand broadband service to at least 145,000 unserved or underserved residential units over 4 years, with a minimum of 36,250 new units per-year. The company was accused of making false statements in its progress reports, with an audit finding that Charter fraudulently declared at least 14,000 addresses already served by the company as being "new" deployments. The commission threatened the possibility of further regulatory remedies, including revocation of its cable franchises.<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup></p><p>On July 27, 2018, the NYPSC voted to retroactively reverse its approval of Charter's acquisition of TWC, thus revoking its franchises in the state of New York. The commission cited Charter's repeated failures to meet deadlines on expansion promised as part of the TWC purchase, "attempts to skirt obligations to serve rural communities", and "purposeful obfuscation of its performance and compliance obligations to the Commission and its customers." Within 60 days, Charter was to submit a plan to divest and migrate its New York state cable operations (which serve around 2 million customers) to new owners.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_57-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-57">[57]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-59" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup></p><p>Charter CEO <a href="/wiki/Tom_Rutledge" title="Tom Rutledge">Tom Rutledge</a> threatened legal action against the Commission.<sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup> The company was later granted repeated extensions of its deadline.<sup id="cite_ref-61" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-62" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup></p><p>In April 2019, Charter agreed to new conditions, under which it must complete its expansion of 145,000 new premises by September 30, 2021 (being credited for 64,827 premises up until December 2018), all of which must be outside of New York City, and are subject to milestone requirements. Charter must also contribute $12 million to a fund "for broadband expansion projects at locations to be selected by the Department and the Broadband Program Office", with half of this funding to be provided to either Charter or a competitor via a competitive bidding process.<sup id="cite_ref-63" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup></p>

<h4 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Coverage">Coverage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Charter_Communications&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Coverage" data-section="12" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Charter_Spectrum_in_Gillette,_Wyoming.jpg" class="image"><noscript><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/74/Charter_Spectrum_in_Gillette%2C_Wyoming.jpg/220px-Charter_Spectrum_in_Gillette%2C_Wyoming.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="5472" data-file-height="3648"></noscript><span class="lazy-image-placeholder" style="width: 220px;height: 147px;" data-src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/74/Charter_Spectrum_in_Gillette%2C_Wyoming.jpg/220px-Charter_Spectrum_in_Gillette%2C_Wyoming.jpg" data-alt="" data-width="220" data-height="147" data-class="thumbimage"> </span></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Charter_Spectrum_in_Gillette,_Wyoming.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A Charter Spectrum office in <a href="/wiki/Gillette,_Wyoming" title="Gillette, Wyoming">Gillette, Wyoming</a></div></div></div>
<p>Charter Communications offers service to an estimated 25 million people in 41 states with significant coverage in 48 states.<sup id="cite_ref-64" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-65" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup></p><p>In November 2013, the company announced the re-branding of its residential services to Charter Spectrum<sup id="cite_ref-66" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup> which encompass an upgrade to an all-digital network for its video, voice and broadband services. The company relied heavily on a predominantly <a href="/wiki/Coaxial_cable" title="Coaxial cable">coaxial cable</a>-based network. The newer <a href="/wiki/Fiber_optic" class="mw-redirect" title="Fiber optic">fiber-optic</a> service-delivery system provides higher <a href="/wiki/Bandwidth_(computing)" title="Bandwidth (computing)">bandwidth</a> speeds than are available with its coaxial cable infrastructure.<sup id="cite_ref-67" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup></p>
<h4 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Call_centers">Call centers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Charter_Communications&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Call centers" data-section="13" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h4>
<p>On May 2, 2006, the company announced it would restructure seven of its <a href="/wiki/Call_centre" title="Call centre">call centers</a> in the United States in the following locations:
</p>
<div class="div-col columns column-width" style="-moz-column-width: 30em; -webkit-column-width: 30em; column-width: 30em;">
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/St._Louis,_Missouri" class="mw-redirect" title="St. Louis, Missouri">St. Louis, Missouri</a> – Residential HSI/Phone Support, July 31, 2006; converted into a Charter Phone service call center</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Bay_City,_Michigan" title="Bay City, Michigan">Bay City, Michigan</a> – September 2006; converted into a Charter Dispatch center</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Birmingham,_Alabama" title="Birmingham, Alabama">Birmingham, Alabama</a> – December 2006; converted into a Charter Dispatch center</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Fort_Worth,_Texas" title="Fort Worth, Texas">Fort Worth, Texas</a> – December 1, 2006; shuttered</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Irwindale,_California" title="Irwindale, California">Irwindale, California</a> – December 2009; restructured</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Brookfield,_Connecticut" title="Brookfield, Connecticut">Brookfield, Connecticut</a> – March 2007; restructured</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Kingsport,_Tennessee" title="Kingsport, Tennessee">Kingsport, Tennessee</a> – March 2007; converted to Dispatch Center with location change</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Amherst,_Nova_Scotia" title="Amherst, Nova Scotia">Amherst, Nova Scotia</a> – December 2010; third-party contract, Teletech, not renewed</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Louisville,_Kentucky" title="Louisville, Kentucky">Louisville, Kentucky</a> – Residential HSI/Phone Support, Charter Business technical support, Network Operations Center</li></ul></div>
<p>Orders completed online or through retail partners with Charter Communications are directed to a call center located in <a href="/wiki/Tempe,_Arizona" title="Tempe, Arizona">Tempe, Arizona</a>, operated by Teletech (Direct Alliance). This call center has inbound/outbound sales agents, as well as online chat agents. Outsourced call centers were implemented in 2006 and are located in Canada, Honduras and the Philippines.

<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Former_operations">Former operations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Charter_Communications&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Former operations" data-section="14" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>On March 27, 2006, Charter announced that it would sell cable systems serving approximately 43,000 customers in <a href="/wiki/Nevada" title="Nevada">Nevada</a>, <a href="/wiki/Colorado" title="Colorado">Colorado</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_Mexico" title="New Mexico">New Mexico</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Utah" title="Utah">Utah</a> to Orange Broadband Holding Company (since renamed <a href="/wiki/Baja_Broadband" title="Baja Broadband">Baja Broadband</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-69" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup></p><p>Charter also sold cable systems in West Virginia and Virginia to Cebridge Connections (now known as <a href="/wiki/Suddenlink_Communications" title="Suddenlink Communications">Suddenlink Communications</a>) and cable systems in Kentucky and Illinois to New Wave Communications.<sup id="cite_ref-70" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup> The company eventually returned to those areas (excluding Illinois) in 2016 when it acquired Time Warner Cable.
</p><p>On October 14, 2008, the <i>Fairmont Sentinel</i> reported that Charter was selling parts of their system to <a href="/wiki/Midcontinent_Communications" class="mw-redirect" title="Midcontinent Communications">Midcontinent Communications</a>, including Charter's offices in <a href="/wiki/Bemidji,_Minnesota" title="Bemidji, Minnesota">Bemidji</a> and <a href="/wiki/International_Falls,_Minnesota" title="International Falls, Minnesota">International Falls</a>, Minnesota.<sup id="cite_ref-71" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup> Starting February 1, 2009, Midcontinent took over some of Charter's cable system in Minnesota including <a href="/wiki/Balaton,_Minnesota" title="Balaton, Minnesota">Balaton</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bemidji" class="mw-redirect" title="Bemidji">Bemidji</a>, <a href="/wiki/Canby,_Minnesota" title="Canby, Minnesota">Canby</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ely,_Minnesota" title="Ely, Minnesota">Ely</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fairmont,_Minnesota" title="Fairmont, Minnesota">Fairmont</a>, <a href="/wiki/International_Falls" class="mw-redirect" title="International Falls">International Falls</a>, <a href="/wiki/Littlefork" class="mw-redirect" title="Littlefork">Littlefork</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sherburn,_Minnesota" title="Sherburn, Minnesota">Sherburn</a>, and surrounding communities. Other areas in Minnesota would have sold to <a href="/wiki/Comcast" title="Comcast">Comcast</a>, but the deal fell through.<sup id="cite_ref-72" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-72">[72]</a></sup></p><p>On October 22, 2010, Charter sold 32 head-ends serving 65,000 customers in <a href="/wiki/Alabama" title="Alabama">Alabama</a>, <a href="/wiki/Arkansas" title="Arkansas">Arkansas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Georgia_(U.S._state)" title="Georgia (U.S. state)">Georgia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Louisiana" title="Louisiana">Louisiana</a>, <a href="/wiki/Missouri" title="Missouri">Missouri</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Texas" title="Texas">Texas</a> to <a href="/wiki/Cobridge_Communications" title="Cobridge Communications">Cobridge Communications</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-73" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-73">[73]</a></sup></p>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(3)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Criticism">Criticism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Charter_Communications&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Criticism" data-section="15" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-3 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-3">
<p>In 2007, <i><a href="/wiki/PC_World" title="PC World">PC World</a></i> ranked Charter's <a href="/wiki/Cable_modem" title="Cable modem">cable Internet</a> service as the worst among 14 major <a href="/wiki/Internet_service_provider" title="Internet service provider">Internet service providers</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-74" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-74">[74]</a></sup> In addition, Charter High-Speed was rated 19th out of 22 cable ISPs on dslreports.com,<sup id="cite_ref-75" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-75">[75]</a></sup> and <i><a href="/wiki/Consumer_Reports" title="Consumer Reports">Consumer Reports</a></i> indicated in its February 2008 issue that Charter's television/Internet/telephone bundle collectively was the worst of all major national carriers.<sup id="cite_ref-76" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-76">[76]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-77" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-77">[77]</a></sup></p><p>It was reported by Tony Bradle on <a href="/wiki/About.com" class="mw-redirect" title="About.com">about.com</a> that Charter Communications redirected error pages and Windows Live Search results to a Charter search page without notifying customers. Users may opt out of redirection by clicking a link from the Charter search page; however, the opt-out link saves a cookie on the customer's computer, so deleting cookies will require the user to opt out again.<sup id="cite_ref-78" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-78">[78]</a></sup></p><p>It was reported that on January 21, 2008, during a routine sweep of inactive accounts, Charter accidentally and irretrievably deleted the email accounts of approximately 14,000 customers. The company since decided to give a $150 account credit to each affected user.<sup id="cite_ref-79" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-79">[79]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-80" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-80">[80]</a></sup> In May 2008, Charter announced that it planned to monitor web sites visited by its high-speed Internet customers via a partnership with <a href="/wiki/Targeted_advertising" title="Targeted advertising">targeted advertising</a> firm <a href="/wiki/NebuAd" title="NebuAd">NebuAd</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-81" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-81">[81]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-82" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-82">[82]</a></sup> Charter dropped the program in June following public backlash.<sup id="cite_ref-83" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-83">[83]</a></sup></p>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(4)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Lawsuits">Lawsuits</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Charter_Communications&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Lawsuits" data-section="16" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-4 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-4">
<p>In 2002, the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Justice" title="United States Department of Justice">United States Department of Justice</a> investigated the company, leading to the <a href="/wiki/Indictment" title="Indictment">indictment</a> of four former executives in 2005 for improper financial reporting related primarily to the inflation of cable subscriber numbers to improve financial figures.<sup id="cite_ref-84" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-84">[84]</a></sup></p><p>In 2004, Charter settled a class-action lawsuit concerning the questionable financial reporting associated with the U.S. Department of Justice's 2002 investigation and subsequent indictment of four former executives. Current and former shareholders (and their attorneys) were awarded $144 million as well as an agreement from Charter to maintain and implement proper corporate <a href="/wiki/Governance" title="Governance">governance</a> measures.<sup id="cite_ref-85" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-85">[85]</a></sup></p><p>In June 2010, Charter settled a class-action lawsuit for $18 million concerning wage and overtime claims for current and former field technicians in California, Missouri, Michigan, Minnesota, Illinois, Nevada, Washington, Oregon and Nebraska.<sup id="cite_ref-86" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-86">[86]</a></sup></p><p>In December 2013, a complaint was filed by Steelhead Licensing LLC for patent infringement of U.S. Patent 8082318; it is described as "Controlling service requests transmitted from a client to a server".<sup id="cite_ref-87" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-87">[87]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-88" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-88">[88]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-89" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-89">[89]</a></sup></p><p>In January 2016, the National Association of African-American Owned Media and <a href="/wiki/Byron_Allen" title="Byron Allen">Byron Allen</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Entertainment_Studios" title="Entertainment Studios">Entertainment Studios</a> filed a $10 billion civil rights lawsuit against Charter, claiming discrimination for Charter's refusal to pick up Allen's <a href="/wiki/Entertainment_Studios_Networks" title="Entertainment Studios Networks">eight-channel suite of networks</a> (which mainly carry ES content already syndicated through local television stations and <a href="/wiki/Brokered_programming" title="Brokered programming">paid programming</a>); Allen and the NAAAOM (which has an Entertainment Studios executive as its head) have already filed the same type of suit against several other providers.<sup id="cite_ref-90" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-90">[90]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-91" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-91">[91]</a></sup></p><p>In May 2016, Charter reached a settlement with the FCC regarding allegations by <a href="/wiki/Zoom_Telephonics" title="Zoom Telephonics">Zoom Telephonics</a> that, in 2012, following the introduction of new rate plans and the introduction of DOCSIS 3.0, it had begun to bar new subscribers or those switching to the new plans from utilizing customer-purchased modems. Although Charter ended this practice in 2014 and began to allow certain certified modems to be used, Zoom argued that the company was still deliberately limiting options by requiring the modems to undergo a testing protocol concerning factors beyond  whether they cause interference or unauthorized receipt of service (the only two factors which providers may use to restrict allowable modems under FCC policy). Charter paid a $640,000 fine, and agreed to use a shorter testing process allowing the use of any DOCSIS 3.0-compatible modem, and send compliance reports to the FCC every six months and whenever a modem is blacklisted.<sup id="cite_ref-92" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-92">[92]</a></sup></p><p>On February 1, 2017, Charter was sued by the <a href="/wiki/Attorney_General_of_New_York" title="Attorney General of New York">Attorney General of New York</a> for <a href="/wiki/False_advertising" title="False advertising">failing to provide its advertised Internet speeds to customers</a> in areas that Charter acquired by the purchase of Time Warner Cable.<sup id="cite_ref-wsj-chartersuitny_93-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wsj-chartersuitny-93">[93]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-cnet-nycsuit_94-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cnet-nycsuit-94">[94]</a></sup> The company agreed to a $174.2 million settlement, including both refunds of $75 to affected subscribers (with an additional $75 to those who rented the defective modem hardware for at least 24 months), and offers of complimentary subscriptions to services such as <a href="/wiki/HBO" title="HBO">HBO</a> (3 months) or <a href="/wiki/Showtime_(TV_network)" title="Showtime (TV network)">Showtime</a> (6 months) to all subscribers with an internet and television bundle.<sup id="cite_ref-95" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-95">[95]</a></sup></p><p>On August 28, 2017, Charter agreed to a $225,000 settlement in the state of Missouri over violations of <a href="/wiki/Telemarketing" title="Telemarketing">telemarketing</a> and <a href="/wiki/No-call_list" class="mw-redirect" title="No-call list">No-call list</a> laws.<sup id="cite_ref-96" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-96">[96]</a></sup></p>
