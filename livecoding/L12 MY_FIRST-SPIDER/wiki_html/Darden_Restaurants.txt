<p><b>Darden Restaurants, Inc.</b> is an American multi-brand restaurant operator headquartered in <a href="/wiki/Orlando,_Florida" title="Orlando, Florida">Orlando</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FY_2011_AR_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FY_2011_AR-4">[4]</a></sup> As of April 2017, the firm owns two <a href="/wiki/Fine_dining" class="mw-redirect" title="Fine dining">fine dining</a> restaurant chains: <a href="/wiki/Eddie_V%27s_Prime_Seafood" title="Eddie V's Prime Seafood">Eddie V's Prime Seafood</a> and <a href="/wiki/The_Capital_Grille" title="The Capital Grille">The Capital Grille</a>; and six <a href="/wiki/Casual_dining" class="mw-redirect" title="Casual dining">casual dining</a> restaurant chains: <a href="/wiki/Olive_Garden" title="Olive Garden">Olive Garden</a>, <a href="/wiki/LongHorn_Steakhouse" title="LongHorn Steakhouse">LongHorn Steakhouse</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bahama_Breeze" title="Bahama Breeze">Bahama Breeze</a>, <a href="/wiki/Seasons_52" title="Seasons 52">Seasons 52</a>,  <a href="/wiki/Yard_House" title="Yard House">Yard House</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cheddar%27s_Scratch_Kitchen" title="Cheddar's Scratch Kitchen">Cheddar's Scratch Kitchen</a>. Until July 28, 2014, Darden also owned <a href="/wiki/Red_Lobster" title="Red Lobster">Red Lobster</a>. Darden has more than 1,500 restaurant locations and more than 150,000 employees, making it the world's largest full-service restaurant company.<sup id="cite_ref-hvr_1-3" class="reference"><a </section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(1)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Darden_Restaurants&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History" data-section="1" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-1 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-1">
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Darden_Restaurants.svg" class="image"><noscript><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/39/Darden_Restaurants.svg/220px-Darden_Restaurants.svg.png" decoding="async" width="220" height="70" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="250" data-file-height="80"></noscript><span class="lazy-image-placeholder" style="width: 220px;height: 70px;" data-src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/39/Darden_Restaurants.svg/220px-Darden_Restaurants.svg.png" data-alt="" data-width="220" data-height="70" data-srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/39/Darden_Restaurants.svg/330px-Darden_Restaurants.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/39/Darden_Restaurants.svg/440px-Darden_Restaurants.svg.png 2x" data-class="thumbimage"> </span></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Darden_Restaurants.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The company's former logo, used until 2009.</div></div></div>
<p>William (Bill) Darden opened his first restaurant, The Green Frog, in his hometown of <a href="/wiki/Waycross,_Georgia" title="Waycross, Georgia">Waycross, Georgia</a> in 1938 at age 19. The restaurant, which grew quickly, formed the basis of the organization that later became known as Darden Restaurants.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> He later founded the Red Lobster Inns of America and opened the first Red Lobster restaurant in <a href="/wiki/Lakeland,_Florida" title="Lakeland, Florida">Lakeland, Florida</a> in 1968.<sup id="cite_ref-rlia_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rlia-9">[9]</a></sup> Darden chose Lakeland because he wished to see how a seafood restaurant would fare in a non-coastal region, and Lakeland was the innermost city in Florida. The initial Red Lobster franchise was applauded by diners and critics alike. The restaurant became successful and by 1970 had expanded to three locations in the state with two more under construction. While the locations were profitable, the company lacked the resources to expand further, so Darden sold the company to food giant <a href="/wiki/General_Mills" title="General Mills">General Mills</a> that year.<sup id="cite_ref-answers_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-answers-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="General_Mills_ownership">General Mills ownership</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Darden_Restaurants&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: General Mills ownership" data-section="2" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/General_Mills" title="General Mills">General Mills</a> upgraded the <a href="/wiki/Restaurant_chain" class="mw-redirect" title="Restaurant chain">chain</a> to a more casual dining/family fare oriented format, opened a new company headquarters in Orlando and retained Darden as company manager. In 1975, when Darden was promoted to Vice President of General Mills, Joseph (Joe) R. Lee, the company's first restaurant manager, was promoted as President of Red Lobster. Under General Mills ownership, Red Lobster expanded into a chain of almost 400 locations by 1985. The company underwent several restructurings and transformed itself from an inexpensive fast-food seller into a chain of casual dining seafood restaurants by 1988.<sup id="cite_ref-answers_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-answers-10">[10]</a></sup></p><p>One of the company's first ventures into the diversification of its portfolio was the <a href="/wiki/York_Steak_House" title="York Steak House">York Steak House</a> chain of English-themed steak and chop restaurants in the 1970s. The <a href="/wiki/Franchising" title="Franchising">franchised</a> steak and potatoes restaurant was a <a href="/wiki/Cafeteria-style_restaurant" class="mw-redirect" title="Cafeteria-style restaurant">cafeteria-style restaurant</a> with <a href="/wiki/Salad_bar" title="Salad bar">salad bar</a> and hot station. By the end of the 1980s, the chain had been mostly closed down, though some independent locations still exist. These places were very similar to <a href="/wiki/Ruby_Tuesday_(restaurant)" title="Ruby Tuesday (restaurant)">Ruby Tuesday</a> now.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p><p>In 1982, Darden opened the first <a href="/wiki/Olive_Garden" title="Olive Garden">Olive Garden</a> concept restaurant in Orlando. The chain took off, and by 1989 General Mills had opened over 145 restaurants, making the chain the fastest-growing unit in the company's restaurant holdings. While Olive Garden did not meet critical success, it was popular, and its per-restaurant sales soon grew to match those of Red Lobster. The company eventually became the largest chain of Italian-themed full-service restaurants in the United States.<sup id="cite_ref-answers_10-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-answers-10">[10]</a></sup></p><p>In 1990, <a href="/wiki/China_Coast" title="China Coast">China Coast</a> was launched as an attempt to create a (U.S.) national casual dining restaurant that featured <a href="/wiki/American_Chinese_cuisine" title="American Chinese cuisine">American Chinese cuisine</a>. While the chain eventually expanded to some 50 restaurants, its sales were lackluster and lost an estimated US$20 million. By the end of 1995, the company was shuttered, and the remaining locations were either converted to Red Lobsters or Olive Gardens or closed altogether.<sup id="cite_ref-answers_10-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-answers-10">[10]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Darden">Darden</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Darden_Restaurants&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Darden" data-section="3" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>In 1995, General Mills decided to spin off its restaurant chains to focus on consumer food products. The new company was named Darden Restaurants, after Red Lobster's founder. General Mills stockholders received one share of Darden for every common share of General Mills held. General Mills restaurants had $108 million net income in that year. At the end of 1995, Darden operated 1,250 restaurants in 49 states with 73 locations in Canada.<sup id="cite_ref-answers_10-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-answers-10">[10]</a></sup></p><p>Darden Restaurants was spun off from <a href="/wiki/General_Mills" title="General Mills">General Mills</a> beginning on May 9, 1995, when it began trading on when-issue basis at $9.75 a share.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> The company became a fully separate entity on May 31, 1995, when its shares went on sale on the <a href="/wiki/NYSE" class="mw-redirect" title="NYSE">NYSE</a>. The shares opened at $10.75, 17% below expectations, but climbed to $11.125 by the close of trading.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p><p>Darden executives planned to have two additional chains in place by 1998. In March 1996, Darden launched a test of a Bahama Breeze Caribbean Grille concept featuring food and drinks found in the islands of the <a href="/wiki/Caribbean_Sea" title="Caribbean Sea">Caribbean Sea</a> and a Caribbean theme.<sup id="cite_ref-answers_10-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-answers-10">[10]</a></sup></p><p>Markets were oversaturated with restaurants in 1997, forcing Darden to close 48 poorly performing locations and lose $91 million due to the restructuring. Red Lobster and Olive Garden were given makeovers in 1998. Darden also made a profit of $102 million that year.<sup id="cite_ref-answers_10-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-answers-10">[10]</a></sup></p><p>1999 saw Darden opening additional location after recovering from the 1996–1997 losses. The company then began testing a new concept entitled <a href="/wiki/Smokey_Bones" title="Smokey Bones">Smokey Bones</a> BBQ Sports Bar that opened in late 1999 in Orlando. The restaurant is a sports bar concept featuring barbecue and related foodstuffs in an Appalachian mountain-lodge setting.<sup id="cite_ref-answers_10-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-answers-10">[10]</a></sup></p><p>In 2003, Seasons 52 was under development with the goal to "provide guests the opportunity to indulge while still eating well". Seasons 52 restaurants were only being opened in the Florida or Atlanta markets during its initial phase.<sup id="cite_ref-nrn_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nrn-16">[16]</a></sup></p><p>Darden announced in January 2007 that the company was willing to expand by purchasing existing 100-location chains or even considering franchisors.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> By May, Darden indicated that its Smokey Bones division would be sold and/or shut down including the two Rocky River Grillhouse, the proposed replacement concept for Smokey Bones.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> In August, Darden acquired rival <a href="/wiki/Atlanta" title="Atlanta">Atlanta</a>-based restaurant holder Rare Hospitality for US$1.4 billion, gaining Rare's two chains, <a href="/wiki/The_Capital_Grille" title="The Capital Grille">The Capital Grille</a> and <a href="/wiki/LongHorn_Steakhouse" title="LongHorn Steakhouse">LongHorn Steakhouse</a>. As part of the Rare acquisition, Darden set up its Specialty Restaurant Group to include Capital Grille, Bahama Breeze and Seasons 52.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> In December, Darden announced that it would sell its Smokey Bones chain to Barbeque Integrated, Inc., an affiliate of <a href="/wiki/Sun_Capital_Partners" title="Sun Capital Partners">Sun Capital Partners</a>, Inc., for approximately $80 million. The sale was completed in January 2008.<sup id="cite_ref-rlia_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rlia-9">[9]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Expansion_and_ownership_changes">Expansion and ownership changes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Darden_Restaurants&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Expansion and ownership changes" data-section="4" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>In 2010, Seasons 52 started a new expansion phase opening up in 11 more states over the next three years.<sup id="cite_ref-nrn_16-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nrn-16">[16]</a></sup></p><p>In January 2011, Darden announced <a href="/wiki/Colocation_(business)" title="Colocation (business)">co-locating</a> their Olive Garden and Red Lobster brands in smaller markets to share kitchen but continue separate menus and eating areas.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> As part of the February Darden analyst conference, an analyst indicated that the corporation may be targeting another restaurant chain for acquisition possibly one of <a href="/wiki/BJ%27s_Restaurant_%26_Brewery" class="mw-redirect" title="BJ's Restaurant &amp; Brewery">BJ's</a>, <a href="/wiki/California_Pizza_Kitchen" title="California Pizza Kitchen">California Pizza Kitchen</a> or Yard House.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> In October 2011, Darden acquired 2 chains, Eddie V's Prime Seafood and Wildfish Seafood Grille, for $59 million cash transaction and were placed within its Specialty Restaurant Group,<sup id="cite_ref-nrn2_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nrn2-23">[23]</a></sup> Also in October, Darden signed an area-development agreement with <a href="/wiki/Americana_Group" title="Americana Group">Americana Group</a> of Kuwait to develop and operate at least 60 locations using the Red Lobster, Olive Garden and LongHorn Steakhouse concepts.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p><p>In July 2012, Darden acquired the Yard House 39 location beer-centric chain for $585 million from <a href="/wiki/TSG_Consumer_Partners" title="TSG Consumer Partners">TSG Consumer Partners</a>. Yard House will be alongside the other upscale restaurants in Darden's Specialty Restaurant Group.<sup id="cite_ref-lat_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-lat-25">[25]</a></sup> On December 23, 2013, Darden's stock rose 3 percent after activist investor Starboard Value, a <a href="/wiki/Hedge_fund" title="Hedge fund">hedge fund</a>, took a stake in the company.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Red_Lobster_sale">Red Lobster sale</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Darden_Restaurants&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Red Lobster sale" data-section="5" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>On December 19, 2013, Darden announced plans to sell or spin-off the <a href="/wiki/Red_Lobster_(restaurant)" class="mw-redirect" title="Red Lobster (restaurant)">Red Lobster</a> brand, citing pressure from stock investors.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> This was in direct response to the company spending US$100 million on a new digital platform. At the time, the project was already at least one year behind schedule and above budget. A large number of layoffs occurred in its marketing department, and the company's second in command also left.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p><p>On May 12, 2014, Darden announced that as part of the spinoff of Red Lobster, it was converting the co-located Red Lobster and Olive Garden locations into standalone Olive Garden locations.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> On May 16, 2014, Darden announced it would be selling the <a href="/wiki/Red_Lobster_(restaurant)" class="mw-redirect" title="Red Lobster (restaurant)">Red Lobster</a> seafood restaurant chain to <a href="/wiki/Golden_Gate_Capital" title="Golden Gate Capital">Golden Gate Capital</a> for <a href="/wiki/USD" class="mw-redirect" title="USD">US$</a>2.1 billion.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> Darden announced the completion of the sale of Red Lobster on July 28, 2014.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Acquisitions">Acquisitions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Darden_Restaurants&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Acquisitions" data-section="6" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>Differences between Starboard and Darden management soon emerged over the hedge fund's proposal to split the company in two and spin off a third to handle their real estate portfolio, a move Starboard said would greatly boost <a href="/wiki/Shareholder_value" title="Shareholder value">shareholder value</a>. Matters came to head when management announced a plan to spin off the underperforming Red Lobster early in 2014. Starboard led a large group of investors in asking management to delay the move and see if better options, such as its plans to revitalize the chain, were available.<sup id="cite_ref-WSJ_Jan_2014_story_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WSJ_Jan_2014_story-32">[32]</a></sup> When management instead decided to sell the chain to <a href="/wiki/Private_equity" title="Private equity">private equity</a> firm <a href="/wiki/Golden_Gate_Capital" title="Golden Gate Capital">Golden Gate Capital</a> in May, Starboard and other investors sharply criticized the $2.1 billion "<a href="/wiki/Fire_sale" title="Fire sale">fire sale</a>" price as a serious undervaluing of Red Lobster and its assets such as the underlying real estate. It also claimed management had refused shareholders' requests for a special meeting to discuss the deal.<sup id="cite_ref-CNN_Red_Lobster_sale_story_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CNN_Red_Lobster_sale_story-33">[33]</a></sup></p><p>Chief Executive Officer <a href="/wiki/Clarence_Otis,_Jr." class="mw-redirect" title="Clarence Otis, Jr.">Clarence Otis, Jr.</a> announced he would be resigning at the end of the year, the same day the Red Lobster sale was complete.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> Management said afterwards that it would work on a needed turnaround plan for Olive Garden, also struggling. However, after <a href="/wiki/CNBC" title="CNBC">CNBC</a> reported on a leaked document, supposedly offered to potential lenders and buyers earlier in the year, that described Red Lobster's financial position far more optimistically than management had in its contemporary public statements,<sup id="cite_ref-CNBC_fishy_disclosure_report_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CNBC_fishy_disclosure_report-35">[35]</a></sup> one of the investors, a union pension fund, filed suit alleging material misrepresentation.<sup id="cite_ref-Union_pension_fund_suit_story_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Union_pension_fund_suit_story-36">[36]</a></sup> Management claimed the document had been prepared by Golden Gate in consultation with Red Lobster's executives, who could have been expected to have that view of the chain's future.<sup id="cite_ref-CNBC_fishy_disclosure_report_35-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CNBC_fishy_disclosure_report-35">[35]</a></sup> Eugene Lee was named permanent CEO on February 23, 2015, after serving as interim CEO in October 2014.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></p><p>Starboard assembled its own slate of <a href="/wiki/Corporate_director" class="mw-redirect" title="Corporate director">directors</a> to challenge all the sitting board members in the company's upcoming shareholder elections. In support of their candidacy it released a 294-slide <a href="/wiki/Presentation" title="Presentation">presentation</a> in early September about how the company had gone wrong and how its directors would restore it to health. While it received considerable media attention for its detailed focus on Olive Garden, in particular the chain's "wasteful" practice of serving too many of its free unlimited <a href="/wiki/Breadstick" title="Breadstick">breadsticks</a> at once (to prevent food waste due to staleness: instead of one per customer plus an additional one per table; additional breadsticks are served fresh on demand) and not salting the water it boiled pasta in, so as to secure a longer <a href="/wiki/Warranty" title="Warranty">warranty</a> on the pots, it also attacked management for spending lavishly on the chain's corporate headquarters while paying the general managers of individual restaurants less than its competitors did.<sup id="cite_ref-Starboard_slide_presentation_Business_Insider_story_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Starboard_slide_presentation_Business_Insider_story-38">[38]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-BI_Starboard_presentation_story_39-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BI_Starboard_presentation_story-39">[39]</a></sup> Management responded two days later that it was already implementing many of the suggested changes, and said the free breadsticks merely represented "Italian generosity".<sup id="cite_ref-Management_response_to_Darden_40-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Management_response_to_Darden-40">[40]</a></sup> Nevertheless, in October, shareholders replaced the entire board with Starboard's slate, in what an observer called an "epic fail" for management, since that rarely happens.<sup id="cite_ref-Darden_board_replaced_41-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Darden_board_replaced-41">[41]</a></sup></p><p>On March 27, 2017, Darden announced its intent to acquire <a href="/wiki/Cheddar%27s_Scratch_Kitchen" title="Cheddar's Scratch Kitchen">Cheddar's Scratch Kitchen</a> for $780 million<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> from shareholders such as <a href="/wiki/L_Catterton" title="L Catterton">L Catterton</a> and <a href="/wiki/Oak_Investment_Partners" title="Oak Investment Partners">Oak Investment Partners</a>. On March 28, 2017, when Darden announced that it had acquired Cheddar's Scratch Kitchen and "lifted its full-year earnings outlook," the company became the biggest gainer that day on the <a href="/wiki/S%26P_500" class="mw-redirect" title="S&amp;P 500">S&amp;P 500</a>, with shares growing nearly 9 percent.<sup id="cite_ref-ft-darden-march_43-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ft-darden-march-43">[43]</a></sup> The acquisition was completed on April 24, 2017.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup></p>
