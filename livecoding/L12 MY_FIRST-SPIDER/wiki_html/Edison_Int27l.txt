</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(1)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Edison_International&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History" data-section="1" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-1 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-1">
<p>The company was first incorporated in 1909 as <b>Southern California Edison Company</b> after Southern California acquired the assets of <a href="/wiki/Edison_Electric_Company" class="mw-redirect" title="Edison Electric Company">Edison Electric Company</a>; it was known as Southern California Edison until 1996 when it adopted its current name in recognition of its growing business abroad and in other industry sectors.  Edison first became a holding company in 1988 when it made a small change to its original name, becoming known as SCEcorp.
</p><p>Edison International became the naming rights holder for the <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles_Angels" title="Los Angeles Angels">Anaheim Angels</a>' stadium (which was previously known as <a href="/wiki/Angel_Stadium" title="Angel Stadium">Anaheim Stadium</a>) in 1998 in a deal running for 20 years. The company backed out of the naming rights deal after the 2003 season, and the stadium is now called Angel Stadium.
</p><p>In 2001 Edison's main holding, Southern California Edison faced bankruptcy after a state senate bill regarding financial assistance came up short (by $1 billion).<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p><p>On August 6, 2002, Edison International recorded core earnings per share of 56 cents in the second quarter of 2002.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(2)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Recent_moves">Recent moves</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Edison_International&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Recent moves" data-section="2" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-2 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-2">
<p>In November 2010 Edison sold its 48% interest in the <a href="/wiki/Four_Corners_Generating_Station" title="Four Corners Generating Station">Four Corners coal-fired power plant</a> in New Mexico (units 4 and 5 while units 1,2 and 3 will be closed because of being older) for $294 million to <a href="/wiki/Arizona_Public_Service" title="Arizona Public Service">Pinnacle West Capital Corp</a> (of Arizona Public Service).  The move was the direct result of a new California law requiring utility companies to exit <a href="/wiki/Coal#Coal_as_fuel" title="Coal">coal-fired power production</a> (even the renewing of contracts is disallowed), and forces Edison to purchase more power from the market.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p><p><a href="/wiki/San_Onofre_Nuclear_Generating_Station" title="San Onofre Nuclear Generating Station">San Onofre Nuclear Generating Station</a>
Unit 2 shut in early January 2012 for refueling and replacement of the reactor vessel head.
Both reactors at San Onofre have been shut since January 2012 due to premature wear found on tubes in massive steam generators installed in 2010 and 2011.
</p><p>In April 2012, in a sign of mounting concern over the shutdown, the top U.S. nuclear official, Gregory Jaczko, toured the facility with Senator Dianne Feinstein, a Democrat, and U.S. Representative Darrell Issa, a Republican. In June, 2013, the company announced it would permanently shut down operations at the plant, the beginning of a multi-year decommissioning process at the site.
</p>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(3)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Energy_production_mix">Energy production mix</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Edison_International&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Energy production mix" data-section="3" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-3 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-3">
<p>At least 21% of the fuel <a href="/wiki/Southern_California_Edison" title="Southern California Edison">Southern California Edison</a> uses to generate power comes from clean energy (half of that nuclear power, the other half renewable energy sources ranging from biomass to wind).<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>  The group is a major player in Southern California where it provides 13 million people with electricity.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p><p>Southern California Edison began to focus on renewable energy in 1980.
</p>
