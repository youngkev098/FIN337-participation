</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(1)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cintas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History" data-section="1" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-1 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-1">
<p>Cintas Corporation began in 1929 as the <b>Acme Industrial Laundry Company</b> by Richard (Doc) Farmer. He collected chemical-soaked rags from factories and washed and returned them to customers for a fee. In the early 1940s, rags were replaced by shop towels—which are uniform in size and shape and much more absorbent than old rags—and tablecloths. By then, the company’s name had changed to Acme Wiper and Industrial Laundry.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p><p>His grandson, <a href="/wiki/Richard_T._Farmer" title="Richard T. Farmer">Richard "Dick" Farmer</a>, joined the family business in 1956 after graduating from <a href="/wiki/Miami_University" title="Miami University">Miami University</a> in <a href="/wiki/Oxford,_Ohio" title="Oxford, Ohio">Oxford, Ohio</a>. Acme had just 15 employees at the time. Dick Farmer helped uniform rental sales increase from $300,000 in 1959 to $847,000 in 1963.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2020)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> He became CEO of the company in 1968.<sup id="cite_ref-Forbes_family_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Forbes_family-8">[8]</a></sup> He then<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Chronological_items" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers"><span title="The time period mentioned near this tag is ambiguous. (February 2020)">when?</span></a></i>]</sup> put together a business plan to open small uniform rental plants all over the United States. The first opened in Cleveland in October 1968. In 1972, the company changed its name to Cintas and then went public in 1983.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p><p>Farmer also tried products that were new at the time, such as fabrics that resisted wrinkles and stains, to grow the company to an almost 30 percent market share in uniforms. Cintas' recent<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Chronological_items" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers"><span title="The time period mentioned near this tag is ambiguous. (November 2017)">when?</span></a></i>]</sup> growth has been primarily through its acquisition of more than 220 companies, eliminating overheads and cutting costs. Since its inception, Cintas has bought itself into markets such as <a href="/wiki/First_aid" title="First aid">first aid</a> and safety, <a href="/wiki/Fire_protection" title="Fire protection">fire protection</a>, facility services, and tile and <a href="/wiki/Carpet_cleaning" title="Carpet cleaning">carpet cleaning</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:182px;"><a href="/wiki/File:MLS2Cincy_announcement_-_Scott_Farmer_(42393947252)_(cropped).jpg" class="image"><noscript><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/02/MLS2Cincy_announcement_-_Scott_Farmer_%2842393947252%29_%28cropped%29.jpg/180px-MLS2Cincy_announcement_-_Scott_Farmer_%2842393947252%29_%28cropped%29.jpg" decoding="async" width="180" height="208" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="2460" data-file-height="2844"></noscript><span class="lazy-image-placeholder" style="width: 180px;height: 208px;" data-src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/02/MLS2Cincy_announcement_-_Scott_Farmer_%2842393947252%29_%28cropped%29.jpg/180px-MLS2Cincy_announcement_-_Scott_Farmer_%2842393947252%29_%28cropped%29.jpg" data-alt="" data-width="180" data-height="208" data-class="thumbimage"> </span></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:MLS2Cincy_announcement_-_Scott_Farmer_(42393947252)_(cropped).jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Scott Farmer, seen here in 2018, became the <a href="/wiki/Chief_executive_officer" title="Chief executive officer">CEO</a> of Cintas in 2003.</div></div></div>
<p>In July 1997, Dick Farmer's son Scott Farmer, who has been employed at the company since 1981, became president and <a href="/wiki/Chief_operating_officer" title="Chief operating officer">chief operating officer</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Bloomberg_Scott_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bloomberg_Scott-9">[9]</a></sup> Dick Farmer retired as <a href="/wiki/Chief_executive_officer" title="Chief executive officer">chief executive officer</a> in 2003, and Scott was promoted to take his place. Dick Farmer remained chairman emeritus after his retirement.<sup id="cite_ref-Forbes_family_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Forbes_family-8">[8]</a></sup></p><p>In 2015, Cintas acquired Zee Medical from McKesson Corporation for approximately $130 million.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>  In 2016, Cintas acquired G&amp;K Services for $2.2 billion.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p><p>In 2016, CEO Scott Farmer became chairman of the board.<sup id="cite_ref-Bloomberg_Scott_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bloomberg_Scott-9">[9]</a></sup></p><p>The company is valued at US$12.97 billion (2017).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>

</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(3)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Criticism">Criticism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cintas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Criticism" data-section="3" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-3 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-3">
<p>In 2003, <a href="/wiki/UNITE_HERE" title="UNITE HERE">UNITE HERE</a> and the International Brotherhood of the <a href="/wiki/Teamsters" class="mw-redirect" title="Teamsters">Teamsters</a> have been engaged in a campaign against Cintas, alleging unfair labor practices. Unite obtained license numbers of Cintas workers in Pennsylvania, to contact them at home and has since been ordered to pay the workers $2,500 each. The ruling was subsequently upheld by the U.S. Court of Appeals for the Third Circuit.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Following the death of a Tulsa, Oklahoma, worker in March 2007, both unions and several Members of Congress called for stricter health and safety standards at the company's laundries.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> In May 2007, Cintas hired a <a href="/wiki/Washington,_D.C." title="Washington, D.C.">Washington, D.C.</a>-based lobbying firm, the <a href="/wiki/Podesta_Group" title="Podesta Group">Podesta Group</a>, to manage their relations with Congress.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
