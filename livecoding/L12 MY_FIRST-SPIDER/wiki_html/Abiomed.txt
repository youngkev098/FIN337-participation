</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(1)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Abiomed&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History" data-section="1" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-1 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-1">
<p>Abiomed was founded in Danvers by David M. Lederman<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> in 1981 as Applied Biomedical Corporation, and began working on the development of an <a href="/wiki/Artificial_heart" title="Artificial heart">artificial heart</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Funded by federal research grants,<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Lederman partnered with <a href="/wiki/The_Texas_Heart_Institute" title="The Texas Heart Institute">The Texas Heart Institute</a> to develop the <a href="/wiki/AbioCor" title="AbioCor">AbioCor</a>, implanting the first artificial heart into a Kentucky man in July 2001.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>  Fourteen of the AbioCor devices were implanted, during clinical trials from 2001 to 2004, with the longest-living recipient surviving 512 days.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> The AbioCor won FDA approval in 2006 for patients who are near death and do not qualify for a heart transplant.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p><p>In 2004, Michael R. Minogue became president and CEO of Abiomed.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> In 2005, Abiomed purchased <a href="/wiki/Ventricular_assist_device" title="Ventricular assist device">ventricular assist device</a> company Impella CardioSystems AG of Aachen, Germany,<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> maker of the <a href="/wiki/Impella" title="Impella">Impella</a> heart pump, developed by Thorsten Siess,<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> who is now the Chief Technology Officer at Abiomed.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p><p>Minogue helped found the Medical Technology Veterans Program (MVP), a career training and mentorship initiative designed to help veterans entering the civilian workforce transition into jobs in the medical device and life sciences industries.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> He currently serves as chairman of the program. In 2019, Abiomed was announced the 4th best <a href="/wiki/Stock" title="Stock">stock</a> of the 2010s, with total return of 1,983%.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p><p>In July 2014, Abiomed acquired German heart pump maker ECP in a deal worth up to $30 million. The deal included a nearly $2.8 million buyout of AIS GmbH Aachen Innovative Solutions, which owns some of the patents licensed to ECP.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Impella_products">Impella products</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Abiomed&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Impella products" data-section="2" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>Since 2005 when Abiomed acquired the <a href="/wiki/Impella" title="Impella">Impella</a> technology,<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> the latter's heart devices have received a series of FDA approvals.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p><p>In 2007, the Impella 2.5 heart pump was among 35 healthcare products to receive a 2007 Medical Design Excellence Award.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p><p>As of March 2019, the Impella heart pump products included the Impella 2.5, Impella 5.0/LD, Impella CP and Impella RP.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> On 28 February 2018, the <a href="/wiki/FDA" class="mw-redirect" title="FDA">FDA</a> approved the concept for the new Impella Ventricular Support Systems - P140003/S018. The system consists of left-side heart pump models (listed above) "intended to help pump blood in patients who need short-term support (up to 6 days)... mounted at the end of a thin, flexible tube (catheter), a console that drives the pump, and an infusion system that flushes the pump". The FDA report added the following specifics about the system: "The Impella Ventricular Support System helps pump blood by drawing blood out of the heart and pumping it into the aorta, partially or fully bypassing the left ventricle. It is implanted into the left side of a patient's heart through a small incision in the femoral artery (major artery in the leg). It can also be implanted through a small incision in a subclavian artery (an artery in the chest)."<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> Impella pumps sell for about <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://www.forbes.com/sites/elliekincaid/2018/12/18/abiomed-sells-600-million-worth-of-its-pencil-sized-heart-pump-but-now-some-doctors-have-big-questions/#6eb69a17753a">$23,000</a>.
</p>
