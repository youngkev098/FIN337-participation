</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(1)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Avery_Dennison&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History" data-section="1" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-1 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-1">
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:MODODennison.jpg" class="image"><noscript><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cc/MODODennison.jpg/220px-MODODennison.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="1848" data-file-height="1389"></noscript><span class="lazy-image-placeholder" style="width: 220px;height: 165px;" data-src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cc/MODODennison.jpg/220px-MODODennison.jpg" data-alt="" data-width="220" data-height="165" data-class="thumbimage"> </span></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:MODODennison.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Dennison Mending Tape from the second half of the 20th century.</div></div></div>
<p>The company was founded in <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles" title="Los Angeles">Los Angeles</a>, <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a>, in 1935 as <b>Kum Kleen Products</b>, a partnership of Mr. and Mrs. <a href="/wiki/R._Stanton_Avery" title="R. Stanton Avery">Ray Stanton Avery</a>.  The name was changed to <b>Avery Adhesives</b> in 1937.  In 1946, the company was incorporated as <b>Avery Adhesive Label Corp.</b>, and the name was subsequently changed to <b>Avery Adhesive Products, Inc.</b> in 1958, and to <b>Avery Products Corporation</b> in 1964.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>  The name was changed again to <b>Avery International Corporation</b> in 1976, and it became <b>Avery Dennison</b> after the company merged with the Dennison Manufacturing Company in 1990.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p><p>H. Russell Smith, a college friend of Avery’s, joined the company in 1946 as a one-third owner and for nearly 40 years was Avery’s partner in entrepreneurship, serving at various times as the company’s president, chief executive officer and chairman of the board.  Smith incorporated the 50-person company in 1946, started its core label materials business, expanded operations across the U.S. and into Europe, and opened a renowned research center. As the company’s representative to the financial community, he led its listing on the New York Stock Exchange in 1967. At Smith’s death in 2014, CEO Dean Scarborough noted that “our company owes its existence and character in large part to Russ Smith’s business acumen, personal integrity and generosity of spirit.  He was the perfect partner for Stan Avery.”<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Dennison_Manufacturing">Dennison Manufacturing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Avery_Dennison&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Dennison Manufacturing" data-section="2" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>The <b>Dennison Manufacturing Company</b> was founded by Andrew Dennison and his son <a href="/wiki/Aaron_Lufkin_Dennison" title="Aaron Lufkin Dennison">Aaron Lufkin Dennison</a>, residents of Brunswick. Maine, in 1844, as a <a href="/wiki/Jewelry" class="mw-redirect" title="Jewelry">jewelry</a>- and <a href="/wiki/Watch" title="Watch">watch</a>-box manufacturing company located in the Dunlap Block of Maine Street.  Five years later Aaron turned the Dennison Manufacturing Company over to his younger brother, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Eliphalet_Whorf_Dennison&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Eliphalet Whorf Dennison (page does not exist)">Eliphalet Whorf Dennison</a>, who took over and developed the company into a significant-size industrial enterprise. It was in 1898 that the business moved to a location in Framingham, Massachusetts. (Aaron Dennison went on to co-found the <a href="/wiki/Waltham_Watch_Company" title="Waltham Watch Company">Waltham Watch Company</a> in 1850, a leader in the <a href="/wiki/American_system_of_watch_manufacturing" title="American system of watch manufacturing">American system of watch manufacturing</a> using interchangeable parts.)
</p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Office_and_Consumer_Products">Office and Consumer Products</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Avery_Dennison&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Office and Consumer Products" data-section="3" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>Avery Dennison created a separate division for office products such as binders, file labels and name badges in 1982. The division and its products, sold under the Avery brand and logo, contrasted with the company’s larger materials division in that its products were finished (“converted”) materials, and they were aimed at consumers as well as businesses. Over the next 30 years, the division grew, as personal computing created a market for printable media both at home and at work. However, with the rise of email and the decline in conventional mail, the office products market as a whole began to decline. On July 1, 2013, Avery Dennison completed the sale of Office and Consumer Products and a second business, Designed and Engineered Solutions, to CCL Industries. CCL purchased the Avery office products brand along with the business. Avery Dennison retains its full name, history, brand and logo.
</p>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(2)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Operations">Operations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Avery_Dennison&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Operations" data-section="4" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-2 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-2">
<p>The company is headquartered in <a href="/wiki/Glendale,_California" title="Glendale, California">Glendale</a>, California. It ranked number 435 on the 2015 <a href="/wiki/Fortune_500" title="Fortune 500">Fortune 500</a> list with total sales of $7.0 billion.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Its main lines of business are pressure-sensitive materials (contributing 73% of 2015 revenues) and retail branding and information solutions (contributing 26% of 2015 revenues).<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> The company operates in more than 50 countries and employs 25,000 people worldwide.<sup id="cite_ref-InvestorFAQs_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-InvestorFAQs-9">[9]</a></sup> Its first overseas subsidiary was established in the <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Netherlands</a> in 1955.
</p>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(3)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Divisions">Divisions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Avery_Dennison&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Divisions" data-section="5" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-3 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-3">
<p>The company's operations are organized into three business units:
</p><p>The <b>Pressure-Sensitive Materials</b> segment manufactures and sells pressure-sensitive roll-label materials, films for graphic applications, reflective highway-safety products, a variety of specialized tapes, performance polymers, and extruded films.
</p><p>Printers take its label materials, which are manufactured in large rolls, and convert them into finished labels by printing, cutting and applying them to packaging for consumer goods such as shampoo, beverages and pharmaceuticals.
</p><p>The <b>Retail Branding and Information Solutions</b> segment designs, manufactures, and sells various branding and information management products and <a href="/wiki/Solution" title="Solution">solutions</a> for apparel and general retail, including tickets, graphic, barcode and <a href="/wiki/Radio-frequency_identification" title="Radio-frequency identification">radio-frequency identification</a> (RFID) tags, labels and inserts, woven and printed labels, external embellishments, price management systems, a variety of fasteners, and related supplies and equipment.
</p><p>The information management products that the business provides, including its RFID-based systems, help apparel and other retailers to manage inventory both within stores and across supply chains that today extend for thousands of miles. The ability to accurately identify inventory and replenish it can help improve the shopping experience, as goods are less likely to be out of stock.
</p><p>The company also operates <b>Avery Dennison Medical</b>, which provides products such as wearable sensors, barrier films, wound dressings and a variety of tapes and securement products for the healthcare industry.  Results for Vancive Medical Technologies are reported in Other specialty converting businesses. Vancive Medical Technologies products are sold to medical products and device manufacturers.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(4)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Patent_infringement">Patent infringement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Avery_Dennison&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Patent infringement" data-section="6" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-4 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-4">
<p>In 2012, Avery Dennison and 3M Company agreed to settle patent and antitrust litigation between the parties. The litigation began when 3M alleged that Avery Dennison infringed 3M's patents related to retroreflective sheeting used for road signs and other highway and transportation products and requested an injunction to prevent Avery Dennison from selling its OmniCube retroreflective product. The court denied 3M's request, and after Avery Dennison brought claims of its own against 3M for patent infringement and antitrust violations, the parties agreed to dismiss three pending cases.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources.
