</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(1)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Raymond_James_Financial&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History" data-section="1" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-1 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-1">
<p>Raymond James was founded in 1962 when St. Petersburg broker, <a href="/wiki/Robert_James_(businessman)" title="Robert James (businessman)">Robert James</a>, formed Robert A. James Investments. In 1964, it merged with Raymond &amp; Associates, founded by Edward Raymond in 1963, to form Raymond James &amp; Associates.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Robert James' son, <a href="/wiki/Thomas_James_(businessman)" title="Thomas James (businessman)">Tom James</a>, joined  in 1966 and assumed leadership of the firm in 1970. The firm  planned to go public in 1969, but market conditions delayed its plan until 1983.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-4">[4]</a></sup> Tom turned over the CEO's post to Paul Reilly in 2010,<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> and  his title as Chairman of the Board to Paul Reilly in 2016.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> He  remains on the leadership team as Chairman Emeritus.
</p><p>In 2012, the firm purchased the Canadian assets of <a href="/wiki/Allied_Irish_Bank_(GB)" title="Allied Irish Bank (GB)">Allied Irish Bank</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-4">[4]</a></sup> In April 2012, they merged with <a href="/wiki/Morgan_Keegan_%26_Company" class="mw-redirect" title="Morgan Keegan &amp; Company">Morgan Keegan &amp; Company</a>, creating one of the country's largest full-service <a href="/wiki/Wealth_management" title="Wealth management">wealth management</a> and investment banking firms not headquartered in New York.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p><p>As of the fiscal quarter ending September 30, 2018, the firm had delivered 123 consecutive quarters of profitability.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>  In June 2016, it was listed as a <a href="/wiki/Fortune_500" title="Fortune 500">Fortune 500</a> company for the first time.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p><p>In September 2016, the firm announced its acquisition of Deutsche Bank Wealth Management's US private client services unit, <a href="/wiki/Alex._Brown_%26_Sons" title="Alex. Brown &amp; Sons">Alex Brown &amp; Sons</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p><p>As of October 2019, Raymond James has 8,000 financial advisors throughout the United States, Canada and overseas. Total client <a href="/wiki/Assets_under_management" title="Assets under management">assets under management</a> are approximately $838 billion.<sup id="cite_ref-auto_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-auto-11">[11]</a></sup> The corporate headquarters is located in <a href="/wiki/St._Petersburg,_Florida" title="St. Petersburg, Florida">St. Petersburg, Florida</a>.
</p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Current_operations">Current operations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Raymond_James_Financial&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Current operations" data-section="2" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>Raymond James has four main lines of operation: private client group, capital markets (made up of equity and fixed income capital markets as well as public finance), asset management group (made up of asset management services and Carillon tower advisers) and banking.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:0_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(2)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Controversies">Controversies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Raymond_James_Financial&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Controversies" data-section="3" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-2 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-2">
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="SEC_v._Dennis_Herula">SEC v. Dennis Herula</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Raymond_James_Financial&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: SEC v. Dennis Herula" data-section="4" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>In 2004, the SEC fined Raymond James Financial Services, Inc. $6.9 million for failure to supervise former broker Dennis Herula. Herula was accused of participating with others in a <a href="/wiki/Ponzi_scheme" title="Ponzi scheme">Ponzi scheme</a> that raised about $44.5 million from investors in 1999-2000. Herula himself raised about $16.5 million of investor funds, most of which was later transferred to his wife's brokerage account at Raymond James. He was arrested in Bermuda and pleaded guilty to criminal charges of wire fraud and sentenced to 188 months in jail.<sup id="cite_ref-investmentnews_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-investmentnews-14">[14]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Supervision_of_branch_managers">Supervision of branch managers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Raymond_James_Financial&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Supervision of branch managers" data-section="5" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>In 2005, the <a href="/wiki/National_Association_of_Securities_Dealers" class="mw-redirect" title="National Association of Securities Dealers">National Association of Securities Dealers</a> fined Raymond James $2.75 million for lax supervision of producing branch managers. The investigation began with one Raymond James manager, who worked from an office in her Wisconsin home, handling approximately 700 accounts and selling mainly mutual funds and variable annuities. The Wisconsin manager was accused of selling unsuitable aggressive mutual funds and variable annuities over a four-year period.<sup id="cite_ref-investmentnews_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-investmentnews-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Auction_rate_securities">Auction rate securities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Raymond_James_Financial&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Auction rate securities" data-section="6" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>On June 29, 2011, Raymond James announced an agreement to repurchase <a href="/wiki/At_par" class="mw-redirect" title="At par">at par</a> <a href="/wiki/Auction_rate_security" title="Auction rate security">auction rate securities</a> (ARS) sold to clients through its domestic broker/dealer subsidiaries prior to February 13, 2008. The agreement—reached with the <a href="/wiki/Securities_and_Exchange_Commission" class="mw-redirect" title="Securities and Exchange Commission">Securities and Exchange Commission</a> and with state securities regulators led by Florida and Texas—resolved more than three years of investigation related to activity in the ARS market. Without admitting or denying the allegations, the firm also agreed to pay a fine totaling $1.75 million to the state regulators, but was not fined by the SEC.
</p><p>As a result of this agreement, a pre-tax charge of $45 million was recorded in the quarter ending June 30, 2011. This charge was a result of an estimate of the current fair value of the securities to be repurchased by the company being less than their par value. It is expected that the ultimate realized loss will be substantially less as issuers refinance or redeem these securities, interest rates rise and/or collateral values improve.
</p><p>Raymond James sold $2.3 billion worth of ARS, underwrote $1.2 billion, and was the auction dealer for over $725 million. Since the $330 billion market for ARS crashed in 2008, at least 19 underwriters and broker-dealers were sued in <a href="/wiki/Class_action" title="Class action">class action</a> suits.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Excessive_commissions">Excessive commissions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Raymond_James_Financial&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Excessive commissions" data-section="7" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>In September 2011, the <a href="/wiki/Financial_Industry_Regulatory_Authority" title="Financial Industry Regulatory Authority">Financial Industry Regulatory Authority</a> ordered Raymond James &amp; Associates, Inc. and Raymond James Financial Services, Inc. to pay restitution of $1.69 million to 15,500 of their clients for charging excessive commissions on more than 27,000 <a href="/wiki/Security_(finance)" title="Security (finance)">securities</a> transactions. The trades were made in client accounts between 2006 and 2010.
</p><p>FINRA also fined RJA $225,000 and RJFS $200,000.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Notable_FINRA_Fines">Notable FINRA Fines</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Raymond_James_Financial&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Notable FINRA Fines" data-section="8" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>On May 18, 2016 Raymond James was fined <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.finra.org/newsroom/2016/finra-fines-raymond-james-17-million-systemic-anti-money-laundering-compliance">$17 Million for Systemic Anti-Money Laundering Compliance Failures</a>. "RJA was fined $8 million and RJFS was fined $9 million for failing to establish and implement adequate AML procedures, which resulted in the firms’ failure to properly prevent or detect, investigate, and report suspicious activity for several years. RJA's former AML Compliance Officer, Linda L. Busby, was also fined $25,000 and suspended for three months.".<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup>  On December 21, 2017  Raymond James was fined <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.finra.org/newsroom/2017/finra-fines-raymond-james-2-million-failing-supervise-email-communications">$2 million for failing to reasonably supervise email communications</a>.  "FINRA found that during a nine-year review period, Raymond James’ email review system was flawed in significant respects, allowing millions of emails to evade meaningful review. This created the unreasonable risk that certain misconduct by firm personnel could go undetected by the firm. The combinations of words and phrases – otherwise known as the “lexicon” – used to flag emails for review were not reasonably designed to detect certain potential misconduct that Raymond James, in light of its size, structure, business model, and experience from prior disciplinary actions, knew or should have anticipated would recur from time to time. The firm also failed to devote adequate personnel and resources to the team that reviewed emails flagged by the system, even as the number of emails increased over time."<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
