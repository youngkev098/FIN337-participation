</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(1)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=PerkinElmer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History" data-section="1" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-1 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-1">
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Founding_companies">Founding companies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=PerkinElmer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Founding companies" data-section="2" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>PerkinElmer was founded in 1937<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> by <a href="/wiki/Richard_Scott_Perkin" title="Richard Scott Perkin">Richard Perkin</a> and <a href="/wiki/Charles_Wesley_Elmer" class="mw-redirect" title="Charles Wesley Elmer">Charles Elmer</a> as an optical design and consulting company. In 1944, Perkin-Elmer entered the analytical-instruments business, and in the early 1990s, partnered with <a href="/wiki/Cetus_Corporation" title="Cetus Corporation">Cetus Corporation</a> (and later <a href="/wiki/Hoffmann-La_Roche" title="Hoffmann-La Roche">Hoffmann-La Roche</a>) to pioneer the <a href="/wiki/Polymerase_chain_reaction" title="Polymerase chain reaction">polymerase chain reaction</a> (PCR) equipment industry. Analytical-instruments business was also operated from 1954 to 2001 in Germany, by the <i>Bodenseewerk Perkin-Elmer GmbH</i> located in <a href="/wiki/%C3%9Cberlingen" title="Überlingen">Überlingen</a> at <a href="/wiki/Lake_Constance" title="Lake Constance">Lake Constance</a>, and England (Perkin Elmer Ltd) at Beaconsfield in Buckinghamshire.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/EG%26G" title="EG&amp;G">EG&amp;G</a> was founded in 1931 by two <a href="/wiki/MIT" class="mw-redirect" title="MIT">MIT</a> professors, <a href="/wiki/Harold_Edgerton" class="mw-redirect" title="Harold Edgerton">Harold Edgerton</a> and Kenneth Germeshausen in a Boston garage. The company was originally incorporated in 1947 as <a href="/wiki/EG%26G" title="EG&amp;G">EG&amp;G</a>.
</p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Computer_Systems_Division">Computer Systems Division</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=PerkinElmer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Computer Systems Division" data-section="3" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>Perkin-Elmer was involved in computer manufacture for a time. The Perkin-Elmer Computer Systems Division was formed through the purchase of <a href="/wiki/Interdata" title="Interdata">Interdata, Inc.</a>, an independent computer manufacturer, in 1973–74 for some $63 million.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-encyclopedia_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-encyclopedia-4">[4]</a></sup> This merger made Perkin-Elmer's annual sales rise to over $200 million.<sup id="cite_ref-encyclopedia_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-encyclopedia-4">[4]</a></sup> This was also known as Perkin-Elmer's Data Systems Group.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p><p>The 32-bit computers were very similar to an IBM System/370, but ran the OS/32MT operating system. 
</p><p><a href="/wiki/The_Wollongong_Group" title="The Wollongong Group">The Wollongong Group</a> provided the commercial version of the Unix port to the Interdata 7/32 hardware, known as <a href="/wiki/Version_7_Unix" title="Version 7 Unix">Edition 7 Unix</a>. The port was originally done by the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Wollongong" title="University of Wollongong">University of Wollongong</a> in New South Wales, Australia, and was the first UNIX port to hardware other than the <a href="/wiki/Digital_Equipment_Corporation" title="Digital Equipment Corporation">Digital Equipment Corporation</a> <a href="/wiki/Programmed_Data_Processor" title="Programmed Data Processor">PDP</a> family.
By 1982 the Wollongong Group Edition 7 Unix and <a href="/wiki/PWB/UNIX" title="PWB/UNIX">Programmer's Workbench</a> (PWB) were available on models such as the Perkin-Elmer 3210 and 3240 minicomputers.
</p><p>In 1985, the computing division of Perkin-Elmer was spun off as <a href="/wiki/Concurrent_Computer_Corporation" class="mw-redirect" title="Concurrent Computer Corporation">Concurrent Computer Corporation</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-concurrent_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-concurrent-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="1999">1999</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=PerkinElmer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: 1999" data-section="4" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>Modern PerkinElmer traces its history back to a merger between divisions of what had been two <a href="/wiki/S%26P_500" class="mw-redirect" title="S&amp;P 500">S&amp;P 500</a> companies, <a href="/wiki/EG%26G" title="EG&amp;G">EG&amp;G</a> Inc. (formerly <a href="/wiki/New_York_Stock_Exchange" title="New York Stock Exchange">NYSE</a>: <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://www.nyse.com/quote/XNYS:EGG">EGG</a>) of <a href="/wiki/Wellesley,_Massachusetts" title="Wellesley, Massachusetts">Wellesley, Massachusetts</a> and Perkin-Elmer (formerly <a href="/wiki/New_York_Stock_Exchange" title="New York Stock Exchange">NYSE</a>: <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://www.nyse.com/quote/XNYS:PKN">PKN</a>) of <a href="/wiki/Norwalk,_Connecticut" title="Norwalk, Connecticut">Norwalk, Connecticut</a>. On May 28, 1999, the non-government side of EG&amp;G Inc. purchased the Analytical Instruments Division of Perkin-Elmer, its traditional business segment, for US$425 million, also assuming the Perkin-Elmer name and forming the new <b>PerkinElmer</b> company, with new officers and a new Board of Directors.<sup id="cite_ref-peceoletter_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-peceoletter-7">[7]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-nyt990715_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nyt990715-8">[8]</a></sup> At the time, EG&amp;G made products for diverse industries including automotive, medical, aerospace and photography.<sup id="cite_ref-nyt990715_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nyt990715-8">[8]</a></sup></p><p>The old Perkin-Elmer Board of Directors and Officers remained at that reorganized company under its new name, <a href="/wiki/PE_Corporation" class="mw-redirect" title="PE Corporation">PE Corporation</a>. It had been the <a href="/wiki/Life_Sciences" class="mw-redirect" title="Life Sciences">Life Sciences</a> division of Perkin-Elmer, and its two component <a href="/wiki/Tracking_stock" title="Tracking stock">tracking stock</a> business groups, <a href="/wiki/Celera_Genomics" class="mw-redirect" title="Celera Genomics">Celera Genomics</a> (<a href="/wiki/New_York_Stock_Exchange" title="New York Stock Exchange">NYSE</a>: <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://www.nyse.com/quote/XNYS:CRA">CRA</a>) and <a href="/wiki/PE_Biosystems" class="mw-redirect" title="PE Biosystems">PE Biosystems</a> (formerly <a href="/wiki/New_York_Stock_Exchange" title="New York Stock Exchange">NYSE</a>: <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://www.nyse.com/quote/XNYS:PEB">PEB</a>), were centrally involved in the highest profile <a href="/wiki/Biotechnology" title="Biotechnology">biotechnology</a> events of the decade, the intense race against the <a href="/wiki/Human_Genome_Project" title="Human Genome Project">Human Genome Project</a> consortium, which then resulted in the <a href="/wiki/Genomics" title="Genomics">genomics</a> segment of the <a href="/wiki/Technology_bubble" class="mw-redirect" title="Technology bubble">technology bubble</a>. Perkin-Elmer purchased the Boston operations of NEN Life Sciences in 2001.
</p>

<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Recently">Recently</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=PerkinElmer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Recently" data-section="5" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>In 1992, the company merged with <a href="/wiki/Applied_Biosystems" title="Applied Biosystems">Applied Biosystems</a>. In 1997 they merged with <a href="/wiki/PerSeptive_Biosystems" class="mw-redirect" title="PerSeptive Biosystems">PerSeptive Biosystems</a>. On July 14, 1999, the new analytical instruments maker PerkinElmer cut 350 jobs, or 12%, in its cost reduction reorganization.<sup id="cite_ref-nyt990715_8-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nyt990715-8">[8]</a></sup> In 2006, PerkinElmer sold off the Fluid Sciences division for approximately $400M; the aim of the selloff was to increase the strategic focus on its higher-growth health sciences and photonic markets. Following on from the selloff, a number of small businesses were acquired, including <a href="/wiki/Spectral_Genomics" title="Spectral Genomics">Spectral Genomics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Improvision" title="Improvision">Improvision</a>, Evotec-Technologies, Euroscreen, ViaCell, and Avalon Instruments. The brand "Evotec-Technologies" remains the property of Evotec, the former owner company. PerkinElmer had a license to use the brand till the end of year 2007.
</p><p>PerkinElmer has continued to expand its interest in medicine with the acquisitions of clinical laboratories, In July 2006, it acquired <a href="/w/index.php?title=NTD_Labs&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="NTD Labs (page does not exist)">NTD Labs</a> located on Long Island, New York. The laboratory specializes in prenatal screening during the first trimester of pregnancy. In 2007, it purchased ViaCell, Inc. for $300 million,<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> which included its offices in Boston and <a href="/wiki/Cord_blood" title="Cord blood">cord blood</a> storage facility in Kentucky near Cincinnati. The company was renamed <a href="/w/index.php?title=ViaCord&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="ViaCord (page does not exist)">ViaCord</a>.
</p><p>In March 2008, PerkinElmer purchased Pediatrix Screening (formerly Neo Gen Screening), a laboratory located in Bridgeville, PA specializing in screening newborns for various inborn errors of metabolism such as <a href="/wiki/Phenylketonuria" title="Phenylketonuria">phenylketonuria</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hypothyroidism" title="Hypothyroidism">hypothyroidism</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Sickle-cell_disease" class="mw-redirect" title="Sickle-cell disease">sickle-cell disease</a>. It renamed the laboratory PerkinElmer Genetics, Inc.
</p><p>In May 2011, PerkinElmer announced the signature of an agreement to acquire CambridgeSoft, and the successful acquisition of ArtusLabs.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p><p>In September 2011, PerkinElmer bought <a href="/wiki/Caliper_Life_Sciences" title="Caliper Life Sciences">Caliper Life Sciences</a> for $600 million.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p><p>In January 2017, the company announced it would acquire the Indian in vitro diagnostic company, Tulip Diagnostics.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> In May of the same year the company acquired <a href="/w/index.php?title=Euroimmun_Medical_Laboratory_Diagnostics&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Euroimmun Medical Laboratory Diagnostics (page does not exist)">Euroimmun Medical Laboratory Diagnostics</a> for approximately $1.3 billion.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p><p>In 2018, the company acquired Australian biotech company, RHS Ltd., Chinese manufacturer of analytical instruments, Shanghai Spectrum Instruments Co. Ltd., and US company Cisbio Bioassays, which  specializes in diagnostics and drug discovery solutions.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>

<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Hubble_optics_project">Hubble optics project</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=PerkinElmer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Hubble optics project" data-section="7" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Hubble_mirror_polishing.jpg" class="image"><noscript><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/91/Hubble_mirror_polishing.jpg/250px-Hubble_mirror_polishing.jpg" decoding="async" width="250" height="185" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="439" data-file-height="324"></noscript><span class="lazy-image-placeholder" style="width: 250px;height: 185px;" data-src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/91/Hubble_mirror_polishing.jpg/250px-Hubble_mirror_polishing.jpg" data-alt="" data-width="250" data-height="185" data-class="thumbimage"> </span></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Hubble_mirror_polishing.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Polishing of Hubble's primary mirror begins at Perkin-Elmer corporation, <a href="/wiki/Danbury,_Connecticut" title="Danbury, Connecticut">Danbury</a>, <a href="/wiki/Connecticut" title="Connecticut">Connecticut</a>, May 1979. The engineer pictured is Dr. Martin Yellin, an optical engineer working for Perkin-Elmer on the project.</div></div></div>
<p>Perkin-Elmer's Danbury Optical System unit<sup id="cite_ref-latimes_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-latimes-17">[17]</a></sup> was commissioned to build the optical components of the <a href="/wiki/Hubble_Space_Telescope" title="Hubble Space Telescope">Hubble Space Telescope</a>. The construction of the main mirror began in 1979 and completed in 1981. The polishing process ran over budget and behind schedule, producing significant friction with <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA</a>. Due to a miscalibrated <a href="/wiki/Null_corrector" title="Null corrector">null corrector</a>, the primary mirror was also found to have a significant <a href="/wiki/Spherical_aberration" title="Spherical aberration">spherical aberration</a> after reaching orbit on <a href="/wiki/STS-31" title="STS-31">STS-31</a>. Perkin-Elmer's own calculations and measurements revealed the primary mirror's surface discrepancies, but the company chose to withhold that data from NASA. A NASA investigation heavily criticized Perkin-Elmer for management failings, disregarding written quality guidelines, and ignoring test data that revealed the miscalibration.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> Corrective optics were installed on the telescope during the first Hubble service and repair mission <a href="/wiki/STS-61" title="STS-61">STS-61</a>. The correction, <a href="/wiki/Corrective_Optics_Space_Telescope_Axial_Replacement" title="Corrective Optics Space Telescope Axial Replacement">Corrective Optics Space Telescope Axial Replacement</a>, was applied entirely to the secondary mirror and replaced existing instrumentation; the aberration of the primary mirror remained uncorrected.
</p><p>The company agreed to pay US $15 million, essentially forgoing its fees in polishing the mirror, to avoid a threatened liability lawsuit under the <a href="/wiki/False_Claims_Act" title="False Claims Act">False Claims Act</a> by the Federal government.  <a href="/wiki/Hughes_Aircraft" class="mw-redirect" title="Hughes Aircraft">Hughes Aircraft</a> which acquired the Danbury Optical System unit one month after the launch of the telescope, paid $10 million. The Justice Department asserted that the companies should have known about the flawed testing.  Trade group <a href="/wiki/Aerospace_Industries_Association" title="Aerospace Industries Association">Aerospace Industries Association</a> protested when concerns were raised in the aerospace industry that aerospace companies might be held liable for failed equipment.<sup id="cite_ref-latimes_17-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-latimes-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="KH-9_Hexagon">KH-9 Hexagon</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=PerkinElmer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: KH-9 Hexagon" data-section="8" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>Perkin-Elmer built the optical systems for the <a href="/wiki/KH-9_Hexagon" title="KH-9 Hexagon">KH-9 Hexagon</a> series of <a href="/wiki/Spy_satellite" class="mw-redirect" title="Spy satellite">spy satellites</a> at a facility in <a href="/wiki/Danbury,_Connecticut" title="Danbury, Connecticut">Danbury</a>, <a href="/wiki/Connecticut" title="Connecticut">Connecticut</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-ap2011-12-25-10-26-53_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ap2011-12-25-10-26-53-19">[19]</a></sup></p><p>In the 1970s, an aerial panoramic camera lens was capable of recording the entire state of Pennsylvania in two flyovers, with resolution that enabled one to count the autos on the <a href="/wiki/Pennsylvania_Turnpike" title="Pennsylvania Turnpike">Pennsylvania Turnpike</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
