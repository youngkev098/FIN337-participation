<p><b>Tyson Foods, Inc.</b> is an American <a href="/wiki/Multinational_corporation" title="Multinational corporation">multinational corporation</a> based in <a href="/wiki/Springdale,_Arkansas" title="Springdale, Arkansas">Springdale, Arkansas</a>, that operates in the <a href="/wiki/Food_industry" title="Food industry">food industry</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Corporation" title="Corporation">company</a> is the world's second largest processor and marketer of <a href="/wiki/Broiler_industry" title="Broiler industry">chicken</a>, beef, and pork  after <a href="/wiki/JBS_S.A." title="JBS S.A.">JBS S.A.</a> and annually exports the largest percentage of beef out of the United States. Together with its subsidiaries, it operates major food brands, including <a href="/wiki/Jimmy_Dean_(brand)" title="Jimmy Dean (brand)">Jimmy Dean</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hillshire_Farm" title="Hillshire Farm">Hillshire Farm</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sara_Lee_Corporation" title="Sara Lee Corporation">Sara Lee</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ball_Park_Franks" title="Ball Park Franks">Ball Park</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wright_Brand_Foods,_Inc." class="mw-redirect" title="Wright Brand Foods, Inc.">Wright Brand</a>, Aidells, and State Fair.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a </section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(1)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Profile">Profile</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tyson_Foods&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Profile" data-section="1" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-1 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-1">
<p>The company was established by <a href="/wiki/John_W._Tyson" title="John W. Tyson">John W. Tyson</a> in 1935,<sup id="cite_ref-tf_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-tf-5">[5]</a></sup> and benefited during <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a> when chicken was not included in foods that were rationed.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> As of 2014, the company employs 115,000 people, who work at more than 300 facilities, over 100 of which are in the US.<sup id="cite_ref-tf_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-tf-5">[5]</a></sup> Tyson had about 97,000 employees in 27 states; locations are concentrated in the Midwest, with 16 locations in Arkansas, 11 in Texas, 9 in Iowa, and the remainder of the mostly eastern US with less than 3–4 locations.<sup id="cite_ref-tf_5-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-tf-5">[5]</a></sup> Tyson also works with 6,729 independent contract chicken growers.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p><p>Tyson is one of the largest U.S. marketers of chicken, beef and pork to retail grocers, broad line <a href="/wiki/Foodservice_distributor" title="Foodservice distributor">foodservice distributors</a> and national fast food and full-service restaurant chains; fresh beef and pork; frozen and fully cooked chicken, beef and pork products; case-ready beef and pork; supermarket deli chicken products; meat toppings for the pizza industry and retail frozen pizza; club store chicken, beef and pork; <a href="/wiki/Ground_beef" title="Ground beef">ground beef</a> and <a href="/wiki/Flour_tortillas" class="mw-redirect" title="Flour tortillas">flour tortillas</a>. It supplies <a href="/wiki/Yum!_Brands" title="Yum! Brands">Yum! Brands</a> chains that use chicken, including <a href="/wiki/KFC" title="KFC">KFC</a> and <a href="/wiki/Taco_Bell" title="Taco Bell">Taco Bell</a>, as well as <a href="/wiki/McDonald%27s" title="McDonald's">McDonald's</a>, <a href="/wiki/Burger_King" title="Burger King">Burger King</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wendy%27s" title="Wendy's">Wendy's</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wal-Mart" class="mw-redirect" title="Wal-Mart">Wal-Mart</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kroger" title="Kroger">Kroger</a>, <a href="/wiki/IGA_(supermarkets)" title="IGA (supermarkets)">IGA</a>, <a href="/wiki/Beef_O%27Brady%27s" title="Beef O'Brady's">Beef O'Brady's</a>, small restaurant businesses, and prisons.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p><p>The company makes a wide variety of animal-based and prepared products at its 123 <a href="/wiki/Food_processing" title="Food processing">food processing</a> plants. It produces many different products, including <a href="/wiki/Buffalo_wings" class="mw-redirect" title="Buffalo wings">Buffalo wings</a>, boneless Buffalo wings, <a href="/wiki/Chicken_nugget" title="Chicken nugget">chicken nuggets</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Chicken_fingers" title="Chicken fingers">tenders</a>. Every week, its 54 chicken plants, 13 beef plants, and six pork plants slaughter and package 42.5 million chickens, 170,938 cattle, and 347,891 pigs. Their largest meat packing facility is their beef production plant in <a href="/wiki/Dakota_City,_Nebraska" title="Dakota City, Nebraska">Dakota City</a>, Nebraska. Other plants include feed mills, hatcheries, farms and tanneries.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Acquisitions_and_investments">Acquisitions and investments</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tyson_Foods&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Acquisitions and investments" data-section="2" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:TysonLogo.png" class="image"><noscript><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/c/cb/TysonLogo.png" decoding="async" width="170" height="99" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="170" data-file-height="99"></noscript><span class="lazy-image-placeholder" style="width: 170px;height: 99px;" data-src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/c/cb/TysonLogo.png" data-alt="" data-width="170" data-height="99" data-class="thumbimage"> </span></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:TysonLogo.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The Tyson logo, used as a corporate logo from 1978 to 2017. It has been used, with minor changes, since 1972. It continues to be used as a logo on Tyson brand products.</div></div></div>
<p>In 2001, Tyson Foods acquired <a href="/wiki/IBP,_Inc." title="IBP, Inc.">IBP, Inc.</a>, the largest beef packer and number two pork processor in the United States., for US$3.2 billion in cash and <a href="/wiki/Stock" title="Stock">stock</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Along with its purchase of IBP, it also acquired the naming rights to an event center in <a href="/wiki/Sioux_City,_Iowa" title="Sioux City, Iowa">Sioux City, Iowa</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Tyson has also acquired such companies as <a href="/wiki/Hudson_Foods_Company" title="Hudson Foods Company">Hudson Foods Company</a>, Garrett poultry, Washington Creamery, Franz Foods, Prospect Farms, Krispy Kitchens, Ocoma Foods, Cassady Broiler, Vantress Pedigree, Wilson Foods, Honeybear Foods, Mexican Original, <a href="/wiki/Valmac_Industries" class="mw-redirect" title="Valmac Industries">Valmac Industries</a>, Heritage Valley, Lane Poultry, Cobb-Vantress, Holly Farms, <a href="/wiki/Wright_Brand_Foods,_Inc." class="mw-redirect" title="Wright Brand Foods, Inc.">Wright Brand Foods, Inc.</a> and Don Julio Foods. On May 29, 2014, the company announced a $6.13 billion cash offer to acquire all the shares in <a href="/wiki/Hillshire_Brands" title="Hillshire Brands">Hillshire Brands</a>, two days after a $6.4 billion cash and shares bid for Hillshire by <a href="/wiki/Pilgrim%27s_Corp" class="mw-redirect" title="Pilgrim's Corp">Pilgrim's Corp</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> In June 2014, Tyson won the bidding war against Pilgrim's Pride, agreeing to buy the maker of Jimmy Dean sausage and Ball Park hot dogs for $8.5 billion.<sup id="cite_ref-WinningBid_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WinningBid-10">[10]</a></sup> On July 28, 2014, the company said it would sell its Mexican and Brazilian poultry businesses to <a href="/wiki/JBS_S.A." title="JBS S.A.">JBS S.A.</a> for $575 million and use the proceeds to pay down debt from its pending $7.7 billion purchase of <a href="/wiki/Hillshire_Brands" title="Hillshire Brands">Hillshire Brands</a> Co.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> In May 2018, Tyson announced the acquisition of American Proteins, Inc. and AMPRO Products, Inc. for approximately $850 million.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p><p>In early 2018, Tyson, through its venture capital arm Tyson Ventures,<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> invested in <a href="/wiki/Beyond_Meat" title="Beyond Meat">Beyond Meat</a>, <a href="/wiki/Memphis_Meats" title="Memphis Meats">Memphis Meats</a>, and Future Meat Technologies,<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> companies developing plant-based <a href="/wiki/Meat_substitute" class="mw-redirect" title="Meat substitute">meat substitutes</a> and cultured <a href="/wiki/Clean_meat" class="mw-redirect" title="Clean meat">clean meat</a>, respectively. Former CEO Tom Hayes said that "it might seem counterintuitive", but the investments are part of an effort to meet future consumer demand in a sustainable way.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p><p>On June 1, 2018, Tyson announced that it would sell the Sara Lee, Van's, Chef Pierre and  Bistro Collection brands to <a href="/wiki/Kohlberg_%26_Company" title="Kohlberg &amp; Company">Kohlberg &amp; Company</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> The sale was completed on August 1, forming Sara Lee Frozen Bakery, which will be based in <a href="/wiki/Oakbrook_Terrace,_Illinois" title="Oakbrook Terrace, Illinois">Oakbrook Terrace, Illinois</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p><p>On August 9, 2018, Tyson announced that it would sell its pizza crust business, including TNT Crust, to <a href="/wiki/Austin,_Texas" title="Austin, Texas">Austin</a>-based Peak Rock Capital, who completed the acquisition on September 4.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p><p>On August 20, 2018, Tyson announced its intent to acquire food supplier Keystone Foods from <a href="/wiki/Marfrig" title="Marfrig">Marfrig</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> Tyson announced it had completed the acquisition on November 30, 2018.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p><p>On February 7, 2019, Tyson Foods reached an agreement to acquire the European and Thai businesses of Brazilian food company <a href="/wiki/BRF_S.A." title="BRF S.A.">BRF</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> The acquisition was completed on June 3, 2019.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p><p>On January 10, 2020, Tyson Foods announced that it sold its Golden Island jerky business to <a href="/wiki/Jack_Link%27s" class="mw-redirect" title="Jack Link's">Jack Link's</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Tyson_Renewable_Energy">Tyson Renewable Energy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tyson_Foods&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Tyson Renewable Energy" data-section="3" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>Tyson's processing plants generate a vast supply of animal fats. In late 2006, the company created a business unit called Tyson Renewable Energy to examine ways of commercializing the use of this leftover material by converting it into biofuels.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup>  The unit is also examining the potential use of poultry litter to generate energy and other products.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> On April 16, 2007, Tyson announced a joint venture with <a href="/wiki/ConocoPhillips" title="ConocoPhillips">ConocoPhillips</a> to produce roughly 175 million gallons of biodiesel a year—enough to run Tyson Foods' truck fleet for 3.5 years.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Corporate_charity">Corporate charity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tyson_Foods&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Corporate charity" data-section="4" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>Since 2000, Tyson Foods has donated millions of dollars in cash to help non-profit organizations across the country. <a href="/wiki/Forbes" title="Forbes">Forbes</a> named Tyson Foods the second most proportionally generous company for its donations in 2007 totaling 1.6 percent ($8 million) of its annual operating income.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> Tyson initiated the KNOW Hunger campaign in early 2011 to raise awareness of hunger in the United States. After the Joplin tornado of 2011, Tyson sent 77,000 pounds of food to the city.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> It also sent 100,000 pounds of food to the communities along the Gulf of Mexico after the April 20, 2010, oil spill.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> Tyson has supported "Little Free Pantries,"<sup id="cite_ref-:0_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-33">[33]</a></sup> and has partnered with the <a href="/wiki/Chicago_Urban_League" title="Chicago Urban League">Chicago Urban League</a> for educational programs on misconceptions about <a href="/wiki/Supplemental_Nutrition_Assistance_Program" title="Supplemental Nutrition Assistance Program">SNAP</a> (food stamp) benefits.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Religious_activities">Religious activities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tyson_Foods&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Religious activities" data-section="5" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>In addition to placing 128 part-time chaplains (including both Protestant and Catholic Christians and Muslim Imams) in 78 Tyson plants,<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> in 2006 the company invited their customers to download a prayer book, containing prayers from many faiths, including Christianity, Judaism, Islam, and <a href="/wiki/Native_American_spirituality" class="mw-redirect" title="Native American spirituality">American Indian spirituality</a>, from the company's website to read during mealtime.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></p>

</p><p><a href="/wiki/Donald_J._Tyson" title="Donald J. Tyson">Don Tyson</a> served as the company's CEO and chairman from 1967 to 1991.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Leland_Tollett" title="Leland Tollett">Leland Tollett</a> was CEO from 1991 until 1998.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup></p><p>John H. Tyson served as CEO from 1999 to 2006.<sup id="cite_ref-john_h_tyson_40-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-john_h_tyson-40">[40]</a></sup></p><p><a href="/wiki/Richard_L._Bond" title="Richard L. Bond">Richard L. Bond</a> was CEO of the company from 2006 until January 7, 2009, when he stepped down and his position was filled by temporary replacement Leland Tollett. <a href="/wiki/Donnie_Smith_(businessman)" title="Donnie Smith (businessman)">Donnie Smith</a> served as CEO from November 2009 to 2016. In November 2016, the company announced Smith would step down at the end of the year and would be succeeded by company president Tom Hayes.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup></p><p>Hayes was replaced by Noel White in September 2018.<sup id="cite_ref-Noel_White_42-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Noel_White-42">[42]</a></sup></p>

<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Environmental_record">Environmental record</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tyson_Foods&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Environmental record" data-section="10" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>Tyson has been involved in several lawsuits related to air and water pollution. In June 2003, the company admitted to illegally dumping untreated wastewater from its poultry processing plant near <a href="/wiki/Sedalia,_Missouri" title="Sedalia, Missouri">Sedalia</a>, Missouri, pleading guilty to 20 felony violations of the federal <a href="/wiki/Clean_Water_Act" title="Clean Water Act">Clean Water Act</a>. As part of the plea agreement, the company agreed to pay $7.5 million in fines, hire an outside consultant to perform an environmental audit, and institute an "enhanced environmental management system" at the Sedalia plant.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> At the same time, Tyson also settled a case filed by the Missouri attorney general's office related to the same illegal dumping.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p><p>The <a href="/wiki/United_States_Environmental_Protection_Agency" title="United States Environmental Protection Agency">United States Environmental Protection Agency</a> began the investigation into the discharges in 1997, and federal officials served two criminal search warrants at the plant in 1999. According to EPA and U.S. <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Justice" title="United States Department of Justice">Department of Justice</a> officials, Tyson continued to illegally dump wastewater after the search warrants were executed, prompting an EPA senior trial attorney to remark that: "Having done this work for nearly 20 years, I don't recall any case where violations continued after the execution of two search warrants. That's stunning." Under the federal and state plea agreements, Tyson agreed to pay $5.5 million to the federal government, $1 million to the Pettis County School Fund and $1 million to the Missouri Natural Resources Protection Fund to help remedy the damage.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup></p><p>In 2002, three residents of Western <a href="/wiki/Kentucky" title="Kentucky">Kentucky</a>, together with the <a href="/wiki/Sierra_Club" title="Sierra Club">Sierra Club</a>, filed a lawsuit concerning the discharge of dangerous quantities of ammonia from Tyson's Western Kentucky factories. Tyson settled the suit in January 2005, agreeing to spend $500,000 to mitigate and monitor the ammonia levels.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup></p><p>In 2004, Tyson was one of six poultry companies to pay a $7.3 million settlement fee to the city of Tulsa, Oklahoma, to settle charges that the use of chicken waste as fertilizer had created phosphorus pollution in Tulsa's main drinking water sources.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Employment_of_undocumented_immigrants">Employment of undocumented immigrants</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tyson_Foods&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Employment of undocumented immigrants" data-section="11" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>In 2001, Tyson was charged with conspiracy to smuggle undocumented workers to work on its production lines. Tyson plant managers arranged for delivery of illegal workers with undercover immigration officials. Prosecutors alleged that the conspiracy to import workers dates back to 1994 when plant managers began to find it difficult to fill positions with "cheap legal help". Of the six managers who were indicted, two accepted plea bargain deals, and one committed suicide one month after being charged. In March 2003, a federal jury acquitted Tyson of having knowingly hired illegal immigrants.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup></p><p>In May 2006, Tyson suspended operations at nine plants during a nationwide day of immigration demonstrations citing expected lack of workers.<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup></p><p>In October 2006, a federal judge granted class-action status to a lawsuit brought by Tyson employees who allege that Tyson's practice of hiring illegal immigrants depresses wages 10–30%. The suit further contends that the company violated federal <a href="/wiki/Racketeer_Influenced_and_Corrupt_Organizations_Act" title="Racketeer Influenced and Corrupt Organizations Act">racketeering laws</a> by conspiring with <a href="/wiki/National_Council_of_La_Raza" class="mw-redirect" title="National Council of La Raza">National Council of La Raza</a> and League of United Latin American Countries not to question the employment applications of anyone with a Hispanic surname.<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Price_manipulation">Price manipulation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tyson_Foods&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Price manipulation" data-section="12" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>In 2004, a federal jury found that Tyson Fresh Meats had used <a href="/wiki/Captive_supply" title="Captive supply">captive supply</a> agreements to artificially lower fed cattle prices in violation of the <a href="/wiki/Packers_and_Stockyards_Act" title="Packers and Stockyards Act">Packers and Stockyards Act</a>. Damages were found to be $1.28 billion. A U.S. Court of Appeals voided the verdict because it determined Tyson had a legitimate business justification to artificially lower cattle prices.<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup></p><p>In 2016, Maplevale sued Tyson and others for alleged price fixing. In January 2018 Winn-Dixie Stores and its sister grocery, Bi-Lo Holdings, also sued Tyson and others; weeks later, <a href="/wiki/Sysco" title="Sysco">Sysco</a> and <a href="/wiki/US_Foods" title="US Foods">US Foods</a> separately sued Tyson and others. Tyson and 16 other companies were accused of working together to restrict the supply of chickens and to manipulate chicken prices; these activities allegedly started in 2008.<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup> Expressing the magnitude of the Mapleville allegations, <a href="/wiki/NBC_News" title="NBC News">NBC News</a> stated an American family of four spends an average of $1100 per year on chicken, and if industry-wide price fixing allegations are true, "about $330 of that should still be in your wallet each year".<sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Undisclosed_use_of_antibiotics">Undisclosed use of antibiotics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tyson_Foods&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Undisclosed use of antibiotics" data-section="13" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>In 2007, Tyson began labeling and advertising its chicken products as "Raised without Antibiotics." After being advised by the USDA that Tyson's use of protozoa-killing <a href="/wiki/Ionophores" class="mw-redirect" title="Ionophores">ionophores</a> in unhatched eggs constituted antibiotic use, Tyson and the USDA compromised on rewording Tyson's slogan as "raised without antibiotics that impact <a href="/wiki/Antimicrobial_resistance" title="Antimicrobial resistance">antibiotic resistance in humans</a>." Tyson competitors <a href="/wiki/Perdue_Farms" title="Perdue Farms">Perdue Farms</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sanderson_Farms" title="Sanderson Farms">Sanderson Farms</a> sued claiming that Tyson's claim violated truth-in-advertising/labeling standards. In May 2008, a federal judge ordered Tyson to stop using the label.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Ionophores are used to control cocidiosis, a parasite common in all birds and the medication is not used in human medicine.
</p><p>In June 2008, USDA inspectors discovered that Tyson had also been using <a href="/wiki/Gentamicin" title="Gentamicin">gentamicin</a>, an antibiotic, in eggs. According to USDA Undersecretary for Food Safety Richard Raymond, Tyson hid the use of this antibiotic from federal inspectors, with Tyson not denying the claim and stating that the use of this chemical is standard industry practice. Tyson agreed to voluntarily remove its "raised without antibiotics" label in future packaging and advertising.<sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup></p><p>As of March 31, 2019, their website states that "All chickens raised for the Tyson® retail brand are grown without using any antibiotics – ever."<sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Employee_abuse">Employee abuse</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tyson_Foods&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Employee abuse" data-section="14" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>An <a href="/wiki/Oxfam" title="Oxfam">Oxfam</a> report issued in 2016 cited anonymous employees who stated they have been denied bathroom breaks so frequently that they have started wearing adult diapers to work. According to the report:<sup id="cite_ref-59" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup></p>
<blockquote><p>Workers struggle to cope with this denial of a basic human need. They urinate and defecate while standing on the line; they wear diapers to work; they restrict intake of liquids and fluids to dangerous degrees; they endure pain and discomfort while they worry about their health and job security.</p></blockquote>
<p>According to <a href="/w/index.php?title=Celeste_Monforton&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Celeste Monforton (page does not exist)">Celeste Monforton</a>, professor of occupational health at <a href="/wiki/George_Washington_University" title="George Washington University">George Washington University</a>, on average, more than one Tyson Foods employee is injured and amputates a finger or limb per month.<sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-61" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Animal_abuse">Animal abuse</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tyson_Foods&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Animal abuse" data-section="15" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>From December 2004 through February 2005, an undercover investigator for <a href="/wiki/People_for_the_Ethical_Treatment_of_Animals" title="People for the Ethical Treatment of Animals">People for the Ethical Treatment of Animals</a> (PETA) claimed to have worked on the slaughter line of a Tyson Foods chicken processing plant in Heflin, Alabama. Using a hidden camera, he allegedly documented the treatment of the more than 100,000 chickens killed every day in the plant. <a href="/wiki/PETA" class="mw-redirect" title="PETA">PETA</a> alleges that workers were instructed to rip the heads off of birds who missed the throat-cutting machines. He claims he saw birds scalded alive in the feather removal tank, and he said that managers said it was acceptable to scald 40 birds alive per shift.  The investigator claims plant employees were also seen throwing around dead birds just for fun. <a href="/wiki/PETA" class="mw-redirect" title="PETA">PETA</a> has asked Tyson to implement <a href="/wiki/Controlled_atmosphere_killing" class="mw-redirect" title="Controlled atmosphere killing">controlled atmosphere killing</a> (CAK). For this reason, <a href="/wiki/PETA" class="mw-redirect" title="PETA">PETA</a> is boycotting businesses that use Tyson as a supplier, such as <a href="/wiki/KFC" title="KFC">KFC</a> and distribution channels such as Sunset Strips. The video, taken by the investigator of the killings, was posted on <a href="/wiki/YouTube" title="YouTube">YouTube</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-62" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup></p><p>In 2006, Tyson completed a study to determine whether CAK, which uses gas to render chickens unconscious before slaughter, could be a more humane practice than conventional <a href="/wiki/Electrical_stunning" class="mw-redirect" title="Electrical stunning">electrical stunning</a>. According to Bill Lovette, Tyson's senior group vice president of poultry and prepared foods, the study found no difference between the humaneness of the two methods. The company plans to ask scientists at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Arkansas" title="University of Arkansas">University of Arkansas</a> to initiate a similar study to test these initial results. The research will be led by the newly created Chair in Food Animal Wellbeing at the Dale Bumpers College of Agricultural, Food and Life Sciences of the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Arkansas" title="University of Arkansas">University of Arkansas</a>. Tyson has committed $1.5 million to help establish the Chair, which will be involved in overseeing research and classes focused on the humane management and treatment of food animals.<sup id="cite_ref-63" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup></p><p>A 2016 undercover investigation by the animal rights organization <a href="/wiki/Compassion_Over_Killing" class="mw-redirect" title="Compassion Over Killing">Compassion Over Killing</a> showed workers at four separate Tyson processing plants throwing, punching and kicking chickens as well as sticking plastic rods through their beaks. They also wrung birds' necks, ran over them with forklifts and left injured birds in heaping piles to die.<sup id="cite_ref-64" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Food_recalls">Food recalls</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tyson_Foods&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Food recalls" data-section="16" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>On January 30, 2019, Tyson Foods announced a recall for over 36,000 pounds of chicken nuggets that were at risk of being contaminated with small pieces of rubber. The recall followed allegations by consumers who submitted complaints to the U.S. Agriculture Department. Tyson identified the contaminated nuggets as those received by Arizona, California, Illinois, Jersey, and Utah club store distribution centers.<sup id="cite_ref-65" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup> On March 21, 2019, the company issued a recall for 69,000 pounds of chicken strips potentially contaminated with pieces of metal,<sup id="cite_ref-66" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup> following six complaints submitted to the <a href="/wiki/Food_Safety_and_Inspection_Service" title="Food Safety and Inspection Service">Food Safety and Inspection Service</a>, including three alleged oral injuries. An expanded recall for nearly 12 million pounds of chicken strips was issued on May 4, 2019.<sup id="cite_ref-67" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup></p><p>On June 7, 2019, Tyson Foods announced a recall for over 190,000 pounds of chicken fritters which potentially contained hard plastic, calling the action "voluntary" and "out of caution" following reports from three consumers. The products were not sold in retail stores but supplied to various <a href="/wiki/Food_service" class="mw-redirect" title="Food service">food service</a> locations, including schools.<sup id="cite_ref-68" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Position_on_cuts_to_federal_food_assistance">Position on cuts to federal food assistance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tyson_Foods&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Position on cuts to federal food assistance" data-section="17" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>Tyson Foods has made political donations to both major parties.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_33-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-33">[33]</a></sup></p><p>Tyson "pledged to invest $50 million by 2020 in various efforts to fight food insecurity" in 2015. The company exceeded that goal, with contributions of over $60 million to start the year 2020.<sup id="cite_ref-69" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup> However, it has also "pledged hundreds of thousands in campaign contributions to the Republican Party, whose leaders aim to gut federal spending on food aid." It is not clear that Tyson's philanthropic efforts could be sufficient to compensate for "between 500,000 and 1 million people" removed from the <a href="/wiki/Supplemental_Nutrition_Assistance_Program" title="Supplemental Nutrition Assistance Program">Supplemental Nutrition Assistance Program</a> in 2017.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_33-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Coronavirus_pandemic">Coronavirus pandemic</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tyson_Foods&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Coronavirus pandemic" data-section="18" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>In April, 2020, a Tyson pork processing plant in <a href="/wiki/Columbus_Junction,_Iowa" title="Columbus Junction, Iowa">Columbus Junction, Iowa</a> closed down after 148 workers tested positive for coronavirus, and two workers died.<sup id="cite_ref-70" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup></p>
