</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(1)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Formation_and_history">Formation and history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mettler_Toledo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Formation and history" data-section="1" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-1 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-1">
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Toledo_Scale_Company">Toledo Scale Company</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mettler_Toledo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Toledo Scale Company" data-section="2" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Vashon_Island_Coffee_Roasterie_-_scale_01.jpg" class="image"><noscript><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e8/Vashon_Island_Coffee_Roasterie_-_scale_01.jpg/220px-Vashon_Island_Coffee_Roasterie_-_scale_01.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="146" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="4288" data-file-height="2848"></noscript><span class="lazy-image-placeholder" style="width: 220px;height: 146px;" data-src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e8/Vashon_Island_Coffee_Roasterie_-_scale_01.jpg/220px-Vashon_Island_Coffee_Roasterie_-_scale_01.jpg" data-alt="" data-width="220" data-height="146" data-class="thumbimage"> </span></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Vashon_Island_Coffee_Roasterie_-_scale_01.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Toledo counter-top fan scale</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Toledo_dial_scale.jpg" class="image"><noscript><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/82/Toledo_dial_scale.jpg/220px-Toledo_dial_scale.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="263" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="843" data-file-height="1007"></noscript><span class="lazy-image-placeholder" style="width: 220px;height: 263px;" data-src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/82/Toledo_dial_scale.jpg/220px-Toledo_dial_scale.jpg" data-alt="" data-width="220" data-height="263" data-class="thumbimage"> </span></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Toledo_dial_scale.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Toledo dial scale</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Inspected_scale_2.jpg" class="image"><noscript><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ae/Inspected_scale_2.jpg/220px-Inspected_scale_2.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="293" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="768" data-file-height="1024"></noscript><span class="lazy-image-placeholder" style="width: 220px;height: 293px;" data-src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ae/Inspected_scale_2.jpg/220px-Inspected_scale_2.jpg" data-alt="" data-width="220" data-height="293" data-class="thumbimage"> </span></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Inspected_scale_2.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A Toledo scale used in a hospital cafeteria</div></div></div>
<p>Allen DeVilbiss, Jr. (1873-1911) was an inventor who lived in <a href="/wiki/Toledo,_Ohio" title="Toledo, Ohio">Toledo, Ohio</a>, United States. He became interested in the concept of weighing machines, and conceived the idea of an automatic computing pendulum scale. He was able to prove his concept with a local butcher, who realized that customers appreciated the automatic computation which eliminated the risk of overcharging. While his invention gained in popularity, DeVilbiss was not interested in making it a viable business.<sup id="cite_ref-honestweight_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-honestweight-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability#Self-published_sources" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability"><span title="The material near this tag may rely on a self-published source. (December 2017)">self-published source</span></a></i>]</sup></p><p>In 1900, Henry Theobald (1868-1924) was fired from his job at the <a href="/wiki/NCR_Corporation" title="NCR Corporation">National Cash Register Company</a>. He decided to start his own business, and was convinced that selling the automatic computing scales could be a good business. He solicited additional financial investors and purchased the company from DeVilbiss. On July 10, 1901, the <i>Toledo Computing Scale and Cash Register Company</i> was incorporated.<sup id="cite_ref-honestweight_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-honestweight-3">[3]</a></sup></p><p>By May 1902, Theobald's company was selling more than 100 <a href="/wiki/Cash_register" title="Cash register">cash registers</a> with scales per month. By that time, John H. Patterson, Theobald's boss from NCR, threatened to sue the new company for patent infringements. As an alternative, Patterson offered to purchase all cash register patents and property, along with the stipulation that Theobald would no longer engage in the cash register business. The investors, who worried about the costs of patent infringement litigation, agreed to the sale in June 1902. Since cash registers were no longer part of Theobald's business, he changed the name to <i>Toledo Computing Scale Company</i>. Theobald later coined the phrase "No Springs, Honest Weight" as a slogan for the new company.<sup id="cite_ref-honestweight_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-honestweight-3">[3]</a></sup></p><p>In the years that followed, Theobald realized that the weighing scale was the most important part of the retail transaction between the merchant and the customer. He felt that many of his competitors who used <a href="/wiki/Spring_scale" title="Spring scale">spring scale</a> technology, especially <a href="/wiki/Computing-Tabulating-Recording_Company#Computing_Scale_Company_of_America" title="Computing-Tabulating-Recording Company">Dayton Scale Company</a>, were allowing merchants to cheat their customers by incorrectly calculating the total price of a measured good. He campaigned for more government regulation of weights and measures to eliminate dishonest weighing systems. On October 1, 1907, <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts" title="Massachusetts">Massachusetts</a> adopted the first <a href="/wiki/Weights_and_Measures_Act" title="Weights and Measures Act">weights and measures laws</a> in the United States.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p><p>In 1912, the company name changed once again, to <i>Toledo Scale Company</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Additionally, a new scale line featuring a double pendulum mechanism and a dial face was introduced and was most suited for industrial applications.<sup id="cite_ref-tsc_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-tsc-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Mettler_Instruments_AG">Mettler Instruments AG</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mettler_Toledo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Mettler Instruments AG" data-section="3" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>In 1945, Dr. Erhard Mettler, a Swiss engineer, started a precision mechanics company in Küsnacht, Switzerland. He invented the substitution principle with a single-pan balance, capable of being produced in series. Analytical balances with a single weighing pan gradually replaced conventional two-pan balances in the laboratory.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Mettler_Toledo_UV7_Spectrophotometer.jpg" class="image"><noscript><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0b/Mettler_Toledo_UV7_Spectrophotometer.jpg/220px-Mettler_Toledo_UV7_Spectrophotometer.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="286" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="2125" data-file-height="2759"></noscript><span class="lazy-image-placeholder" style="width: 220px;height: 286px;" data-src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0b/Mettler_Toledo_UV7_Spectrophotometer.jpg/220px-Mettler_Toledo_UV7_Spectrophotometer.jpg" data-alt="" data-width="220" data-height="286" data-class="thumbimage"> </span></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Mettler_Toledo_UV7_Spectrophotometer.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Mettler Toledo UV7 Spectrophotometer</div></div></div>
<p>Mettler diversified its product line in 1970 with the introduction of its automated titration systems, and the acquisition of balance manufacturer Microwa AG. Mettler acquired its 500-employee competitor August Sauter KG, of <a href="/wiki/Albstadt" title="Albstadt">Albstadt</a>-<a href="/wiki/Ebingen" title="Ebingen">Ebingen</a>, Germany, in 1971, for its specialized industrial and retail scales.<sup id="cite_ref-History_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-History-7">[7]</a></sup></p><p>In 1980, Dr. Mettler sold his business to Ciba-Geigy AG. A third pillar - after laboratory and industrial - was created: the retail business. Technological progress had made it possible to advance retail scales to instruments for the management of perishable goods.
</p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Mettler_Toledo">Mettler Toledo</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mettler_Toledo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Mettler Toledo" data-section="4" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>In 1989, Reliance Electric sold the Toledo Scale division to Ciba-Geigy AG. The division was then merged with Mettler Instruments. The merger vastly increased the global scope of the company, which, as a result, operated in 18 countries. Mettler acquired another competitor, <a href="/wiki/Ohaus" title="Ohaus">Ohaus</a> Corp., in 1990.<sup id="cite_ref-History_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-History-7">[7]</a></sup> In 1992, the company was incorporated as <i>Mettler Toledo, Inc.</i><sup id="cite_ref-tsc_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-tsc-6">[6]</a></sup></p><p>In 1996-97, Mettler Toledo, Inc. was sold by Ciba-Geigy AG to the New York-based AEA Investors Inc., in preparation for a subsequent initial public offering. The initial public offering was completed and began trading on the <a href="/wiki/New_York_Stock_Exchange" title="New York Stock Exchange">New York Stock Exchange</a>, under the ticker symbol <a href="/wiki/New_York_Stock_Exchange" title="New York Stock Exchange">NYSE</a>: <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://www.nyse.com/quote/XNYS:MTD">MTD</a>.
</p><p>The resultant company is currently headquartered in <a href="/wiki/Greifensee" title="Greifensee">Greifensee</a>, Switzerland, with offices for its many brands based around the world.
</p>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(2)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Operational_structure">Operational structure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mettler_Toledo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Operational structure" data-section="5" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-2 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-2">
<p>Mettler-Toledo is a global manufacturer and marketer of precision instruments for use in laboratory, industrial, and food retailing applications. They are geographically diversified with sales in 2012 derived 34% from Europe, 34% from the Americas, and 32% from Asia and other countries. The Company has an extensive global sales and service organization with approximately 6,000, or approximately one-half, of the employees providing sales and service in 36 countries. The Company has a manufacturing presence in Europe, the United States, and China.<sup id="cite_ref-annualreport_2-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-annualreport-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Laboratory_Instruments">Laboratory Instruments</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mettler_Toledo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Laboratory Instruments" data-section="6" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>In 1952, the measurement of up to one ten-millionth of a gram became possible with the laboratory <a href="/wiki/Weighing_scale" title="Weighing scale">balance</a> products. "The Mettler Balance" proved to be a catchphrase in the laboratory.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Consequently, with the advancement of microprocessor technology over the years, a wide range of products for the laboratory were invented, like automated <a href="/wiki/Titrator" class="mw-redirect" title="Titrator">titrators</a> and thermal analyzers. Mettler-Toledo laboratory products and technology were able to improve processes in research and development, drug discovery, and quality control. Application-specific software for balances provides data analysis opportunities to enhance accuracy, productivity, and compliance. The most recent product development for the laboratory was the array-based UV/VIS spectrophotometer which encompasses four different units including UV5, UV7, UV5Bio, and UV5Nano.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h4 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="RAININ_Instrument">RAININ Instrument</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mettler_Toledo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: RAININ Instrument" data-section="7" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h4>
<p>In 2001, Mettler-Toledo purchased Rainin Instrument, LLC, which is now a wholly owned subsidiary of Mettler-Toledo.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Rainin supplies precision manual liquid handling instruments and calibration services worldwide. Under the Rainin brand, Mettler Toledo provides advanced liquid handling solutions<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Use_plain_English#Buzzwords" title="Wikipedia:Use plain English"><span title="The material near this tag may use buzzwords designed solely to impress you or obscure meaning. (October 2019)">buzzword</span></a></i>]</sup> for life scientists worldwide, offering a wide selection of ergonomic manual pipettes, electronic pipettes, multichannel pipettes, patented BioClean LTS, and universal pipette tips. Rainin developed the first complete pipetting solution<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Use_plain_English#Buzzwords" title="Wikipedia:Use plain English"><span title="The material near this tag may use buzzwords designed solely to impress you or obscure meaning. (October 2019)">buzzword</span></a></i>]</sup> called Pipetting 360°, which significantly increases the reproducibility of basic research steps. An estimated 3 million scientists have benefited from improved research results and reductions in repetitive strain injuries (RSI) with the ergonomic Rainin LTS pipettes and tips.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h4 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="AutoChem">AutoChem</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mettler_Toledo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: AutoChem" data-section="8" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h4>
<p>The Mettler-Toledo AutoChem Inc. develops process analytical technology (PAT), automated reactors, and in situ sampling for use in the chemical, pharmaceutical, and academia industries. Specifically, in situ spectroscopy and automated sampling provides continuous analysis of chemical reactions; inline particle size analysis enables crystallization, suspension, and emulsion development with real-time particle size and shape measurements; and automated reactors and reaction calorimetry provides process knowledge to eliminate scale-up and safety incidents. As Mettler Toledo built the franchise from a startup operation in the early 1990s, several companies were acquired. These acquisitions included ASI Applied System Inc., Lasentec®, and the Virtual Lab software business from Avantium to assemble the technology platforms required to develop and maintain its market leading position.
</p>
<h4 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="THORNTON">THORNTON</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mettler_Toledo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: THORNTON" data-section="9" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h4>
<p>Thornton, Inc. Process Analytics Division <sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> founded in 1964 develops, manufactures, markets, and supplies instrumentation and sensors used for liquid process measurement and control applications. The instrumentation provides pure water treatment measurement &amp; control for the pharmaceutical industry; and parameters of conductivity/resistivity, TOC, pH, DO, dissolved ozone and flow for the semiconductor and power industries. Thornton acquired microbial detection startup Instant BioScan, Inc. in 2015 to become the first manufacturer of instrumentation within the pharmaceutical industry to offer real-time measurement solutions<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Use_plain_English#Buzzwords" title="Wikipedia:Use plain English"><span title="The material near this tag may use buzzwords designed solely to impress you or obscure meaning. (October 2019)">buzzword</span></a></i>]</sup> for all three regulated parameters by global pharmacopeias for ultra pure water, adding bioburden analysis to their portfolio.
</p>
<h4 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="INGOLD">INGOLD</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mettler_Toledo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: INGOLD" data-section="10" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h4>

</p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Industrial_Instruments_division">Industrial Instruments division</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mettler_Toledo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Industrial Instruments division" data-section="11" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>The Company manufactures numerous industrial weighing instruments and related terminals and offer software for the pharmaceutical, chemical, food and other industries. In addition, it manufactures metal detection and other end-of-line product inspection systems used in production and packaging. The Company supplies automatic identification and data capture <a href="/wiki/Solution" title="Solution">solutions</a>, which integrate in-motion weighing, dimensioning, and identification technologies for transport, shipping, and logistics customers. The Company also offers heavy industrial scales and related software.
</p>

<h4 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Hi-Speed">Hi-Speed</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mettler_Toledo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Hi-Speed" data-section="13" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h4>

<h4 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Safeline">Safeline</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mettler_Toledo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Safeline" data-section="14" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h4>
<p>Safeline started in 1989 with an industrial metal detector for food inspection applications, and was acquired by Mettler Toledo in 1997. The company manufacturers a range of metal detectors for food, pharmaceutical, and non-food applications.
</p><p>To expand the detection <a href="/wiki/Solution" title="Solution">solutions</a>, Mettler Toledo acquired AVS Raytech and AVS Metrology in 2000. AVS Raytech manufactured x-ray inspection equipment. The x-ray inspection equipment combined with detection products allowed companies to identify multiple contaminants as well as verifying product integrity and portion control.

<h4 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="CI-VISION">CI-VISION</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mettler_Toledo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: CI-VISION" data-section="15" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h4>
<p>Mettler-Toledo CI-Vision is a wholly owned subsidiary of Mettler-Toledo Inc. The Mettler-Toledo Product Inspection Group, consisting of CI-Vision, Hi-Speed, and Safeline, is a supplier of in-line checkweighers, metal detectors, machine vision systems, and x-ray inspection systems.
</p>
