<p><b>HCA Healthcare</b> is an American for-profit operator of <a href="/wiki/Health_care" title="Health care">health care</a> facilities that was founded in 1968. It is based in <a href="/wiki/Nashville,_Tennessee" title="Nashville, Tennessee">Nashville</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tennessee" title="Tennessee">Tennessee</a> and in 2019 it managed 185 hospitals and 119 freestanding surgery centers,<sup id="cite_ref-NEWA_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NEWA-2">[2]</a></sup> including surgery centers, freestanding ERs, urgent care centers, and physician clinics in the United States and United Kingdom.<sup id="cite_ref-hcahealthcare1_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hcahealthcare1-3">[3]</a></sup> HCA went public on the <a href="/wiki/New_York_Stock_Exchange" title="New York Stock Exchange">New York Stock Exchange</a> (NYSE) in 1969 followed by a substantial growth period for the next two decades. The company was ranked No. 67 in the 2019 <a href="/wiki/Fortune_500" title="Fortune 500">Fortune 500</a> list of the largest United States corporations by total revenue.<sup id="cite_ref-4" <h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_years">Early years</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=HCA_Healthcare&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Early years" data-section="2" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>

<p>HCA was founded in 1968, in Nashville, Tennessee by Dr. <a href="/wiki/Thomas_F._Frist_Sr." title="Thomas F. Frist Sr.">Thomas F. Frist Sr.</a>, Dr. <a href="/wiki/Thomas_F._Frist_Jr." title="Thomas F. Frist Jr.">Thomas F. Frist Jr.</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jack_C._Massey" title="Jack C. Massey">Jack C. Massey</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> The first hospital that HCA owned was Park View Hospital, near downtown Nashville.<sup id="cite_ref-nytimes1998_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nytimes1998-9">[9]</a></sup> The small group of founders worked out of a small house not far from Park View for the first few years of operation.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p><p>In 1969, HCA conducted its <a href="/wiki/Initial_public_offering" title="Initial public offering">initial public offering</a> (IPO) on the New York Stock Exchange (NYSE).<sup id="cite_ref-nytimes1998_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nytimes1998-9">[9]</a></sup> As HCA grew, the small house that served as office space was no longer large enough, and in 1972, it built new offices behind Nashville's <a href="/wiki/Centennial_Park_(Nashville)" title="Centennial Park (Nashville)">Centennial Park</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>

<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Growth_and_merger">Growth and merger</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=HCA_Healthcare&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Growth and merger" data-section="3" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>During the 1970s and 1980s the corporation went through a tremendous growth period acquiring hundreds of hospitals across the US, 255 owned by HCA, and another 208 managed by HCA.
</p><p>In 1988, the hospital operator was acquired for $5.1 billion in a <a href="/wiki/Management_buyout" title="Management buyout">management buyout</a> led by chairman Thomas F. Frist, Jr.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> and completed a successful <a href="/wiki/Initial_public_offering" title="Initial public offering">initial public offering</a> in 1992. In 1993 HCA merged with Louisville, Kentucky-based Columbia Hospital Corporation to form <b>Columbia/HCA</b>. In April 1998, Birmingham, Alabama-based <a href="/wiki/HealthSouth_Corporation" class="mw-redirect" title="HealthSouth Corporation">HealthSouth Corporation</a> announced it was acquiring the majority of HCA's surgical division.
</p>

<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Recent_history">Recent history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=HCA_Healthcare&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Recent history" data-section="4" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>In 2006, <a href="/wiki/Kohlberg_Kravis_Roberts" title="Kohlberg Kravis Roberts">Kohlberg Kravis Roberts</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bain_Capital" title="Bain Capital">Bain Capital</a>, together with <a href="/wiki/Merrill_Lynch" class="mw-redirect" title="Merrill Lynch">Merrill Lynch</a> and the Frist family (which had founded the company) completed a $33.6 billion acquisition, making the company privately held again, 17 years after it had first been taken private in a management buyout. At the time of its announcement, the HCA buyout was the first of several to set new records for the largest, eclipsing the 1989 buyout of <a href="/wiki/RJR_Nabisco" title="RJR Nabisco">RJR Nabisco</a>. It would later be surpassed by the buyouts of <a href="/wiki/EQ_Office" title="EQ Office">EQ Office</a> and <a href="/wiki/Energy_Future_Holdings" title="Energy Future Holdings">TXU</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p><p>In May 2010, HCA announced that the corporation would once again go public with an expected $4.6-billion IPO. In March 2011, HCA sold 126.2 million shares for $30 each, raising about $3.79 billion, at that time, the largest private-equity backed IPO in U.S. history.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p><p>In 2017, it acquired three hospitals from <a href="/wiki/Tenet_Healthcare" title="Tenet Healthcare">Tenet Healthcare</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/R._Milton_Johnson" title="R. Milton Johnson">Milton Johnson</a> was the prior CEO of HCA, and retired at the end of 2018.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> He was replaced by Sam Hazen.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p><p>In December 2018, a historical marker was installed in the parking lot of HCA's Sarah Cannon Cancer Center in Nashville, formerly the location of HCA's first hospital, Park View Hospital.<sup id="cite_ref-nbjmarksthespot_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nbjmarksthespot-18">[18]</a></sup></p><p>The $1.5 billion purchase of Asheville, North Carolina-based <a href="/wiki/Mission_Health_System" title="Mission Health System">Mission Health System</a>, a nonprofit,<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> announced in March 2018, was completed February 1, 2019.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p><p>In January 2020, HCA Healthcare acquired Valify, a healthcare cost management company.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(2)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Facilities">Facilities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=HCA_Healthcare&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Facilities" data-section="5" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-2 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-2">
<p>As of 2019<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=HCA_Healthcare&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, HCA operated 185 hospitals and approximately 1,800 sites of care, including surgery centers, freestanding ERs, urgent care centers, and physician clinics located in 21 U.S. states and in the United Kingdom.<sup id="cite_ref-hcahealthcare1_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hcahealthcare1-3">[3]</a></sup> In July 2007, HCA sold its hospitals in Switzerland.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p><p>As of 2018, HCA had 50 hospitals and 31 surgery centers in Florida.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> In Texas, as of 2018 it had 53 hospitals.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> In Tennessee, where it began, it had 13 hospitals as of 2019.<sup id="cite_ref-hcahealthcare1_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hcahealthcare1-3">[3]</a></sup></p><p>Between 2003 and 2017, HCA did not enter any new markets; in 2017, it acquired the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Memorial_University_Medical_Center&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Memorial University Medical Center (page does not exist)">Memorial University Medical Center</a> in Savannah, Georgia which added to its existing seven hospitals in the state. In 2019 it purchased <a href="/wiki/Mission_Health_System" title="Mission Health System">Mission Health System</a> which operates hospitals in <a href="/wiki/North_Carolina" title="North Carolina">North Carolina</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="United_Kingdom">United Kingdom</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=HCA_Healthcare&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: United Kingdom" data-section="6" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>

</p>

<li><a href="/wiki/Wellington_Hospital,_United_Kingdom" class="mw-redirect" title="Wellington Hospital, United Kingdom">The Wellington Hospital</a></li></ul><p>It opened urgent care walk-in centres at London Bridge Hospital and the Portland Hospital in March 2018. It claims that patients, on average, wait just seven minutes to see a nurse and 17 minutes to see a doctor.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p><p><a href="/wiki/The_Princess_Grace_Hospital" title="The Princess Grace Hospital">The Princess Grace Hospital</a> specializes in breast cancer and surgery, aided by Professor <a href="/wiki/Kefah_Mokbel" title="Kefah Mokbel">Kefah Mokbel</a> and Dr. Nick Perry who, in 2005, founded The London Breast Institute.
</p>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(3)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Fraud_investigations">Fraud investigations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=HCA_Healthcare&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Fraud investigations" data-section="7" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-3 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-3">
<p>In 1993, lawsuits were filed by former employees regarding alleged improprieties in HCA's billing of Medicare which amounted to hundreds of millions of dollars. With federal investigations still underway, HCA was acquired by Columbia Healthcare; their growth and expansion continued under the company's founder <a href="/wiki/Rick_Scott" title="Rick Scott">Rick Scott</a>. In 1997, amid growing evidence that HCA "had kept two sets of books, one to show the government and one with actual expenses listed" <sup id="cite_ref-Appleby_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Appleby-5">[5]</a></sup> Scott resigned, and became a venture capitalist. Thomas Frist, a co-founder of HCA and brother of U.S. Senator <a href="/wiki/Bill_Frist" title="Bill Frist">Bill Frist</a>, took Scott's place.<sup id="cite_ref-Appleby_5-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Appleby-5">[5]</a></sup></p><p>In March 1997, investigators from the FBI, the Internal Revenue Service and the Department of Health and Human Services served search warrants at Columbia/HCA facilities in El Paso and on dozens of doctors with suspected ties to the company.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> Following the raids, the Columbia/HCA board of directors forced Rick Scott to resign as chairman and CEO.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> He was paid a settlement of $9.88 million and left with 10 million shares of stock worth over $350 million, mostly from his initial investment.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> In 1999, Columbia/HCA changed its name back to HCA, Inc. HCA also admitted fraudulently billing Medicare and other health programs by inflating the seriousness of diagnoses and to giving doctors partnerships in company hospitals as a <a href="/wiki/Kickback_(bribery)" title="Kickback (bribery)">kickback</a> for the doctors referring patients to HCA. It filed false cost reports, fraudulently billing Medicare for home health care workers, and paid kickbacks in the sale of home health agencies and to doctors to refer patients. In addition, it gave doctors "loans" that were never intended to be repaid as well as free rent, free office furniture, and free drugs from hospital pharmacies.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p><p>After Scott stepped down, Frist Jr. returned as chairman and CEO. He called on longtime friend and colleague <a href="/wiki/Jack_O._Bovender,_Jr." class="mw-redirect" title="Jack O. Bovender, Jr.">Jack O. Bovender, Jr.</a> to help him turn the company around. Frist and Bovender, who became CEO in 2001, pulled off what <i>Fortune</i> magazine called a remarkable corporate rescue.<sup id="cite_ref-CNN_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CNN-7">[7]</a></sup> In settlements reached in 2000 and 2002, Columbia/HCA pleaded guilty to 14 felonies and admitted systematically overcharging the government by claiming marketing costs as reimbursable, striking illegal deals with home care agencies, and filing false data about use of hospital space.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Corporate_Office_1972.jpg" class="image"><noscript><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/56/Corporate_Office_1972.jpg/220px-Corporate_Office_1972.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="152" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="756" data-file-height="522"></noscript><span class="lazy-image-placeholder" style="width: 220px;height: 152px;" data-src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/56/Corporate_Office_1972.jpg/220px-Corporate_Office_1972.jpg" data-alt="" data-width="220" data-height="152" data-class="thumbimage"> </span></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Corporate_Office_1972.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Corporate office in 1972</div></div></div>
<p>In late 2002, HCA agreed to pay the U.S. government $631 million, plus interest, and pay $17.5 million to state Medicaid agencies, in addition to $250 million paid up to that point to resolve outstanding Medicare expense claims.<sup id="cite_ref-Appleby_5-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Appleby-5">[5]</a></sup> In all, civil lawsuits cost HCA more than $2 billion to settle.<sup id="cite_ref-DOJ_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DOJ-6">[6]</a></sup> The name subsequently reverted to "Hospital Corporation of America." HCA abandoned the use of its name in its home market and instead promotes its Nashville hospitals under the TriStar brand. The federal investigations, which spanned nearly a decade, drew to a close in 2003 with "the government receiving a total of over $2 billion in criminal fines and civil penalties for systematically defrauding federal health care programs."<sup id="cite_ref-DOJ_6-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DOJ-6">[6]</a></sup> The case has been referred to as the longest and costliest investigation for health-care fraud in U.S. history.<sup id="cite_ref-CNN_7-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CNN-7">[7]</a></sup></p><p>In July 2005, U.S. Senator Bill Frist sold all of his HCA shares two weeks before disappointing earnings sent the stock on a 9-point plunge. Frist claimed that he sold his shares to avoid the appearance of a conflict of interest if he ran for president. Other executives sold their stock at the same time. Shareholders sued HCA and alleged that the company made false claims about its profits to drive up the price, which then fell when the company reported disappointing financial results. Eleven of HCA's senior officers were sued for accounting fraud and <a href="/wiki/Insider_trading" title="Insider trading">insider trading</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> HCA settled the lawsuit in August 2007, agreeing to pay $20 million to the shareholders.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
