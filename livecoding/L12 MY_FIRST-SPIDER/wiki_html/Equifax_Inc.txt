With over 10,000 employees worldwide, Equifax has US$3.1 billion in annual revenue and is traded on the <a href="/wiki/New_York_Stock_Exchange" title="New York Stock Exchange">New York Stock Exchange</a> (<a href="/wiki/New_York_Stock_Exchange" title="New York Stock Exchange">NYSE</a>) under the symbol EFX.<sup id="cite_ref-auto_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-auto-6">[6]</a></sup></p><p>Like all credit reporting agencies, the company is required by <a href="/wiki/Fair_Credit_Reporting_Act" title="Fair Credit Reporting Act">U.S. law</a> to provide consumers with one free credit report every year.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p><p>Equifax was the subject of more than 57,000 consumer complaints to the <a href="/wiki/Consumer_Financial_Protection_Bureau" title="Consumer Financial Protection Bureau">Consumer Financial Protection Bureau</a> from October 2012 to September 17, 2017, with most complaints relating to incomplete, inaccurate, outdated, or misattributed information held by the company.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p><p>In September 2017, Equifax announced a <a href="/wiki/Data_breach" title="Data breach">cyber-security breach</a>, which it claims to have occurred between mid-May and July 2017,<sup id="cite_ref-auto1_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-auto1-9">[9]</a></sup> where cybercriminals accessed approximately 145.5 million U.S. Equifax consumers' personal data, including their full names, <a href="/wiki/Social_Security_number" title="Social Security number">Social Security numbers</a>, birth dates, addresses, and <a href="/wiki/Driver%27s_licenses_in_the_United_States" title="Driver's licenses in the United States">driver license numbers</a>. Equifax also confirmed at least 209,000 consumers' credit card credentials were taken in the attack. On March 1, 2018, Equifax announced that 2.4 million additional U.S. customers were affected by the breach.<sup id="cite_ref-NBC_News_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NBC_News-10">[10]</a></sup> The company claims to have discovered evidence of the cybercrime event on July 29, 2017.  Residents in the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a> and <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a> were also impacted. The vulnerability in which Chinese hackers leveraged was <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://nvd.nist.gov/vuln/detail/CVE-2017-5638">CVE-2017-5638</a>, the hackers managed to stay in Equifax systems undetected for approximately 134 days.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p><p>In March 2018, the <a href="/wiki/U.S._Securities_and_Exchange_Commission" title="U.S. Securities and Exchange Commission">Security and Exchange Commission</a> accused Jun Ying, Equifax's former <a href="/wiki/Chief_information_officer" title="Chief information officer">CIO</a>, of illicit insider trading, by selling company stock before the breach was publicly disclosed.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> After an investigation by the <a href="/wiki/Federal_Bureau_of_Investigation" title="Federal Bureau of Investigation">FBI</a>, Ying pleaded guilty, was sentenced to four months of prison plus a year of supervised release, and was fined $55,000.00 and ordered to pay restitution of $117,117.61 on June 2019.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>  An Equifax manager, Sudhakar Reddy Bonthu, also pleaded guilty to insider trading and received a sentence of 8 months of home confinement.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p><p>In July 2019, <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times" title="The New York Times">The New York Times</a></i>, the <i><a href="/wiki/New_York_Post" title="New York Post">New York Post</a></i> and other media reported Equifax had agreed to pay approximately $650 million to settle with the <a href="/wiki/Federal_Trade_Commission" title="Federal Trade Commission">Federal Trade Commission</a> (FTC) to resolve investigations by several state attorneys general, the <a href="/wiki/Consumer_Financial_Protection_Bureau" title="Consumer Financial Protection Bureau">Consumer Financial Protection Bureau</a>, the FTC, and a consumer class-action lawsuit related to the data breach.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p><p>By September 2019, however, Equifax had added qualifications and "hurdles" to its claims process which put in doubt whether the previously announced cash settlement of $125 per affected consumer would actually be awarded.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p><p>On 19 December 2019, a federal judge in Atlanta awarded class-action attorneys representing consumers approximately $77.5 million, suggesting that individual consumers might expect to receive around six or seven dollars.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>

</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(1)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Equifax&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History" data-section="1" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-1 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-1">
<p>Equifax was founded by Cator and Guy Woolford in <a href="/wiki/Atlanta" title="Atlanta">Atlanta, Georgia</a>, as Retail Credit Company in 1899.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> The company grew quickly and by 1920 had offices throughout the United States and Canada. By the 1960s, Retail Credit Company was one of the nation's largest credit bureaus, holding files on millions of American and Canadian citizens. Even though the company continued to do credit reporting, the majority of their business was making reports to <a href="/wiki/Insurance" title="Insurance">insurance</a> companies when people applied for new insurance policies including life, auto, fire and medical insurance. All of the major insurance companies used RCC to get information on health, habits, morals, use of vehicles and finances. They also investigated insurance claims and made employment reports when people were seeking new jobs. Most of the credit work was then being done by a <a href="/wiki/Subsidiary" title="Subsidiary">subsidiary</a>, Retailers Commercial Agency.
</p><p>Retail Credit Company's extensive information holdings, and its willingness to sell them to anyone, attracted criticism of the company in the 1960s and 1970s. These included that it collected "...<span class="nowrap"> </span>facts, statistics, inaccuracies and rumors ... about virtually every phase of a person's life; his marital troubles, jobs, school history, childhood, sex life, and political activities." The company was also alleged to reward its employees for collecting derogatory information on consumers.<sup id="cite_ref-wired_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wired-23">[23]</a></sup></p><p>As a result, when the company moved to computerize its records, which would lead to much wider availability of the personal information it held, the U.S. Congress held hearings in 1970. These led to the enactment of the <a href="/wiki/Fair_Credit_Reporting_Act" title="Fair Credit Reporting Act">Fair Credit Reporting Act</a> in the same year which gave consumers rights regarding information stored about them in corporate databanks. It is alleged that the hearings prompted the Retail Credit Company to change its name to Equifax in 1975 to improve its image.<sup id="cite_ref-wired_23-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wired-23">[23]</a></sup></p><p>The company later expanded into commercial credit reports on companies in the United States, Canada and the UK, where it came into competition with companies such as <a href="/wiki/Dun_%26_Bradstreet" title="Dun &amp; Bradstreet">Dun &amp; Bradstreet</a> and <a href="/wiki/Experian" title="Experian">Experian</a>. The insurance reporting was phased out. The company also had a division selling specialist credit information to the insurance industry but spun off this service, including the Comprehensive Loss Underwriting Exchange (CLUE) database as <a href="/wiki/ChoicePoint" class="mw-redirect" title="ChoicePoint">ChoicePoint</a> in 1997. The company formerly offered digital certification services, which it sold to <a href="/wiki/GeoTrust" title="GeoTrust">GeoTrust</a> in September 2001. In the same year, Equifax spun off its payment services division, forming the publicly listed company <a href="/wiki/Certegy" title="Certegy">Certegy</a>, which subsequently acquired <a href="/wiki/Fidelity_National_Information_Services" class="mw-redirect" title="Fidelity National Information Services">Fidelity National Information Services</a> in 2006. Certegy effectively became a subsidiary of <a href="/wiki/Fidelity_National_Financial" title="Fidelity National Financial">Fidelity National Financial</a> as a result of this reverse acquisition merger <i>(See <a href="/wiki/Certegy" title="Certegy">Certegy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fidelity_National_Information_Services" class="mw-redirect" title="Fidelity National Information Services">Fidelity National Information Services</a> for further information)</i>.
</p><p>In October 2010, Equifax announced it acquired Anakam, Inc, an <a href="/wiki/Identity_verification_service" title="Identity verification service">identity verification</a> software company headquartered in <a href="/wiki/San_Diego" title="San Diego">San Diego, California</a>, which invented and pioneered <a href="/wiki/SMS" title="SMS">SMS</a> (text-message based) <a href="/wiki/Multi-factor_authentication" title="Multi-factor authentication">two-factor authentication</a>. Terms of the deal were not disclosed.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p><p>Equifax purchased eThority, a <a href="/wiki/Business_intelligence" title="Business intelligence">business intelligence</a> (BI) company headquartered in <a href="/wiki/Charleston,_South_Carolina" title="Charleston, South Carolina">Charleston, South Carolina</a>, in October 2011. eThority is partnering with <a href="/wiki/TALX" class="mw-redirect" title="TALX">TALX</a>, a <a href="/wiki/St._Louis" title="St. Louis">St. Louis</a>-based business unit of Equifax, and will remain in Charleston.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p><p>Equifax Workforce Solutions is one of the 55 contractors hired by the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Health_and_Human_Services" title="United States Department of Health and Human Services">United States Department of Health and Human Services</a> to work on the <a href="/wiki/HealthCare.gov" title="HealthCare.gov">HealthCare.gov</a> web site.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(2)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Products">Products</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Equifax&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Products" data-section="2" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-2 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-2">
<p>For most of its existence, Equifax has operated primarily in the <a href="/wiki/Business-to-business" title="Business-to-business">business-to-business</a> sector, selling consumer credit and insurance reports and related analytics to businesses in a range of industries.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2007)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Business customers include <a href="/wiki/Retail" title="Retail">retailers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Insurance" title="Insurance">insurance</a> firms, <a href="/wiki/Healthcare_provider" class="mw-redirect" title="Healthcare provider">healthcare providers</a>, utilities, government agencies, as well as <a href="/wiki/Bank" title="Bank">banks</a>, <a href="/wiki/Credit_union" title="Credit union">credit unions</a>, personal and specialty finance companies and other financial institutions. Equifax sells businesses credit reports, analytics, <a href="/wiki/Demographic" class="mw-redirect" title="Demographic">demographic</a> data, and software. Credit reports provide detailed information on the personal credit and payment history of individuals, indicating how they have honored financial obligations such as paying bills or repaying a <a href="/wiki/Loan" title="Loan">loan</a>. Credit grantors use this information to decide what sort of products or services to offer their customers, and on what terms.  Equifax also provides commercial credit reports, similar to Dun &amp; Bradstreet, containing financial and non-financial data on businesses of all sizes. Equifax collects and provides data through the National Consumer Telecom and Utilities Exchange (NCTUE), an exchange of non-credit data including consumer payment history on telecommunications and utility accounts.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p><p>In 1999, Equifax began offering services to the credit consumer sector in addition, such as credit fraud and identity theft prevention products. Equifax and other credit monitoring agencies are required by law to provide US residents with one free credit file disclosure every 12 months; the <a href="/wiki/Annualcreditreport.com" class="mw-redirect" title="Annualcreditreport.com">Annualcreditreport.com</a> website incorporates data from U.S. Equifax credit records.
</p><p>In 2016, Equifax partnered with CreditMantri, a credit facilitator based in <a href="/wiki/Chennai" title="Chennai">Chennai</a> (<a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>), to offer free credit score and loan reports to its customers.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p><p>Equifax also offers fraud prevention products based on <a href="/wiki/Device_fingerprinting" class="mw-redirect" title="Device fingerprinting">device fingerprinting</a> such as "FraudIQ Authenticate Device".<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(3)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Security_Failings">Security Failings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Equifax&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Security Failings" data-section="3" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-3 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-3">
<p>According to Hon. Michael Crapo, Chairman of the Committee, "Cybersecurity is one of the most pressing issues facing companies, as well as consumers and Governments alike, and is one of the biggest threats to our financial system. The amount of data that the private industry and Government collect and store is very concerning. There is intrinsic vulnerability in collecting and storing personal financial information, and we need to have a meaningful discussion on how to protect and limit access to it." <sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="2016_advance-warnings_of_insecure_systems">2016 advance-warnings of insecure systems</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Equifax&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: 2016 advance-warnings of insecure systems" data-section="4" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>According to an October 2017 report from <a href="/wiki/Motherboard_(website)" class="mw-redirect" title="Motherboard (website)">Motherboard</a>, around December 2016, a security researcher examining Equifax's servers observed an online portal, apparently created for Equifax employees only, was accessible to the open Internet.
</p>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r886047036">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>"I didn't have to do anything fancy," the researcher told Motherboard, explaining that the site was vulnerable to a basic "forced browsing" bug. The researcher requested anonymity out of professional concerns.  "All you had to do was put in a search term and get millions of results, just instantly—in cleartext, through a web app," they said. In total, the researcher downloaded the data of hundreds of thousands of Americans in order to show Equifax the vulnerabilities within its systems. They said they could have downloaded the data of all of Equifax's customers in 10 minutes: "I've seen a lot of bad things, but not this bad."</p><div class="templatequotecite">— <cite><a href="/wiki/Motherboard_(website)" class="mw-redirect" title="Motherboard (website)">Motherboard</a></cite></div></blockquote>
<p>The same types of sensitive private information of American consumers (names, birth dates, social security numbers, etc.) were exposed as in the May–July breach, according to Motherboard.  Additionally, the security researchers said they were able to gain <a href="/wiki/Shell_(computing)" title="Shell (computing)">shell</a> access on Equifax's servers and discovered and reported to Equifax additional vulnerabilities. According to the reporting, despite receiving this warning from the security researcher, the affected portal was not closed until six months later in June, well after the March and May–July breaches had begun.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup>  Moreover, the employee portal was reportedly not the same server targeted in the later breaches, which Motherboard speculates may suggest multiple breaches by more than one party may have occurred.
</p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="March_2017_security_breach">March 2017 security breach</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Equifax&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: March 2017 security breach" data-section="5" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>On September 18, 2017, <i><a href="/wiki/Bloomberg_News" title="Bloomberg News">Bloomberg News</a></i> reported that Equifax had been the victim of a "major breach of its computer systems" in March 2017, and that in early March it had begun "notifying a small number of outsiders and banking customers" about this attack.<sup id="cite_ref-Bloomberg02_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bloomberg02-34">[34]</a></sup></p><p>According to Bloomberg, a person familiar with the breach believed this early-March intrusion may have been carried out by the same party that breached Equifax's computer systems again in May. According to Bloomberg, Equifax enlisted <a href="/wiki/Mandiant" title="Mandiant">Mandiant</a> (owned by <a href="/wiki/FireEye" title="FireEye">FireEye, Inc.</a>) to assist in investigating the March attack.  The same cybersecurity firm was hired following the May–July breach.<sup id="cite_ref-Bloomberg02_34-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bloomberg02-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span id="May.E2.80.93July_2017_data_breach"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="May–July_2017_data_breach">May–July 2017 data breach</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Equifax&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: May–July 2017 data breach" data-section="6" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/2017_Equifax_data_breach" title="2017 Equifax data breach">2017 Equifax data breach</a></div>
<p>Between May and July 2017, yet-identified hackers were able to use a known exploit on one of Equifax' web servers that had yet to be updated to access the credit records of more than 140 million Americans as well as some British and Canadian citizens before the breach was detected and shut down. Equifax disclosed the breach on September 7, 2017 after determining the means and scope of the breach.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-35">[35]</a></sup> The event was considered "one of the biggest data breaches in history."<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></p><p>Several consumers filed lawsuits in small-claims court against Equifax due to the breach, while Equifax later came to a $575 million settlement with the Federal Trade Commission to offer either a cash payment or credit monitoring for those affected by the breach.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> The data from the breach has yet to be seen on black markets or the dark web by security experts, making it difficult to identify the origin of the breach. However, in February 2020, the United States Department of Justice indicted four Chinese military people on nine charges related to the breach, which China has denied.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-cbs_china_deny_39-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cbs_china_deny-39">[39]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="2017_exposure_of_Argentine_consumer_data">2017 exposure of Argentine consumer data</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Equifax&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: 2017 exposure of Argentine consumer data" data-section="7" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>In September 2017, <a href="/wiki/Brian_Krebs" title="Brian Krebs">Brian Krebs</a> revealed that the Argentine arm of Equifax had left private data from approximately 14,000 consumers, and more than 100 staff members, available to anyone who entered "admin" as both the username and password for one of its online systems.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="2017_withdrawal_of_vulnerable_mobile_apps">2017 withdrawal of vulnerable mobile apps</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Equifax&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: 2017 withdrawal of vulnerable mobile apps" data-section="8" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>On September 7, 2017, the same day as Equifax announced a <a href="/wiki/Equifax_data_breach" class="mw-redirect" title="Equifax data breach">large security breach</a>, Equifax removed its official <a href="/wiki/Mobile_app" title="Mobile app">mobile apps</a> from the Apple <a href="/wiki/App_Store_(iOS)" title="App Store (iOS)">App Store</a> and from <a href="/wiki/Google_Play" title="Google Play">Google Play</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> While these apps themselves were not reportedly connected to that breach, they had security flaws of their own, being vulnerable to <a href="/wiki/Man-in-the-middle_attack" title="Man-in-the-middle attack">man-in-the-middle attacks</a> owing to some parts using <a href="/wiki/HTTP" class="mw-redirect" title="HTTP">HTTP</a> instead of <a href="/wiki/HTTPS" title="HTTPS">HTTPS</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-fc-2017_43-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fc-2017-43">[43]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="2017_exposure_of_American_salary_data">2017 exposure of American salary data</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Equifax&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: 2017 exposure of American salary data" data-section="9" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>On October 8, 2017, Krebs reported that <a href="/wiki/The_Work_Number" title="The Work Number">The Work Number</a>, a website operated by Equifax's <a href="/wiki/TALX" class="mw-redirect" title="TALX">TALX</a> division, exposed the salary histories for employees of tens of thousands of US companies to anyone in possession of the employee's Social Security Number and date of birth.<sup id="cite_ref-krebsonsecurity.com_44-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-krebsonsecurity.com-44">[44]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-boingboing.net_45-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-boingboing.net-45">[45]</a></sup> For roughly half the US population, both of the latter pieces of data are known to be in possession of criminals, following Equifax's <a href="/wiki/Equifax_data_breach" class="mw-redirect" title="Equifax data breach">May–July 2017 security breach</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-krebsonsecurity.com_44-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-krebsonsecurity.com-44">[44]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-boingboing.net_45-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-boingboing.net-45">[45]</a></sup> In July 2019, Equifax settled with the <a href="/wiki/Federal_Trade_Commission" title="Federal Trade Commission">Federal Trade Commission</a> for $700 million. This number contains a $380,500,000 consumer restitution fund, part of the class action lawsuit.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Website_malware">Website malware</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Equifax&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Website malware" data-section="10" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>On October 12, 2017, Equifax's website was reported to have been offering visitors <a href="/wiki/Malware" title="Malware">malware</a> via <a href="/wiki/Drive-by_download" title="Drive-by download">drive-by download</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-atdrive_47-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-atdrive-47">[47]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-mashdrive_48-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mashdrive-48">[48]</a></sup> The malware was disguised as an update for <a href="/wiki/Adobe_Flash" title="Adobe Flash">Adobe Flash</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-atdrive_47-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-atdrive-47">[47]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-mashdrive_48-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mashdrive-48">[48]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-pcmag20171012_49-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pcmag20171012-49">[49]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup>  At that time, only 3 out of 65 top anti-malware products provided protection against the particular malware, meaning that many visitors were at risk of having their computers infected if visiting the Equifax website.<sup id="cite_ref-pcmag20171012_49-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pcmag20171012-49">[49]</a></sup></p><p>On October 13, 2017, the attack was revealed to have been performed by hijacking third-party <a href="/wiki/Website_analytics" class="mw-redirect" title="Website analytics">analytics</a> <a href="/wiki/JavaScript" title="JavaScript">JavaScript</a> from <a href="/wiki/Digital_River" title="Digital River">Digital River</a> brand FireClick.<sup id="cite_ref-ars-hack_51-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ars-hack-51">[51]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup></p><p>Also on October 13, 2017, the <a href="/wiki/U.S._Internal_Revenue_Service" class="mw-redirect" title="U.S. Internal Revenue Service">U.S. Internal Revenue Service</a> was reported to have suspended a $7.2 million contract with Equifax, as a result of the attack.<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup></p>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(4)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Lawsuits_and_fines">Lawsuits and fines</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Equifax&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Lawsuits and fines" data-section="11" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-4 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-4">
<table class="box-Update plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content ambox-Update" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">This section needs to be <b>updated</b>.<span class="hide-when-compact"> Please update this article to reflect recent events or newly available information.</span>  <small class="date-container"><i>(<span class="date">July 2019</span>)</i></small></div></td></tr></tbody></table><p>The company has been fined by the Federal Trade Commission on two occasions for violating the Fair Credit Reporting Act ("FCRA"). In 2000, Equifax, along with Experian and TransUnion, was fined $2.5 million for blocking and delaying phone calls from consumers trying to get information about their credit. In 2003, the FTC took Equifax to court for the same reason and settled its lawsuit with the company for a fine of $250,000.<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup></p><p>In July 2013, a federal jury in Oregon awarded $18.6 million to Julie Miller of Marion County against Equifax for violations of the Fair Credit Reporting Act.<sup id="cite_ref-'Excuse_me,_I'm_not_dead'_St._Louis_County_woman_pleads_to_her_bank_56-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-'Excuse_me,_I'm_not_dead'_St._Louis_County_woman_pleads_to_her_bank-56">[56]</a></sup> In her lawsuit, Miller alleged Equifax had merged her credit reports with another person with a different Social Security number, date of birth, and address. Miller contacted Equifax repeatedly in writing and over the telephone, but Equifax refused to delete dozens of false collection accounts from Miller's credit report.<sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup> The award included $18.4 million in punitive damages, and $180,000 in compensatory damages. Miller's lawyer, Justin Baxter, explained that the false reporting damaged Miller's reputation, she was denied credit, and her private information was given to businesses Miller had no relationship with.<sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup> The jury's verdict is believed to be the largest award in an individual case under the Fair Credit Reporting Act.<sup id="cite_ref-59" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup> An Equifax spokesperson said that Equifax is considering appealing the jury's verdict.<sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup> A federal judge reduced the award to $1.62 million in 2014.<sup id="cite_ref-61" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup></p><p>In 2014, Equifax and Heartland Bank were sued by Kimberly Haman of the St. Louis area for reporting she was dead.<sup id="cite_ref-Woman_sues_in_effort_to_prove_she_is_alive_62-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Woman_sues_in_effort_to_prove_she_is_alive-62">[62]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Woman_Listed_as_Deceased_Files_Lawsuit_Claiming_She's_Alive_63-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Woman_Listed_as_Deceased_Files_Lawsuit_Claiming_She's_Alive-63">[63]</a></sup>  A Heartland Bank spokesperson said the bank "immediately investigated and contacted the credit reporting agencies after Haman reported" she was still alive.<sup id="cite_ref-Woman_sues_in_effort_to_prove_she_is_alive_62-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Woman_sues_in_effort_to_prove_she_is_alive-62">[62]</a></sup> An Equifax "spokesperson told the Post-Dispatch that Equifax blocked the Heartland account information from appearing on Haman's credit report after a reporter's inquiry."<sup id="cite_ref-'Excuse_me,_I'm_not_dead'_St._Louis_County_woman_pleads_to_her_bank_56-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-'Excuse_me,_I'm_not_dead'_St._Louis_County_woman_pleads_to_her_bank-56">[56]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Woman_sues_in_effort_to_prove_she_is_alive_62-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Woman_sues_in_effort_to_prove_she_is_alive-62">[62]</a></sup></p><p>In April 2014, Equifax was sued in New York federal court by God Gazarov, who claimed the company erroneously reports him as having no credit history because of his unusual first name.<sup id="cite_ref-God_Just_Wants_Some_Credit,_So_He's_Suing_Equifax_64-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-God_Just_Wants_Some_Credit,_So_He's_Suing_Equifax-64">[64]</a></sup></p><p>On November 4, 2017, it was reported that a group of five Oklahomans had sued the company, claiming that Equifax "violated laws which require financial institutions to protect the security of their customers' personal information."<sup id="cite_ref-65" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup> Equifax selected the law firm <a href="/wiki/DLA_Piper" title="DLA Piper">DLA Piper</a> to work on the case in D.C. It had turned to <a href="/wiki/Edelman_(firm)" title="Edelman (firm)">Edelman</a> for earlier crisis control after the October 2017 privacy breach.<sup id="cite_ref-66" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup></p><p>Consumer lawsuits claiming damages under the FCRA have been successful in small claims court.<sup id="cite_ref-67" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup></p><p>Equifax software engineer Sudhakar Reddy was charged with <a href="/wiki/Insider_trading" title="Insider trading">insider trading</a> for purchasing <a href="/wiki/Option_(finance)" title="Option (finance)">options</a> prior to the disclosure of the 2017 data breach.<sup id="cite_ref-68" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-69" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup></p><p>In January 2020, Equifax agreed to a global settlement with the Federal Trade Commission, the Consumer Financial Protection Bureau, and 50 U.S. states and territories. For those that were affected by the data breach, there were open suggestions to file claims against it. The settlement includes up to $425 million to help people affected by the data breach. <sup id="cite_ref-70" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup> Equifax ultimately reached a settlement with regulators for up to $700 million. <sup id="cite_ref-71" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup></p>
