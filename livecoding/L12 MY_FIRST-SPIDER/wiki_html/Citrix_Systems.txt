By the 1990s, Citrix came to prominence as an industry leader in <a href="/wiki/Thin_client" title="Thin client">thin client</a> technology, enabling purpose-built devices to access remote servers and resources. The company launched its first <a href="/wiki/Initial_public_offering" title="Initial public offering">initial public offering</a> in 1995 and, with few competitors, experienced large revenue increases between 1995 and 1999.
</p><p>Citrix acquired Sequoia Software Corp. in 2001 and ExpertCity, a provider of remote desktop products, in 2003. This was followed by more than a dozen other acquisitions from 2005 to 2012, which allowed Citrix to expand into server and desktop virtualization, cloud computing, <a href="/wiki/Infrastructure_as_a_Service" class="mw-redirect" title="Infrastructure as a Service">Infrastructure as a Service</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Software_as_a_service" title="Software as a service">Software as a Service</a> offerings. In 2014, Citrix acquired Framehawk and used its technology to improve the delivery of virtual desktops and applications over wireless networks. In 2016, as part of a US$1.8 billion product deal with <a href="/wiki/LogMeIn" title="LogMeIn">LogMeIn</a>, Citrix <a href="/wiki/Corporate_spin-off" title="Corporate spin-off">spun off</a> the GoTo product line into a new business entity, entitled GetGo. In 2017, Citrix completed the merger of GetGo with LogMeIn's products.
</p><p>Citrix has corporate headquarters in both <a href="/wiki/Fort_Lauderdale,_Florida" title="Fort Lauderdale, Florida">Fort Lauderdale, Florida</a> and <a href="/wiki/Santa_Clara,_California" title="Santa Clara, California">Santa Clara, California</a>, with <a href="/wiki/Subsidiary" title="Subsidiary">subsidiary</a> operations in California and Massachusetts, and additional development centers in Australia, Canada, Denmark, Germany, India, and the United Kingdom. Citrix in January 2018 reported revenue from continuing operations of $2.82 billion for fiscal year 2017, compared to $2.74 billion for fiscal year 2016, a 3% increase.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> As of April 2017, the company employed approximately 8,100 employees worldwide.<sup id="cite_ref-2017-DEF14A_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2017-DEF14A-6">[6]</a></sup></p>

</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(1)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Citrix_Systems&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History" data-section="1" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-1 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-1">
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_history">Early history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Citrix_Systems&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Early history" data-section="2" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>Citrix was founded in <a href="/wiki/Richardson,_Texas" title="Richardson, Texas">Richardson, Texas</a> in 1989 by former <a href="/wiki/IBM" title="IBM">IBM</a> developer <a href="/wiki/Ed_Iacobucci" title="Ed Iacobucci">Ed Iacobucci</a> with $3 million in funding.<sup id="cite_ref-NetIndustries_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NetIndustries-7">[7]</a></sup> Following its initial setup and development, Iacobucci moved the company to his former home of <a href="/wiki/Coral_Springs,_Florida" title="Coral Springs, Florida">Coral Springs, Florida</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-NetIndustries_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NetIndustries-7">[7]</a></sup> The company's first employees were five other engineers from IBM that Iacobucci convinced to join his team. Iacobucci served as chairman of the company and Roger Roberts became the <a href="/wiki/Chief_executive_officer" title="Chief executive officer">CEO</a> of Citrix in 1990.<sup id="cite_ref-NetIndustries_7-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NetIndustries-7">[7]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Gibbs99_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gibbs99-8">[8]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Freer97_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Freer97-9">[9]</a></sup> Citrix was originally named Citrus, but changed its name after an existing company claimed <a href="/wiki/Trademark" title="Trademark">trademark</a> rights.<sup id="cite_ref-Heisler_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Heisler-10">[10]</a></sup> The Citrix name is a <a href="/wiki/Portmanteau" title="Portmanteau">portmanteau</a> of <a href="/wiki/Citrus_Project" title="Citrus Project">Citrus</a> and <a href="/wiki/UNIX" class="mw-redirect" title="UNIX">UNIX</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Sarna10_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sarna10-11">[11]</a></sup></p><p>The company's first product was Citrix Multiuser, an extension of <a href="/wiki/OS/2" title="OS/2">OS/2</a> developed over two years. Citrix licensed the OS/2 <a href="/wiki/Source_code" title="Source code">source code</a> from <a href="/wiki/Microsoft" title="Microsoft">Microsoft</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-NetIndustries_7-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NetIndustries-7">[7]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Gibbs99_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gibbs99-8">[8]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Lunan91_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lunan91-12">[12]</a></sup> and developed its own <a href="/wiki/Independent_Computing_Architecture" title="Independent Computing Architecture">Independent Computing Architecture</a> (ICA) protocol for Citrix Multiuser.<sup id="cite_ref-Serwan14_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Serwan14-13">[13]</a></sup> Multiuser allowed multiple users working on separate computers remote access to software on a server, even from computers not built to run OS/2.<sup id="cite_ref-Lunan91_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lunan91-12">[12]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Maney97_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Maney97-14">[14]</a></sup> Three days before the product launched in 1991, Microsoft announced they would be switching from OS/2 to <a href="/wiki/Microsoft_Windows" title="Microsoft Windows">Windows</a>. The switch made Multiuser nearly unusable without significant changes to make it compatible with Windows or <a href="/wiki/DOS" title="DOS">DOS</a>. The company discussed closing in 1991, but investments from <a href="/wiki/Intel" title="Intel">Intel</a>, Microsoft and <a href="/wiki/Kleiner_Perkins_Caufield_%26_Byers" class="mw-redirect" title="Kleiner Perkins Caufield &amp; Byers">Kleiner Perkins Caufield &amp; Byers</a> among others, allowed the company to work on a new version of Multiuser.<sup id="cite_ref-NetIndustries_7-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NetIndustries-7">[7]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Gibbs99_8-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gibbs99-8">[8]</a></sup></p><p>Multi-Win version 2.0 was released in 1992. It was compatible with DOS applications and allowed up to five users.<sup id="cite_ref-Salemi92_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Salemi92-15">[15]</a></sup> In 1993, Citrix released a new remote applications server, WinView, which had the ability to run DOS and Windows applications.<sup id="cite_ref-Durlester93_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Durlester93-16">[16]</a></sup> By 1994, the company's yearly revenue equaled US$10 million.<sup id="cite_ref-NetIndustries_7-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NetIndustries-7">[7]</a></sup></p><p>The company launched WinFrame, a <a href="/wiki/Multi-user" class="mw-redirect" title="Multi-user">multi-user</a> <a href="/wiki/Operating_system" title="Operating system">operating system</a> based on Microsoft's <a href="/wiki/Windows_NT" title="Windows NT">Windows NT</a>, in 1995.<sup id="cite_ref-NetIndustries_7-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NetIndustries-7">[7]</a></sup> The new product allowed up to 15 users and was the first <a href="/wiki/Thin_client" title="Thin client">thin client</a> for Windows.<sup id="cite_ref-Rigney96_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rigney96-17">[17]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-PCMag_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PCMag-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Rise_in_popularity">Rise in popularity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Citrix_Systems&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Rise in popularity" data-section="3" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>Citrix had its <a href="/wiki/Initial_public_offering" title="Initial public offering">initial public offering</a> in December 1995.<sup id="cite_ref-MiamiHerald12_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MiamiHerald12-19">[19]</a></sup> The company's share price doubled from $15 to $30.<sup id="cite_ref-NetIndustries_7-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NetIndustries-7">[7]</a></sup> During the mid-1990s, Citrix became the leader of its growing industry with very few competitors, and the company's revenues doubled year over year between 1995 and 1999.<sup id="cite_ref-Gibbs99_8-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gibbs99-8">[8]</a></sup></p><p>Following weeks of discussions, Iacobucci was able to persuade Microsoft to agree to license Citrix technology for Windows NT Server 4.0, which resulted in Windows Terminal Server Edition in 1998.<sup id="cite_ref-Gibbs99_8-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gibbs99-8">[8]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Freer97_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Freer97-9">[9]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-ESJ98_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ESJ98-20">[20]</a></sup> This agreement allowed Citrix to keep its position in the marketplace and be NT 4.0 compatible.<sup id="cite_ref-Maney97_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Maney97-14">[14]</a></sup> Citrix also earned $75 million through the agreement, along with a royalty arrangement that was valued at approximately $100 million.<sup id="cite_ref-Maney97_14-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Maney97-14">[14]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-MiamiHerald12_19-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MiamiHerald12-19">[19]</a></sup></p><p>Citrix released MetaFrame 1.0 in conjunction with Terminal Server Edition. Due to weaknesses in Microsoft's <a href="/wiki/Remote_Desktop_Protocol" title="Remote Desktop Protocol">Remote Desktop Protocol</a> (RDP), Terminal Server Edition worked best using the ICA protocol developed by Citrix and found in MetaFrame. This meant that Citrix technology was purchased and installed on most machines running Terminal Server Edition.<sup id="cite_ref-Freer97_9-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Freer97-9">[9]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-ESJ98_20-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ESJ98-20">[20]</a></sup></p><p>In 1997, the company opened a new headquarters in <a href="/wiki/Fort_Lauderdale,_Florida" title="Fort Lauderdale, Florida">Fort Lauderdale, Florida</a>. It also opened offices in <a href="/wiki/Sydney" title="Sydney">Sydney</a>, <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">London</a> and <a href="/wiki/Paris" title="Paris">Paris</a> that same year.<sup id="cite_ref-MiamiHerald12_19-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MiamiHerald12-19">[19]</a></sup></p><p>In 1998, <a href="/wiki/Mark_B._Templeton" title="Mark B. Templeton">Mark Templeton</a> became the CEO of Citrix after serving as vice president of marketing.<sup id="cite_ref-MiamiHerald12_19-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MiamiHerald12-19">[19]</a></sup> Also in 1998, it licensed its ICA protocol to IBM and Key Tronics.<sup id="cite_ref-NetIndustries_7-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NetIndustries-7">[7]</a></sup> Citrix licensed its ICA protocol to <a href="/wiki/Motorola" title="Motorola">Motorola</a> for use in digital wireless handsets in 1999.<sup id="cite_ref-Gibbs99_8-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gibbs99-8">[8]</a></sup></p><p>During 1999, the thin-client model Citrix used, became a software trend and the company's customers increased to 15 million.<sup id="cite_ref-NetIndustries_7-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NetIndustries-7">[7]</a></sup> Major clients included <a href="/wiki/Sears" title="Sears">Sears</a>, <a href="/wiki/AT%26T" title="AT&amp;T">AT&amp;T</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Chevron_Corporation" title="Chevron Corporation">Chevron</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Freer97_9-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Freer97-9">[9]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Maney97_14-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Maney97-14">[14]</a></sup> A sudden drop in stocks in 2000 led to Iacobucci leaving the company and CEO Mark Templeton being demoted to president and senior executive officer. Templeton was later reinstated in 2001.<sup id="cite_ref-MiamiHerald12_19-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MiamiHerald12-19">[19]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Bryant12_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bryant12-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Expansion">Expansion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Citrix_Systems&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Expansion" data-section="4" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>In 2001 Citrix acquired the Sequoia Software Corp. for $185 million.<sup id="cite_ref-NetIndustries_7-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NetIndustries-7">[7]</a></sup> That same year it released MetaFrame XP, a new platform using MetaFrame technology.<sup id="cite_ref-Vanover01_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Vanover01-22">[22]</a></sup> This was later rebranded by Citrix as Presentation Server, in 2005.<sup id="cite_ref-Stansel05_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Stansel05-23">[23]</a></sup></p><p>On July 9, 2002, Citrix announced a 10% job cut. At the time the company employed about 1,900 workers. After the announcement the stock hit a five-year low.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p><p>Citrix acquired ExpertCity, a provider of remote desktop products, in December 2003 for $225 million in cash and stock. The acquisition was the largest for the company up to that date.<sup id="cite_ref-Burke03_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Burke03-25">[25]</a></sup> Through the acquisition, Citrix gained ExpertCity's existing products <a href="/wiki/GoToMyPC" title="GoToMyPC">GoToMyPC</a> and <a href="/wiki/GoToAssist" class="mw-redirect" title="GoToAssist">GoToAssist</a>, and ExpertCity became the Citrix Online division of the company.<sup id="cite_ref-Burke03_25-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Burke03-25">[25]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Germain09_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Germain09-26">[26]</a></sup> In 2004, the company introduced Citrix GoToMeeting.<sup id="cite_ref-MiamiHerald12_19-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MiamiHerald12-19">[19]</a></sup></p><p>Between 2005 and 2012, the company acquired over a dozen companies that allowed them to expand in new markets. Citrix acquired acceleration hardware maker NetScaler in 2005, which allowed the company to offer optimized application delivery.<sup id="cite_ref-Cowley05_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cowley05-27">[27]</a></sup> The company entered the <a href="/wiki/Virtual_private_server" title="Virtual private server">server</a> and <a href="/wiki/Desktop_virtualization" title="Desktop virtualization">desktop virtualization</a> market with the purchase of XenSource in August 2007.<sup id="cite_ref-LaMonica07_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LaMonica07-28">[28]</a></sup>
Citrix expanded cloud and <a href="/wiki/Cloud_computing#Infrastructure_as_a_service" title="Cloud computing">Infrastructure as a Service</a> (IaaS) offerings in August 2010 with the acquisition of VMLogix.<sup id="cite_ref-Kepes10_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kepes10-29">[29]</a></sup> In February 2011, Citrix entered the European <a href="/wiki/Software_as_a_Service" class="mw-redirect" title="Software as a Service">Software as a Service</a> (SaaS) market with the acquisition of <a href="/wiki/Netviewer" title="Netviewer">Netviewer</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Williams10_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Williams10-30">[30]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-CRN11_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CRN11-31">[31]</a></sup></p><p>In 2007, the company opened a headquarters in Silicon Valley.<sup id="cite_ref-MiamiHerald12_19-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MiamiHerald12-19">[19]</a></sup> In 2008, the company changed the name of its Presentation Server product line to XenApp.<sup id="cite_ref-Spruijt_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Spruijt-32">[32]</a></sup> Also in 2008, Citrix announced an expanded alliance with Microsoft on desktop virtualization.<sup id="cite_ref-MiamiHerald12_19-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MiamiHerald12-19">[19]</a></sup> On January 29, 2009, Citrix announced that 460 employee positions would be cut, comprising 10% of its workforce.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> In August 2010, Citrix announced a partnership with <a href="/wiki/Google" title="Google">Google</a> to bring the company's products to <a href="/wiki/Chrome_OS" title="Chrome OS">Chrome OS</a> devices.<sup id="cite_ref-Fleck10_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fleck10-34">[34]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Miller14_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Miller14-35">[35]</a></sup> On July 14, 2015, Citrix added full support for <a href="/wiki/Windows_10" title="Windows 10">Windows 10</a> to its <a href="/wiki/Desktop_virtualization" title="Desktop virtualization">desktop virtualization</a> products.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></p><p>The company became a leader in IaaS after the acquisition of <a href="/wiki/Cloud.com" class="mw-redirect" title="Cloud.com">Cloud.com</a>, provider of cloud infrastructure for companies, in July 2011.<sup id="cite_ref-Harris11_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Harris11-37">[37]</a></sup> Citrix began offering <a href="/wiki/Desktop_virtualization" title="Desktop virtualization">VDI</a>-in-a-box to small and medium businesses with the acquisition of Kaviza in May 2011.<sup id="cite_ref-McLaughlin11_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-McLaughlin11-38">[38]</a></sup> The company acquired technology for cloud-based file sharing and storage through its purchase of <a href="/wiki/ShareFile" title="ShareFile">ShareFile</a> in October 2011.<sup id="cite_ref-Williams11_39-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Williams11-39">[39]</a></sup></p><p>In May 2012, Citrix acquired Virtual Computer, maker of intelligent desktop virtualization. The technology is used in the company's XenClient Enterprise edition.<sup id="cite_ref-Alspach12_40-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Alspach12-40">[40]</a></sup> Citrix entered the <a href="/wiki/Mobile_content#Video" title="Mobile content">mobile video</a> and <a href="/wiki/Telecommunication" title="Telecommunication">telecom</a> markets in June 2012 when the company acquired ByteMobile.<sup id="cite_ref-Thomas12_41-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Thomas12-41">[41]</a></sup> Also in 2012, the company acquired <a href="/wiki/Zenprise" title="Zenprise">Zenprise</a>. Zenprise's <a href="/wiki/Mobile_application_management" title="Mobile application management">Mobile application management</a> (MAM) technology was released as <a href="/wiki/XenMobile" title="XenMobile">XenMobile</a> in February 2013.<sup id="cite_ref-Khnaser12_42-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Khnaser12-42">[42]</a></sup></p><p>Citrix acquired Framehawk in January 2014 in order to use the company's technology to improve the delivery of virtual desktops and applications over wireless networks, including <a href="/wiki/Cellular_network" title="Cellular network">cellular</a>, where speed and quality may be poor.<sup id="cite_ref-Kusnetzky14_43-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kusnetzky14-43">[43]</a></sup> In May 2014, Citrix acquired Scalextreme<sup id="cite_ref-venturebeat1_44-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-venturebeat1-44">[44]</a></sup> to bolster its cloud capabilities for its core business unit of XenDesktop and Xenapp such as auto scaling, patching and automation of complex deployments from the cloud.
</p><p>On January 29, 2015, Citrix announced that 700 full-time and 200 contractor positions would be eliminated.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> This constituted about 10% of its workforce. The cuts were expected to save between $90 and $100 million a year. Two hundred of the layoffs occurred in Fort Lauderdale, Florida, where the company is headquartered.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup></p><p>The company had 10,081 employees as of February 2015.<sup id="cite_ref-10K15_47-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10K15-47">[47]</a></sup> In December 2015, Citrix employed approximately 9,500 people, but noted that its November restructure was due to eliminate nearly 700 full-time jobs.<sup id="cite_ref-10K2015_2-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10K2015-2">[2]</a></sup> As of December 31, 2018, Citrix had approximately 8,200 employees.<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Recent_history">Recent history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Citrix_Systems&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Recent history" data-section="5" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>Citrix reported net income of $251.7 million in 2014, down from $339.5 million in 2013.<sup id="cite_ref-Reuters1_49-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Reuters1-49">[49]</a></sup> In July 2015, the company announced several changes to its board of directors, including Robert Calderoni's becoming executive chairman and adding Jesse Cohn as a senior partner of activist hedge fund <a href="/wiki/Elliott_Management_Corporation" title="Elliott Management Corporation">Elliott Management</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Dahlberg15_50-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dahlberg15-50">[50]</a></sup> That same month the company announced that president and CEO Mark Templeton would retire after a replacement was found,<sup id="cite_ref-Darrow15_51-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Darrow15-51">[51]</a></sup> and on October 21, the company named its executive chairman, Robert Calderoni, as interim president and CEO.<sup id="cite_ref-Pounds15_52-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pounds15-52">[52]</a></sup></p><p>In January 2016, Kirill Tatarinov, a former Microsoft executive, was named the president and CEO of Citrix and joined the company's board. Calderoni remained executive chairman of the board.<sup id="cite_ref-Pounds16_53-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pounds16-53">[53]</a></sup></p><p>In July 2016, as part of a deal with Boston-based SaaS company LogMeIn, Citrix announced it had spun off its GoTo product line, which included <a href="/wiki/GoToMeeting" title="GoToMeeting">GoToMeeting</a>, GoToWebinar, GoToWebcast, GoToTraining, GoToAssist and GoToMyPC, into a wholly owned subsidiary called GetGo.<sup id="cite_ref-Ray16_54-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ray16-54">[54]</a></sup> In February 2017, Citrix completed a merger through which GetGo became a subsidiary of LogMeIn. The transaction was valued at approximately $1.8 billion.<sup id="cite_ref-Ohnesorge17_55-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ohnesorge17-55">[55]</a></sup>  David Henshall became the company's CEO in July 2017.<sup id="cite_ref-Fortune17_56-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fortune17-56">[56]</a></sup></p><p>Also in 2017, Citrix expanded its partnership with Google. In May, Google announced it would add support to run Citrix XenApp on its Chrome <a href="/wiki/Web_browser" title="Web browser">web browser</a>, including graphics processing unit acceleration.<sup id="cite_ref-Condon17_57-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Condon17-57">[57]</a></sup> In July, the companies announced they were working to allow Citrix Cloud to deploy virtualized apps and desktops on Google Cloud.<sup id="cite_ref-Tsidulko17_58-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Tsidulko17-58">[58]</a></sup></p><p>In October 2017, Citrix told regulators of plans to lay off staff "across most functions" and consolidate offices in the fall 2017 into 2018.<sup id="cite_ref-Pounds17-Herald_59-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pounds17-Herald-59">[59]</a></sup> The company carried out layoffs in <a href="/wiki/Raleigh,_North_Carolina" title="Raleigh, North Carolina">Raleigh, North Carolina</a>, and office closures in <a href="/wiki/Santa_Barbara,_California" title="Santa Barbara, California">Santa Barbara, California</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Tempe,_Arizona" title="Tempe, Arizona">Tempe, Arizona</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Diamond17_60-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Diamond17-60">[60]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Yamamura17_61-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Yamamura17-61">[61]</a></sup></p><p>Citrix unveiled its Citrix Analytics security software at the 2017 Citrix Synergy conference in <a href="/wiki/Orlando,_Florida" title="Orlando, Florida">Orlando, Florida</a>, in May 2017.<sup id="cite_ref-Gagliordi17_62-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gagliordi17-62">[62]</a></sup> The software detects and responds to security threats by relying on <a href="/wiki/Artificial_intelligence" title="Artificial intelligence">artificial intelligence</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Edmond17_63-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Edmond17-63">[63]</a></sup></p><p>Citrix unveiled its Citrix Analytics for Performance at the 2019 Citrix Summit conference in [Orlando,Florida],in May 2019.The software quantifies User experience for Virtual applications and desktop users and leverages machine learning to troubleshoot performance issues
</p><p>In 2018, Citrix changed the names of some of its products to represent its shift to a cloud operating model.<sup id="cite_ref-64" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup> The company stated that it made the name changes to present a unified product line so that end users can create, manage, and deploy workspaces with apps and data from a single console. The company dropped its Xen and NetScaler brand names: XenApp and XenDesktop became Citrix Virtual Apps and Desktops; XenMobile became Citrix Endpoint Management; ShareFile integration became Citrix Content Collaboration; XenServer became Citrix Hypervisor; Cedexis became Citrix Intelligent Traffic Management; and NetScaler products kept their identities, yet the "NetScaler" brand name was replaced with "Citrix".<sup id="cite_ref-CRN-Afifi-Sabet-170518_65-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CRN-Afifi-Sabet-170518-65">[65]</a></sup></p>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(2)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Operations">Operations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Citrix_Systems&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Operations" data-section="6" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-2 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-2">
<p>Citrix is governed by a ten-member board of directors.<sup id="cite_ref-Reuters17_66-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Reuters17-66">[66]</a></sup> Citrix has headquarters in <a href="/wiki/Fort_Lauderdale,_Florida" title="Fort Lauderdale, Florida">Fort Lauderdale, Florida</a> and <a href="/wiki/Santa_Clara,_California" title="Santa Clara, California">Santa Clara, California</a>. Its other <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> offices are in <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a> and <a href="/wiki/North_Carolina" title="North Carolina">North Carolina</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Weinsten14_67-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Weinsten14-67">[67]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Ohnesorge14_68-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ohnesorge14-68">[68]</a></sup> Citrix research and development centers are located in the U.S., <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>, <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>, <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Greece" title="Greece">Greece</a><sup id="cite_ref-69" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-70" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup> and the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Ribeiro_71-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ribeiro-71">[71]</a></sup></p><p>Citrix is organized into three units: Workspace Services, Delivery Network, and Mobility Apps.<sup id="cite_ref-Symington15_72-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Symington15-72">[72]</a></sup> Citrix licenses its services and products directly to clients, including IT professionals, SMEs, and through companies called <a href="/wiki/Value-added_reseller" title="Value-added reseller">value-added resellers</a> that resell the products and services after adding additional features.
</p><p>Citrix is publicly traded under the <a href="/wiki/Ticker_symbol" title="Ticker symbol">ticker symbol</a> CTXS.<sup id="cite_ref-Forbes14_73-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Forbes14-73">[73]</a></sup> In 2014, the company ranked 741 on the <a href="/wiki/Fortune_1000" title="Fortune 1000">Fortune 1000</a> and 1,793 on <a href="/wiki/Forbes_Global_2000" title="Forbes Global 2000">Forbes Global 2000</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Forbes14_73-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Forbes14-73">[73]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Fortune14_74-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fortune14-74">[74]</a></sup> Citrix's revenue in 2015 equaled US$3.28 billion,<sup id="cite_ref-10K2015_2-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10K2015-2">[2]</a></sup> an increase from $3.14 billion in 2014 and $2.91 billion in 2013.<sup id="cite_ref-Whittaker15_75-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Whittaker15-75">[75]</a></sup></p>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(3)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Acquisitions">Acquisitions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Citrix_Systems&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Acquisitions" data-section="7" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-3 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-3">
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/List_of_mergers_and_acquisitions_by_Citrix" title="List of mergers and acquisitions by Citrix">List of mergers and acquisitions by Citrix</a></div>
<p>Citrix has expanded and added new technologies and services through a number of mergers and acquisitions.<sup id="cite_ref-Pounds15_52-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pounds15-52">[52]</a></sup> Its first acquisition was DataPac in 1997, which Citrix purchased in order to utilize DataPac's technology and its position in the Asia-Pacific region.<sup id="cite_ref-Citrix_1997_76-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Citrix_1997-76">[76]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Duursma_2006_Citrix_77-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Duursma_2006_Citrix-77">[77]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Costello_1997_DataPad_78-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Costello_1997_DataPad-78">[78]</a></sup> Other major acquisitions include ExpertCity in 2004, NetScaler in 2005, XenSource in 2007 and <a href="/wiki/ShareFile" title="ShareFile">ShareFile</a> in 2011. As of 2015, Citrix has acquired nearly 50 companies.<sup id="cite_ref-invests_79-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-invests-79">[79]</a></sup></p><p>In November, 2018, Citrix paid $200M to acquire Sapho, a software startup that develops micro apps for workers.<sup id="cite_ref-80" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-80">[80]</a></sup></p>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(4)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Products">Products</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Citrix_Systems&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Products" data-section="8" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-4 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-4">
<p>Citrix creates <a href="/wiki/Software" title="Software">software</a> that allows the individuals of an enterprise to work and collaborate remotely regardless of <a href="/wiki/Cross-platform" class="mw-redirect" title="Cross-platform">device</a> or network. The main areas the company works in are <a href="/wiki/Client_(computing)" title="Client (computing)">desktop</a> and apps; <a href="/wiki/Desktop_virtualization#Desktop_as_a_Service" title="Desktop virtualization">Desktop as a Service</a> (DaaS); networking and <a href="/wiki/Cloud_computing" title="Cloud computing">cloud</a>; and <a href="/wiki/Software_as_a_service" title="Software as a service">Software as a service</a> (SaaS).<sup id="cite_ref-Butler12_81-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Butler12-81">[81]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Alpha13_82-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Alpha13-82">[82]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Desktops_and_apps">Desktops and apps</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Citrix_Systems&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Desktops and apps" data-section="9" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>Citrix offers a number of products related to <a href="/wiki/Desktop_virtualization" title="Desktop virtualization">desktop</a> and <a href="/wiki/Application_virtualization" title="Application virtualization">application virtualization</a>. These tools allow access to Windows desktops and applications independently of the machine they are actually on, and from any device with any operating system.<sup id="cite_ref-Krebs10_83-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Krebs10-83">[83]</a></sup> Citrix XenApp provides application virtualization (now integrated as part of XenDesktop), and Citrix <a href="/wiki/XenDesktop" class="mw-redirect" title="XenDesktop">XenDesktop</a>, Citrix VDI-in-a-Box and XenClient all provide desktop virtualization.<sup id="cite_ref-Morgan11_84-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Morgan11-84">[84]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Finley15_85-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Finley15-85">[85]</a></sup> The DesktopPlayer for Mac allows online and offline access to Windows virtual desktops from Macs.<sup id="cite_ref-Wood14_86-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wood14-86">[86]</a></sup> Citrix Workspace Cloud is a platform for building and delivering desktops and <a href="/wiki/Application_software" title="Application software">applications</a> from the <a href="/wiki/Cloud_computing" title="Cloud computing">cloud</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Ward15_87-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ward15-87">[87]</a></sup> ShareFile allows companies and organizations to sync and share files.<sup id="cite_ref-Kerravala13_88-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kerravala13-88">[88]</a></sup> XenMobile offers <a href="/wiki/Mobile_app" title="Mobile app">mobile app</a> and device management, <a href="/wiki/Citrix_Receiver" class="mw-redirect" title="Citrix Receiver">Citrix Receiver</a> is a <a href="/wiki/Client_(computing)" title="Client (computing)">client software</a> that allows universal access to <a href="/wiki/Virtual_application" title="Virtual application">virtual applications</a> and desktops, and AppDNA, software that provides application migration and management.<sup id="cite_ref-Morgan11_84-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Morgan11-84">[84]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span id="Desktop_as_a_Service_.28DaaS.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Desktop_as_a_Service_(DaaS)">Desktop as a Service (DaaS)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Citrix_Systems&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Desktop as a Service (DaaS)" data-section="10" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>Citrix technology enables service providers to provide <a href="/wiki/Desktop_virtualization#Desktop_as_a_Service" title="Desktop virtualization">Desktop as a Service</a> (DaaS) offerings to their customers, including business apps and desktops.<sup id="cite_ref-Butler12_81-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Butler12-81">[81]</a></sup> These products include: Worx Mobile Apps for secure <a href="/wiki/Email" title="Email">email</a>, <a href="/wiki/Web_browser" title="Web browser">browser</a>, and document sharing;<sup id="cite_ref-Donovan13_89-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Donovan13-89">[89]</a></sup> and Citrix Workspace Suite for mobile workspaces.<sup id="cite_ref-know_90-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-know-90">[90]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Networking_and_cloud">Networking and cloud</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Citrix_Systems&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Networking and cloud" data-section="11" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>Citrix products related to cloud computing and <a href="/wiki/Telecommunications_network" title="Telecommunications network">networking</a> include Citrix XenServer for server virtualization<sup id="cite_ref-Finley15_85-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Finley15-85">[85]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Rath12_91-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rath12-91">[91]</a></sup> and its NetScaler brand of <a href="/wiki/Computer_appliance" title="Computer appliance">network appliances</a>, including <a href="/wiki/Wide_area_network" title="Wide area network">WAN</a> optimization devices, <a href="/wiki/SD-WAN" title="SD-WAN">Software-Defined WAN</a> delivery equipment, Application Delivery Controllers (ADC), Gateways, and AppFirewall web application firewall.<sup id="cite_ref-Alpha13_82-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Alpha13-82">[82]</a></sup> All this are managed by their cloud management software <a href="/wiki/Citrix_Cloud" title="Citrix Cloud">Citrix Cloud</a>. The company also has ByteMobile Adaptive Traffic Management, which aims to optimize mobile video services through traffic management, policy control and <a href="/wiki/Cache_(computing)" title="Cache (computing)">caching</a>, and ByteMobile Insight, which provides mobile data and subscriber analytics.<sup id="cite_ref-moves_92-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-moves-92">[92]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Perez12_93-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Perez12-93">[93]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span id="Software_as_a_Service_.28SaaS.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Software_as_a_Service_(SaaS)">Software as a Service (SaaS)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Citrix_Systems&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Software as a Service (SaaS)" data-section="12" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>Citrix <a href="/wiki/Software_as_a_service" title="Software as a service">software as a service</a> (SaaS) products are focused on collaboration and communications. The offerings include <a href="/wiki/Podio" title="Podio">Podio</a>, a cloud-based collaboration service, and OpenVoice, which provides audio conferencing.<sup id="cite_ref-Finley15_85-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Finley15-85">[85]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Kerravala13_88-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kerravala13-88">[88]</a></sup></p>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(5)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Corporate_responsibility">Corporate responsibility</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Citrix_Systems&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Corporate responsibility" data-section="13" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-5 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-5">
<p>The company's philanthropic activities include corporate giving—such as corporate donations of <a href="/wiki/Gifts_in_kind" title="Gifts in kind">in-kind gifts</a>—and employee match programs.<sup id="cite_ref-Bloomberg03_94-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bloomberg03-94">[94]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Nyholt13_95-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Nyholt13-95">[95]</a></sup> In addition, Citrix employees are allowed to take two paid volunteer days each year and participate in the company's annual "Global Day of Impact"—an event that encourages Citrix employees to volunteer in their local communities.<sup id="cite_ref-Weening11_96-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Weening11-96">[96]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Palminteri13_97-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Palminteri13-97">[97]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-TriangleBizJournal13_98-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TriangleBizJournal13-98">[98]</a></sup></p><p>Near its Fort Lauderdale headquarters, Citrix has provided business training to non-profit teams. In particular, the company helped a local non-profit organization launch a computer on wheels to offer training to low-income neighborhoods. In 2007, the company connected a <a href="/wiki/Broward_County,_Florida" title="Broward County, Florida">Broward County, Florida</a> neighborhood with <a href="/wiki/Agogo,_Ghana" title="Agogo, Ghana">Agogo, Ghana</a> through donated technology and training.<sup id="cite_ref-Hemlock07_99-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hemlock07-99">[99]</a></sup> Furthermore, the company's Raleigh office began a program called "Project Code" in 2014, which leads youth from local <a href="/wiki/Boys_%26_Girls_Clubs_of_America" title="Boys &amp; Girls Clubs of America">Boys &amp; Girls Clubs</a> through coding exercises and teaches them about computer science.<sup id="cite_ref-Barr15_100-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Barr15-100">[100]</a></sup></p><p>In addition to its philanthropic activities, Citrix has donated some of its open-source technology to non-profit software organizations to continue its development and gain more contributors. Citrix gave <a href="/wiki/Apache_CloudStack" title="Apache CloudStack">Cloudstack</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Apache_Software_Foundation" class="mw-redirect" title="Apache Software Foundation">Apache Foundation</a> in 2012 and <a href="/wiki/Xen" title="Xen">Xen</a> <a href="/wiki/Hypervisor" title="Hypervisor">hypervisor</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Linux_Foundation" title="Linux Foundation">Linux Foundation</a> in 2013.<sup id="cite_ref-Jackson13_101-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jackson13-101">[101]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Duckett14_102-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Duckett14-102">[102]</a></sup></p>
