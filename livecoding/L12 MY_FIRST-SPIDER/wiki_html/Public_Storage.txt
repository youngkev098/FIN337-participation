<p><b>Public Storage</b> is an American international <a href="/wiki/Self_storage" title="Self storage">self storage</a> company headquartered in <a href="/wiki/Glendale,_California" title="Glendale, California">Glendale, California</a>, that is run as a <a href="/wiki/Real_estate_investment_trust" title="Real estate investment trust">real estate investment trust</a> (REIT). It is the largest brand of self-storage services in the US.<sup id="cite_ref-one_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-one-4">[4]</a></sup> In 2008, it was the largest of four publicly traded storage REITs.<sup id="cite_ref-Gordon2008_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gordon2008-5">[5]</a></sup> There are more than 2,200 Public Storage self-storage locations in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">US</a>, <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a> and <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>. It also owns 42 percent of an office parks subsidiary, sells packing supplies, and provides other services. As a REIT, it is owned by real estate investors, who receive more than 90 percent of the company's profits as a return-on-investment.<sup id="cite_ref-one_4-1" class="reference"><a </section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(1)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Public_Storage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History" data-section="1" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-1 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-1">
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Origins">Origins</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Public_Storage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Origins" data-section="2" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>The idea for Public Storage was conceived by Southern California real estate developer B. Wayne Hughes in the early 1970s.<sup id="cite_ref-three_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-three-7">[7]</a></sup> During a trip to Texas, he observed that local real estate developers were doing well creating mini-storage facilities outside of Dallas and Houston; he brought the <a href="/wiki/Self_storage" title="Self storage">self-storage</a> concept back with him to California.<sup id="cite_ref-three_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-three-7">[7]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-four_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-four-8">[8]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-vault_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-vault-9">[9]</a></sup> Hughes partnered with Kenneth Volk and the two founded Public Storage in August 1972 with a $50,000 initial investment,<sup id="cite_ref-three_7-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-three-7">[7]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-vault_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-vault-9">[9]</a></sup> at first calling it "Private Storage Spaces Inc."<sup id="cite_ref-book_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-book-10">[10]</a></sup></p><p>The first warehouse was built in 1972 in <a href="/wiki/El_Cajon,_California" title="El Cajon, California">El Cajon, California</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-vault_9-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-vault-9">[9]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-latimes_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-latimes-11">[11]</a></sup> According to Hughes, "Private Storage Spaces Inc." confused people into thinking it was private, so the name was changed to "Public Storage" to match the PS acronym.<sup id="cite_ref-three_7-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-three-7">[7]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-book_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-book-10">[10]</a></sup> The founders initially planned to build the storage warehouses as a temporary source of income until the land became more valuable and could be redeveloped for another use.<sup id="cite_ref-archive_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-archive-12">[12]</a></sup></p><p>Within three months, the first location was <a href="/wiki/Breaking_even" class="mw-redirect" title="Breaking even">breaking even</a> with a 35 percent occupancy.<sup id="cite_ref-three_7-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-three-7">[7]</a></sup> The units were rented for a similar price per square foot as apartments or office space,  but cost 35 to 50 percent less to build and maintain.<sup id="cite_ref-three_7-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-three-7">[7]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-four_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-four-8">[8]</a></sup> A property management subsidiary called Public Storage Management Inc. was formed in 1973.<sup id="cite_ref-four_8-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-four-8">[8]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-book_10-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-book-10">[10]</a></sup> By 1974, 20 locations had been built.<sup id="cite_ref-four_8-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-four-8">[8]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-vault_9-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-vault-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Real_Estate_Limited_Partnership_financing">Real Estate Limited Partnership financing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Public_Storage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Real Estate Limited Partnership financing" data-section="3" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Public_Storage_office_in_Newington,_Virginia.jpg" class="image"><noscript><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/34/Public_Storage_office_in_Newington%2C_Virginia.jpg/220px-Public_Storage_office_in_Newington%2C_Virginia.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="3264" data-file-height="2448"></noscript><span class="lazy-image-placeholder" style="width: 220px;height: 165px;" data-src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/34/Public_Storage_office_in_Newington%2C_Virginia.jpg/220px-Public_Storage_office_in_Newington%2C_Virginia.jpg" data-alt="" data-width="220" data-height="165" data-class="thumbimage"> </span></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Public_Storage_office_in_Newington,_Virginia.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A Public Storage office in <a href="/wiki/Newington,_Virginia" title="Newington, Virginia">Newington, Virginia</a></div></div></div>
<p>Hughes disliked loans, so he financed the purchase and development of new properties primarily through real estate <a href="/wiki/Limited_partnerships" class="mw-redirect" title="Limited partnerships">limited partnerships</a> (RELPs).<sup id="cite_ref-four_8-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-four-8">[8]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-two_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-two-13">[13]</a></sup> At first, Public Storage built warehouses and sold them to independent RELPs for a development fee.<sup id="cite_ref-four_8-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-four-8">[8]</a></sup> The company's own RELP, called Public Storage Partners Ltd, was formed in 1975<sup id="cite_ref-book_10-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-book-10">[10]</a></sup> and closed its first deal for $3 million in investments two years later.<sup id="cite_ref-four_8-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-four-8">[8]</a></sup> Public Storage paid cash to acquire property and build a <a href="/wiki/Self_storage" title="Self storage">self-storage facility</a>, then used the property's income to pay investors back and earn a portion as profit.<sup id="cite_ref-four_8-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-four-8">[8]</a></sup> Public Storage Inc. also earned revenues from a portion of each deal that was made.<sup id="cite_ref-seven_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-seven-14">[14]</a></sup></p><p>Early investors earned three to four times their money back due to increasing property values in Southern California, high occupancy rates, and increasing storage rental prices. By the mid-1980s, Public Storage was raising $200 to $300 million in investments each year.<sup id="cite_ref-four_8-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-four-8">[8]</a></sup> The RELP format allowed the firm to continue building more locations in the 1970s and 1980s when most of the industry had halted growth because of higher interest rates on loans.<sup id="cite_ref-four_8-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-four-8">[8]</a></sup> In the mid-1980s, co-founder Volk retired and his interest in Public Storage was purchased by Hughes.<sup id="cite_ref-three_7-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-three-7">[7]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Forbes" title="Forbes">Forbes</a></i> estimated that by this time the company was worth $800 million.<sup id="cite_ref-three_7-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-three-7">[7]</a></sup></p><p>Investment funding was used to build mini-storage warehouses beyond California, targeting the 39 largest US cities. The firm opened self-storage locations in close proximity to each other, so the  sites could share a development office, and  justify local television advertising.<sup id="cite_ref-three_7-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-three-7">[7]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-four_8-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-four-8">[8]</a></sup> The firm's growth slowed in the mid-1980s as new competition increased the cost of property and slowed the increase in rental prices to consumers.<sup id="cite_ref-four_8-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-four-8">[8]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-seven_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-seven-14">[14]</a></sup> Poor weather and difficult labor markets outside of California delayed development projects; the company also made poor investments in office parks.<sup id="cite_ref-four_8-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-four-8">[8]</a></sup> Interest in real estate investing also diminished.<sup id="cite_ref-seven_14-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-seven-14">[14]</a></sup></p><p>By the late 1980s, the company opened its 1,000th Public Storage location<sup id="cite_ref-four_8-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-four-8">[8]</a></sup> and the company was three times larger than its nearest competitor in the US market.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> By 1989, $2.7 billion had been invested from 200,000 investors.<sup id="cite_ref-four_8-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-four-8">[8]</a></sup> Public Storage helped popularize the use of self-storage businesses as a real estate investment vehicle<sup id="cite_ref-appraisal_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-appraisal-16">[16]</a></sup> and became one of the longest-running RELP investment vehicles.<sup id="cite_ref-four_8-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-four-8">[8]</a></sup> By 1991, Public Storage had set up more than 150 RELPs and <a href="/wiki/Real_estate_investment_trust" title="Real estate investment trust">real estate investment trusts</a> (REITs).<sup id="cite_ref-twentyone_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-twentyone-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span id="Real_Estate_Investment_Trust_.28REIT.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Real_Estate_Investment_Trust_(REIT)">Real Estate Investment Trust (REIT)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Public_Storage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Real Estate Investment Trust (REIT)" data-section="4" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>According to <i><a href="/wiki/Financial_World" title="Financial World">Financial World</a></i>, by 1989 the real estate limited partnership (RELP) market that Public Storage relied on for funding "all but vanished".<sup id="cite_ref-four_8-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-four-8">[8]</a></sup> A book by Public Storage Inc. said the <a href="/wiki/Tax_Reform_Act_of_1986" title="Tax Reform Act of 1986">Tax Reform Act of 1986</a> reduced the tax benefits of RELPs and was followed by "a tough time for real estate companies."<sup id="cite_ref-book_10-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-book-10">[10]</a></sup> In December 1990, five of its partnerships were converted to Real Estate Investment Trusts (REITs) in December.<sup id="cite_ref-twentyone_17-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-twentyone-17">[17]</a></sup> It also began consolidating its partnerships and acquiring many of the companies in which it held an interest.<sup id="cite_ref-one_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-one-4">[4]</a></sup></p><p>Storage Equities was founded by Public Storage in 1980<sup id="cite_ref-Mullaney1998_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mullaney1998-18">[18]</a></sup> to purchase self-storage facilities.<sup id="cite_ref-book_10-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-book-10">[10]</a></sup> It was one of 17 self-storage REITs that Public Storage held an interest in.<sup id="cite_ref-Pederson2003_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pederson2003-19">[19]</a></sup> Between 1992 and 1995, Storage Equities paid Public Storage $31 million in management and consulting fees.<sup id="cite_ref-three_7-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-three-7">[7]</a></sup> In 1995, Public Storage and its subsidiaries were merged with its self-storage REIT, Storage Equities Inc., and re-structured as a single REIT called Public Storage Inc.<sup id="cite_ref-one_4-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-one-4">[4]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-book_10-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-book-10">[10]</a></sup> One analyst said the acquisition capitalized on <a href="/wiki/Conflict_of_interest" title="Conflict of interest">conflict of interest</a> concerns, while Hughes said the merger was set up to alleviate them.<sup id="cite_ref-three_7-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-three-7">[7]</a></sup></p><p>In 1995, the company spun-off its box, locks, and packing and moving supplies business into the PS Orangeco subsidiary; Public Storage said this was done to avoid the risk of losing the company's tax-free REIT status if too large a portion of the company's business is no longer related to real estate.<sup id="cite_ref-daiuhfiauwhefona_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-daiuhfiauwhefona-20">[20]</a></sup> This frustrated institutional investors that can only invest in real estate companies and could no longer invest in the new non-real estate subsidiaries.<sup id="cite_ref-daiuhfiauwhefona_20-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-daiuhfiauwhefona-20">[20]</a></sup> By 1998, Public Storage had $141 million in quarterly revenues,<sup id="cite_ref-one_4-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-one-4">[4]</a></sup> $2 billion in assets and 1,200 facilities in 38 states.<sup id="cite_ref-one_4-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-one-4">[4]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-twentyone_17-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-twentyone-17">[17]</a></sup> Later that year, Public Storage acquired a competing storage company called Storage Trust Realty in a $600 million transaction.<sup id="cite_ref-vault_9-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-vault-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Recent_history">Recent history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Public_Storage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Recent history" data-section="5" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>Public Storage grew steadily in the early 2000s<sup id="cite_ref-fiftyseven_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fiftyseven-21">[21]</a></sup> and was added to the S&amp;P 500 in 2005.<sup id="cite_ref-appraisal_16-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-appraisal-16">[16]</a></sup> In 2006 it acquired Shurgard Storage Centers in a transaction totaling $5.5 billion, acquiring 624 locations, including 141 in Europe.<sup id="cite_ref-six_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-six-22">[22]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-eleven_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-eleven-23">[23]</a></sup> Public Storage had attempted to acquire the company in 2000 and again in 2005, but its offers were rejected.<sup id="cite_ref-vault_9-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-vault-9">[9]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-latimes_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-latimes-11">[11]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-eleven_23-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-eleven-23">[23]</a></sup> The company has continued to make numerous acquisitions, such as a March 2010 purchase of 30 locations from A-American Self Storage.<sup id="cite_ref-appraisal_16-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-appraisal-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(2)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Operations">Operations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Public_Storage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Operations" data-section="6" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-2 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-2">
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Public_Storage_Los_Angeles_CA.jpg" class="image"><noscript><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3c/Public_Storage_Los_Angeles_CA.jpg/220px-Public_Storage_Los_Angeles_CA.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="640" data-file-height="480"></noscript><span class="lazy-image-placeholder" style="width: 220px;height: 165px;" data-src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3c/Public_Storage_Los_Angeles_CA.jpg/220px-Public_Storage_Los_Angeles_CA.jpg" data-alt="" data-width="220" data-height="165" data-class="thumbimage"> </span></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Public_Storage_Los_Angeles_CA.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A Public Storage facility in Los Angeles</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:PublicStorageRichmondHill.jpg" class="image"><noscript><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a5/PublicStorageRichmondHill.jpg/220px-PublicStorageRichmondHill.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="146" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="4928" data-file-height="3264"></noscript><span class="lazy-image-placeholder" style="width: 220px;height: 146px;" data-src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a5/PublicStorageRichmondHill.jpg/220px-PublicStorageRichmondHill.jpg" data-alt="" data-width="220" data-height="146" data-class="thumbimage"> </span></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:PublicStorageRichmondHill.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Public Storage in Ontario</div></div></div>
<p>Public Storage is the largest self-storage brand in the US.<sup id="cite_ref-one_4-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-one-4">[4]</a></sup> As of 2014, there were 2,250 Public Storage locations in North America and 193 locations in Europe; European locations are operated by Shurgard Europe, which Public Storage owns 49 percent of. There are also 2,546 office parks operated by PS Business Parks, which Public Storage owns a 42 percent interest in.<sup id="cite_ref-annual_3-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-annual-3">[3]</a></sup> The largest self-storage business in <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://publicstoragecanada.com/">Canada</a> is operated by a separate company that is allowed to use the Public Storage brand.<sup id="cite_ref-one_4-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-one-4">[4]</a></sup></p><p>Self-storage locations tend to be in dense clusters in major cities,<sup id="cite_ref-one_4-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-one-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template"><span style="white-space: nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Link_rot" title="Wikipedia:Link rot"><span title=" Dead link since January 2017">dead link</span></a></i>]</span></sup> especially near freeways and intersections.<sup id="cite_ref-two_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-two-13">[13]</a></sup> Public Storage has very few employees for a company of its size. Customer access to each storage location is automated. Some locations have a husband-wife couple that live on-site and are paid close to minimum wage to monitor the facility.<sup id="cite_ref-twelve_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-twelve-24">[24]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-BarlettSteele1992_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BarlettSteele1992-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Failure_to_pay_rental_fees">Failure to pay rental fees</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Public_Storage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Failure to pay rental fees" data-section="7" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>The contents of a storage unit are put up for auction if the rental fees are not paid for sixty days. Although the TV show <i><a href="/wiki/Storage_Wars" title="Storage Wars">Storage Wars</a></i> created increased interest in the auctions, most units do not contain anything of substantial economic value.<sup id="cite_ref-eight_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-eight-26">[26]</a></sup> Sometimes auctioning the renter's property can result in disputes between Public Storage and the renter.<sup id="cite_ref-wsj_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wsj-27">[27]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-soldier_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-soldier-28">[28]</a></sup> In 2007, a customer's belongings were auctioned for non-payment while he was serving the US military in Iraq. After receiving negative publicity, Public Storage apologized and gave him $8,000 as compensation for his sold belongings.<sup id="cite_ref-soldier_28-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-soldier-28">[28]</a></sup></p>

<h3 class="in-block"><span id="Theft.2C_insurance_and_damages"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Theft,_insurance_and_damages">Theft, insurance and damages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Public_Storage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Theft, insurance and damages" data-section="8" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>In 2005, Public Storage said in a public filing that there had been "an increasing number of claims and litigation against owners and managers of rental properties relating to moisture infiltration, which can result in mold or other property damage."<sup id="cite_ref-wsj_27-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wsj-27">[27]</a></sup> The company's rental contract says it is not responsible for the storage unit's contents, even if damage is caused by defects in the unit,<sup id="cite_ref-wsj_27-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wsj-27">[27]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Wall_Street_Journal" title="The Wall Street Journal">The Wall Street Journal</a></i> reported that there were "surprisingly few remedies" for theft or property damage at self-storage facilities.<sup id="cite_ref-wsj_27-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wsj-27">[27]</a></sup></p><p>Many Public Storage customers have filed complaints with the <a href="/wiki/Better_Business_Bureau" title="Better Business Bureau">Better Business Bureau</a> regarding insurance policies sold by Public Storage representatives, after experiencing burglaries of their storage units then having their insurance claims denied.<sup id="cite_ref-KPIX_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-KPIX-30">[30]</a></sup> Investigative journalists from TV news stations in California,<sup id="cite_ref-KPIX_30-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-KPIX-30">[30]</a></sup> Kansas,<sup id="cite_ref-Wagar_2013_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wagar_2013-31">[31]</a></sup> and Washington<sup id="cite_ref-KING5_2014_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-KING5_2014-32">[32]</a></sup> have reported on difficulties consumers had when filing insurance claims for burglaries with Willis<sup id="cite_ref-KPIX_30-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-KPIX-30">[30]</a></sup> and The New Hampshire Insurance Company,<sup id="cite_ref-KING5_2014_32-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-KING5_2014-32">[32]</a></sup> which are affiliated with Public Storage. For example, claims have been denied because the storage unit had an intact lock; affiliated insurance companies cited insufficient evidence of forced entry, though burglars often replace the unit's lock in an attempt to conceal the burglary.<sup id="cite_ref-KING5_2014_32-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-KING5_2014-32">[32]</a></sup> Insurance commissioners in two states have criticized the practices of insurance companies affiliated with Public Storage.<sup id="cite_ref-KPIX_30-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-KPIX-30">[30]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-KING5_2014_32-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-KING5_2014-32">[32]</a></sup> An ongoing <a href="/wiki/Class_action_lawsuit" class="mw-redirect" title="Class action lawsuit">class action lawsuit</a> alleges Public Storage misleads consumers into thinking that insurance premiums are charged at cost, whereas a substantial amount of those premiums are retained as profits by Public Storage.<sup id="cite_ref-Harris_2014_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Harris_2014-33">[33]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Simson_2015_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Simson_2015-34">[34]</a></sup> Sales of these insurance policies do have a high profit, but generate less than five percent of the company's total revenue.<sup id="cite_ref-Harris_2014_33-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Harris_2014-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(3)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Financials">Financials</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Public_Storage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Financials" data-section="9" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-3 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-3">
<p>Public Storage is a "self-administered, self-managed" <a href="/wiki/Real_estate_investment_trust" title="Real estate investment trust">real estate investment trust</a> (REIT).<sup id="cite_ref-one_4-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-one-4">[4]</a></sup> A REIT is an organization that primarily purchases and operates real estate investments and returns at least 90 percent of its incomes to investors.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> It combines the capital of a large number of investors for real estate projects.<sup id="cite_ref-fiftyseven_21-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fiftyseven-21">[21]</a></sup> As of 2008, Public Storage was the largest of four publicly traded self-storage REITs.<sup id="cite_ref-Gordon2008_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gordon2008-5">[5]</a></sup> As of 2013, it had a profit margin of 50 percent, the third-highest in the S&amp;P 500.<sup id="cite_ref-twelve_24-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-twelve-24">[24]</a></sup> Public Storage has risen 17% annually, including dividends over the past 20 years (as of <a href="/wiki/First_quarter_of_a_calendar_year" class="mw-redirect" title="First quarter of a calendar year">Q1</a> 2016).<sup id="cite_ref-:0_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-36">[36]</a></sup> This is a growth rate which is double the <a href="/wiki/S%26P_500" class="mw-redirect" title="S&amp;P 500">S&amp;P 500</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_36-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-36">[36]</a></sup></p><p>More than 90 percent of Public Storage's revenues are from its self-storage operations; it also provides insurance, packing products, and has a 44 percent interest in <a href="/wiki/PS_Business_Parks" title="PS Business Parks">PS Business Parks</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-vault_9-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-vault-9">[9]</a></sup> In 1984, PS Reinsurance was formed to sell insurance for a storage unit's contents.<sup id="cite_ref-book_10-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-book-10">[10]</a></sup> In 1995, PS Orangeco was created as a subsidiary, selling boxes, packaging, truck rentals, and other moving supplies.<sup id="cite_ref-book_10-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-book-10">[10]</a></sup></p><p>Ronald L. Havner, Jr., then-CEO of Public Storage, earned $10.5 million in 2017, 439 times the salary of his company's median worker.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
