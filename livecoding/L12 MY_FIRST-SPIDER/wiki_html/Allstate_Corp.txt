</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(1)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Allstate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History" data-section="1" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-1 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-1">
<p>In 1925, <a href="/wiki/Sears" title="Sears">Sears</a> held a national contest to decide the name of a new brand of car tires. After over two million name submissions, "Allstate" was chosen. The trademark was adopted the next year. The tires' success in the catalog and retail stores prompted Sears Chairman General <a href="/wiki/Robert_E._Wood" title="Robert E. Wood">Robert E. Wood</a> to praise the Allstate tire's contribution to Sears' retail store success.<sup id="cite_ref-searshist_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-searshist-1">[1]</a></sup></p><p>The idea for Allstate Insurance Company came during a bridge game on a commuter train in 1930, when insurance broker Carl L. Odell proposed to Wood, his neighbor, the idea of selling auto insurance by direct mail. The idea appealed to Wood, and he passed the proposal to the Sears board of directors, which approved it. <i>Allstate Insurance Company</i>, named after Sears’ tire brand, went into business on April 17, 1931, offering auto insurance by direct mail and through the Sears catalog.<sup id="cite_ref-Encyclopedia_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Encyclopedia-8">[8]</a></sup>  This was in line with one of the objectives of a company to sell automobile insurance in the same manner as <a href="/wiki/Sears" title="Sears">Sears</a> sold its merchandise.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p><p>Lessing J. Rosenwald was Allstate's first board chairman, and Odell was named vice president and secretary.<sup id="cite_ref-Funding_Universe_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Funding_Universe-10">[10]</a></sup></p><p>In 1933, at the Century of Progress World's Fair in Chicago, Allstate's Richard E. Roskam sold insurance at a booth in the Sears pavilion. In 1934, Allstate opened its first permanent sales office in a Chicago Sears store.<sup id="cite_ref-Allstate_Insurance_Company_Digital_Newsroom_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Allstate_Insurance_Company_Digital_Newsroom-11">[11]</a></sup></p><p>In 1941, only about a quarter of US drivers had auto liability insurance. This led to the state of New York passing a law which established the financial responsibility of drivers for damage or injuries resulting from auto mishaps. That law inspired legislation in other states, and by the mid-1950s nearly every state had some sort of financial responsibility law on its books.<sup id="cite_ref-Encyclopedia_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Encyclopedia-8">[8]</a></sup></p><p>In 1949 the Allstate Headquarters Building was completed at 3245 W. Arthington Street in <a href="/wiki/Chicago" title="Chicago">Chicago</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>  The mid-rise building is currently vacant (as of 2013) and in danger of demolition.  The building is noted for its early post-war mid-rise construction.  This location was vacated when the company relocated in the post-war years.
</p><p>The company's "You're in Good Hands with Allstate" slogan was created in 1950 by Allstate's general sales manager Davis W. Ellis.  At the end of the decade, it was used in the company's first network television advertising campaign, which featured actor <a href="/wiki/Ed_Reimers" title="Ed Reimers">Ed Reimers</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Funding_Universe_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Funding_Universe-10">[10]</a></sup></p><p>Allstate added products throughout the 1950s, including fire insurance in 1954 and homeowners and life insurance in 1957.  Allstate began selling insurance to Canadians in 1953. Allstate Insurance Company of Canada was incorporated in 1964.<sup id="cite_ref-Encyclopedia_8-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Encyclopedia-8">[8]</a></sup> (In 1952 and 1953, Sears also sold an automobile called <a href="/wiki/Allstate_(automobile)" title="Allstate (automobile)">Allstate</a>.)
</p><p>In 1967, the company's home office was moved from <a href="/wiki/Skokie,_Illinois" title="Skokie, Illinois">Skokie</a> to <a href="/wiki/Northbrook,_Illinois" title="Northbrook, Illinois">Northbrook, Illinois</a>. Allstate continued to sell additional types of insurance to customers throughout the decade, including worker's compensation insurance in 1964, <a href="/wiki/Surety_bond" class="mw-redirect" title="Surety bond">surety bonds</a> in 1966, inland-marine coverage in 1967 and a business package policy in 1969.<sup id="cite_ref-Funding_Universe_10-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Funding_Universe-10">[10]</a></sup></p><p>The brand itself expanded in the 1950s and 1960s and could be found on a variety of products like fire extinguishers and motor scooters. In 1952, an Allstate car was produced, but it was a flop; it was pulled from stores by 1953. The Allstate brand was eventually limited to insurance, tires, and car batteries by the late 1960s before becoming insurance-only in the mid-1970s.<sup id="cite_ref-searshist_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-searshist-1">[1]</a></sup> In 1991, the company went public<sup id="cite_ref-history_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-history-3">[3]</a></sup> before becoming completely independent in 1995.<sup id="cite_ref-searshist_1-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-searshist-1">[1]</a></sup></p><p>In 1984, Neighborhood Office Agent program was introduced to make agents more accessible to customers.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p><p>In 1985, Allstate began to move agents out of Sears stores and locate agents in neighborhood offices. In June 1993, 19.8% of Allstate became public through a stock offering. Allstate became completely independent in June 1995, when Sears spun off the remaining 80% stake in the company, distributing 350.5 million shares of Allstate stock to its stockholders.<sup id="cite_ref-Encyclopedia_8-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Encyclopedia-8">[8]</a></sup></p><p>In 1993, Allstate went public when Sears sold 19.8% of the company.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>  At the time, it was the largest IPO to date.
</p><p>In 1996, their website www.allstate.com was launched.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p><p>In 1999, Allstate unveiled a new business model that created a single contract for exclusive, independent agents selling Allstate insurance. It also created a network of call centers.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p><p>In 1999, Allstate purchased the personal lines division of <a href="/wiki/CNA_Financial" title="CNA Financial">CNA Financial</a><sup id="cite_ref-Allstate-Oct-1999-8-K_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Allstate-Oct-1999-8-K-14">[14]</a></sup> and subsequently renamed it to <a href="/wiki/Encompass_Insurance_Company" title="Encompass Insurance Company">Encompass Insurance Company</a> which is written by independent insurance agents, as opposed to the direct writing that constitutes the core part of its business.<sup id="cite_ref-Allstate-Nov-2000-10-Q_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Allstate-Nov-2000-10-Q-15">[15]</a></sup></p><p>In 2003, actor <a href="/wiki/Dennis_Haysbert" title="Dennis Haysbert">Dennis Haysbert</a> became Allstate's spokesman, using the tagline "That’s Allstate’s stand."<sup id="cite_ref-Drive_Steady_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Drive_Steady-16">[16]</a></sup></p><p>In 2010, actor <a href="/wiki/Dean_Winters" title="Dean Winters">Dean Winters</a> became a part of Allstate's campaign "<a href="/wiki/Mayhem_(advertising_character)" title="Mayhem (advertising character)">Mayhem</a>" “personifying the pitfalls, like collisions and storm damage, that can befall drivers”.
</p><p>In May 2011, Allstate announced that it was purchasing <a href="/wiki/Esurance" title="Esurance">Esurance</a> and rate-comparison site Answer Financial for approximately $1 billion. At the time, Esurance was selling policies in 30 states and was in the midst of a five-year growth period that saw them double the number of policies in force. Allstate, for its part, was losing policy holders to the three major online policy retailers: Esurance, <a href="/wiki/Progressive_Corporation" title="Progressive Corporation">Progressive</a>, and <a href="/wiki/GEICO" title="GEICO">GEICO</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p><p>In 2012 <a href="/wiki/Allstate_Solutions_Private_Limited" class="mw-redirect" title="Allstate Solutions Private Limited">Allstate Solutions Private Limited</a>  (also called Allstate India) was inaugurated in Bangalore which is a technology and operations centre to provides software development and business process outsourcing services to its US parent.<sup id="cite_ref-Allstate's_India_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Allstate's_India-19">[19]</a></sup> Allstate's Bangalore operation is focused on the areas of <a href="/wiki/Business_intelligence" title="Business intelligence">business intelligence</a>, <a href="/wiki/Analytics" title="Analytics">analytics</a>, testing and mobility.
</p><p>In January 2017, Allstate acquired SquareTrade, a consumer electronics and appliance protection plan provider. It was said that the acquisition cost approximately $1.4 billion from a group of shareholders.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p><p>In 2018, Allstate collaborated with Red Cross to distribute 1,000 disaster kits statewide in Hawaii.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> In 2019, Allstate donated $75,000 to the Red Cross and again partnered with the organization to distribute over 2,900 disaster kits in California.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>

<h3 class="in-block"><span id=".22Good_hands.22"></span><span class="mw-headline" id='"Good_hands"'>"Good hands"</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Allstate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title='Edit section: "Good hands"' data-section="6" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>Allstate's slogan "You're in good hands" was created in the 1950s by Allstate Insurance Company's sales executive, Davis W. Ellis based on a similar phrase he used to reassure his wife about a doctor caring for their child. It has been the slogan ever since 1950. Allstate also referred to themselves as the <i>Good Hands People</i>.
</p><p>In the 1960s and 1970s, TV, print and radio advertising featured Allstate's spokesman, Ed Reimers.  Reimers was often shown making the cupped-hand gesture.  For 22 years, he remained the spokesman.<sup id="cite_ref-Los_Angeles_Times_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Los_Angeles_Times-24">[24]</a></sup></p><p>A study in 2000 by Northwestern University's Medill Graduate Department of Integrated Marketing Communications found that the Allstate slogan "You're in good hands" ranked as the most recognizable in America.<sup id="cite_ref-Ad_Slogans_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ad_Slogans-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span id=".22Allstate.27s_logo.22"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="&quot;Allstate's_logo&quot;">"Allstate's logo"</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Allstate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: &quot;Allstate's logo&quot;" data-section="7" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>Allstate's original hands logo was designed by Theodore Conterio in the 1950s. The employees were told whoever could come up with the best logo would win $50. Theodore Conterio came up with the design based on the slogan, "You're in good hands," and won the $50 prize.
</p>

<p>Allstate has stated intentions of reducing its exposure in <a href="/wiki/Hurricane" class="mw-redirect" title="Hurricane">hurricane</a>-prone <a href="/wiki/Florida" title="Florida">Florida</a>. <h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="From_Good_Hands_to_Boxing_Gloves"><i>From Good Hands to Boxing Gloves</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Allstate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=30" title="Edit section: From Good Hands to Boxing Gloves" data-section="30" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>This is the title of a book written by David Berardinelli, JD;, Michael Freeman, PhD, DC, MPH; and Aaron DeShaw, DC, JD with a foreword by <a href="/wiki/Eugene_Anderson" title="Eugene Anderson">Eugene R. Anderson</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup></p><p>The book relates profit-boosting strategies that consulting firm <a href="/wiki/McKinsey_%26_Company" title="McKinsey &amp; Company">McKinsey &amp; Company</a> presented to Allstate to maximize profits and diminish the amount of money sent to clients who put in a claim. McKinsey specializes in redesigning product delivery systems for Fortune 100 companies (including controversial clients such as <a href="/wiki/Enron" title="Enron">Enron</a>) to maximize profits. McKinsey's recommendation to Allstate, according to Berardinelli, was to low-ball claims so that desperate customers in dire straits would be more likely to accept a settlement offer while Allstate continued to make a profit and collect interest on the insurance payment. Allstate would offer its "good hands" in the way of a low-ball claim and, if the customer did not accept, to get out "boxing gloves."<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup></p><p>The book was reviewed by <i>Business Week</i> magazine.<sup id="cite_ref-bw_47-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bw-47">[47]</a></sup>  According to that article, Allstate responded to Berardinelli's allegations by claiming that Berardinelli's allegations were "unfounded and unproven." Rather than trying to cheat customers, the company stated that its major goal was to benefit policyholders by identifying "exaggerated and fraudulent claims" and that its "processes are absolutely sound . . . to investigate, evaluate, and promptly resolve each claim fairly, based on the merits."<sup id="cite_ref-bw_47-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bw-47">[47]</a></sup></p><p>Court decisions on the issues debated by Berardinelli and Allstate have gone both ways. According to the Business Week article, as of 2006, "Courts and regulators in a number of states, including New York, Pennsylvania, and Washington, have forced Allstate to halt or change its practice of handing out a controversial 'Do I Need an Attorney?' form to people involved in accidents." On the other hand, the article also states that "Although plaintiffs have had piecemeal success in bad-faith cases against Allstate, the insurer points to seven court rulings that have rejected attacks on CCPR. Last December a Montana state court noted that while CCPR practices may be illegal 'if misapplied in a particular case, they nevertheless are neutral with no manifestly illegal purpose.'"  While many of the cost-reduction strategies McKinsey recommended at Allstate remain in place, some have been reined in following legal and regulatory challenges in several states.<sup id="cite_ref-bw_47-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bw-47">[47]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Use_of_Colossus">Use of Colossus</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Allstate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=31" title="Edit section: Use of Colossus" data-section="31" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>Many criticisms leveled against Allstate (and other insurers), including Barardinelli's book, involved the use of a software program called "Colossus" to process claims.  In 2010, Allstate paid a $10 million fine to settle a lawsuit brought by 41 states concerning inconsistencies in the manner in which Colossus was used.  It also agreed to standardize its use of the software. However, “it is important to note that we found no systemic underpayment of bodily injury claims,” New York Insurance Superintendent James J. Wrynn said in a press release.<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup></p>
