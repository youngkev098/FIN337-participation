</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(1)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mohawk_Industries&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History" data-section="1" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-1 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-1">
<p>William Shuttleworth and his four sons arrived in the United States in 1875 and set up a carpet mill in the <a href="/wiki/Hudson_Valley" title="Hudson Valley">Hudson Valley</a> upon arrival. After William Shuttleworth died, the four sons moved to <a href="/wiki/Amsterdam,_New_York" class="mw-redirect" title="Amsterdam, New York">Amsterdam, New York</a> in 1878 and took over an empty factory there.<sup id="cite_ref-dailygazette_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dailygazette-6">[6]</a></sup> The company incorporated as the Shuttleworth Brothers Company in 1902.<sup id="cite_ref-funding_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-funding-7">[7]</a></sup></p><p>The company adopted the name Mohawk Carpet Mills (or Mohawk Mills, for short) in 1920, when it merged with McCleary, Wallin and Crouse, another mill in Amsterdam.<sup id="cite_ref-NYTMHS_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NYTMHS-8">[8]</a></sup> It became the country's sole weaver to offer an entire line of domestic carpets, also creating the industry's first textured design and sculptured weave.<sup id="cite_ref-funding_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-funding-7">[7]</a></sup></p><p>In 1956, Mohawk Carpet Mills merged with  Alexander Smith, Inc. to become Mohasco Corporation, a company large enough to appear on the first <a href="/wiki/Fortune_500" title="Fortune 500">Fortune 500</a> rankings. Mohasco faced competition from new tufted carpet operations in Georgia, which used synthetic fibers such as nylon and polyester rather than the traditional wool used in woven carpets. To compete in a changing industry, Mohasco gradually moved manufacturing south into the Carolinas and eventually Georgia. In 1963, the company turned some of its attention to furniture manufacturing.
</p><p>Carpet manufacturing at the Amsterdam site ended by 1968 and the last corporate offices left by 1987.<sup id="cite_ref-dailygazette_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dailygazette-6">[6]</a></sup> In 1988, Mohasco was purchased by MHS Holdings in a leveraged buyout, and the company's carpet business was spun off from Mohasco to form Mohawk Industries.<sup id="cite_ref-NYTMHS_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NYTMHS-8">[8]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Commerce_March_2017_139_(Mohawk_Industries).jpg" class="image"><noscript><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4c/Commerce_March_2017_139_%28Mohawk_Industries%29.jpg/220px-Commerce_March_2017_139_%28Mohawk_Industries%29.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="6000" data-file-height="4000"></noscript><span class="lazy-image-placeholder" style="width: 220px;height: 147px;" data-src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4c/Commerce_March_2017_139_%28Mohawk_Industries%29.jpg/220px-Commerce_March_2017_139_%28Mohawk_Industries%29.jpg" data-alt="" data-width="220" data-height="147" data-class="thumbimage"> </span></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Commerce_March_2017_139_(Mohawk_Industries).jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Mohawk Industries factory in <a href="/wiki/Commerce,_Texas" title="Commerce, Texas">Commerce, Texas</a></div></div></div>
<p>In 1992, Mohawk went public with its shares traded first on the <a href="/wiki/NASDAQ" class="mw-redirect" title="NASDAQ">NASDAQ</a> Stock Exchange under the ticker symbol "NMS" and currently on the <a href="/wiki/New_York_Stock_Exchange" title="New York Stock Exchange">New York Stock Exchange</a> under the ticker symbol "MHK."<sup id="cite_ref-NYTIPO_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NYTIPO-10">[10]</a></sup>
Mohasco's remaining assets were sold to other investors, and the company was later dissolved. Through 2016, Mohawk Industries has acquired 34 companies into its conglomerate, and now operates manufacturing facilities in 15 countries—the U.S., Mexico, Brazil, Ireland, the U.K., Belgium, France, the Netherlands, Luxembourg, Spain, Italy, the Czech Republic, Bulgaria, Russia and Malaysia.<sup id="cite_ref-funding_7-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-funding-7">[7]</a></sup> The company's present Chairman and CEO is <a href="/wiki/Jeffrey_Lorberbaum" title="Jeffrey Lorberbaum">Jeffrey Lorberbaum</a>.
</p><p>Mohawk's open line brands include American Olean, Daltile, Durkan, Eliane, Feltex, Godfrey Hirst, IVC, Karastan, Kerama Marazzi, Marazzi, Moduleo, Mohawk, Mohawk Home, Pergo, Quick-Step, Ragno and Unilin.<sup id="cite_ref-Forbes_2015_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Forbes_2015-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
