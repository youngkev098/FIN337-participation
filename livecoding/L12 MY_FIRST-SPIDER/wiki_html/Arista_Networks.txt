<p><b>Arista Networks</b> (formerly <b>Arastra</b><sup id="cite_ref-name-change_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-name-change-2">[2]</a></sup>) is an American <a href="/wiki/Computer_network" title="Computer network">computer networking</a> company headquartered in <a href="/wiki/Santa_Clara,_California" title="Santa Clara, California">Santa Clara, California</a>, USA.  The company designs and sells <a href="/wiki/Multilayer_switch" title="Multilayer switch">multilayer</a> <a href="/wiki/Network_switch" title="Network switch">network switches</a> to deliver <a href="/wiki/Software-defined_networking" title="Software-defined networking">software-defined networking</a> (SDN) solutions for large <a href="/wiki/Datacenter" class="mw-redirect" title="Datacenter">datacenter</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cloud_computing" title="Cloud computing">cloud computing</a>, <a href="/wiki/Supercomputer" title="Supercomputer">high-performance computing</a>, and <a href="/wiki/High-frequency_trading" title="High-frequency trading">high-frequency trading</a> environments. These products include an array of <a href="/wiki/10_Gigabit_Ethernet" title="10 Gigabit Ethernet">10</a>/25/<a href="/wiki/40_Gigabit_Ethernet" class="mw-redirect" title="40 Gigabit Ethernet">40</a>/50/<a href="/wiki/100_Gigabit_Ethernet" title="100 Gigabit Ethernet">100 Gigabit Ethernet</a> low-<a href="/wiki/Network_delay" title="Network delay">latency</a> <a href="/wiki/Cut-through_switching" title="Cut-through switching">cut-through</a> switches, including the 7124SX,<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> which remained the fastest switch<sup id="cite_ref-lowest-latency_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-lowest-latency-4">[4]</a></sup> using <a href="/wiki/Small_form-factor_pluggable_transceiver#SFP.2B" title="Small form-factor pluggable transceiver">SFP+ optics</a> through September 2012,<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> with its sub-500 <a href="/wiki/Nanosecond" title="Nanosecond">nanosecond</a> (ns) latency, and the 7500 series, Arista’s modular 10G/40G/<a href="/wiki/100_Gigabit_Ethernet" title="100 Gigabit Ethernet">100Gbit</a>/s switch.  Arista's own <a href="/wiki/Linux" title="Linux">Linux</a>-based <a href="/wiki/Network_operating_system" title="Network operating system">network operating system</a>, Extensible Operating System (EOS), runs on all Arista products.

</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(1)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Corporate_history">Corporate history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arista_Networks&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Corporate history" data-section="1" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-1 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-1">
<p>In 1982, <a href="/wiki/Andy_Bechtolsheim" title="Andy Bechtolsheim">Andy Bechtolsheim</a> cofounded <a href="/wiki/Sun_Microsystems" title="Sun Microsystems">Sun Microsystems</a>, and was its chief hardware designer.  In 1995, <a href="/wiki/David_Cheriton" title="David Cheriton">David Cheriton</a> cofounded Granite Systems with Bechtolsheim, a company that developed <a href="/wiki/Gigabit_Ethernet" title="Gigabit Ethernet">Gigabit Ethernet</a> products, which was acquired by <a href="/wiki/Cisco_Systems" title="Cisco Systems">Cisco Systems</a> in 1996.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>  In 2001, Cheriton and Bechtolsheim founded another start up, Kealia,<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> which was acquired by Sun in 2004.  From 1996 to 2003, Bechtolsheim and Cheriton occupied executive positions at Cisco, leading the development of the <a href="/wiki/Cisco_Catalyst" title="Cisco Catalyst">Catalyst</a> product line, along with Kenneth Duda who had been Granite Systems' first employee.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p><p>In 2004, the three then went on to found Arastra (later renamed Arista<sup id="cite_ref-name-change_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-name-change-2">[2]</a></sup>).  Bechtolsheim and Cheriton were able to fund the company themselves.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>  In May 2008, <a href="/wiki/Jayshree_Ullal" title="Jayshree Ullal">Jayshree Ullal</a> left Cisco after 15 years at the firm, and was appointed CEO of Arista in October 2008.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p><p>In June 2014, Arista Networks had its <a href="/wiki/Initial_public_offering" title="Initial public offering">initial public offering</a> on the <a href="/wiki/New_York_Stock_Exchange" title="New York Stock Exchange">New York Stock Exchange</a> under the symbol ANET.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p><p>In December 2014, Cisco filed two lawsuits against Arista alleging intellectual property infringement.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>, and the <a href="/wiki/United_States_International_Trade_Commission" title="United States International Trade Commission">United States International Trade Commission</a> issued limited exclusion and cease-and-desist orders concerning two of the features patented by Cisco<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> and upheld an import ban on infringing products.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup>  In 2016, on appeal, the ban was reversed following product changes and two overturned Cisco patents, and Cisco's claim of damages was ruled against.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> In August 2018, Arista agreed to pay Cisco <span style="white-space: nowrap"><a href="/wiki/United_States_dollar" title="United States dollar">US$</a>400 million</span> as part of a settlement that included a release for all claims of infringement by Cisco, dismissal of Arista's antitrust claims against Cisco, and a 5-year stand-down between the companies.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p><p>In August 2018, Arista Networks acquired Mojo Networks.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p><p>Arista's CEO, Jayshree Ullal, was named to <a href="/wiki/Barron%27s_(newspaper)" title="Barron's (newspaper)">Barron’s</a> list of World’s Best CEOs in 2018 and 2019.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>

<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Extensible_Operating_System">Extensible Operating System</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arista_Networks&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Extensible Operating System" data-section="3" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>

software">Proprietary</a></td></tr><tr><th scope="row" style="white-space: nowrap;">Official website</th><td><span class="url"><a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.arista.com/en/products/eos">www<wbr></wbr>.arista<wbr></wbr>.com<wbr></wbr>/en<wbr></wbr>/products<wbr></wbr>/eos</a></span></td></tr></tbody></table><p>EOS is Arista's <a href="/wiki/Network_operating_system" title="Network operating system">network operating system</a>, and comes as one <a href="/wiki/Boot_image" title="Boot image">image</a> that runs across all Arista devices<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> or in a <a href="/wiki/Virtual_machine" title="Virtual machine">virtual machine</a> (VM).<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup>  EOS runs on an unmodified <a href="/wiki/Linux_kernel" title="Linux kernel">Linux kernel</a> under a <a href="/wiki/Fedora_(operating_system)" title="Fedora (operating system)">Fedora</a>-based <a href="/wiki/Userland_(computing)" class="mw-redirect" title="Userland (computing)">userland</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup>  There are more than 100 independent regular <a href="/wiki/Process_(computing)" title="Process (computing)">processes</a>, called agents, responsible for different aspects and features of the switch, including drivers that manage the switching <a href="/wiki/Application-specific_integrated_circuit" title="Application-specific integrated circuit">application-specific integrated circuit</a> (ASICs), the <a href="/wiki/Command-line_interface" title="Command-line interface">command-line interface</a> (CLI), <a href="/wiki/Simple_Network_Management_Protocol" title="Simple Network Management Protocol">Simple Network Management Protocol</a> (SNMP), <a href="/wiki/Spanning_Tree_Protocol" title="Spanning Tree Protocol">Spanning Tree Protocol</a>, and various routing protocols.  All the state of the switch and its various protocols is centralized in another process, called Sysdb.  Separating processing (carried by the agents) from the state (in Sysdb) gives EOS two important properties.  The first is software fault containment, which means that if a software fault occurs, any damage is limited to one agent.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-eos_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-eos-24">[24]</a></sup>  The second is stateful restarts, since the state is stored in Sysdb, when an agent restarts it picks up where it left off.<sup id="cite_ref-eos_24-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-eos-24">[24]</a></sup>  Since agents are independent processes, they can also be upgraded while the switch is running (a feature called ISSU – In-Service Software Upgrade).
</p><p>The fact that EOS runs on Linux allows the usage of common Linux tools on the switch itself, such as <a href="/wiki/Tcpdump" title="Tcpdump">tcpdump</a> or configuration management systems.  EOS provides extensive <a href="/wiki/Application_programming_interface" title="Application programming interface">application programming interfaces</a> (APIs) to communicate with and control all aspects of the switch. To showcase EOS' extensibility, Arista developed a module named CloudVision<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> that extends the CLI to use Extensible Messaging and Presence Protocol (<a href="/wiki/XMPP" title="XMPP">XMPP</a>) as a shared message bus to manage and configure switches.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup>  This was implemented simply by integrating an existing open-source XMPP Python library with the CLI.
</p>
<h4 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Programmability">Programmability</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arista_Networks&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Programmability" data-section="4" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h4>
<p>In addition to all the standard programming and scripting abilities traditionally available in a Linux environment, EOS can be programmed using different mechanisms:
</p>
<ul><li>Advanced Event Management<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> can be used to react to various events and automatically trigger CLI commands, execute arbitrary scripts or send alerts when state changes occur in the switch, such as an interface going down or a virtual machine <a href="/wiki/Live_migration" title="Live migration">migrating</a> to another host.</li>
<li>Event Monitor tracks changes made to the <a href="/wiki/Medium_access_control" title="Medium access control">medium access control</a> (MAC), <a href="/wiki/Address_Resolution_Protocol" title="Address Resolution Protocol">Address Resolution Protocol</a> (ARP), and <a href="/wiki/Routing_table" title="Routing table">routing table</a> in a local <a href="/wiki/SQLite" title="SQLite">SQLite</a> database for later querying using standard Structured Query Language (<a href="/wiki/SQL" title="SQL">SQL</a>) queries.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></li>
<li>eAPI (External API)<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> offers a versioned <a href="/wiki/JSON-RPC" title="JSON-RPC">JSON-RPC</a> interface to execute CLI commands and retrieve their output in structured <a href="/wiki/JSON" title="JSON">JSON</a> objects.</li></ul><h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Ethernet_switches">Ethernet switches</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arista_Networks&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Ethernet switches" data-section="5" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>Arista's product line can be separated into different product families:
</p>
<ul><li>7500R series: Modular chassis with a <a href="/wiki/Virtual_output_queueing" title="Virtual output queueing">virtual output queueing</a> (VOQ) fabric supporting from 4 to 16 <a href="/wiki/Store_and_forward" title="Store and forward">store and forward</a> <a href="/wiki/Line_card" title="Line card">line cards</a> delivering line-rate non-blocking 10GbE, 40GbE, and 100GbE<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> performance in a 150 Tbit/s fabric supporting a maximum of 576 100GbE ports with 384 GB of packet buffer.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup>  Each 100GbE ports can also operate as 40GbE or 4x10GbE ports,<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> thus effectively providing 2304 line-rate 10GbE ports with large routing tables.</li>
<li>7300X, 7300X3 and 7320X series: Modular chassis with 4 or 8 line cards in a choice of 10G, 40G and 100G options with 6.4Tbit/s of capacity per line card, for a fabric totaling up to 50Tbit/s of capacity for up to 1024 10GbE ports.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> Unlike the 7500 series, <a href="/wiki/10GBASE-T" class="mw-redirect" title="10GBASE-T">10GBASE-T</a> is available on 7300 series line cards.</li>
<li>7280R series: 1U and 2U systems with a common architecture<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> to the 7500R Series,<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> deep buffer VOQ and large routing tables. Many different speed and port combinations from 10GbE to 100GbE.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></li>
<li>7200X series: 2U low-latency high-density line-rate 100GbE and 40GbE switches, with up to 12.8Tbit/s of forwarding capacity.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup></li>
<li>7170 Series: High Performance Multi-function Programmable Platforms,<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> a set of fixed 100G platforms based on Barefoot<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> Tofino<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> packet processor enabling the data plane to be customized using EOS and P4 profiles.</li>
<li>7160 series: 1U programmable high performance range of 10 GbE, 25 GbE and 100 GbE with the support for AlgoMatch<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> technology and a software upgradeable packet processor</li></ul><ol><li>7150S series: 1U ultra-low latency <a href="/wiki/Cut-through_switching" title="Cut-through switching">cut-through</a> line-rate 10 Gb switches.  Port-to-port latency is sub-380ns, regardless of the frame size.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup>  Unlike the earlier 7100 series, the switch silicon can be re-programmed<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> to add new features that work at wire-speed, such as <a href="/wiki/Virtual_Extensible_LAN" title="Virtual Extensible LAN">Virtual Extensible LAN</a> (VXLAN) or <a href="/wiki/Network_address_translation" title="Network address translation">network address translation</a> (NAT/PAT).</li></ol><ul><li>7050X and 7060X series: 1U and 2U low-latency <a href="/wiki/Cut-through_switching" title="Cut-through switching">cut-through</a> line-rate 10GbE/25GbE, 40GbE and 100GbE switches.  This product line offers higher port density than the 7150 series, in a wider choice of port options and interface speeds at the expense of slightly increased latency (1µs or less). The 7050X and 7060X Series are based on Broadcom Trident and Tomahawk merchant silicon.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup></li>
<li>7020R series: 1U <a href="/wiki/Store_and_forward" title="Store and forward">store and forward</a> line-rate with a choice of either a 1Gb top-of-rack switch, with 6x10Gb uplinks or a 10G with 100G uplinks.  These switches use a Deep Buffer architecture, with 3GB of packet memory.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup></li>
<li>7010 series: 1U low power (52W) line-rate 1Gb top-of-rack switch, with 4x10Gb uplinks.<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup></li></ul><p>The low-latency of Arista switches has made the platform prevalent in <a href="/wiki/High-frequency_trading" title="High-frequency trading">high-frequency trading</a> environments, such as the <a href="/wiki/Chicago_Board_Options_Exchange" title="Chicago Board Options Exchange">Chicago Board Options Exchange</a><sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> (largest U.S. options exchange), <a href="/wiki/Lehman_Brothers" title="Lehman Brothers">Lehman Brothers</a><sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> or <a href="/wiki/RBC_Capital_Markets" title="RBC Capital Markets">RBC Capital Markets</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup>  As of October 2009, one third of its customers were big <a href="/wiki/Wall_Street" title="Wall Street">Wall Street</a> firms.<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup></p><p>Arista's devices are <a href="/wiki/Multilayer_switch" title="Multilayer switch">multilayer switches</a>, which support a range of <a href="/wiki/Layer_3" class="mw-redirect" title="Layer 3">layer 3</a> protocols,<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup> including <a href="/wiki/IGMP" class="mw-redirect" title="IGMP">IGMP</a>, <a href="/wiki/Virtual_Router_Redundancy_Protocol" title="Virtual Router Redundancy Protocol">Virtual Router Redundancy Protocol</a> (VRRP), <a href="/wiki/Routing_Information_Protocol" title="Routing Information Protocol">Routing Information Protocol</a> (RIP), <a href="/wiki/Border_Gateway_Protocol" title="Border Gateway Protocol">Border Gateway Protocol</a> (BGP), <a href="/wiki/Open_Shortest_Path_First" title="Open Shortest Path First">Open Shortest Path First</a> (OSPF), <a href="/wiki/IS-IS" title="IS-IS">IS-IS</a>, and <a href="/wiki/OpenFlow" title="OpenFlow">OpenFlow</a>.  The switches are also capable of layer 3 or <a href="/wiki/Layer_4" class="mw-redirect" title="Layer 4">layer 4</a> <a href="/wiki/Equal-cost_multi-path_routing" title="Equal-cost multi-path routing">equal-cost multi-path routing</a> (ECMP), and applying per-port L3/L4 <a href="/wiki/Access-control_list" title="Access-control list">access-control lists</a> (ACLs) entirely in hardware.
</p><p>All of Arista's switches are built using merchant silicon instead of custom switching <a href="/wiki/Application-specific_integrated_circuit" title="Application-specific integrated circuit">application-specific integrated circuits</a> (ASICs).  This strategy enables Arista to leverage latest advances in <a href="/wiki/Semiconductor_device_fabrication" title="Semiconductor device fabrication">processor manufacturing technology</a> at a lower price, due to the prohibitive costs associated with developing and producing custom chips.  Other major competitors such as <a href="/wiki/Cisco_Systems" title="Cisco Systems">Cisco Systems</a> and <a href="/wiki/Juniper_Networks" title="Juniper Networks">Juniper Networks</a> have also started using the same strategy,<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup> which led to multiple competing products built on top of the same chips.  For instance <a href="/wiki/Broadcom" class="mw-redirect" title="Broadcom">Broadcom</a>'s Trident chip is used in some <a href="/wiki/Cisco_Nexus_switches" title="Cisco Nexus switches">Cisco Nexus switches</a>, Juniper QFX switches, Force10, <a href="/wiki/IBM" title="IBM">IBM</a> and HP switches.<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup>  The integration of the chips with the rest of the system (including integration with the <a href="/wiki/Medium_access_control" title="Medium access control">medium access control</a> (MAC), physical layer (<a href="/wiki/PHY" title="PHY">PHY</a>), and device drivers on the <a href="/wiki/Control_plane" title="Control plane">control plane</a>) and software are what differentiate the competing products.
</p><p>In November 2013, Arista Networks introduced the Spline network, combining leaf and spine architectures into a single-tier network, which aims to cut operating costs.<sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup></p><p>In September 2015, Arista introduced the series 7060X, 7260X, and 7320X, refreshing then extant series 7050X, 7250X, and 7300X, with new, higher performance 100 GbE options.
</p>

<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Extreme_Networks" title="Extreme Networks">Extreme Networks</a></li>
