<p><b>Exelon Corporation</b> is an American <a href="/wiki/Fortune_100" class="mw-redirect" title="Fortune 100">Fortune 100</a> <a href="/wiki/Energy_industry" title="Energy industry">energy company</a> headquartered in <a href="/wiki/Chicago" title="Chicago">Chicago</a>, Illinois. It generates revenues of approximately $33.5 billion and employs approximately 33,400 people.<sup id="cite_ref-Exelon_About_Us_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Exelon_About_Us-2">[2]</a></sup> Exelon is the largest electric parent company in the United States by revenue, the largest regulated utility in the United States with approximately 10 million customers, and also the largest operator of nuclear power plants in the United States.<sup id="cite_ref-abcnews.go.com_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-abcnews.go.com-3">[3]</a></sup> It was created in October 2000 by the merger of <a href="/wiki/PECO_Energy_Company" title="PECO Energy Company">PECO Energy Company</a> of Philadelphia and <a href="/wiki/Unicom_Corp" title="Unicom Corp">Unicom Corp</a> of Chicago, which owned <a href="/wiki/Commonwealth_Edison" title="Commonwealth Edison">Commonwealth Edison</a>.<sup </section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(1)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Exelon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History" data-section="1" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-1 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-1">
<p>Exelon Corporation was created as the result of a merger between PECO Energy Company and the Unicom Corporation in October 2000.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-4">[4]</a></sup> Unicom was based in Chicago and the city became the home of the new entity.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> The merger was overseen by the CEO of Unicom, John Rowe who joined the corporation in 1998 and went on to lead the newly-formed Exelon until 2012, becoming the nation's longest-serving utility executive.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:0_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-4">[4]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:1_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-14">[14]</a></sup></p><p>Under Rowe, Exelon explored a number of mergers and acquisitions. On June 30, 2005, the <a href="/wiki/Federal_Energy_Regulatory_Commission" title="Federal Energy Regulatory Commission">Federal Energy Regulatory Commission</a> approved the merger of Exelon and <a href="/wiki/Public_Service_Enterprise_Group_Inc." class="mw-redirect" title="Public Service Enterprise Group Inc.">Public Service Enterprise Group Inc.</a>, a <a href="/wiki/New_Jersey" title="New Jersey">New Jersey</a> utility. Under this merger, Exelon would have become the largest utility in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> The two companies later broke off the agreement<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-16">[16]</a></sup> due to pressure put on the NJ Board of Public Utilities by public interest groups, including New Jersey Citizen Action.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> The merger sat pending in front of the NJBPU for nineteen months before Exelon concluded that they were fighting a losing battle.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_16-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-16">[16]</a></sup> On April 28, 2011, Exelon announced a merger with <a href="/wiki/Constellation_Energy" class="mw-redirect" title="Constellation Energy">Constellation Energy</a> for $7.9 billion with the combined company owning more than 34 gigawatts of power generation (55 percent nuclear, 24 percent natural gas, 8 percent renewable including hydro, 7 percent oil and 6 percent coal).<sup id="cite_ref-hydro280411_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hydro280411-18">[18]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> The merger was completed on March 12, 2012.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p><p>In 2012, when announcing the cancellation of new nuclear construction for <a href="/wiki/Victoria_County_Station" title="Victoria County Station">Victoria County Station</a>, Texas, Exelon stated that economic and market conditions, especially low natural gas prices, made the "construction of new merchant nuclear power plants in competitive markets uneconomical now and for the foreseeable future".<sup id="cite_ref-nei-20120829_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nei-20120829-21">[21]</a></sup></p><p>In 2008, Christopher Crane was named CEO following Rowe's retirement and the completion of the merger with Constellation Energy.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:1_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-14">[14]</a></sup> Exelon announced the proposed purchase of <a href="/wiki/Pepco_Holdings" title="Pepco Holdings">Pepco Holdings</a>, Inc on April 30, 2014, for $6.8 billion in an all-cash transaction. The merger was rejected by the <a href="/wiki/District_of_Columbia_Public_Service_Commission" title="District of Columbia Public Service Commission">District of Columbia Public Service Commission</a> in August 2015, though it was approved by other federal and state regulators. The companies appealed the decision.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup>  On March 23, 2016, the merger was approved by the Washington DC Public Service Commission, under a revised set of terms. The merger was completed later that day making Exelon the largest regulated utility in the United States by customer count and total revenue.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Controversies">Controversies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Exelon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Controversies" data-section="2" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/PECO_Energy" class="mw-redirect" title="PECO Energy">PECO Energy</a>, one of Exelon's local power companies, has been involved in two controversies with activists. In the 1970s, activists delayed the opening of nuclear power plants.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> In 2015, Earth Quaker Action Team began a campaign to pressure PECO to expand the solar power it purchases, and to purchase it locally to create jobs.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p><p>Exelon's merger with <a href="/wiki/Pepco" title="Pepco">Pepco</a> faced stiff opposition from community groups and the D.C. Mayor <a href="/wiki/Muriel_Bowser" title="Muriel Bowser">Muriel Bowser</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>

<p>Exelon Generation is the corporation's energy provider. It has two primary business units, Exelon Nuclear and Exelon Power.<sup id="cite_ref-:2_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:2-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<h4 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Exelon_Nuclear">Exelon Nuclear</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Exelon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Exelon Nuclear" data-section="6" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h4>
<p>Exelon is a leading nuclear power plant operator in the United States with over 19,000 megawatts through Exelon Nuclear Partners, a division of Exelon Generation.<sup id="cite_ref-Carney_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Carney-31">[31]</a></sup></p>

<h4 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Exelon_Power">Exelon Power</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Exelon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Exelon Power" data-section="7" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h4>

<h4 class="in-block"><span id="Exelon_PowerLabs.2C_LLC"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Exelon_PowerLabs,_LLC"><a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://www.exelonpowerlabs.com/">Exelon PowerLabs, LLC</a></span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Exelon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Exelon PowerLabs, LLC" data-section="12" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h4>
<p>Exelon PowerLabs is a wholly owned subsidiary of Exelon Corporation. Since 1911, PowerLabs has transformed over the years from being a support to the power industry, to becoming the primary calibrations and testing laboratory for Exelon. It has four individual labs strategically located from the upper-Midwest to the Northeast, enabling experienced staff in engineering, metrology, and nuclear power generation to support the urgent demands of our nation’s nuclear facilities, power grids, and critical supply chains.
</p><p>As an approved nuclear supplier, its Quality Program satisfies the requirements in 1<a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://www.exelonpowerlabs.com/calibration-services/appendix-b-calibrations/">0 CFR 50; Appendix B, 10 CFR 21, NQA-1, and ANSI N45.2</a>. It is routinely audited by the Nuclear Procurement Issues Committee (NUPIC) and the Nuclear Industry Assessment Committee (NIAC) — whose results are the basis for evaluation as a supplier to the nuclear industry.
</p><p>Due to the breadth of its experience in the nuclear industry, it has amassed an extensive inventory of lab equipment to perform a full- spectrum of calibration, testing, and analysis services. We also maintain <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://www.exelonpowerlabs.com/calibration-services/17025-accredited-calibrations/">A2LA Accreditation</a>, assuring technical competence as a laboratory to ISO 17025 standards and extending services to any quality-driven or highly regulated industry.

<p>Exelon's <a href="/wiki/Political_action_committee" title="Political action committee">political action committee</a> (PAC) is EXELONPAC.<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup> Since divesting from coal, the company is better positioned than many of its competitors to benefit from carbon pricing under a <a href="/wiki/Cap_and_trade" class="mw-redirect" title="Cap and trade">cap and trade</a> plan for reducing carbon dioxide emissions.<sup id="cite_ref-Windfall_56-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Windfall-56">[56]</a></sup> "[Former] Exelon CEO <a href="/wiki/John_Rowe_(CEO)_Exelon" class="mw-redirect" title="John Rowe (CEO) Exelon">John Rowe</a> is a vociferous and longtime advocate of climate change legislation. In 2009, <i><a href="/wiki/Forbes_magazine" class="mw-redirect" title="Forbes magazine">Forbes</a></i> reported that if the <a href="/wiki/American_Clean_Energy_and_Security_Act" title="American Clean Energy and Security Act">Waxman-Markey</a> climate legislation became law, 'the present value of Exelon's earnings stream would increase by $14 a share, or 28%.'"<sup id="cite_ref-Carney_31-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Carney-31">[31]</a></sup> Exelon also disclosed multiple contributions to political nonprofit organizations, the largest of which was $290,000 given to the <a href="/wiki/American_Energy_Alliance" class="mw-redirect" title="American Energy Alliance">American Energy Alliance</a> – a 501(c)(4) nonprofit with ties to the conservative billionaire brothers Charles and David Koch and led by former <a href="/wiki/Koch_Industries" title="Koch Industries">Koch Industries</a> lobbyist Thomas Pyle.

<p>In 2005, Exelon was required to pay a $602,000 fine for exceeding the permitted <a href="/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide" title="Sulfur dioxide">sulfur dioxide</a> emission limit from April to October 2004 at its Cromby Generating Station in <a href="/wiki/Chester_County,_Pennsylvania" title="Chester County, Pennsylvania">Chester County, Pennsylvania</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup></p><p>Exelon and Illinois state officials waited for four years until 2006 before disclosing that Exelon's <a href="/wiki/Braidwood_Nuclear_Generating_Station" title="Braidwood Nuclear Generating Station">Braidwood Nuclear Generating Station</a>, a nuclear plant 60 miles southwest of Chicago, had spilled millions of gallons of water containing <a href="/wiki/Tritium" title="Tritium">tritium</a>, a radioactive form of hydrogen, multiple times over a decade.  Exelon officials eventually apologized and said the risks from the leak were "minimal", with tritium levels in surrounding wells all found to be below regulatory limits.<sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-59" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup></p><p>In 2009, the <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_Regulatory_Commission" title="Nuclear Regulatory Commission">Nuclear Regulatory Commission</a> announced its plan for a $65,000 fine against Exelon for permitting its contracted security guards that were guarding its <a href="/wiki/Peach_Bottom_Nuclear_Generating_Station" title="Peach Bottom Nuclear Generating Station">Peach Bottom Nuclear Generating Station</a>, a two-reactor nuclear plant located in Delta, Pennsylvania, to sleep on the job. The incidents did not come to light until a videotape of the security guards was leaked to news media.<sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup> As a result, Exelon terminated the security contract of the <a href="/wiki/Wackenhut" class="mw-redirect" title="Wackenhut">Wackenhut</a> security firm that had been involved and now operates its own in-house nuclear security force.<sup id="cite_ref-61" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup></p>
