</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(1)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Company_overview">Company overview</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=C._H._Robinson&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Company overview" data-section="1" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-1 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-1">
<p>The company is headquartered in Eden Prairie, MN, with more than 300 offices and over 15,000 employees<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> in North America, Europe, Asia, and South America.<sup id="cite_ref-Times_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Times-8">[8]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Reuters2_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Reuters2-9">[9]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Produce_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Produce-12">[12]</a></sup> The company has contractual relationships with over 66,000 transportation companies, including motor carriers, railroads, air freight, and ocean carriers.<sup id="cite_ref-:3_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:3-13">[13]</a></sup> The company also provides services including supply chain analysis, freight consolidation, core carrier program management, and information reporting.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> C.H. Robinson also has a brand, <a href="/wiki/Robinson_Fresh" title="Robinson Fresh">Robinson Fresh</a>, through which it engages in buying, selling, and marketing fresh produce and products. Robinson Fresh buys and sells fresh produce to grocery retailers, restaurants, produce wholesalers, and foodservice distributors through a network of independent produce growers and suppliers.<sup id="cite_ref-Forbes2_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Forbes2-2">[2]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Yahoo_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Yahoo-4">[4]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Business_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Business-5">[5]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-CNN1_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CNN1-7">[7]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Gale2_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gale2-15">[15]</a></sup></p><p>C.H. Robinson handled 14.3 million shipments and worked with more than 46,000 customers in 2014.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-16">[16]</a></sup> C.H. Robinson's transportation of goods accounts for about 94% of its gross net revenues with the remainder coming from sourcing and marketing fresh produce.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span id="Workplace_.26_employer_accolades"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Workplace_&amp;_employer_accolades">Workplace &amp; employer accolades</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=C._H._Robinson&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Workplace &amp; employer accolades" data-section="2" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>The company has consistently been recognized for its workplace and employee benefits, and was named the No. 2 Large Company in the 2014 Best Places to Work ranking by the Minneapolis/St. Paul Business Journal. C.H. Robinson has been named a Top 10000 Large Company in Minneapolis/St. Paul Business Journal’s Best Places to Work rankings every year since 2006. In 2013, the company earned a spot on Top Workplaces’ Top 150–National Top Workplaces 2013.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> The Star Tribune has recognized C.H. Robinson as a Top Workplace in several of the past 10 years, and in 2014 recognized the company as a “workplace achiever.”<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p><p>The company’s work with nonprofits and other charity and community causes has also earned numerous recognitions, including selection as a finalist for the Minnesota Business Magazine 2015 Long-Term Achievement Award for “demonstrating a long-term commitment (at least 10 years) to positively impacting the state’s community of nonprofits or other worthy causes.”<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> In 2014, Minnesota Business Magazine awarded its Workplace Giving Campaign Award to C.H. Robinson. The company also received a Hunger Hero Award from Second Harvest Heartland in 2014, recognizing C.H. Robinson’s “compassionate and transformational impact on those who are hungry in the heartland.”<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(2)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=C._H._Robinson&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: History" data-section="3" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-2 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-2">
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Origins_and_early_history">Origins and early history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=C._H._Robinson&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Origins and early history" data-section="4" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>In the early 1900s, <b>C</b>harles <b>H</b>enry Robinson owned a small wholesale brokerage house that provided produce throughout North Dakota and Minnesota.<sup id="cite_ref-ILCSO_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ILCSO-22">[22]</a></sup> He partnered with the Nash Finch brothers on April 11, 1905, and became the company’s first president.<sup id="cite_ref-Produce_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Produce-12">[12]</a></sup> Nash Finch Company was the leading wholesaler in the region, owning and operating grocery stores.<sup id="cite_ref-Gale_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gale-3">[3]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> Following Charles Henry Robinson’s death in 1909, the Nash Brothers assumed control of the C.H. Robinson Company.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p><p>C.H. Robinson became the procurement arm for Nash Finch Company as it rapidly expanded in Iowa, Minnesota, Wisconsin, Illinois, and Texas. In the 1940s, the FTC found Nash Finch Company to have a price advantage, and under the Robinson-Patman Act of 1936, C.H. Robinson Co. was split into two companies. The first, C.H. Robinson Co., was formed by the offices that sold produce to Nash Finch’s warehouses, and ownership was retained by C.H. Robinson employees. The second company, C.H. Robinson, Inc., was still owned by Nash Finch.<sup id="cite_ref-Gale_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gale-3">[3]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Produce_12-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Produce-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Expansion_into_logistics_and_trucking">Expansion into logistics and trucking</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=C._H._Robinson&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Expansion into logistics and trucking" data-section="5" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>C.H. Robinson’s entry into the trucking business came after the Federal Highway Act of 1956 expanded interstate commerce and the roadways of America.<sup id="cite_ref-Gale2_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gale2-15">[15]</a></sup> C.H. Robinson and other shippers had previously relied on trains to transport goods. In 1968, the firm entered the regulated truck business as a contract carrier named Meat Packers Express based in Omaha, Nebraska.<sup id="cite_ref-Gale_3-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gale-3">[3]</a></sup> Robco Transportation Inc. was formed by merging Meat Packers Express with additional carriers three years later and was sold in 1986.<sup id="cite_ref-Produce_12-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Produce-12">[12]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-ILCSO_22-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ILCSO-22">[22]</a></sup></p><p>In the mid-1960s, C.H. Robinson Co. and C.H. Robinson, Inc., consolidated their operations under the name C.H. Robinson Co. Nash Finch still held a 25% stake in the brokerage company, with C.H. Robinson employees owning the remainder. By 1976, the Nash Finch shares had been bought out and the company was 100% employee owned. C.H. Robinson focused on using emerging technology, and adopted IBM mainframes in 1979.<sup id="cite_ref-Produce_12-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Produce-12">[12]</a></sup></p><p>The Motor Carrier Act of 1980 deregulated transportation industries in America and increased competition for logistics providers and shippers.<sup id="cite_ref-Gale2_15-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gale2-15">[15]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-ILCSO_22-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ILCSO-22">[22]</a></sup> C.H. Robinson created a contract carrier program, expanded its freight contract operations, and established itself as a middleman sourcing operation for virtually all shippable goods. The company’s average annual growth, measured by truckloads, doubled and C.H. Robinson posted more than $700 million in sale within five years. Forty per cent was generated by truck brokerage, with the remainder of revenue coming from produce sales.<sup id="cite_ref-Gale_3-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gale-3">[3]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Produce_12-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Produce-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Renaming_and_IPO">Renaming and IPO</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=C._H._Robinson&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Renaming and IPO" data-section="6" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>The company renamed itself C.H. Robinson Worldwide, Inc., in 1997 and had an IPO that raised $190 million for the 101 employees who sold their shares.<sup id="cite_ref-Gale_3-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gale-3">[3]</a></sup> The initial market value totaled $743 million, and the firm began trading on NASDAQ under the symbol CHRW.<sup id="cite_ref-:2_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:2-25">[25]</a></sup> Gross revenues for 1997 reached $1.79 billion, while net revenues amounted to $206 million, a 15.1% increase over the previous year.<sup id="cite_ref-Gale_3-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gale-3">[3]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Produce_12-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Produce-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(3)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Acquisitions">Acquisitions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=C._H._Robinson&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Acquisitions" data-section="7" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-3 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-3">
<p>Beginning in 1989, C.H. Robinson began expanding its international logistics operations with the opening of its Monterrey, Mexico office. It acquired C.S. Greene International in 1992 to add international freight forwarding, air freight operations, and refrigerated containers. In 1993, C.H. Robinson bought a 30% stake in Transeco, a major French motor carrier, and eventually bought the entire company. C.H. Robinson enhanced its operations by purchasing companies including Daystar International, a distributor of fruit juice, and FoodSource. It also became an exclusive marketer for Tropicana, Motts, Glory and Welch’s.
</p><p>The company continued expanding its logistics services by purchasing regional logistics firms like the Chicago-based American Backhaulers, Inc., for $136 million in 1999. Also acquired the Minnesota-based Trans-Consolidated Inc.<sup id="cite_ref-Gale_3-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gale-3">[3]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Produce_12-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Produce-12">[12]</a></sup></p><p>At the end of the 1990s, the company began making targeted acquisitions to broaden its global presence and position it to better deliver shipping and logistics services in international markets. These acquisitions include: Preferred Translocation Systems; the Argentina-based Comexter Group; the Western European transportation provider Norminter; New York-based Vertex Transportation Inc.; Smith Terminal International Services, one of the largest third-party logistics providers in Florida; the Germany-based international freight logistics provider Frank M. Viet GmbH Internationale Spedition; Dalian Decheng Shipping Agency Co.; FoodSource Procurement LLC; Apreo Logistics S.A.; and other major European, Indian, Chinese, and North American logistics providers.<sup id="cite_ref-Gale_3-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gale-3">[3]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Reuters_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Reuters-6">[6]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-CNN1_7-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CNN1-7">[7]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Produce_12-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Produce-12">[12]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:4_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:4-29">[29]</a></sup></p><p>In 2012, C.H Robinson purchased Phoenix International for $635 million and doubled its ocean freight capacity.<sup id="cite_ref-Reuters_6-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Reuters-6">[6]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> The company also acquired the Polish shipping firm Apreo Logisitics S.A., which provides trucking, air, and ocean shipping services throughout Europe.<sup id="cite_ref-:4_29-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:4-29">[29]</a></sup></p><p>The company launched a technology-enabled platform called Navisphere in 2012. The service provides facilities to  C.H. Robinson employees, customers, and service providers to manage supply chain logistics, transportation, and sourcing activities on a global scale.<sup id="cite_ref-:3_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:3-13">[13]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p><p>TMC, a division of C.H. Robinson,<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> provides support for C.H. Robinson’s network through its Managed TMS a combination of global transportation management system (TMS) software and logistics process management.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p><p>To connect its shipping and logistics services with the global e-commerce market and expand its opportunities in less than truckload markets, the company acquired <a href="/wiki/Freightquote" title="Freightquote">Freightquote</a>, a privately owned online transportation broker that provides shipping rate comparisons for national and regional freight carriers, in 2015.<sup id="cite_ref-:4_29-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:4-29">[29]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
