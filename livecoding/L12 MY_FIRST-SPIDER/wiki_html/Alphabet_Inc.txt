class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sec-2">[2]</a></sup> and became the parent company of Google and several former Google <a href="/wiki/Subsidiary" title="Subsidiary">subsidiaries</a>.<sup Page</a> serving as CEO and <a href="/wiki/Sergey_Brin" title="Sergey Brin">Sergey Brin</a> as president.<sup id="cite_ref-bloomberg2015_6-0" class="reference"><a </section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(1)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alphabet_Inc.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History" data-section="1" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-1 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-1">
<p>On August 10, 2015, <a href="/wiki/Google" title="Google">Google Inc.</a> announced plans to create a new public <a href="/wiki/Holding_company" title="Holding company">holding company</a>, Alphabet Inc. Google CEO <a href="/wiki/Larry_Page" title="Larry Page">Larry Page</a> made this announcement in a blog post on Google's official blog.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Alphabet would be created to restructure Google by moving subsidiaries from Google to Alphabet, narrowing Google's scope. The company would consist of Google as well as other businesses including <a href="/wiki/X_(company)" title="X (company)">X Development</a>, <a href="/wiki/Calico_(company)" title="Calico (company)">Calico</a>, <a href="/wiki/Google_Nest" title="Google Nest">Nest</a>, <a href="/wiki/Verily" title="Verily">Verily</a>, Fiber, <a href="/wiki/Makani_(company)" title="Makani (company)">Makani</a>, <a href="/wiki/CapitalG" title="CapitalG">CapitalG</a>, and <a href="/wiki/GV_(company)" title="GV (company)">GV</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-bloomberg2015_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bloomberg2015-6">[6]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Sundar_Pichai" title="Sundar Pichai">Sundar Pichai</a>, Product Chief, became the new CEO of Google, replacing <a href="/wiki/Larry_Page" title="Larry Page">Larry Page</a>, who transitioned to the role of running Alphabet, along with Google co-founder Sergey Brin.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-NYT_2015_Dougherty_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NYT_2015_Dougherty-15">[15]</a></sup></p><p>In his announcement, Page described the planned holding company as follows:<sup id="cite_ref-gIsForGoogle_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gIsForGoogle-4">[4]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:0_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r886047036">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>Alphabet is mostly a collection of companies. The largest of which, of course, is Google. This newer Google is a bit slimmed down, with the companies that are pretty far afield of our main internet products contained in Alphabet instead. ... Fundamentally, we believe this allows us more management scale, as we can run things independently that aren't very related.</p></blockquote>
<p>Page says the motivation behind the reorganization is to make Google "cleaner and more accountable and better". He also said he wanted to improve "the transparency and oversight of what we're doing", and to allow greater control of unrelated companies.<sup id="cite_ref-gIsForGoogle_4-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gIsForGoogle-4">[4]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-wire-2015-new-company_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wire-2015-new-company-9">[9]</a></sup></p><p>Former executive Eric Schmidt (now Technical Advisor) revealed in the conference in 2017 the inspiration for this structure came from Warren Buffett and his management structure of Berkshire Hathaway a decade ago.<sup id="cite_ref-:1_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-17">[17]</a></sup> Schmidt said it was he who encouraged Page and Brin to meet with Buffett in Omaha to see how Berkshire Hathaway was a holding company made of subsidiaries with strong CEOs who were trusted to run their businesses.<sup id="cite_ref-:1_17-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-17">[17]</a></sup></p><p>Before it became a subsidiary of Alphabet, Google Inc. was first structured as the owner of Alphabet. The roles were reversed after a placeholder subsidiary was created for the ownership of Alphabet, at which point the newly formed subsidiary was merged with Google. Google's stock was then converted to Alphabet's stock. Under the <a href="/wiki/Delaware_General_Corporation_Law" title="Delaware General Corporation Law">Delaware General Corporation Law</a> (where Alphabet is incorporated), a holding company reorganization such as this can be done without a vote of shareholders, as this reorganization was.<sup id="cite_ref-SEC_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SEC-18">[18]</a></sup> The restructuring process was completed on October 2, 2015.<sup id="cite_ref-sec_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sec-2">[2]</a></sup> Alphabet retains Google Inc.'s stock price history and continues to trade under Google Inc.'s former ticker symbols "GOOG" and "GOOGL"; both classes of stock are components of major <a href="/wiki/Stock_market_index" title="Stock market index">stock market indices</a> such as the <a href="/wiki/S%26P_500" class="mw-redirect" title="S&amp;P 500">S&amp;P 500</a> and <a href="/wiki/NASDAQ-100" title="NASDAQ-100">NASDAQ-100</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p><p>On December 3, 2019, Page and Brin jointly announced that they would step down from their respective roles, remaining as employees and still the majority vote on the board of directors. <a href="/wiki/Sundar_Pichai" title="Sundar Pichai">Sundar Pichai</a>, the CEO of Google, is to assume the CEO role at Alphabet while retaining the same at Google.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(2)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Structure">Structure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alphabet_Inc.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Structure" data-section="2" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-2 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-2">
<p>Alphabet's largest subsidiary is Google. Its other subsidiaries include <a href="/wiki/Calico_(company)" title="Calico (company)">Calico</a>, <a href="/wiki/DeepMind" title="DeepMind">DeepMind</a>, <a href="/wiki/GV_(company)" title="GV (company)">GV</a>, <a href="/wiki/Google_Capital" class="mw-redirect" title="Google Capital">CapitalG</a>, <a href="/wiki/X_(company)" title="X (company)">X</a>, <a href="/wiki/Google_Fiber" title="Google Fiber">Google Fiber</a>, <a href="/wiki/Makani_(company)" title="Makani (company)">Makani</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sidewalk_Labs" title="Sidewalk Labs">Sidewalk Labs</a>, <a href="/wiki/Verily_Life_Sciences" class="mw-redirect" title="Verily Life Sciences">Verily</a>, <a href="/wiki/Waymo" title="Waymo">Waymo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wing_(company)" title="Wing (company)">Wing</a> and <a href="/wiki/Loon_LLC" title="Loon LLC">Loon</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> As of September 1, 2017<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Alphabet_Inc.&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, their equity is held by a subsidiary known as <b>XXVI Holdings, Inc.</b> (referring to the Roman numeral of 26, the number of letters in the alphabet), so that they can be valued and legally separated from Google. At the same time, it was announced that Google will be reorganized as a <a href="/wiki/Limited_liability_company" title="Limited liability company">limited liability company</a>, Google LLC.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p><p><a href="/wiki/Eric_Schmidt" title="Eric Schmidt">Eric Schmidt</a> said at an <a href="/wiki/Internet_Association" title="Internet Association">Internet Association</a> event in 2015 that there may eventually be more than 26 Alphabet subsidiaries. He also said that he was currently meeting with the CEOs of the current and proposed Alphabet subsidiaries. He said, "You'll see a lot coming."<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p><p>While many companies or divisions formerly a part of Google became subsidiaries of Alphabet, Google remains the umbrella company for Alphabet's Internet-related businesses. These include widely used products and services long associated with Google, such as the <a href="/wiki/Android_(operating_system)" title="Android (operating system)">Android mobile operating system</a>, YouTube, and <a href="/wiki/Google_Search" title="Google Search">Google Search</a>, which remain direct components of Google.<sup id="cite_ref-bloomberg2015_6-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bloomberg2015-6">[6]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p><p>Former subsidiaries include <a href="/wiki/Nest_Labs" class="mw-redirect" title="Nest Labs">Nest Labs</a>, which was merged into Google in February 2018<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Chronicle_(company)" class="mw-redirect" title="Chronicle (company)">Chronicle</a> which was merged with Google Cloud in June 2019.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(3)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Corporate_identity">Corporate identity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alphabet_Inc.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Corporate identity" data-section="3" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-3 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-3">
<p>Page explained the origin of the company's name:<sup id="cite_ref-:0_16-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r886047036"><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>We liked the name Alphabet because it means a collection of letters that represent language, one of humanity's most important innovations, and is the core of how we index with Google search! We also like that it means alpha‑bet (<a href="/wiki/Alpha_(finance)" title="Alpha (finance)">Alpha</a> is investment return above benchmark), which we strive for!</p></blockquote>
<p>In a 2018 talk, Schmidt disclosed that the original inspiration for the name came from the location of the then Google Hamburg office's street address: <a href="/w/index.php?title=ABC-Stra%C3%9Fe&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="ABC-Straße (page does not exist)">ABC-Straße</a><span class="noprint" style="font-size:85%; font-style: normal;"> [<a href="https://de.wikipedia.org/wiki/ABC-Stra%C3%9Fe" class="extiw" title="de:ABC-Straße">de</a>]</span>.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p><p>Alphabet has chosen the domain abc.xyz with the <a href="/wiki/.xyz" title=".xyz">.xyz</a> <a href="/wiki/Top-level_domain" title="Top-level domain">top-level domain</a> (TLD), which was introduced in 2014. It does not own the domain alphabet.com, which is owned by a <a href="/wiki/Fleet_management" title="Fleet management">fleet management</a> division of <a href="/wiki/BMW" title="BMW">BMW</a>. BMW has said that it is "necessary to examine the legal trademark implications" of the proposals. Additionally, it does not own the domain abc.com, which is the promoted domain of the <a href="/wiki/The_Walt_Disney_Company" title="The Walt Disney Company">Disney</a>-owned <a href="/wiki/American_Broadcasting_Company" title="American Broadcasting Company">American Broadcasting Company</a> (abc.com redirects to a subdomain of <a href="/wiki/Go.com" title="Go.com">go.com</a>, through which most of Disney's sites are hosted).<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-tc-alphabet-bmw_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-tc-alphabet-bmw-32">[32]</a></sup></p><p>The website features an <a href="/wiki/Easter_egg_(media)" title="Easter egg (media)">Easter egg</a> in the paragraph where Larry Page writes, "Sergey and I are seriously in the business of starting new things. Alphabet will also include our <a href="/wiki/Google_X_Labs" class="mw-redirect" title="Google X Labs">X lab</a>, which incubates new efforts like <a href="/wiki/X_(company)#Wing" title="X (company)">Wing</a>, our <a href="/wiki/Drone_delivery" class="mw-redirect" title="Drone delivery">drone delivery</a> effort. We are also stoked about growing our investment arms, Ventures and Capital, as part of this new structure." The period after "drone delivery effort" is a <a href="/wiki/Hyperlink" title="Hyperlink">hyperlink</a> to "hooli.xyz",<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> a reference to the television series <i><a href="/wiki/Silicon_Valley_(TV_series)" title="Silicon Valley (TV series)">Silicon Valley</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(4)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Revenue">Revenue</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alphabet_Inc.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Revenue" data-section="4" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-4 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-4">
<p>As per its 2017 annual report, 86% of Alphabet's revenues came from performance advertising (through user clicks using <a href="/wiki/Adsense" class="mw-redirect" title="Adsense">Adsense</a> and <a href="/wiki/Google_Ads" title="Google Ads">Google Ads</a>) and brand advertising.<sup id="cite_ref-agm2017_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-agm2017-35">[35]</a></sup> Of these, 53% came from its international operations. This translated to a total revenue of US$110,855 million in 2017 and a net income of US$12,662 million.
</p><p>On February 1, 2016, Alphabet Inc. surpassed <a href="/wiki/Apple_Inc." title="Apple Inc.">Apple</a> to become the world's most valuable publicly traded company until February 3, 2016, when Apple surged back over Alphabet to retake the position. Experts cited Apple's lack of innovation as well as increasing Chinese competition as reasons for the poor performance.<sup id="cite_ref-alphabet-most-valuable_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-alphabet-most-valuable-36">[36]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-apple-most-valuable_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-apple-most-valuable-37">[37]</a></sup></p><p>As of 2019<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Alphabet_Inc.&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, Alphabet is ranked No. 15 on the <a href="/wiki/Fortune_500" title="Fortune 500">Fortune 500</a> rankings of the largest United States corporations by total revenue.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></p><p>On January 16, 2020, Alphabet became the fourth US company to reach a $1 <a href="/wiki/Trillion" title="Trillion">trillion</a> market value<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> entering the <a href="/wiki/List_of_public_corporations_by_market_capitalization" title="List of public corporations by market capitalization">Trillion dollar companies club</a> for the first time.
</p>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(5)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Investments_and_acquisitions">Investments and acquisitions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alphabet_Inc.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Investments and acquisitions" data-section="5" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-5 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-5">
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Investments">Investments</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alphabet_Inc.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Investments" data-section="6" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>In November 2017, Alphabet Inc. led a Series A round of $71 million along with <a href="/wiki/Andreessen_Horowitz" title="Andreessen Horowitz">Andreessen Horowitz</a> and <a href="/wiki/20th_Century_Fox" class="mw-redirect" title="20th Century Fox">20th Century Fox</a> in music startup <a href="/wiki/UnitedMasters" title="UnitedMasters">UnitedMasters</a>, founded by <a href="/wiki/Steve_Stoute" title="Steve Stoute">Steve Stoute</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Acquisitions">Acquisitions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alphabet_Inc.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Acquisitions" data-section="7" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/List_of_mergers_and_acquisitions_by_Alphabet" title="List of mergers and acquisitions by Alphabet">List of mergers and acquisitions by Alphabet</a></div>
<p>An analysis of the company's investments in 2017 suggested that it was the most active investor in that period, outdoing the capital arm of <a href="/wiki/Intel" title="Intel">Intel</a> and also its own best customer. Alphabet, Inc. acquired seven of its own capital-backed startups in the 2017 financial year, with <a href="/wiki/Cisco" class="mw-redirect" title="Cisco">Cisco</a> second having acquired six of the company's previous investments.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup></p><p>Flatiron Health, a startup founded by two former Google employees and backed by Alphabet, Inc., announced that it was to be acquired by health conglomerate <a href="/wiki/Hoffmann-La_Roche" title="Hoffmann-La Roche">Hoffmann-La Roche</a> for $1.8 billion. The company provides electronic medical records and analysis to identify improved treatments for <a href="/wiki/Oncology" title="Oncology">oncology</a> patients.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup></p>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(6)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Lawsuit">Lawsuit</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alphabet_Inc.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Lawsuit" data-section="8" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-6 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-6">
<p>Alphabet Inc. sued <a href="/wiki/Uber" title="Uber">Uber</a> over technology similar to Alphabet's proprietary self-driving car technology.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Chronological_items" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers"><span title="The time period mentioned near this tag is ambiguous. (February 2020)">when?</span></a></i>]</sup> Alphabet's <a href="/wiki/Autonomous_vehicle" class="mw-redirect" title="Autonomous vehicle">autonomous vehicle</a> technology has been under development for a decade by Alphabet's <a href="/wiki/Waymo" title="Waymo">Waymo</a> (self-driving vehicle division). The <a href="/wiki/Proprietary_software" title="Proprietary software">proprietary technology</a> is related to 14,000 documents believed to have been downloaded and stolen by a former Waymo engineer, subsequently employed by Uber.<sup id="cite_ref-suit_43-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-suit-43">[43]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-selfdrive_44-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-selfdrive-44">[44]</a></sup> The lawsuit was settled in February 2018, with Uber agreeing not to use the self-driving technology in dispute and also agreed to provide <a href="/wiki/Waymo" title="Waymo">Waymo</a> with an equity stake of 0.34%, equating to around $245 million at the firm's early 2018 value.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup></p>
