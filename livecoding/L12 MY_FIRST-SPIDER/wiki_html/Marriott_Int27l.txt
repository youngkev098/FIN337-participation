<p><b>Marriott International, Inc.</b> is an American <a href="/wiki/Multinational_corporation" title="Multinational corporation">multinational</a> diversified hospitality company that manages and franchises a broad portfolio of <a href="/wiki/Hotel" title="Hotel">hotels</a> and related <a href="/wiki/Lodging_facilities" class="mw-redirect" title="Lodging facilities">lodging facilities</a>. Founded by <a href="/wiki/J._Willard_Marriott" title="J. Willard Marriott">J. Willard Marriott</a>, the company is now led by his son, executive chairman <a href="/wiki/Bill_Marriott" title="Bill Marriott">Bill Marriott</a>, and president and chief executive officer <a href="/wiki/Arne_Sorenson" title="Arne Sorenson">Arne Sorenson</a>. Marriott International is the third largest hotel chain in the world.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> It has 30 brands with 7,003 properties in 131 countries and territories around the world,<sup id="cite_ref-10Q_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10Q-1">[1]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-10-K_2-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10-K-2">[2]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> over 1,332,826 rooms (as of March 31, 2019),<sup id="cite_ref-10Q_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10Q-1">[1]</a></sup> including 2,035 that are managed with 559,569 rooms, 4,905 that are franchised or licensed with 756,156 rooms, and 63 that are owned or leased with 17,101 rooms,<sup id="cite_ref-10Q_1-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10Q-1">[1]</a></sup> plus an additional 475,000 rooms in the development pipeline and an additional 25,000 rooms approved for development but not yet under signed contracts.<sup id="cite_ref-10Q_1-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10Q-1">[1]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a <div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Key_Bridge_Marriott_-_Watergate_scandal_location.jpg" class="image"><noscript><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d3/Key_Bridge_Marriott_-_Watergate_scandal_location.jpg/220px-Key_Bridge_Marriott_-_Watergate_scandal_location.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="129" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="3326" data-file-height="1947"></noscript><span class="lazy-image-placeholder" style="width: 220px;height: 129px;" data-src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d3/Key_Bridge_Marriott_-_Watergate_scandal_location.jpg/220px-Key_Bridge_Marriott_-_Watergate_scandal_location.jpg" data-alt="" data-width="220" data-height="129" data-class="thumbimage"> </span></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Key_Bridge_Marriott_-_Watergate_scandal_location.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The <a href="/wiki/Key_Bridge_(Washington,_D.C.)" title="Key Bridge (Washington, D.C.)">Key Bridge</a> Marriott is the company's longest operating hotel.</div></div></div>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Founding_and_early_years">Founding and early years</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Marriott_International&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Founding and early years" data-section="2" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Marriott_Corporation" title="Marriott Corporation">Marriott Corporation</a></div>
<p>Marriott was founded by <a href="/wiki/John_Willard_Marriott" class="mw-redirect" title="John Willard Marriott">John Willard Marriott</a> in 1927 when he and his wife, <a href="/wiki/Alice_Marriott" title="Alice Marriott">Alice Sheets Marriott</a>, opened a <a href="/wiki/Root_beer" title="Root beer">root beer</a> stand in <a href="/wiki/Washington,_D.C." title="Washington, D.C.">Washington, D.C.</a><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> As a <a href="/wiki/Mormon_missionaries" class="mw-redirect" title="Mormon missionaries">Latter-day Saint missionary</a> in the humid summers in Washington, D.C., Marriott was convinced that what residents of the city needed was a place to get a cool drink.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> The Marriotts later expanded their enterprise into a chain of <b>Hot Shoppes</b> <a href="/wiki/Restaurant" title="Restaurant">restaurants</a><sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> and the company went public in 1953 as <b>Hot Shoppes, Inc.</b><sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p><p>The company opened its first hotel, the <a href="/wiki/Twin_Bridges_Motor_Hotel" title="Twin Bridges Motor Hotel">Twin Bridges Marriott Motor Hotel</a>, in <a href="/wiki/Arlington,_Virginia" class="mw-redirect" title="Arlington, Virginia">Arlington, Virginia</a>, in 1957. Their second hotel, the Key Bridge Marriott in the <a href="/wiki/Rosslyn,_Arlington,_Virginia" class="mw-redirect" title="Rosslyn, Arlington, Virginia">Rosslyn</a> neighborhood of the same city, is Marriott International's longest continuously operating hotel and celebrated its 50th anniversary in 2009.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Their son, J. W. (Bill) Marriott, Jr., led the company to spectacular worldwide growth during his more than 50-year career. In March 2012, at age 80, he turned the CEO responsibilities over to Arne Sorenson, while he assumed the title of executive chairman.
</p><p>Hot Shoppes, Inc. was renamed the <b>Marriott Corporation</b> in 1967.<sup id="cite_ref-whendidithappen.com_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-whendidithappen.com-15">[15]</a></sup></p><p>The company opened two theme parks in 1976.<sup id="cite_ref-whendidithappen.com_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-whendidithappen.com-15">[15]</a></sup> One <a href="/wiki/Six_Flags_Great_America" title="Six Flags Great America">Marriott's Great America</a> was located outside Chicago; the other <a href="/wiki/California%27s_Great_America" title="California's Great America">Marriott's Great America</a> was located outside San Francisco. Marriott sold both properties in 1984.
</p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Marriott_International">Marriott International</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Marriott_International&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Marriott International" data-section="3" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>Marriott International was formed in 1993 when the <a href="/wiki/Marriott_Corporation" title="Marriott Corporation">Marriott Corporation</a> split into two companies, Marriott International and <a href="/wiki/Host_Marriott_Corporation" class="mw-redirect" title="Host Marriott Corporation">Host Marriott Corporation</a>. In 1995, Marriott was the first hotel company worldwide to offer guests the option to book reservations online, via the company's implementation of MARSHA (Marriott's Automatic Reservation System for Hotel Accommodations).<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p><p>In April 1995, Marriott International acquired a 49% interest in <a href="/wiki/The_Ritz-Carlton_Hotel_Company" title="The Ritz-Carlton Hotel Company">Ritz-Carlton Hotel Company LLC</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> Marriott International believed that it could increase sales and profit margins for The Ritz-Carlton, a troubled chain with a significant number of properties either losing money or barely breaking even. The cost to Marriott was estimated to have been about $200<span class="nowrap"> </span>million in cash and assumed debt. The next year, Marriott spent $331<span class="nowrap"> </span>million to take over The Ritz-Carlton, Atlanta and buy a majority interest in two properties owned by William Johnson, a real estate developer who had purchased The Ritz-Carlton, <a href="/wiki/Boston" title="Boston">Boston</a> in 1983 and expanded his Ritz-Carlton holdings over the next twenty years.
</p><p>The Ritz-Carlton began expansion into the lucrative timeshare market and undertook other new initiatives made financially possible by the deep pockets of Marriott, which also lent its own in-house expertise in certain areas. There were other benefits for Ritz-Carlton flowing from its relationship with Marriott, such as being able to take advantage of the parent company's reservation system and buying power. The partnership was solidified in 1998 when Marriott acquired majority ownership of The Ritz-Carlton. Today, there are 91 Ritz-Carlton properties around the world.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span id="Restructuring_.282000.E2.80.932013.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Restructuring_(2000–2013)">Restructuring (2000–2013)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Marriott_International&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Restructuring (2000–2013)" data-section="4" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Remains_of_WTC1_and_WTC2_after_9-11.jpg" class="image"><noscript><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d0/Remains_of_WTC1_and_WTC2_after_9-11.jpg/220px-Remains_of_WTC1_and_WTC2_after_9-11.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="115" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="2076" data-file-height="1088"></noscript><span class="lazy-image-placeholder" style="width: 220px;height: 115px;" data-src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d0/Remains_of_WTC1_and_WTC2_after_9-11.jpg/220px-Remains_of_WTC1_and_WTC2_after_9-11.jpg" data-alt="" data-width="220" data-height="115" data-class="thumbimage"> </span></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Remains_of_WTC1_and_WTC2_after_9-11.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Marriott's hotel in New York after the collapse of the World Trade Center towers on 9/11</div></div></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Marriott_World_Trade_Center" title="Marriott World Trade Center">Marriott World Trade Center</a> was destroyed during the <a href="/wiki/September_11,_2001,_attacks" class="mw-redirect" title="September 11, 2001, attacks">September 11, 2001, attacks</a>.
</p><p>In 2002 Marriott International began a major restructuring by spinning off many Senior Living Services Communities (which is now part of <a href="/wiki/Sunrise_Senior_Living" title="Sunrise Senior Living">Sunrise Senior Living</a>)  and Marriott Distribution Services, so that it could focus on hotel ownership and management. The changes were completed in 2003.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p><p>Marriott International owned <a href="/wiki/Ramada" title="Ramada">Ramada</a> International Hotels &amp; Resorts until its sale on September 15, 2004, to <a href="/wiki/Cendant" title="Cendant">Cendant</a>.
</p><p>In 2005, Marriott International and Marriott Vacation Club International comprised two of the 53 entities that contributed the maximum of $250,000 to the second inauguration of President <a href="/wiki/George_W._Bush" title="George W. Bush">George W. Bush</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p><p>On July 19, 2006, Marriott announced that all lodging buildings it operated in the United States and Canada would become <a href="/wiki/Smoking_ban" title="Smoking ban">non-smoking</a> beginning in September 2006. "The new policy includes all guest rooms, restaurants, lounges, meeting rooms, public space and employee work areas."<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:San_Diego_Marriott.jpg" class="image"><noscript><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b0/San_Diego_Marriott.jpg/170px-San_Diego_Marriott.jpg" decoding="async" width="170" height="188" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="1200" data-file-height="1328"></noscript><span class="lazy-image-placeholder" style="width: 170px;height: 188px;" data-src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b0/San_Diego_Marriott.jpg/170px-San_Diego_Marriott.jpg" data-alt="" data-width="170" data-height="188" data-class="thumbimage"> </span></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:San_Diego_Marriott.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/San_Diego_Marriott_Marquis_%26_Marina" title="San Diego Marriott Marquis &amp; Marina">San Diego Marriott Marquis &amp; Marina</a>, one of the highest revenue-generating Marriotts in the United States</div></div></div>
<p>There were bombings at the <a href="/wiki/Islamabad_Marriott_Hotel_bombing" title="Islamabad Marriott Hotel bombing">Islamabad Marriott</a> in 2008 and at the <a href="/wiki/2009_Jakarta_bombings" title="2009 Jakarta bombings">Jakarta Marriott</a> in 2009.
</p><p>On November 11, 2010, Marriott announced plans to add over 600 hotel properties by 2015. The bulk of the additions would be in emerging markets: India, where it planned to have 100 hotel properties, China, and Southeast Asia.<sup id="cite_ref-htanna_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-htanna-24">[24]</a></sup></p><p>On January 21, 2011, Marriott said that pornography would not be included in the entertainment offered at new hotels, which would use an Internet-based <a href="/wiki/Video_on_demand" title="Video on demand">video on demand</a> system.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p><p>On December 13, 2011, J.W. Marriott, Jr. announced he would be stepping down as CEO of the company, while assuming the role of executive chairman. It was announced that <a href="/wiki/Arne_Sorenson" title="Arne Sorenson">Arne Sorenson</a> would be taking over as CEO as of March 2012.<sup id="cite_ref-WashingtonPost_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WashingtonPost-26">[26]</a></sup></p><p>U.S. Republican presidential candidate <a href="/wiki/Mitt_Romney" title="Mitt Romney">Mitt Romney</a> released his 2011 federal income taxes on September 21, 2012, showing that he declared $260,390 in director's fees from Marriott International,<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> despite the fact that news was released on January 13, 2011, that he had already stepped down from the Marriott International board to run for president.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> His released 2010 tax returns showed earnings in 2010 of $113,881 in director's fees from Marriott.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> In February 2012, Bloomberg reported on Romney's years overseeing tax matters for Marriott, which had included several "scams" (quoting Sen. John McCain) and legal actions brought against Marriott, which Marriott lost in court, over its manipulations of the U.S. Tax Code.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p><p>In December 2012, <a href="/wiki/Guinness_World_Records" title="Guinness World Records">Guinness World Records</a> recognized the <a href="/wiki/Five_star_hotel" class="mw-redirect" title="Five star hotel">five-star</a> JW Marriott Marquis Hotel Dubai as the world's tallest hotel.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span id="Recent_developments_.282014.E2.80.93present.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Recent_developments_(2014–present)">Recent developments (2014–present)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Marriott_International&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Recent developments (2014–present)" data-section="5" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:GM_headquarters_in_Detroit.JPG" class="image"><noscript><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/04/GM_headquarters_in_Detroit.JPG/170px-GM_headquarters_in_Detroit.JPG" decoding="async" width="170" height="222" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="431" data-file-height="562"></noscript><span class="lazy-image-placeholder" style="width: 170px;height: 222px;" data-src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/04/GM_headquarters_in_Detroit.JPG/170px-GM_headquarters_in_Detroit.JPG" data-alt="" data-width="170" data-height="222" data-class="thumbimage"> </span></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:GM_headquarters_in_Detroit.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The <a href="/wiki/Detroit" title="Detroit">Detroit</a> Marriott at the <a href="/wiki/Renaissance_Center" title="Renaissance Center">Renaissance Center</a> is one of the tallest hotels in the <a href="/wiki/Western_Hemisphere" title="Western Hemisphere">Western Hemisphere</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></div></div></div>
<h4 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Disruption_of_competing_hotspots">Disruption of competing hotspots</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Marriott_International&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Disruption of competing hotspots" data-section="6" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h4>
<p>On October 3, 2014, the US <a href="/wiki/Federal_Communications_Commission" title="Federal Communications Commission">Federal Communications Commission</a> (FCC) fined Marriott $600,000 for unlawful use of a "containment" feature of a Wi-Fi monitoring system to deliberately interfere with client-owned networks in the convention space of its <a href="/wiki/Gaylord_Opryland_Resort_%26_Convention_Center" title="Gaylord Opryland Resort &amp; Convention Center">Gaylord Opryland Resort &amp; Convention Center</a> in <a href="/wiki/Nashville" class="mw-redirect" title="Nashville">Nashville</a>. The scheme disrupted operation of clients' <a href="/wiki/Mobile_telephone" class="mw-redirect" title="Mobile telephone">mobile telephone</a> hotspots by sending <a href="/wiki/Wi-Fi_deauthentication_attack" title="Wi-Fi deauthentication attack">fraudulent Wi-fi de-authentication</a> packets.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> Marriott International, Inc., the <a href="/wiki/American_Hotel_and_Lodging_Association" title="American Hotel and Lodging Association">American Hotel and Lodging Association</a> and Ryman Hospitality Properties responded by unsuccessfully petitioning the FCC to change the rules to allow them to continue the willful jamming of client-owned networks,<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> a position which they were forced to abandon in early 2015 in response to backlash from clients, mainstream media, major technology companies and national mobile carriers.<sup id="cite_ref-beeb-wifi_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-beeb-wifi-36">[36]</a></sup></p><p>The incident drew unfavorable publicity to Marriott's practice of charging transient lodgers $13–15/day for wi-fi connections, routinely included in the base price at most discount chains,<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> and to exorbitant wi-fi fees (typically $250–1000 per device<sup id="cite_ref-beeb-wifi_36-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-beeb-wifi-36">[36]</a></sup>) charged to convention-goers at a time when the $2.25<span class="nowrap"> </span>billion "incidental fees" annually charged by US-based hotels to their lodgers was already drawing criticism and negative media coverage.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
<h4 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Acquisitions">Acquisitions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Marriott_International&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Acquisitions" data-section="7" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h4>
<p>On January 27, 2015, Marriott acquired Canadian hotel chain <a href="/wiki/Delta_Hotels" title="Delta Hotels">Delta Hotels</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> Delta operated 38 hotels in Canada at the time of acquisition; it has since expanded to the United States, Europe, and Asia.
</p><p>On November 16, 2015, Marriott announced the acquisition of <a href="/wiki/Starwood_Hotels_and_Resorts_Worldwide" class="mw-redirect" title="Starwood Hotels and Resorts Worldwide">Starwood Hotels and Resorts Worldwide</a> for $13<span class="nowrap"> </span>billion.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> A competing offer for Starwood at $14<span class="nowrap"> </span>billion from a consortium led by China's <a href="/wiki/Anbang_Insurance_Group" class="mw-redirect" title="Anbang Insurance Group">Anbang Insurance Group</a> was announced March 3, 2016, moving Starwood to cease the deal with Marriott and pursue the offer from Anbang Insurance Group.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> After Marriott raised its bid to $13.6<span class="nowrap"> </span>billion on March 21, Starwood terminated the Anbang agreement and proceeded again with the merger with Marriott.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> Following all necessary regulatory approvals in the United States and around the world over the course of 2016, Marriott closed the merger with Starwood on September 23, 2016, creating the world's largest hotel company with over 5700 properties, 1.1<span class="nowrap"> </span>million rooms, and a new portfolio of 30 brands.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup></p><p>The Starwood acquisition gave Marriott a larger non-US presence; approximately 75% of Starwood's revenues were from non-US markets. The acquisition was the largest of its sort since 2007, when Blackstone acquired Hilton for $26<span class="nowrap"> </span>billion. Executives noted that total transaction and integration expenditures may exceed $100<span class="nowrap"> </span>million.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup></p>
<h4 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Data_breach">Data breach</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Marriott_International&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Data breach" data-section="8" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h4>
<p>On November 30, 2018, Marriott disclosed that its Starwood Hotel brand had been subject to a security breach. After the disclosure, New York Attorney General Barbara Underwood announced an investigation into the data breach.<sup id="cite_ref-FOX2018_47-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOX2018-47">[47]</a></sup></p><p>The 2018 cyberattack on the Marriott hotel chain<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> that collected personal details of roughly 500<span class="nowrap"> </span>million guests is now known to be a part of a <a href="/wiki/Chinese_intelligence_activity_abroad" title="Chinese intelligence activity abroad">Chinese intelligence</a>-gathering effort that also hacked health insurers and the security clearance files of millions more Americans. The hackers are suspected of working on behalf of the <a href="/wiki/Ministry_of_State_Security_(China)" title="Ministry of State Security (China)">Ministry of State Security</a>, the country's Communist-controlled civilian spy agency.<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup> Initially, Marriott admitted that 500<span class="nowrap"> </span>million customer's personal information had been exposed.<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup> It later amended their admission to "less than 383<span class="nowrap"> </span>million" customers, and claimed many of the customer's payment cards had expired.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2019)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(2)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Operations">Operations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Marriott_International&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Operations" data-section="9" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-2 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-2">
<p>Marriott is the first hotel chain to serve food that is completely free of <a href="/wiki/Trans_fat" title="Trans fat">trans fats</a> at all of its North American properties.<sup id="cite_ref-usatoday_54-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-usatoday-54">[54]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup></p><p>The hotel is noted for providing copies of the <i><a href="/wiki/Book_of_Mormon" title="Book of Mormon">Book of Mormon</a></i> in addition to the Bible in its rooms.<sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup></p><p>As of 2017<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Marriott_International&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, the company had approximately 177,000 employees.<sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup></p>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(3)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Finances">Finances</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Marriott_International&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Finances" data-section="10" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-3 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-3">

Marriott International's shares traded at over $101 per share, and its market capitalization was valued at over US$39.1<span class="nowrap"> </span>billion in October 2018.<sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup></p>

<li><a href="/wiki/Tribute_Portfolio" class="mw-redirect" title="Tribute Portfolio">Tribute Portfolio</a></li></ul><h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Great_America_Parks">Great America Parks</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Marriott_International&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Great America Parks" data-section="25" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>Marriott developed three theme parks, of which two theme parks were opened. They operated as <a href="/wiki/Marriott%27s_Great_America_(disambiguation)" class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" title="Marriott's Great America (disambiguation)">Marriott's Great America</a> from 1976 until 1984. The parks were located in <a href="/wiki/Gurnee,_Illinois" title="Gurnee, Illinois">Gurnee, Illinois</a>; <a href="/wiki/Santa_Clara,_California" title="Santa Clara, California">Santa Clara, California</a>; and a proposed but never-built location in the Washington, D.C., area, and were themed celebrating American history. The American-themed areas under Marriott's tenure of ownership included "Carousel Plaza" (the first section beyond the main gates); small-town-themed "Hometown Square"; "The Great Midwest Livestock Exposition At County Fair" with a Turn of the Century rural-fair theme; "Yankee Harbor", inspired by a 19th-century New England port; "Yukon Territory," resembling a Canadian/Alaskan logging camp; and the French Quarter-modeled "Orleans Place". At the opening, the parks had nearly identical layouts.
</p><p>In 1984, Marriott disposed of its theme park division; both parks were sold and today are associated with national theme park chains. The Gurnee location was sold to <a href="/wiki/Six_Flags" title="Six Flags">Six Flags</a> Theme Parks where it operates today as <a href="/wiki/Six_Flags_Great_America" title="Six Flags Great America">Six Flags Great America</a>. The Santa Clara location was sold to the City of Santa Clara, who retained the underlying property and sold the park to <a href="/wiki/Kings_Entertainment_Company" title="Kings Entertainment Company">Kings Entertainment Company</a>, renamed <a href="/wiki/Paramount_Parks" title="Paramount Parks">Paramount Parks</a> in 1993. From 1993 to 2006, the Santa Clara location was known as Paramount's Great America. In 2006, Paramount Parks was acquired by <a href="/wiki/Cedar_Fair_Entertainment_Company" class="mw-redirect" title="Cedar Fair Entertainment Company">Cedar Fair Entertainment Company</a>; the Santa Clara park operates today as <a href="/wiki/California%27s_Great_America" title="California's Great America">California's Great America</a>. In the years after their sale, the layouts of both of the parks have diverged substantially.
</p>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(5)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Rewards_program">Rewards program</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Marriott_International&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Rewards program" data-section="26" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-5 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-5">
<p>Marriott Bonvoy is the loyalty program used by Marriott and was formed in the 2019 merger of its three former rewards programs: Marriott Rewards, Ritz-Carlton Rewards, and Starwood Preferred Guest.
</p><p>Marriott Rewards was founded in 1983.<sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup> In 1997, Marriott Rewards became the first hotel loyalty program to allow customers to earn points at limited-service hotels (such as Fairfield Inn) and redeem them at full-service hotels (such as JW Marriott).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2020)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p><p>Starwood Preferred Guest (mononymously known as SPG) was founded in 1999 as the first in the industry to enforce a policy of no blackout dates, no capacity controls, and online redemption. In 2012, Starwood Preferred Guest began offering lifetime status and a dedicated Starwood ambassador for loyal members.<sup id="cite_ref-61" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup></p><p>Ritz-Carlton Rewards was founded in 2010. Unlike Marriott Rewards and SPG, members were able to receive air miles instead of reward points and able to earn ten points (or two miles) for every dollar spent on any Ritz-Carlton room rates. Despite the restriction of membership to only one of the two programs, members of Ritz-Carlton Rewards were able earn points in other Marriott hotels, while Marriott Rewards members were able to earn points at a Ritz-Carlton.<sup id="cite_ref-62" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup> The Ritz Carlton Club Vail, The Ritz-Carlton Millenia Singapore, The Ritz London, all Ritz-Carlton Reserve hotels and all Bulgari hotels, however, never participated in Ritz-Carlton Rewards program and its replacement, the Marriott Bonvoy program.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2020)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p><p><br></p>
