</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(1)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Intuitive_Surgical&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History" data-section="1" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-1 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-1">
<p>The research that eventually led to the development of the <i>da Vinci</i> Surgical System was performed in the late 1980s at a non-profit research institute <a href="/wiki/SRI_International" title="SRI International">SRI International</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>  In 1990, SRI received funding from the <a href="/wiki/National_Institutes_of_Health" title="National Institutes of Health">National Institutes of Health</a>.  SRI developed a prototype robotic surgical system that caught the interest of the <a href="/wiki/Defense_Advanced_Research_Projects_Agency" class="mw-redirect" title="Defense Advanced Research Projects Agency">Defense Advanced Research Projects Agency</a> (DARPA), which was interested in the system for its potential to allow surgeons to operate remotely on soldiers wounded on the battlefield.
</p><p>In 1994, Dr. <a href="/wiki/Frederic_Moll" title="Frederic Moll">Frederic Moll</a> became interested in the SRI System, as the device was known at the time. At the time, Moll was employed by <a href="/wiki/Guidant" title="Guidant">Guidant</a>. He tried to interest Guidant in backing it, to no avail. In 1995, Moll was introduced to John Freund, who had recently left Acuson Corporation. Freund negotiated an option to acquire SRI's intellectual property, and incorporated a new company that he named Intuitive Surgical Devices, Inc.
</p><p>At that point Freund, Moll, and Robert (also from Acuson) wrote the business plan for the company and raised its initial venture capital.  Early investors included the <a href="/wiki/Mayfield_Fund" class="mw-redirect" title="Mayfield Fund">Mayfield Fund</a>, Sierra Ventures, and Morgan Stanley.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p><p>The company refined the SRI System into a prototype known originally as "Lenny" (after the young Leonardo da Vinci), which was ready for testing in 1997. As the company's prototypes became more advanced, they were named using da Vinci themes. One was named "Leonardo", and another was "Mona". The final version of the prototype was nicknamed the <i>da Vinci</i> Surgical System, and the name stuck when the system was eventually commercialized. After further testing, Intuitive Surgical began marketing this system in Europe in 1999, while awaiting FDA approval in the United States.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p><p>The company raised $46 million in an initial public offering in 2000.  That same year, the FDA approved use of the <i>da Vinci</i> Surgical System for general <a href="/wiki/Laparoscopic" class="mw-redirect" title="Laparoscopic">laparoscopic</a> surgery, which can be used to address gallbladder disease and gastroesophageal disease.  In 2001, the FDA approved use of the system for prostate surgery.  The FDA has subsequently approved the system for thoracoscopic surgery, cardiac procedures performed with adjunctive incisions, and gynecologic procedures.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p><p>Shortly before going public, Intuitive Surgical was sued for patent infringement by Computer Motion, Inc, its chief rival.  Computer Motion had actually gotten into the robotic surgery field earlier than Intuitive Surgical, with its own system, the <a href="/wiki/ZEUS_Robotic_Surgical_System" class="mw-redirect" title="ZEUS Robotic Surgical System">ZEUS Robotic Surgical System</a>.  Although the ZEUS system was approved in Europe, the U.S. <a href="/wiki/Food_and_Drug_Administration" title="Food and Drug Administration">Food and Drug Administration</a> had not yet approved it for any procedure at the time that the FDA first approved the <i>da Vinci</i> system.  The uncertainty created by the litigation between the companies was a drag on each company's growth. In 2003, Intuitive Surgical and Computer Motion agreed to merge, thus ending the litigation between them.<sup id="cite_ref-INTUITIVE-SURGICAL-INC-Mar-2003-8-K_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-INTUITIVE-SURGICAL-INC-Mar-2003-8-K-7">[7]</a></sup>  The ZEUS system was ultimately phased out in favor of the <i>da Vinci</i> system.
</p><p>Before the buyout of Computer Motion, the stock of Intuitive was selling at around $14 per share, adjusted for stock splits. After the merger, the stock price rose significantly (and by 2015 it was at about $500),<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> primarily because of the growth in systems sold (60 in 2002 compared with 431 in 2014) and the number of surgical procedures performed (less than 1,000 in 2002 compared with 540,000 in 2014).
</p>

<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Da_Vinci_Surgical_System" title="Da Vinci Surgical System">da Vinci Surgical System</a></div>
<table class="infobox hproduct vevent" style="width:22em"><caption class="fn summary"><i>Da Vinci Surgical System</i></caption><tbody><tr><td colspan="2" style="text-align:center"><a href="/wiki/File:Laproscopic_Surgery_Robot.jpg" class="image"><noscript><img alt="Laproscopic Surgery Robot.jpg" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0d/Laproscopic_Surgery_Robot.jpg/150px-Laproscopic_Surgery_Robot.jpg" decoding="async" width="150" height="218" data-file-width="891" data-file-height="1296"></noscript><span class="lazy-image-placeholder" style="width: 150px;height: 218px;" data-src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0d/Laproscopic_Surgery_Robot.jpg/150px-Laproscopic_Surgery_Robot.jpg" data-alt="Laproscopic Surgery Robot.jpg" data-width="150" data-height="218"> </span></a></td></tr><tr><th scope="row">Manufacturer</th><td>Intuitive Surgical</td></tr><tr><th scope="row">Type</th><td><a href="/wiki/Robotic_surgery" class="mw-redirect" title="Robotic surgery">Robotic surgery</a></td></tr></tbody></table><p>The <a href="/wiki/Da_Vinci_Surgical_System" title="Da Vinci Surgical System">da Vinci Surgical System</a> is a <a href="/wiki/Robotic_surgery" class="mw-redirect" title="Robotic surgery">robotic surgical</a> system. The system is controlled by a surgeon from a console. 
It is commonly used for <a href="/wiki/Prostatectomy" title="Prostatectomy">prostatectomies</a> and increasingly for <a href="/wiki/Heart_valve" title="Heart valve">cardiac valve</a> repair and <a href="/wiki/Gynecology" class="mw-redirect" title="Gynecology">gynecologic</a> surgical procedures.<sup id="cite_ref-mw_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mw-10">[10]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-nyt_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nyt-11">[11]</a></sup></p><p>A <i>da Vinci</i> Surgical System costs approximately $1.5 million.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>
The new da Vinci SI released in April 2009 cost about $1.75 million. In addition, there are maintenance contracts plus expenditures for instruments used during surgery. Surgical procedures performed with the robot take longer than traditional ones. Critics have pointed out that hospitals have a hard time recovering the cost and that most clinical data does not support the claim of improved patient outcomes.<sup id="cite_ref-nyt_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nyt-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Efficacy">Efficacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Intuitive_Surgical&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Efficacy" data-section="4" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>While the use of robotic surgery has become an item in the advertisement of medical services, there is a lack of studies that indicated long-term results are superior to results following laparoscopic surgery.<sup id="cite_ref-kolata_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kolata-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
