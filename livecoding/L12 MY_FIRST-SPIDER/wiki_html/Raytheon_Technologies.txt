<p class="mw-empty-elt">  

</p>
<p><b>Raytheon Technologies Corporation</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Multinational_corporation" title="Multinational corporation">multinational</a> <a href="/wiki/List_of_conglomerates" title="List of conglomerates">conglomerate</a> headquartered in <a href="/wiki/Waltham,_Massachusetts" title="Waltham, Massachusetts">Waltham, Massachusetts</a>. It researches, develops, and manufactures advanced technology products in the aerospace and defense industry, including <a href="/wiki/Aircraft_engine" title="Aircraft engine">aircraft engines</a>, <a href="/wiki/Avionics" title="Avionics">avionics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Aerostructure" title="Aerostructure">aerostructures</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cybersecurity" class="mw-redirect" title="Cybersecurity">cybersecurity</a>, <a href="/wiki/Missile" title="Missile">missiles</a>, <a href="/wiki/Anti-aircraft_warfare" title="Anti-aircraft warfare">air defense systems</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Unmanned_aerial_vehicle" title="Unmanned aerial vehicle">drones</a>. The company is also a large military contractor, getting a significant portion of its revenue from the U.S. government.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Former United Technologies CEO and chairman <a href="/wiki/Gregory_J._Hayes" title="Gregory J. Hayes</a> is the CEO of the combined company,<sup id="cite_ref-rtxpresidentceo_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rtxpresidentceo-2">[2]</a></sup> and former Raytheon CEO and chairman <a href="/wiki/Thomas_A._Kennedy" title="Thomas A. Kennedy">Thomas A. Before the merger, UTC <a href="/wiki/Corporate_spin-off" title="Corporate spin-off">spun-off</a> its non-aerospace subsidiaries <a href="/wiki/Otis_Elevator_Company" class="mw-redirect" title="Otis Elevator Company">Otis Elevator Company</a> and <a href="/wiki/Carrier_Corporation" class="mw-redirect" title="Carrier Corporation">Carrier Corporation</a>. UTC is the nominal survivor of the merger but it changed its name to Raytheon Technologies and relocated its headquarters to Waltham.<sup id="cite_ref-mergercomplete_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mergercomplete-4">[4]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p><p>The company is one of the largest aerospace and defense manufacturers in the world by revenue and market capitalization. Through its predecessors, including UTC, its stock has been one of the most durable constituents of the US blue-chip index <a href="/wiki/Dow_Jones_Industrial_Average" title="Dow Jones Industrial Average">Dow Jones Industrial Average</a> for more than 80 years. The stock trades on the <a href="/wiki/New_York_Stock_Exchange" title="New York Stock Exchange">New York Stock Exchange</a> and the ticker symbol is RTX.
</p><p>The company has four subsidiaries: <a href="/wiki/Collins_Aerospace" title="Collins Aerospace">Collins Aerospace</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pratt_%26_Whitney" title="Pratt &amp; Whitney">Pratt &amp; Whitney</a>, <a href="/wiki/Raytheon_Intelligence_%26_Space" title="Raytheon Intelligence &amp; Space">Raytheon Intelligence &amp; Space</a> and <a href="/wiki/Raytheon_Missiles_%26_Defense" title="Raytheon Missiles &amp; Defense">Raytheon Missiles &amp; Defense</a>. 
</p>
