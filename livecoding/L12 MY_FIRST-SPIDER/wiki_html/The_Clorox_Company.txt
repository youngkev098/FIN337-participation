</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(1)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Clorox&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History" data-section="1" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-1 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-1">
<h3 class="in-block"><span id="1913.E2.80.931927"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="1913–1927">1913–1927</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Clorox&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: 1913–1927" data-section="2" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>The beginnings of Clorox bleach and the company date back to May 3, 1913, when five entrepreneurs, Archibald Taft, a banker; Edward Hughes, a purveyor of wood and coal; Charles Husband, a bookkeeper; Rufus Myers, a lawyer; and William Hussey, a miner, invested $100 each to set up the first commercial-scale liquid bleach factory in the United States, on the east side of <a href="/wiki/San_Francisco_Bay" title="San Francisco Bay">San Francisco Bay</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-companyHistoryPage1_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-companyHistoryPage1-21">[21]</a></sup> The firm was first called <b>Electro-Alkaline Company</b>.<sup id="cite_ref-companyHistoryPage1_21-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-companyHistoryPage1-21">[21]</a></sup> The name of its original bleach product, Clorox, was coined as a <a href="/wiki/Portmanteau" title="Portmanteau">portmanteau</a> of <a href="/wiki/Chlorine" title="Chlorine">chlorine</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sodium_hydroxide" title="Sodium hydroxide">sodium hydroxide</a>, the two main ingredients. The original Clorox packaging featured a diamond-shaped logo, and the diamond shape has persisted in one form or another in Clorox branding to the present.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:192px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Clorox_bleach_1922_newspaper_ad.png" class="image"><noscript><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/8/8e/Clorox_bleach_1922_newspaper_ad.png" decoding="async" width="190" height="216" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="190" data-file-height="216"></noscript><span class="lazy-image-placeholder" style="width: 190px;height: 216px;" data-src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/8/8e/Clorox_bleach_1922_newspaper_ad.png" data-alt="" data-width="190" data-height="216" data-class="thumbimage"> </span></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Clorox_bleach_1922_newspaper_ad.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Bottle of Clorox bleach from a 1922 newspaper ad.</div></div></div>
<p>The public, however, did not know very much about liquid bleach when Clorox bleach debuted. Although the Electro-Alkaline Company started slowly and was about to collapse quickly, it would not be until 1916 when investor William Murray took over the company as general manager. His wife, Annie Murray, prompted the creation of a less-concentrated liquid bleach for home use and built customer demand by giving away 15-ounce sample bottles at the family's grocery store in downtown Oakland.<sup id="cite_ref-CompanyHeritageTimeline_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CompanyHeritageTimeline-22">[22]</a></sup> Not long after, word began to spread and, in 1917, the Electro-Alkaline Company began shipping Clorox bleach to the East Coast via the <a href="/wiki/Panama_Canal" title="Panama Canal">Panama Canal</a>.
</p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span id="1928.E2.80.931960s"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="1928–1960s">1928–1960s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Clorox&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: 1928–1960s" data-section="3" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>On May 28, 1928, the company went public on the <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Exchange" title="Pacific Exchange">San Francisco stock exchange</a> and changed its name to <b>Clorox Chemical Company</b>. Butch, an <a href="/wiki/Animation" title="Animation">animated</a> Clorox liquid bleach <a href="/wiki/Bottle" title="Bottle">bottle</a>, was used in its <a href="/wiki/Advertising" title="Advertising">advertising</a> and became well-known, even surviving the 1941 transition from rubber-stoppered bottles to screw-off caps.<sup id="cite_ref-companyHistoryPage3_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-companyHistoryPage3-23">[23]</a></sup></p><p>Clorox was strong enough to survive the <a href="/wiki/Great_Depression" title="Great Depression">Great Depression</a> throughout the 1930s, achieving national distribution of Clorox bleach in the process, but during <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, even though Clorox bleach proved useful as a first aid product for American armed forces, one of the bleach's ingredients was being rationed, as, under U.S. government orders, <a href="/wiki/Chlorine_gas" class="mw-redirect" title="Chlorine gas">chlorine gas</a> shortages forced many bleach manufacturers to reduce the concentration of <a href="/wiki/Sodium_hypochlorite" title="Sodium hypochlorite">sodium hypochlorite</a> in their products, thus diluting them with water. Clorox, however, declined and elected to sell fewer units of a full-strength product, establishing a reputation for quality.<sup id="cite_ref-companyHistoryPage3_23-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-companyHistoryPage3-23">[23]</a></sup></p><p>In 1957, Clorox was purchased by <a href="/wiki/Procter_%26_Gamble" title="Procter &amp; Gamble">Procter &amp; Gamble</a>, which renamed its new subsidiary <b>The Clorox Company</b>. Almost immediately, a rival company objected to the purchase, and it was challenged by the <a href="/wiki/Federal_Trade_Commission" title="Federal Trade Commission">Federal Trade Commission</a>, which feared it would stifle competition in the household products market. The FTC won in 1967 after a 10-year battle, in which the <a href="/wiki/Supreme_Court_of_the_United_States" title="Supreme Court of the United States">U.S. Supreme Court</a> ruled that P&amp;G must divest Clorox, and on January 1, 1969, Clorox became independent again.
</p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span id="1970s.E2.80.931990s"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="1970s–1990s">1970s–1990s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Clorox&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: 1970s–1990s" data-section="4" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>Throughout the 1970s and 1980s, Clorox pursued an aggressive expansion program in which it attempted to establish itself as a major diversified consumer products conglomerate, like P&amp;G. In 1970, Clorox introduced Clorox 2 all-fabric bleach. Later on, in that period, it acquired a number of brands that remain a part of their portfolio today, including <a href="/wiki/Formula_409" title="Formula 409">Formula 409</a>, <a href="/wiki/Liquid-Plumr" title="Liquid-Plumr">Liquid-Plumr</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kingsford_(charcoal)" title="Kingsford (charcoal)">Kingsford</a> charcoal and developed cleaning products such as Tilex instant mildew remover.<sup id="cite_ref-timeline_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-timeline-24">[24]</a></sup> It even acquired a <a href="/wiki/Ranch_dressing" title="Ranch dressing">ranch dressing</a> that was still new to the market, which was known as "Hidden Valley."
</p><p>In 1988, Clorox struck a licensing-and-distribution agreement that brought <a href="/wiki/Brita" title="Brita">Brita</a> water filters to the U.S.<sup id="cite_ref-timeline_24-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-timeline-24">[24]</a></sup> The company acquired sole control of the brand for the U.S. and Canada in 1995 when it acquired Brita International Holdings (Canada). In 2000 it secured the remaining Americas market from Brita.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p><p>In 1990, Clorox purchased <a href="/wiki/Pine-Sol" title="Pine-Sol">Pine-Sol</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-timeline_24-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-timeline-24">[24]</a></sup></p><p>In 1999, Clorox acquired First Brands, the former consumer products division of <a href="/wiki/Union_Carbide" title="Union Carbide">Union Carbide</a>, in the largest transaction in its history. Such brands as <a href="/wiki/Glad_(company)" class="mw-redirect" title="Glad (company)">Glad</a>, Handi-Wipes (which First Brands acquired from <a href="/wiki/Colgate-Palmolive" title="Colgate-Palmolive">Colgate-Palmolive</a> several months before the Clorox acquisition) and <a href="/wiki/STP_(motor_oil_company)" title="STP (motor oil company)">STP</a> became part of the Clorox portfolio. The First Brands acquisition doubled the size of the company and helped it land on the <a href="/wiki/Fortune_500" title="Fortune 500">Fortune 500</a> for the first time the following year.<sup id="cite_ref-timeline_24-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-timeline-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span id="2000s.E2.80.93present"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="2000s–present">2000s–present</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Clorox&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: 2000s–present" data-section="5" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>During the next decade, the company focused on consumer megatrends that included sustainability, health and wellness, multicultural, and affordability/value.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> In 2002, Clorox entered into a joint venture with Procter &amp; Gamble to create food and trash bags, food wraps, and containers under the names <a href="/wiki/Glad_Trash_Bags" class="mw-redirect" title="Glad Trash Bags">Glad</a>, GladWare and related trademarks.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> As part of this agreement, Clorox sold a 10% stake in the Glad products to P&amp;G, which increased to 20% in 2005.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p><p>In 2007, the company acquired <a href="/wiki/Burt%27s_Bees" title="Burt's Bees">Burt's Bees</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> In 2010, Clorox shed businesses that were no longer a good strategic fit for the company, announcing that it was selling the <a href="/wiki/Armor_All" title="Armor All">Armor All</a> and <a href="/wiki/STP_(motor_oil_company)" title="STP (motor oil company)">STP</a> brands to <a href="/wiki/Avista_Capital_Partners" title="Avista Capital Partners">Avista Capital Partners</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> In 2011, Clorox acquired the Aplicare and HealthLink brands, bolstering its presence in the healthcare industry.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p><p>In 2008, The Clorox Company became the first major consumer packaged goods company to develop and nationally launch a <a href="/wiki/Green_marketing" title="Green marketing">green</a> cleaning <a href="/wiki/Product_lining" title="Product lining">line</a>, Green Works, into the mainstream cleaning aisle.<sup id="cite_ref-greenworks_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-greenworks-32">[32]</a></sup> In 2011, the Clorox Company integrated <a href="/wiki/Corporate_social_responsibility" title="Corporate social responsibility">corporate social responsibility</a> (CSR) reporting with financial reporting. The company's annual report for the fiscal year ending in June 2011 shared data on financial performance as well as advances in environmental, social and governance performance.<sup id="cite_ref-socialresponsibility_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-socialresponsibility-33">[33]</a></sup></p><p>The company ranked #453 on the Fortune 500 in 2017.<sup id="cite_ref-fortunelist_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fortunelist-14">[14]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-fortune_2016_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fortune_2016-15">[15]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Nasdaq_web_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Nasdaq_web-34">[34]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Nasdaq_stock_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Nasdaq_stock-35">[35]</a></sup> In 2018 Clorox purchased Nutranext Business, LLC for approximately $700 million. Florida-based Nutranext makes natural multivitamins, specialty minerals used as health aids, and supplements for hair, skin and nails.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> Operating income in 2018 was <a href="/wiki/United_States_Dollar" class="mw-redirect" title="United States Dollar">US$</a>$1.1 billion.<sup id="cite_ref-RefB_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RefB-37">[37]</a></sup> With approximately 8,700 employees worldwide as of 2018, yearly revenue for the period ending June 30, 2018, equaled $6.1 billion.<sup id="cite_ref-RefA_11-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RefA-11">[11]</a></sup> Yearly revenue equaled $6.2 in 2019.<sup id="cite_ref-fiscal_8-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fiscal-8">[8]</a></sup> In 2019, Clorox ranked 7th in Barron’s “100 Most Sustainable U.S. Companies” list. <sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(2)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Brands">Brands</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Clorox&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Brands" data-section="6" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-2 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-2">
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Clorox_Brand_Logo_2019.png" class="image"><noscript><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c2/Clorox_Brand_Logo_2019.png/220px-Clorox_Brand_Logo_2019.png" decoding="async" width="220" height="129" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="1900" data-file-height="1112"></noscript><span class="lazy-image-placeholder" style="width: 220px;height: 129px;" data-src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c2/Clorox_Brand_Logo_2019.png/220px-Clorox_Brand_Logo_2019.png" data-alt="" data-width="220" data-height="129" data-class="thumbimage"> </span></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Clorox_Brand_Logo_2019.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Clorox logo for consumer-facing brands (not to be confused with the corporate mark)</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Clorox_Bleach_products.jpg" class="image"><noscript><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d3/Clorox_Bleach_products.jpg/220px-Clorox_Bleach_products.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="157" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="512" data-file-height="366"></noscript><span class="lazy-image-placeholder" style="width: 220px;height: 157px;" data-src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d3/Clorox_Bleach_products.jpg/220px-Clorox_Bleach_products.jpg" data-alt="" data-width="220" data-height="157" data-class="thumbimage"> </span></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Clorox_Bleach_products.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Clorox products</div></div></div>

<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Advertising_campaigns">Advertising campaigns</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Clorox&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Advertising campaigns" data-section="9" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>The company was listed at <i><a href="/wiki/Advertising_Age" class="mw-redirect" title="Advertising Age">Advertising Age</a><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s</i> 2015 Marketer A-List.<sup id="cite_ref-Ad_Age_mark_46-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ad_Age_mark-46">[46]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Ad_Age_neff_47-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ad_Age_neff-47">[47]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Allegations_of_sexist_marketing">Allegations of sexist marketing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Clorox&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Allegations of sexist marketing" data-section="10" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>During 2006 and 2007, a Clorox commercial that aired nationally showed several generations of women doing laundry. The commercial included the words "Your mother, your grandmother, her mother, they all did the laundry, maybe even a man or two." <a href="/wiki/Feminism" title="Feminism">Feminists</a> criticized the commercial for insinuating that doing laundry is a job for women only.<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup></p><p>The Clorox <a href="/wiki/Advertising_slogan" title="Advertising slogan">slogan</a>, "Mama's got the magic of Clorox", was criticized on similar grounds.<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> The slogan first appeared in a Clorox commercial in 1986.<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup> A modified version of the commercial ran from 2002 to 2004.<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup></p><p>In 2009, Clorox received complaints of <a href="/wiki/Sexism" title="Sexism">sexism</a> for an advertisement that featured a man's white, lipstick-stained dress shirt with the caption, "Clorox. Getting ad guys out of hot water for generations."<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup> The ad, and others, were produced expressly for the television program <i><a href="/wiki/Mad_Men" title="Mad Men">Mad Men</a></i>, capitalizing on “the show's unique vintage style to [create] a link between classic and modern consumer behaviors."<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Reactions_to_product_claims">Reactions to product claims</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Clorox&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Reactions to product claims" data-section="11" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<h4 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Green_Works">Green Works</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Clorox&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Green Works" data-section="12" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h4>
<p>In 2008, the <a href="/wiki/Sierra_Club" title="Sierra Club">Sierra Club</a> endorsed the Clorox Green Works line. Sierra Club Executive Director <a href="/wiki/Carl_Pope_(environmentalist)" title="Carl Pope (environmentalist)">Carl Pope</a> stated that one of non-profit organization's "primary goals is to foster vibrant, healthy communities with clean water and air that are free from pollution. Products like Green Works help to achieve this goal in the home.” The Sierra Club also partnered with Clorox “to promote a line of natural cleaning products for consumers who are moving toward a greener lifestyle."<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup> The partnership "caused schisms" in the club, which contributed in part to Pope's decision to resign.<sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup></p><p>Also in 2008, the <a href="/wiki/National_Advertising_Division" class="mw-redirect" title="National Advertising Division">National Advertising Division</a> told Clorox to either discontinue or modify its advertisements for Green Works on the grounds the cleaners actually do not work as well as traditional cleaners, as Clorox had claimed.<sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup></p><p>In 2009, Clorox received further criticism for its Clorox Green Works line, regarding claims the products are environmentally friendly.<sup id="cite_ref-msn_58-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-msn-58">[58]</a></sup> Several Clorox Green Works products contain ethanol, which environmental groups state is neither cost-effective nor eco-friendly.<sup id="cite_ref-msn_58-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-msn-58">[58]</a></sup> Many Green Works products also contain <a href="/wiki/Sodium_lauryl_sulfate" class="mw-redirect" title="Sodium lauryl sulfate">sodium lauryl sulfate</a>, a known skin irritant.<sup id="cite_ref-msn_58-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-msn-58">[58]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Women%27s_Voices_for_the_Earth" title="Women's Voices for the Earth">Women's Voices for the Earth</a> have questioned whether or not the Clorox Green Works line is <a href="/wiki/Greenwashing" title="Greenwashing">greenwashing</a>, as Clorox's "green" products are far outnumbered by their traditional products, asking "Why sell one set of products that have hazardous ingredients and others that don't?"<sup id="cite_ref-sf_59-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sf-59">[59]</a></sup></p>
