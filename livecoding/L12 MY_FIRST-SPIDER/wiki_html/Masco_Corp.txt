</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(1)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Masco&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History" data-section="1" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-1 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-1">
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Founding_and_early_history">Founding and early history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Masco&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Founding and early history" data-section="2" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>Initially organized in 1929 as <b>Masco Screw Products Company</b> by <a href="/wiki/Alex_Manoogian" title="Alex Manoogian">Alex Manoogian</a> in <a href="/wiki/Detroit,_Michigan" class="mw-redirect" title="Detroit, Michigan">Detroit, Michigan</a>. The company went public on the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Detroit_Stock_Exchange&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Detroit Stock Exchange (page does not exist)">Detroit Stock Exchange</a> in 1936.
</p><p>Masco Screw Products Company produced machined automotive parts for the <a href="/wiki/Detroit" title="Detroit">Detroit</a> automotive companies.<sup id="cite_ref-Tatge_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Tatge-7">[7]</a></sup> The company's first contract came in 1930 with the <a href="/wiki/Hudson_Motor_Car_Company" title="Hudson Motor Car Company">Hudson Motor Company</a> valued at $7,000.00. In 1935, the company's sales reached $100,000 dollars. The following year Masco became a <a href="/wiki/Public_company" title="Public company">publicly traded company</a> traded on the Detroit Stock Exchange. In 1942, Masco's sales exceeded $1,000,000 dollars.<sup id="cite_ref-timeline_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-timeline-3">[3]</a></sup></p><p>Masco Screw Products grew into the Masco Corporation, a large corporate <a href="/wiki/Holding_company" title="Holding company">holding company</a> for numerous acquisitions. Small, family-run businesses were bought out by Masco Corporation with cash and stock in the parent corporation. Between 1997 and 2002, Richard Manoogian, who succeeded his father as chief executive, acquired 42 companies valued at a total of $10 billion.
</p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span id="Masco_in_the_1950s.2C_1960s_and_1970s"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Masco_in_the_1950s,_1960s_and_1970s">Masco in the 1950s, 1960s and 1970s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Masco&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Masco in the 1950s, 1960s and 1970s" data-section="3" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>In 1952, Manoogian began redesigning the single-handle washerless faucet.<sup id="cite_ref-timeline_3-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-timeline-3">[3]</a></sup> The faucet he created was one of the very first single-handle hot/cold faucets; it is now known as Delta. Delta's <a href="/wiki/Television_advertisement" title="Television advertisement">television advertisements</a> were a first for any faucet, and made the product a successful seller. Masco went on to capture the mass market as sales moved from plumbing wholesalers to retail stores.<sup id="cite_ref-Tatge_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Tatge-7">[7]</a></sup> These steps of production and marketing of the Delta faucet began in 1954, and four years later Delta Faucet's annual sales exceeded a million dollars.<sup id="cite_ref-timeline_3-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-timeline-3">[3]</a></sup> This was the last year that the company would be referred to as Masco Screw Products Company.
</p><p>Alex Manoogian, founder, acted as President and <a href="/wiki/Chief_operating_officer" title="Chief operating officer">chief operating officer</a> (COO) from 1929 until 1967. In 1958 his son Richard joined the company. Richard, having spent nine years with the company, succeeded his father one year after the company moved to Taylor, Michigan. Alex died on July 10, 1996, the same year that his son Richard passed on his COO role to Ray Kennedy, allowing him to assume the position of <a href="/wiki/Chief_executive_officer" title="Chief executive officer">chief executive officer</a>. In response to Kennedy's death, Alan Barry was appointed as Masco's President and COO on April 8, 2003.<sup id="cite_ref-timeline_3-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-timeline-3">[3]</a></sup></p><p>In 1961, Masco Screw Products Company changed its name to Masco Corporation. In 1967, the company moved its corporate headquarters to Taylor, Michigan.<sup id="cite_ref-relo_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-relo-8">[8]</a></sup> For the first time, in 1969, Masco was listed on the <a href="/wiki/New_York_Stock_Exchange" title="New York Stock Exchange">New York Stock Exchange</a>. In 1975, Masco first appeared on the <a href="/wiki/Fortune_500" title="Fortune 500">Fortune 500</a>, and Masco's annual sales exceeded one billion dollars for the first time ever in 1984. One year later, Masco began manufacturing <a href="/wiki/Cabinet_(furniture)" class="mw-redirect" title="Cabinet (furniture)">cabinets</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-timeline_3-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-timeline-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Reorganizing_in_the_1980s_and_1990s">Reorganizing in the 1980s and 1990s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Masco&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Reorganizing in the 1980s and 1990s" data-section="4" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>In 1982 Masco's earnings did not grow for the first time in 26 years. The next year however, “In 1983, earnings climbed again for this maker of faucets and diverse product mix of builders’ hardware, because of increased housing starts and a strong <a href="/wiki/Do_it_yourself" title="Do it yourself">do-it-yourself</a> market.”<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p><p>In 1985, Masco purchased the remaining outstanding stock of Flint &amp; Walling, Inc (after purchasing 30% in 1971 and controlling interest in 1978).
</p><p>In 1986, Masco started a home furnishings division by buying Henredon for $298 million and Drexel Heritage for $356 million. Other companies added to the division were <a href="/wiki/Lexington,_North_Carolina" title="Lexington, North Carolina">Lexington</a> Home Brands, Hickorycraft, Marge Carson, La Barge and Marbro in 1987, and fabric company Robert Allen/Ametex in 1988. In 1989, Universal Furniture, BenchCraft and Cal-Style were added in a $480 million deal, as well as Sunbury Textiles. The division added Berkline in 1994 and sold Marge Carson and Cal-Style in 1995.<sup id="cite_ref-plummets_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-plummets-10">[10]</a></sup></p><p>Masco reorganized its numerous companies; in 1988, the specialty products divisions of Masco Industries and Masco Corporation combined to form TriMas Corporation. Three years after this merger, TriMas was listed on the New York Stock Exchange (NYSE).  The remainder of Masco Industries became Masco Tech Inc. and in 1993 was listed on the NYSE. At that point in time three different Masco Companies existed: Masco Corp., Masco Tech., and TriMas Corp.
</p><p>Two years later, Masco entered the services business. TriMas and Masco Tech merged to form one company offering a wide array of products. Masco announced a 2 for 1 <a href="/wiki/Stock_split" title="Stock split">stock split</a>, which was the ninth time the Company had split its shares since 1960 and was the 40th year in a row that its dividends were increased. This merger left two companies, Masco Tech. and Masco Corp.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p><p>In 1996, Masco sold most of its Home Furnishings Division to investors for $1.1 billion, creating a new company called LifeStyle Furnishings International.<sup id="cite_ref-plummets_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-plummets-10">[10]</a></sup></p><p>In 1999, Masco involved itself in the <a href="/wiki/Architectural_coatings" title="Architectural coatings">architectural coatings</a> business, and the company's annual sales topped five billion dollars for the first time.
</p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span id="1999.E2.80.93present"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="1999–present">1999–present</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Masco&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: 1999–present" data-section="5" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>In 1999, Masco Corp acquired <a href="/wiki/Arrow_Fastener" class="mw-redirect" title="Arrow Fastener">Arrow Fastener Co., Inc.</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Hand_tool" title="Hand tool">hand tool</a> company expanding its business in the hardware category.
</p><p>In 2000, <a href="/wiki/Heartland_Industrial_Partners" title="Heartland Industrial Partners">Heartland Industrial Partners</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Private_equity" title="Private equity">private equity</a> firm, purchased Masco Tech and changed its name to <b>Metaldyne</b>. Metaldyne is headquartered in <a href="/wiki/Plymouth,_Michigan" title="Plymouth, Michigan">Plymouth, Michigan</a>. Masco also divested Flint &amp; Walling, Inc this year to Zoeller Company. 
</p><p>In 2001, Masco Corp., or simply Masco, entered the windows business and had operating profits beyond a billion dollars.<sup id="cite_ref-timeline_3-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-timeline-3">[3]</a></sup> Late in the year, Masco announced it would write down the value of its stake in LifeStyle Furnishings International by $460 million, since its <a href="/wiki/Equity_(finance)" title="Equity (finance)">equity</a> share was only 15 percent, with the rest being debt. LifeStyle, the third largest furniture maker in the United States with $2 billion in sales, had sold Universal Furniture to <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lacquer_Craft&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Lacquer Craft (page does not exist)">Lacquer Craft</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-plummets_10-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-plummets-10">[10]</a></sup> Then Masco announced <a href="/wiki/Furniture_Brands_International" title="Furniture Brands International">Furniture Brands International</a> would buy Drexel Heritage, Henredon and Maitland-Smith for $275 million, with management of Berkline, BenchCraft and Sunbury taking over those companies. LifeStyle continued to own Lexington, Robert Allen/Ametex and the Beacon Hill showrooms.<sup id="cite_ref-breakup_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-breakup-11">[11]</a></sup></p><p>In 2003, Masco's annual sales topped ten billion dollars and Masco increased its quarterly dividend for the 47th consecutive year. This ranked them in the top ten publicly owned companies achieving increased annual consecutive dividends.<sup id="cite_ref-timeline_3-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-timeline-3">[3]</a></sup></p><p>Throughout his time with the company, Richard Manoogian has achieved a net worth of approximately $750 million. He has been ranked as high as number five on the <a href="/wiki/Forbes_400" title="Forbes 400">Forbes 400</a> list of richest manufacturers.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> The Manoogian family is no longer involved in the management of the company.
</p><p>In 2015, the company announced it would relocate its headquarters from its facility in Taylor, Michigan to Livonia due to downsizing after spinning-off some divisions.<sup id="cite_ref-relo_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-relo-8">[8]</a></sup> Company president Keith Allman opened the new site on July 31, 2017.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
