<p>Regions is ranked 460th on the <a href="/wiki/Fortune_500" title="Fortune 500">Fortune 500</a> and is component headquartered in <a href="/wiki/Alabama" title="Alabama">Alabama</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Regions is also on the <a href="/wiki/List_of_largest_banks_in_the_United_States" title="List of largest banks in the United States">list of largest banks in the United States</a>.
</p><p>Regions is the largest deposit holder in Alabama and <a href="/wiki/Tennessee" title="Tennessee">Tennessee</a>. It is also one of the largest deposit holders in <a href="/wiki/Arkansas" title="Arkansas">Arkansas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Louisiana" title="Louisiana">Louisiana</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mississippi" title="Mississippi">Mississippi</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Florida" title="Florida">Florida</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p><p>The company sponsors <a href="/wiki/Regions_Field" title="Regions Field">Regions Field</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Minor_League_Baseball" title="Minor League Baseball">Minor League Baseball</a> stadium in Birmingham, Alabama, and <a href="/wiki/The_Tradition" title="The Tradition">The Tradition</a> golf tournament. It sponsored the <a href="/wiki/Regions_Charity_Classic" title="Regions Charity Classic">Regions Charity Classic</a>, a golf tournament held in 2009 and 2010.
</p><p>The company has a partnership with <a href="/wiki/Operation_HOPE,_Inc." title="Operation HOPE, Inc.">Operation HOPE, Inc.</a> to provide free financial education to underserved community.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> In 2019, the company expanded its <a href="/wiki/Workday,_Inc." title="Workday, Inc.">Workday, Inc.</a> system in partnership with <a href="/wiki/EnterpriseAlumni" title="EnterpriseAlumni">EnterpriseAlumni</a> to engage and enable ex-employees to extend their corporate social responsibility initiatives.
</p>

</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(1)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Regions_Financial_Corporation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History" data-section="1" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-1 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-1">
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Regions_footprint.png" class="image"><noscript><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Regions_footprint.png/220px-Regions_footprint.png" decoding="async" width="220" height="180" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="1588" data-file-height="1296"></noscript><span class="lazy-image-placeholder" style="width: 220px;height: 180px;" data-src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Regions_footprint.png/220px-Regions_footprint.png" data-alt="" data-width="220" data-height="180" data-class="thumbimage"> </span></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Regions_footprint.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/File:RegionsFootprint.jpg" class="image"><noscript><img alt="RegionsFootprint.jpg" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/1/18/RegionsFootprint.jpg/225px-RegionsFootprint.jpg" decoding="async" width="225" height="169" data-file-width="512" data-file-height="384"></noscript><span class="lazy-image-placeholder" style="width: 225px;height: 169px;" data-src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/1/18/RegionsFootprint.jpg/225px-RegionsFootprint.jpg" data-alt="RegionsFootprint.jpg" data-width="225" data-height="169"> </span></a><br>Regions Geographic Footprint</div></div></div>
<p>Regions Financial Corporation, formerly known as First Alabama Bancshares, was founded on July 13, 1971 with the merger of three Alabama banks: First National Bank of <a href="/wiki/Montgomery,_Alabama" title="Montgomery, Alabama">Montgomery, Alabama</a> (opened 1871), Exchange Security Bank of <a href="/wiki/Birmingham,_Alabama" title="Birmingham, Alabama">Birmingham, Alabama</a> (opened 1928), and First National Bank of <a href="/wiki/Huntsville,_Alabama" title="Huntsville, Alabama">Huntsville, Alabama</a> (opened 1856).<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> The headquarters of First National Bank of Huntsville was a historic building built in 1835.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> It served as a hospital for Union soldiers during the <a href="/wiki/American_Civil_War" title="American Civil War">American Civil War</a>, and once held a rifle owned by <a href="/wiki/Frank_James" title="Frank James">Frank James</a> as collateral for bail money when he was incarcerated across the street in the Madison County Jail.
</p><p>Until their formal merger in March 1985, under revised banking regulations, the banks continued to operate independently.
</p><p>In 1986, changes in the Interstate Banking Bill allowed bank holding companies to purchase bank branches outside the state in which they were chartered. First Alabama Bancshares expanded its operations first into <a href="/wiki/Florida" title="Florida">Florida</a>, continuing into <a href="/wiki/Georgia_(U.S._state)" title="Georgia (U.S. state)">Georgia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tennessee" title="Tennessee">Tennessee</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Arkansas" title="Arkansas">Arkansas</a>. In 1994, to reflect its growth into a regional company, First Alabama Bancshares changed its name to Regions Financial Corporation and the name of its banking subsidiary to Regions Bank.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p><p>Regions added banking branches in Alabama, Georgia, Tennessee, Florida, South Carolina, Texas, Louisiana, and Arkansas. The name "Regions" was purchased from First Commercial Corporation, the Arkansas Bank that Regions subsequently purchased in 1998.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p><p>In 2001, Regions acquired Rebsamen Insurance Company, which was renamed Regions Insurance Group.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:171px;"><a href="/wiki/File:MK_Logo.gif" class="image"><noscript><img alt="MK Logo.gif" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/5/59/MK_Logo.gif" decoding="async" width="169" height="56" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="169" data-file-height="56"></noscript><span class="lazy-image-placeholder" style="width: 169px;height: 56px;" data-src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/5/59/MK_Logo.gif" data-alt="MK Logo.gif" data-width="169" data-height="56" data-class="thumbimage"> </span></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"></div></div></div>
<p>In 2001, Regions acquired <a href="/wiki/Morgan_Keegan_%26_Company" class="mw-redirect" title="Morgan Keegan &amp; Company">Morgan Keegan &amp; Company</a> for $789 million.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> In January 2012, Regions sold Morgan Keegan to <a href="/wiki/Raymond_James" class="mw-redirect" title="Raymond James">Raymond James</a> for $930 million.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> The trust department was retained by Regions and now operates as <a href="/wiki/Regions_Trust" title="Regions Trust">Regions Trust</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p><p>In 2002, the company announced that it will list its common stock on the <a href="/wiki/New_York_Stock_Exchange" title="New York Stock Exchange">New York Stock Exchange</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p><p>In 2002, Regions acquired Independence Bank for approximately $24 million in cash.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p><p>On January 24, 2004, Regions merged with <a href="/wiki/Memphis,_Tennessee" title="Memphis, Tennessee">Memphis, Tennessee</a> based <a href="/wiki/Union_Planters_Bank" class="mw-redirect" title="Union Planters Bank">Union Planters Bank</a> in a $5.9 billion transaction. <a href="/wiki/Jackson_W._Moore" title="Jackson W. Moore">Jackson W. Moore</a>, the former CEO of Union Planters, became CEO of the merged company. He suffered a stroke after the merger closed, but was still able to assume his new post upon recovery. After the merger, Regions adopted Union Planters' former logo of a young cotton plant and used it until the AmSouth conversion. The merger significantly increased Regions' footprint in Tennessee; Union Planters had been the largest Tennessee-based bank.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p><p>In 2006, Regions acquired <a href="/wiki/AmSouth_Bancorporation" title="AmSouth Bancorporation">AmSouth Bancorporation</a>, another Birmingham based bank, in a $10 billion transaction. While Regions was the surviving company, the merged entity adopted AmSouth's corporate structure.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p><p>In 2008, Regions Bank received a $3.5 billion loan as part of the <a href="/wiki/Troubled_Asset_Relief_Program" title="Troubled Asset Relief Program">Troubled Asset Relief Program</a>. On April 4, 2012 Regions repaid the $3.5 billion loan.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p><p>On August 29, 2008, as a result of the <a href="/wiki/Financial_crisis_of_2007%E2%80%932008" class="mw-redirect" title="Financial crisis of 2007–2008">financial crisis of 2007–2008</a>, Integrity Bank of <a href="/wiki/Alpharetta,_Georgia" title="Alpharetta, Georgia">Alpharetta, Georgia</a> was placed into receivership by the <a href="/wiki/Federal_Deposit_Insurance_Corporation" title="Federal Deposit Insurance Corporation">Federal Deposit Insurance Corporation</a> and Regions Bank assumed its operations.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p><p>In February 2009, FirstBank Financial Services of McDonough, Georgia, was also placed into receivership by the FDIC and Regions Bank assumed its operations.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p><p>In July 2018, the company sold its insurance operations to <a href="/wiki/BB%26T" class="mw-redirect" title="BB&amp;T">BB&amp;T</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(2)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Criticism">Criticism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Regions_Financial_Corporation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Criticism" data-section="2" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-2 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-2">
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Customer_fraud">Customer fraud</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Regions_Financial_Corporation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Customer fraud" data-section="3" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>In 2011, Regions paid $200 million to settle with the <a href="/wiki/U.S._Securities_and_Exchange_Commission" title="U.S. Securities and Exchange Commission">U.S. Securities and Exchange Commission</a> over mispricing risky mortgage-backed bonds in its conservative mutual funds in its Morgan Keegan subsidiary.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Overdraft_fees">Overdraft fees</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Regions_Financial_Corporation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Overdraft fees" data-section="4" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>In April 2015, Regions was fined $7.5 million by the <a href="/wiki/Consumer_Financial_Protection_Bureau" title="Consumer Financial Protection Bureau">Consumer Financial Protection Bureau</a> for charging consumers with inappropriate or illegal overdraft fees.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> Regions did not obtain affirmative opt-ins from charging overdraft fees on ATM and point of sale transactions.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> The CFPB also found that Regions misrepresented overdraft and non-sufficient fund fees related to the bank's short-term loan program.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
