<p><b>Lockheed Martin Corporation</b> is an <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">American</a> global <a href="/wiki/Aerospace_manufacturer" title="Aerospace manufacturer">aerospace</a>, <a href="/wiki/Military" title="Military">defense</a>, <a href="/wiki/Information_security" title="Information security">security</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Technology" title="Technology">advanced technologies</a> company with worldwide interests. It was formed by the <a href="/wiki/Horizontal_integration" title="Horizontal integration">merger</a> of <a href="/wiki/Lockheed_Corporation" title="Lockheed Corporation">Lockheed Corporation</a> with <a href="/wiki/Martin_Marietta" title="Martin Marietta">Martin Marietta</a> in March 1995. It is headquartered in <a href="/wiki/North_Bethesda,_Maryland" title="North Bethesda, Maryland">North Bethesda</a>, <a href="/wiki/Maryland" title="Maryland">Maryland</a>, in the <a href="/wiki/Washington_Metropolitan_Area" class="mw-redirect" title="Washington Metropolitan Area">Washington, D.C., area</a>. It is the world's largest <a href="/wiki/Defense_contractor" class="mw-redirect" title="Defense contractor">defense contractor</a>, based on revenue for fiscal year 2014.<sup id="cite_ref-Top_20_Defense_Contractors_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Top_20_Defense_Contractors-4">[4]</a></sup> In 2013, 78% of Lockheed Martin's revenues came from military sales;<sup id="cite_ref-DefenceIQ_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DefenceIQ-5">[5]</a></sup> it topped the <a href="/wiki/Top_100_US_Federal_Contractors" class="mw-redirect" title="Top 100 US Federal Contractors">list of US federal government contractors</a> and received nearly 10% of the funds paid out by the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Defense" title="United States Department of Defense">Pentagon</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> In 2009, US government contracts accounted for $38.4 billion (85%), foreign government contracts for $5.8 billion (13%), and commercial and other contracts for $900 million (2%).<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p><p>Half of the company’s annual sales are to the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Defense" title="United States Department of Defense">U.S. Department of Defense</a>. Lockheed Martin is also a contractor for the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Energy" title="United States Department of Energy">U.S. Department of Energy</a> and the National Aeronautics and Space Administration (<a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p><p>Lockheed Martin operates in four business segments: <a href="/wiki/Lockheed_Martin_Aeronautics" title="Lockheed Martin Aeronautics">Aeronautics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lockheed_Martin_Missiles_%26_Fire_Control" class="mw-redirect" title="Lockheed Martin Missiles &amp; Fire Control">Missiles and Fire Control</a> (MFC), <a href="/wiki/Lockheed_Martin_Mission_Systems_and_Sensors" class="mw-redirect" title="Lockheed Martin Mission Systems and Sensors">Rotary and Mission Systems</a> (RMS), and <a href="/wiki/Lockheed_Martin_Space" class="mw-redirect" title="Lockheed Martin Space">Space</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Lockheed_Martin-2016_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lockheed_Martin-2016-9">[9]</a></sup> The company has received the <a href="/wiki/Collier_Trophy" title="Collier Trophy">Collier Trophy</a> six times, including in 2001 for being part of developing the <a href="/wiki/Lockheed_Martin_X-35" title="Lockheed Martin X-35">X-35</a>/<a href="/wiki/Lockheed_Martin_F-35_Lightning_II" title="Lockheed Martin F-35 Lightning II">F-35B</a> <a href="/wiki/Rolls-Royce_LiftSystem" title="Rolls-Royce LiftSystem">LiftFan Propulsion System</a><sup id="cite_ref-LMCollier_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LMCollier-10">[10]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-LiftFanPatent2_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LiftFanPatent2-11">[11]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Collier_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Collier-12">[12]</a></sup> and most recently in 2018 for the Automatic Ground Collision Avoidance System (Auto-GCAS). Lockheed Martin is currently developing the <a href="/wiki/Lockheed_Martin_F-35_Lightning_II" title="Lockheed Martin F-35 Lightning II">F-35 Lightning II</a> and leads the international supply chain, leads the team for the development and implementation of technology solutions for the new <a href="/wiki/USAF" class="mw-redirect" title="USAF">USAF</a> <a href="/wiki/Space_Fence" title="Space Fence">Space Fence</a> (<a href="/wiki/AFSSS" class="mw-redirect" title="AFSSS">AFSSS</a> replacement),<sup id="cite_ref-SpaceFence_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SpaceFence-13">[13]</a></sup> and is the primary contractor for the development of the <a href="/wiki/Orion_(spacecraft)" title="Orion (spacecraft)">Orion</a> command module.<sup id="cite_ref-OrionSpacecraft_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OrionSpacecraft-14">[14]</a></sup> The company also invests in healthcare systems, <a href="/wiki/Renewable_energy" title="Renewable energy">renewable energy systems</a>, <a href="/wiki/Smart_grid" title="Smart grid">intelligent energy distribution</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_fusion" title="Nuclear fusion">compact nuclear fusion</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-LockheedMartinFusion_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LockheedMartinFusion-15">[15]</a></sup></p>

</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(1)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lockheed_Martin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History" data-section="1" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-1 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-1">
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="1990s">1990s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lockheed_Martin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: 1990s" data-section="2" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>Merger talks between <a href="/wiki/Lockheed_Corporation" title="Lockheed Corporation">Lockheed Corporation</a> and <a href="/wiki/Martin_Marietta" title="Martin Marietta">Martin Marietta</a> began in March 1994, with the companies announcing their $10 billion planned merger on August 30, 1994. The headquarters for the combined companies would be at Martin Marietta headquarters in <a href="/wiki/North_Bethesda,_Maryland" title="North Bethesda, Maryland">North Bethesda, Maryland</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> The deal was finalized on March 15, 1995, when the two companies' shareholders approved the merger.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> The segments of the two companies not retained by the new company formed the basis for the <a href="/wiki/L-3_Communications" class="mw-redirect" title="L-3 Communications">L-3 Communications</a>, a mid-size defense contractor in its own right. Lockheed Martin also later spun off the materials company <a href="/wiki/Martin_Marietta_Materials" class="mw-redirect" title="Martin Marietta Materials">Martin Marietta Materials</a>.
</p><p>The company reportedly received billions of dollars in subsidies to complete the merger.  Executives also received large bonuses directly from the government as a result of the merger.  <a href="/wiki/Norman_R._Augustine" title="Norman R. Augustine">Norman R. Augustine</a> who was at the time CEO of Martin Marietta received an $8.2 million bonus.  This was strongly opposed by Senator <a href="/wiki/Bernie_Sanders" title="Bernie Sanders">Bernie Sanders</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p><p>Both companies contributed important products to the new portfolio. Lockheed products included the <a href="/wiki/Trident_missile" class="mw-redirect" title="Trident missile">Trident missile</a>, <a href="/wiki/P-3_Orion" class="mw-redirect" title="P-3 Orion">P-3 Orion</a> <a href="/wiki/Maritime_patrol_aircraft" title="Maritime patrol aircraft">maritime patrol aircraft</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lockheed_U-2" title="Lockheed U-2">U-2</a> and <a href="/wiki/SR-71" class="mw-redirect" title="SR-71">SR-71</a> <a href="/wiki/Reconnaissance_aircraft" title="Reconnaissance aircraft">reconnaissance airplanes</a>, <a href="/wiki/F-117" class="mw-redirect" title="F-117">F-117 Nighthawk</a>, <a href="/wiki/General_Dynamics_F-16_Fighting_Falcon" title="General Dynamics F-16 Fighting Falcon">F-16 Fighting Falcon</a>, <a href="/wiki/F-22_Raptor" class="mw-redirect" title="F-22 Raptor">F-22 Raptor</a>, <a href="/wiki/C-130_Hercules" class="mw-redirect" title="C-130 Hercules">C-130 Hercules</a>, <a href="/wiki/A-4AR_Fightinghawk" class="mw-redirect" title="A-4AR Fightinghawk">A-4AR Fightinghawk</a> and the DSCS-3 satellite. Martin Marietta products included <a href="/wiki/Titan_(rocket_family)" title="Titan (rocket family)">Titan rockets</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sandia_National_Laboratories" title="Sandia National Laboratories">Sandia National Laboratories</a> (management contract acquired in 1993), <a href="/wiki/Space_Shuttle_external_tank" title="Space Shuttle external tank">Space Shuttle External Tank</a>, <a href="/wiki/Viking_1" title="Viking 1">Viking 1</a> and <a href="/wiki/Viking_2" title="Viking 2">Viking 2</a> landers, the Transfer Orbit Stage (under subcontract to <a href="/wiki/Orbital_Sciences_Corporation" title="Orbital Sciences Corporation">Orbital Sciences Corporation</a>) and various satellite models.
</p><p>On April 22, 1996, Lockheed Martin completed the acquisition of <a href="/wiki/Loral_Corporation" title="Loral Corporation">Loral Corporation</a>'s defense electronics and system integration businesses for $9.1 billion, the deal having been announced in January. The remainder of Loral became <a href="/wiki/Loral_Space_%26_Communications" title="Loral Space &amp; Communications">Loral Space &amp; Communications</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> Lockheed Martin abandoned plans for an $8.3 billion merger with <a href="/wiki/Northrop_Grumman" title="Northrop Grumman">Northrop Grumman</a> on July 16, 1998, due to government concerns over the potential strength of the new group; Lockheed/Northrop would have had control of 25% of the Department of Defense's procurement budget.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p><p>For the <a href="/wiki/Mars_Climate_Orbiter" title="Mars Climate Orbiter">Mars Climate Orbiter</a>, Lockheed Martin incorrectly provided NASA with software using measurements in <a href="/wiki/United_States_customary_units" title="United States customary units">US Customary force units</a> when metric was expected; this resulted in the loss of the Orbiter at a cost of $125 million.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup>  The development of the spacecraft cost $193.1 million.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p><p>In addition to their military products, in the 1990s Lockheed Martin developed the <a href="/wiki/Texture_mapping" title="Texture mapping">texture mapping</a> chip for the <a href="/wiki/Sega_Model_2" class="mw-redirect" title="Sega Model 2">Sega Model 2</a> <a href="/wiki/Arcade_system_board" title="Arcade system board">arcade system board</a> and the entire graphics system for the <a href="/wiki/Sega_Model_3" class="mw-redirect" title="Sega Model 3">Sega Model 3</a>, which were used to power some of the most popular arcade games of the time.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="2000s">2000s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lockheed_Martin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: 2000s" data-section="3" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Lockheed_Martin_headquarters.jpg" class="image"><noscript><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/38/Lockheed_Martin_headquarters.jpg/220px-Lockheed_Martin_headquarters.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="171" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="2147" data-file-height="1670"></noscript><span class="lazy-image-placeholder" style="width: 220px;height: 171px;" data-src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/38/Lockheed_Martin_headquarters.jpg/220px-Lockheed_Martin_headquarters.jpg" data-alt="" data-width="220" data-height="171" data-class="thumbimage"> </span></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Lockheed_Martin_headquarters.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Lockheed Martin's Center For Leadership Excellence (CLE) Building in North Bethesda, Maryland</div></div></div>
<p>In May 2001, Lockheed Martin sold <a href="/wiki/BAE_Systems_Platform_Solutions" class="mw-redirect" title="BAE Systems Platform Solutions">Lockheed Martin Control Systems</a> to <a href="/wiki/BAE_Systems" title="BAE Systems">BAE Systems</a>. On November 27, 2000, Lockheed completed the sale of its <a href="/wiki/Lockheed_Martin_Aerospace_Electronic_Systems" class="mw-redirect" title="Lockheed Martin Aerospace Electronic Systems">Aerospace Electronic Systems</a> business to BAE Systems for $1.67 billion, a deal announced in July 2000. This group encompassed <a href="/wiki/Sanders_Associates" title="Sanders Associates">Sanders Associates</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fairchild_Systems" title="Fairchild Systems">Fairchild Systems</a>, and Lockheed Martin Space Electronics &amp; Communications.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> In 2001, Lockheed Martin won the contract to build the <a href="/wiki/F-35_Lightning_II" class="mw-redirect" title="F-35 Lightning II">F-35 Lightning II</a>; this was the largest fighter aircraft procurement project since the F-16, with an initial order of 3,000 aircraft. In 2001, Lockheed Martin settled a nine–year investigation conducted by NASA's Office of Inspector General with the assistance of the Defense Contract Audit Agency. The company paid the United States government $7.1 million based on allegations that its predecessor, Lockheed Engineering Science Corporation, submitted false lease costs claims to NASA.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p><p>On May 12, 2006, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Washington_Post" title="The Washington Post">The Washington Post</a></i> reported that when Robert Stevens took control of Lockheed Martin in 2004, he faced the dilemma that within 10 years, 100,000 of the about 130,000 Lockheed Martin employees – more than three-quarters – would be retiring.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> On August 31, 2006, Lockheed Martin won a $3.9 billion contract from NASA to design and build the CEV capsule, later named <a href="/wiki/Orion_(Constellation_program)" class="mw-redirect" title="Orion (Constellation program)"><i>Orion</i></a> for the <a href="/wiki/Ares_I" title="Ares I">Ares I</a> rocket in the Constellation Program. In 2009, NASA reduced the capsule crew requirements from the initial six seats to four for transport to the International Space Station.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p><p>On August 13, 2008, Lockheed Martin acquired the government business unit of Nantero, Inc., a company that had developed methods and processes for incorporating <a href="/wiki/Carbon_nanotubes" class="mw-redirect" title="Carbon nanotubes">carbon nanotubes</a> in next-generation electronic devices.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Lockheed_Martin_Acquires_Nantero_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lockheed_Martin_Acquires_Nantero-31">[31]</a></sup>  In 2009, Lockheed Martin bought Unitech.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p>

<p>In January 2020 the <a href="/wiki/Naval_Sea_Systems_Command" title="Naval Sea Systems Command">Naval Sea Systems Command</a> awarded Lockheed Martin with a $138 million contract related with the AEGIS Combat System Engineering Agent (CSEA). The LMT Rotary and Mission Systems (RMS) unit of the company is to develop, integrate, test, and deliver the AEGIS Advanced Capability Build (ACB) 20 integrated combat system. Martin will work on the AEGIS in New Jersey. The project is expected to be completed by December 2020.<sup id="cite_ref-67" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup></p><p>In January 2020, the Pentagon found at least 800 software defects in the Lockheed Martin's F-35 fighter jets owned by the US Armed Forces during an annual review. <p>Lockheed Martin received $36 billion in government contracts in 2008 alone, more than any company in history. It does work for more than two dozen government agencies from the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Defense" title="United States Department of Defense">Department of Defense</a> and the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Energy" title="United States Department of Energy">Department of Energy</a> to the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Agriculture" title="United States Department of Agriculture">Department of Agriculture</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Environmental_Protection_Agency" class="mw-redirect" title="Environmental Protection Agency">Environmental Protection Agency</a>. It's involved in surveillance and information processing for the <a href="/wiki/CIA" class="mw-redirect" title="CIA">CIA</a>, the <a href="/wiki/FBI" class="mw-redirect" title="FBI">FBI</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Internal_Revenue_Service" title="Internal Revenue Service">Internal Revenue Service</a> (IRS), the <a href="/wiki/National_Security_Agency" title="National Security Agency">National Security Agency</a> (NSA), <a href="/wiki/The_Pentagon" title="The Pentagon">The Pentagon</a>, the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Census_Bureau" title="United States Census Bureau">Census Bureau</a> and the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Postal_Service" title="United States Postal Service">Postal Service</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-MoJo_91-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MoJo-91">[91]</a></sup></p><p>In October 2013, Lockheed announced it expected to finalize a $2.2 billion contract with the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Air_Force" title="United States Air Force">United States Air Force</a> for two advanced military communications satellites.<sup id="cite_ref-92" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-92">[92]</a></sup></p><p>Lockheed Martin has already begun to help the military transition to <a href="/wiki/Renewable_energy" title="Renewable energy">renewable energy</a> sources with solar <a href="/wiki/Photovoltaic" class="mw-redirect" title="Photovoltaic">photovoltaic</a> powered <a href="/wiki/Microgrids" class="mw-redirect" title="Microgrids">microgrids</a><sup id="cite_ref-93" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-93">[93]</a></sup> and as the military aims to reach 25% renewable energy by 2025<sup id="cite_ref-94" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-94">[94]</a></sup> in order to improve <a href="/wiki/National_security" title="National security">national security</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-95" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-95">[95]</a></sup></p>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(4)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Corruption_investigations">Corruption investigations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lockheed_Martin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Corruption investigations" data-section="8" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-4 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-4">
<p>On March 3, 2012, the U.S. Justice Department said that Lockheed Martin had agreed to settle allegations that the defense contractor had sold overpriced perishable tools used on many contracts. The DoJ said the allegations were based specifically on the subsidiary Tools &amp; Metals Inc's inflation of costs between 1998 and 2005, which Lockheed Martin then passed on to the U.S. government under its contracts. Further, in March 2006, Todd B. Loftis, a former TMI president, was sentenced to 87 months in prison and ordered to pay a fine of $20 million following his guilty plea.<sup id="cite_ref-96" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-96">[96]</a></sup></p><p>On February 20, 2013, Lockheed Martin Corp complied with the <a href="/wiki/U.S._District_Court" class="mw-redirect" title="U.S. District Court">U.S. District Court</a> in New York, agreeing to pay a $19.5 million lawsuit to conclude a <a href="/wiki/Securities_fraud" title="Securities fraud">securities fraud</a> <a href="/wiki/Class-action" class="mw-redirect" title="Class-action">class-action</a> legal battle that had accused the company of deceiving shareholders in regards to expectations for the company's information technology division.<sup id="cite_ref-97" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-97">[97]</a></sup></p><p>On December 20, 2014, Lockheed Martin Integrated Systems agreed to settle a <a href="/wiki/False_Claims_Act" title="False Claims Act">False Claims Act</a> lawsuit paying $27.5 million to finalize allegations that it had knowingly overbilled the taxpayer for work performed by company staff who did not hold the relevant, essential qualifications for the contract.<sup id="cite_ref-98" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-98">[98]</a></sup></p>

<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Criticism">Criticism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lockheed_Martin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Criticism" data-section="23" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>Lockheed Martin is listed as the largest U.S. government contractor and ranks first for number of incidents, and fifth for size of settlements on the 'contractor misconduct' database maintained by the <a href="/wiki/Project_on_Government_Oversight" class="mw-redirect" title="Project on Government Oversight">Project on Government Oversight</a>, a Washington, D.C.-based watchdog group. Since 1995, the company has agreed to pay $676.8 million to settle 88 instances of misconduct.<sup id="cite_ref-105" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-105">[105]</a></sup></p><p>In 2013, Lt. Gen. Christopher Bogdan criticized the company's F-35 fighter program. The general said: "I want them both to start behaving like they want to be around for 40 years...I want them to take on some of the risk of this program. I want them to invest in cost reductions. I want them to do the things that will build a better relationship. I'm not getting all that love yet."<sup id="cite_ref-106" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-106">[106]</a></sup> The criticism comes in the wake of previous criticism from former <a href="/wiki/United_States_Secretary_of_Defense" title="United States Secretary of Defense">Defense Secretary</a> <a href="/wiki/Robert_Gates" title="Robert Gates">Robert Gates</a> regarding the same program.<sup id="cite_ref-107" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-107">[107]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Lobbying">Lobbying</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lockheed_Martin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Lobbying" data-section="24" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>According to the magazine <i><a href="/wiki/Politico" title="Politico">Politico</a></i>, Lockheed Martin has "a political network that is already the envy of its competitors", and its contracts enjoy wide bipartisan support in the U.S. Congress thanks to it having "perfected the strategy of spreading jobs on weapons programs in key states and congressional districts".<sup id="cite_ref-108" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-108">[108]</a></sup> The company's 2010 lobbying expenditure by the third quarter was $9.9 million (2009 total: $13.7 million).<sup id="cite_ref-109" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-109">[109]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-110" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-110">[110]</a></sup></p><p>Through its <a href="/wiki/Political_action_committee" title="Political action committee">political action committee</a> (PAC), the company provides low levels of financial support to candidates who advocate national defense and relevant business issues.<sup id="cite_ref-111" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-111">[111]</a></sup> It was the largest contributor to the <a href="/wiki/House_Armed_Services_Committee" class="mw-redirect" title="House Armed Services Committee">House Armed Services Committee</a> chairman, <a href="/wiki/Republican_Party_(United_States)" title="Republican Party (United States)">Republican</a> <a href="/wiki/Buck_McKeon" title="Buck McKeon">Buck McKeon</a> of California with over $50,000 donated in the election cycle as of January 2011. It also was the top donor to Sen. <a href="/wiki/Daniel_Inouye" title="Daniel Inouye">Daniel Inouye</a> (D-HI), the chair of the <a href="/wiki/Senate_Appropriations_Committee" class="mw-redirect" title="Senate Appropriations Committee">Senate Appropriations Committee</a> before his death in 2012.<sup id="cite_ref-MoJo_91-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MoJo-91">[91]</a></sup></p><p>Lockheed Martin Employees Political Action Committee is one of the 50 largest in the country, according to FEC data. With contributions from 3,000 employees, it donates $500,000 a year to about 260 House and Senate candidates.<sup id="cite_ref-112" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-112">[112]</a></sup></p>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(7)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Management">Management</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lockheed_Martin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Management" data-section="25" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-7 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-7">
<p>Senior management consists of the <a href="/wiki/CEO" class="mw-redirect" title="CEO">CEO</a>, <a href="/wiki/CFO" class="mw-redirect" title="CFO">CFO</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Executive_Vice_President" class="mw-redirect" title="Executive Vice President">Executive Vice Presidents</a> (EVPs) of four business areas.<sup id="cite_ref-113" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-113">[113]</a></sup> <sup id="cite_ref-114" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-114">[114]</a></sup> The EVPs are responsible for managing major programs.
</p><p>On March 16, 2020, Lockheed announced that CEO <a href="/wiki/Marillyn_Hewson" title="Marillyn Hewson">Marillyn Hewson</a> would become executive chair and be succeeded as CEO by James Taiclet on June 15; Taiclet is currently the head of <a href="/wiki/American_Tower" title="American Tower">American Tower</a>, and was previously the president of <a href="/wiki/Honeywell_Aerospace" title="Honeywell Aerospace">Honeywell Aerospace</a> and before that a VP at <a href="/wiki/United_Technologies" title="United Technologies">United Technologies</a>. Lockheed also announced that it would create the <a href="/wiki/Chief_operating_officer" title="Chief operating officer">chief operating officer</a> role, to which current EVP Frank A. St John would be promoted.<sup id="cite_ref-115" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-115">[115]</a></sup></p><p>Employees in each program are organized into four tiers: Tier1 –Program Manager/VP, Tier2-Functional Teams (Finance, Chief Engineer, Quality, Operations, etc.), Tier3-Integrated Product Teams (IPTs) (Weapon System Development, Weapon System Integration, etc.), and Tier4-detailed product development.  Floor or touch workers belong to component assembly teams.<sup id="cite_ref-116" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-116">[116]</a></sup> Lockheed Martin manages and maintains its relationship with these touch workers through its supervisors and unions.<sup id="cite_ref-117" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-117">[117]</a></sup></p><p>Lockheed Martin manages employees through its Full Spectrum Leadership and LM21 programs.<sup id="cite_ref-118" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-118">[118]</a></sup> The LM21 program relies on <a href="/wiki/Six_Sigma" title="Six Sigma">Six Sigma</a> principles, which are techniques to improve efficiency. Senior management constructs leadership councils and assigns managers to facilitate <a href="/wiki/Kaizen" title="Kaizen">Kaizen</a> events, which target specific processes for improvement.  A manager facilitates teams and processes stakeholders and suppliers to streamline process implementation.<sup id="cite_ref-119" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-119">[119]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-120" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-120">[120]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-libsys.uah.edu_121-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-libsys.uah.edu-121">[121]</a></sup></p><p>Tier2 Functional Leads and Tier3 IPT Leads report to Tier1. IPT leads are responsible for entire systems or products defined by the contract's Statement of Work.<sup id="cite_ref-libsys.uah.edu_121-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-libsys.uah.edu-121">[121]</a></sup></p><p>To control quality, Lockheed Martin trains and builds IPT teams.<sup id="cite_ref-libsys.uah.edu_121-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-libsys.uah.edu-121">[121]</a></sup> and ensures that work is executed correctly through a Technical Performance Measure (TPM) system which emphasizes its Lean and 6 Sigma processes. Middle management uses commitment mechanisms that parallel high commitment and human relations theory.<sup id="cite_ref-122" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-122">[122]</a></sup></p><p>Floor employees assemble aircraft using Flow-to-<a href="/wiki/Takt_time" title="Takt time">takt</a> lean manufacturing process<sup id="cite_ref-123" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-123">[123]</a></sup> which uses properties from both division of labor and scientific management.  By separating tasks based on parts, Lockheed Martin utilizes the division of labor theory,<sup id="cite_ref-124" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-124">[124]</a></sup> specialization on a specific area creates efficiency.
</p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Double_Helix_methodology">Double Helix methodology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lockheed_Martin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Double Helix methodology" data-section="26" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>The "Double Helix methodology" is a systems development methodology used by Lockheed Martin. It combines experimentation, technology, and a warfighter's <a href="/wiki/Concept_of_Operations" class="mw-redirect" title="Concept of Operations">concept of operations</a> to create new tactics and weapons.<sup id="cite_ref-DoubleHelixInfo_125-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DoubleHelixInfo-125">[125]</a></sup></p>
