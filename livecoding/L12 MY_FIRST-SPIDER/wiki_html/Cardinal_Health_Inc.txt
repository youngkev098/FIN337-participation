</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(1)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cardinal_Health&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History" data-section="1" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-1 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-1">
<p>Founded in 1971 as Cardinal Foods by <a href="/wiki/Robert_D._Walter" title="Robert D. Walter">Robert D. Walter</a>, the company was initially a food <a href="/wiki/Wholesaling" title="Wholesaling">wholesaler</a>. After acquiring the Bailey Drug Company in 1979, it began wholesaling drugs as Cardinal Distribution, Incorporated. The company went public on the <a href="/wiki/NASDAQ" class="mw-redirect" title="NASDAQ">NASDAQ</a> stock exchange in 1983, and subsequently began a long string of <a href="/wiki/Takeover" title="Takeover">acquisitions</a> and <a href="/wiki/Merger" class="mw-redirect" title="Merger">mergers</a>.
</p><p>In 1988, Walter sold Cardinal Health's food operations to <a href="/wiki/Roundy%27s" title="Roundy's">Roundy's</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-jhc_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-jhc-5">[5]</a></sup> From 1991 to 1996, the company's sales grew from $1.2 billion to $8.9 billion.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> In a case study for <i><a href="/wiki/Harvard_Business_Review" title="Harvard Business Review">Harvard Business Review</a></i>, Mary Teagarden describes Cardinal Health in this period as "one of a handful of large U.S. companies that had achieved earnings-per-share growth in excess of 20 percent for 15 years straight."<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> The company changed its name to Cardinal Health in 1994, and became the third-largest pharmaceutical wholesaler in the United States.<sup id="cite_ref-jhc_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-jhc-5">[5]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p><p>R. Kerry Clark, a former executive and vice chairman at <a href="/wiki/Procter_%26_Gamble" title="Procter &amp; Gamble">Procter &amp; Gamble</a>, was appointed president and <a href="/wiki/Chief_executive_officer" title="Chief executive officer">CEO</a> in April 2006, with Robert D. Walter retaining Chairmanship of the board.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> In September 2008, the company announced Clark and Walter would retire and <a href="/wiki/George_S._Barrett" title="George S. Barrett">George S. Barrett</a> would become the chairman and CEO.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p><p>Cardinal Health completed the spin-off of its clinical and medical products businesses into an independent medical technology company called <a href="/wiki/CareFusion" title="CareFusion">CareFusion</a> in 2009 with David Schlotterbeck as CEO.<sup id="cite_ref-spinoff_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-spinoff-12">[12]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Cardinal Health is now traded on the <a href="/wiki/NYSE" class="mw-redirect" title="NYSE">NYSE</a> under symbol CAH.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> As of August 2017, it is ranked 15 on the <a href="/wiki/Fortune_500" title="Fortune 500">Fortune 500</a> list with FY2017 annual revenue of $121.5 billion. The firm employs 37,300 people worldwide.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p><p>In December 2013, it was announced that Cardinal Health would team up with <a href="/wiki/CVS_Caremark" title="CVS Caremark">CVS Caremark</a>, which would form the largest generic drug sourcing operation in the United States.<sup id="cite_ref-cvs_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cvs-16">[16]</a></sup> The venture was named Red Oak Sourcing and began operations in July 2014.<sup id="cite_ref-redoak_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-redoak-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Acquisitions">Acquisitions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cardinal_Health&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Acquisitions" data-section="2" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>In 1995, Medicine Shoppe International (St. Louis, est. 1970), the country's largest franchise of retail pharmacies, was acquired. The merger represented the first non-distribution acquisition by Cardinal Health.<sup id="cite_ref-idch_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-idch-18">[18]</a></sup></p><p>In 1996, Cardinal Health acquired <a href="/wiki/Pyxis_Corporation" title="Pyxis Corporation">Pyxis Corporation</a>, a company that developed <a href="/wiki/Automated_dispensing_cabinet" title="Automated dispensing cabinet">automated pill dispensers</a> for hospitals, for $867 million.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p><p>In a 1997 competition between Cardinal Health and <a href="/wiki/McKesson_Corporation" title="McKesson Corporation">McKesson Corporation</a>, Cardinal Health planned to purchase Bergen, to which McKesson responded with a bid to purchase Amerisource. Instead, Amerisource and Bergen merged into <a href="/wiki/AmerisourceBergen" title="AmerisourceBergen">AmerisourceBergen</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-reuters_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-reuters-20">[20]</a></sup> Later that year, Cardinal Health completed the acquisition of Owen Healthcare, the second-largest provider of pharmacy management services in the U.S. at the time.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p><p>In 1999, the firm acquired the Chicago-based medical products manufacturer and distributor, Allegiance Healthcare (formerly a division of <a href="/wiki/Baxter_Healthcare" class="mw-redirect" title="Baxter Healthcare">Baxter Healthcare</a>). Allegiance made surgical drapes, gloves, and gowns, and distributed customized arrangements of medical supplies (called "custom sterile packs" and "procedure-based delivery systems").<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> During 2001, the company spent approximately $30 billion on acquisitions, including Bindley Western Industries, wholesale distributor of pharmaceuticals based in <a href="/wiki/Indianapolis" title="Indianapolis">Indianapolis</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-idch_18-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-idch-18">[18]</a></sup></p><p>In April 2006, Cardinal Health purchased Niagara Falls-based ParMed Pharmaceuticals for $40.1 million. ParMed's focus of selling medicine in smaller quantities complements Cardinal Health's distribution to bigger hospital and drug store chains. In June 2007, the firm announced the completion of a <a href="/wiki/Tender_offer" title="Tender offer">tender offer</a> for VIASYS Healthcare.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p><p>In June 2010, Cardinal Health announced plans to expand its presence in specialty pharmaceutical services with an agreement to purchase Healthcare Solutions Holding for $517 million.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> In December 2010, the company acquired Kinray, one of the last independent pharmaceutical wholesalers in the United States, increasing Cardinal Health's presence in the independent pharmacy market by 40 percent.<sup id="cite_ref-wsj_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wsj-25">[25]</a></sup> Kinray had annual revenue of over $3.5 billion, and served about 2,000 independent retail pharmacy customers.<sup id="cite_ref-wsj_25-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wsj-25">[25]</a></sup> From 2010 to 2014, Cardinal Health acquired 18 companies including Yong Yu, a Chinese drug distributor. Cardinal Health teamed up with CVS to form Red Oak Sourcing, the largest generic drug sourcing operation in the United States, in July 2014, when the companies started buying generic drugs around the world to sell in U.S. markets.<sup id="cite_ref-dp14_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dp14-4">[4]</a></sup></p><p>In March 2015, Cardinal Health signed an agreement to acquire <a href="/wiki/Johnson_%26_Johnson" title="Johnson &amp; Johnson">Johnson &amp; Johnson</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Cordis_(medical)" title="Cordis (medical)">Cordis (medical)</a> division, a cardiology and endovascular device manufacturer, for $1.94 billion. Cordis' largest market is in the United States, but the manufacturer employs approximately 3,000 people around the world and 70 percent of its sales are international.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> The acquisition was completed on October 4, 2015.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p><p>In March 2015, Cardinal Health signed an agreement to acquire Tradex International, a Cleveland-based distributor of latex and other gloves used primarily in the health care and food service industries.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p><p>In April 2017, Cardinal Health announced the plan to acquire leading patient product portfolio from <a href="/wiki/Medtronic" title="Medtronic">Medtronic</a> for $6.1 billion.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> The acquisition was completed on July 30, 2017. The acquisition was funded with a combination of $4.5 billion in new senior unsecured notes, existing cash and borrowings under its existing credit arrangements.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(2)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Finances">Finances</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cardinal_Health&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Finances" data-section="3" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-2 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-2">

<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Restatements">Restatements</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cardinal_Health&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Restatements" data-section="5" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>In September 2004, Cardinal Health announced to restate past results for fiscal 2001, 2002, 2003, and the first three quarters of 2004 downward, after an accounting review and an ongoing federal investigation. In 2005, in connection with the Audit Committee's conclusions reached in September and October 2004, the company made certain reclassification and restatement adjustments to its fiscal 2004 and prior historical consolidated financial statements.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> According to the <i><a href="/wiki/Wall_Street_Journal" class="mw-redirect" title="Wall Street Journal">Wall Street Journal</a></i>, "Analysts called the restatement decision troubling, yet limited in scope."<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="FDA_action">FDA action</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cardinal_Health&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: FDA action" data-section="6" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>In August 2006, Cardinal Health ceased production of its Alaris SE <a href="/wiki/Infusion_pump" title="Infusion pump">infusion pump</a> after approximately 1300 units were seized by the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Food_and_Drug_Administration" class="mw-redirect" title="United States Food and Drug Administration">United States Food and Drug Administration</a> (FDA).<sup id="cite_ref-fdaraid_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fdaraid-37">[37]</a></sup> In February 2007, Cardinal Health signed a <a href="/wiki/Consent_decree" title="Consent decree">consent decree</a> with the FDA which promised procedures to guarantee the safety of the Alaris SE.<sup id="cite_ref-spinoff_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-spinoff-12">[12]</a></sup> After FDA inspections, Cardinal Health entered into a further consent agreement with the FDA in 2009.<sup id="cite_ref-spinoff_12-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-spinoff-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="DEA_investigation_into_Oxycodone_diversion">DEA investigation into Oxycodone diversion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cardinal_Health&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: DEA investigation into Oxycodone diversion" data-section="7" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Drug_diversion" title="Drug diversion">Drug diversion</a></div>
<p>In 2008, Cardinal Health agreed to pay $34 million in civil penalties to settle DEA allegations that it failed to report suspicious orders of hydrocodone. The fine followed a 10-month DEA suspension of a <a href="/wiki/Lakeland,_Florida" title="Lakeland, Florida">Lakeland, Florida</a> distribution facility and two others in New Jersey and Washington.<sup id="cite_ref-Schoenberg_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Schoenberg-38">[38]</a></sup> On February 2, 2012, the <a href="/wiki/Drug_Enforcement_Administration" title="Drug Enforcement Administration">Drug Enforcement Administration</a> again suspended the license of the firm's Lakeland distribution center to distribute controlled substances on charges that it had allowed four Florida pharmacies to purchase excessive amounts of controlled substances, in particular <a href="/wiki/Oxycodone" title="Oxycodone">oxycodone</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-UTF27_39-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-UTF27-39">[39]</a></sup></p><p>Cardinal Health obtained a restraining order against the suspension, but the suspension was upheld on February 29 by a Federal district court because the court agreed with the DEA that Cardinal Health's activities represented an "imminent danger to the public."<sup id="cite_ref-UTF29_40-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-UTF29-40">[40]</a></sup> The company stated that it blocked two of the pharmacies, (Brooks Pharmacy in <a href="/wiki/Bonita_Springs,_Florida" title="Bonita Springs, Florida">Bonita Springs, Florida</a>, and Gulf Coast Medical in <a href="/wiki/Panama_City,_Florida" title="Panama City, Florida">Panama City, Florida</a>), and notified the corporate owners of the two pharmacies that were part of national chains, two <a href="/wiki/CVS_Pharmacy" title="CVS Pharmacy">CVS</a> stores in <a href="/wiki/Sanford,_Florida" title="Sanford, Florida">Sanford, Florida</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-UTF27_39-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-UTF27-39">[39]</a></sup></p><p>In February 2012, Joseph Rannazzisi, chief of the <a href="/wiki/Drug_Enforcement_Administration" title="Drug Enforcement Administration">Drug Enforcement Administration</a>’s Office of Diversion Control, issued immediate suspension orders against Cardinal's supply of <a href="/wiki/Oxycodone" title="Oxycodone">oxycodone</a> to suspected pill mills.<sup id="cite_ref-WashPo16_41-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WashPo16-41">[41]</a></sup>  Deputy Attorney General <a href="/wiki/James_M._Cole" title="James M. Cole">James M. Cole</a> then called Rannazzisi to a meeting at Justice Department headquarters where Cole warned him “it made good sense to listen to what Cardinal had to say”.<sup id="cite_ref-WashPo16_41-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WashPo16-41">[41]</a></sup>  Rannazzisi was fired from the <a href="/wiki/Drug_diversion" title="Drug diversion">drug diversion</a> office in August 2015.<sup id="cite_ref-WashPo16_41-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WashPo16-41">[41]</a></sup>  Cardinal was fined $44 million.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup></p><p>Cardinal, alongside <a href="/wiki/McKesson_Corporation" title="McKesson Corporation">McKesson Corporation</a>, and <a href="/wiki/AmerisourceBergen" title="AmerisourceBergen">AmerisourceBergen</a>, spent $13 million lobbying Congress to pass Congressman <a href="/wiki/Tom_Marino" title="Tom Marino">Tom Marino</a>'s "Ensuring Patient Access and Effective Drug Enforcement Act".<sup id="cite_ref-WashPo16_41-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WashPo16-41">[41]</a></sup>  The bill, which increases the burden of proof enforcers need to show against drug distributors, was signed into law by President <a href="/wiki/Barack_Obama" title="Barack Obama">Barack Obama</a> in April 2016.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup></p>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(4)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Cardinal_Health_Foundation">Cardinal Health Foundation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cardinal_Health&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Cardinal Health Foundation" data-section="8" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-4 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-4">
<p>The Cardinal Health Foundation is the charitable arm of Cardinal Health. The company makes annual product donations of over $9 million through international relief organizations and provides up to $1,000 in matching funds for every Cardinal Health employee that makes a charitable donation.<sup id="cite_ref-biz11_44-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-biz11-44">[44]</a></sup> In 2008, the foundation established its E3 Grant Program.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> Over the past seven years, the Foundation has invested more than $7.15 million in funding to 241 hospitals, health systems or other health-related organizations.<sup id="cite_ref-google.com_46-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-google.com-46">[46]</a></sup></p><p>Cardinal Health also supports organizations such as <a href="/wiki/Ronald_McDonald_House_Charities" title="Ronald McDonald House Charities">Ronald McDonald House Charities</a>, and was named Benefactor of the Year at the 2011 Corporate Caring Awards.<sup id="cite_ref-biz11_44-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-biz11-44">[44]</a></sup> In 2015, the foundation contributed $3 million to the Solutions for Patient Safety project, which has raised over $11 million nationally for efforts to improve safety initiatives in children's hospitals.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup></p>
