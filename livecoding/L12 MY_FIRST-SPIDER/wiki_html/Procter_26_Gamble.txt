<p><b>The Procter &amp; Gamble Company</b> (<b>P&amp;G</b>) is an American multinational <a href="/wiki/Fast_moving_consumer_goods" class="mw-redirect" title="Fast moving consumer goods">consumer goods</a> <a href="/wiki/Corporation" title="Corporation">corporation</a> headquartered in <a href="/wiki/Cincinnati,_Ohio" class="mw-redirect" title="Cincinnati, Ohio">Cincinnati, Ohio</a>, founded in 1837 by <a href="/wiki/William_Procter_(industrialist)" title="William Procter (industrialist)">William Procter</a> and <a href="/wiki/James_Gamble_(industrialist)" title="James Gamble (industrialist)">James Gamble</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> It specializes in a wide range of personal health/consumer health, and <a href="/wiki/Personal_care" title="Personal care">personal care</a> and hygiene products; these products are organized into several segments including  Beauty; Grooming; Health Care; Fabric &amp; Home Care; and Baby, Feminine, &amp; Family Care. Before the sale of <a href="/wiki/Pringles" title="Pringles">Pringles</a> to <a href="/wiki/Kellogg%27s" title="Kellogg's">Kellogg's</a>, its product portfolio also included foods, snacks, and beverages.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> P&amp;G is incorporated in Ohio.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a </section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(1)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Procter_%26_Gamble&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History" data-section="1" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-1 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-1">
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Origins">Origins</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Procter_%26_Gamble&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Origins" data-section="2" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>Candlemaker William Procter, born in <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a>, and soapmaker James Gamble, born in <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a>, both emigrated from the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>. They settled in <a href="/wiki/Cincinnati,_Ohio" class="mw-redirect" title="Cincinnati, Ohio">Cincinnati, Ohio</a> initially and met when they married sisters Olivia and Elizabeth Norris.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Alexander Norris, their father-in-law, persuaded them to become business partners, and in 1837 Procter &amp; Gamble was created.
</p><p>In 1858–1859, sales reached $1 million. By that point, about 80 employees worked for Procter &amp; Gamble. During the <a href="/wiki/American_Civil_War" title="American Civil War">American Civil War</a>, the company won contracts to supply the <a href="/wiki/Union_Army" title="Union Army">Union Army</a> with soap and candles. In addition to the increased profits experienced during the war, the military contracts introduced soldiers from all over the country to Procter &amp; Gamble's products.
</p><p>In the 1880s, Procter &amp; Gamble began to market a new product, an inexpensive soap that floated in water.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-8">[8]</a></sup> The company called the soap <a href="/wiki/Ivory_(soap)" title="Ivory (soap)">Ivory</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-8">[8]</a></sup> William Arnett Procter, William Procter's grandson, began a <a href="/wiki/Profit-sharing" class="mw-redirect" title="Profit-sharing">profit-sharing</a> program for the company's <a href="/wiki/Workforce" title="Workforce">workforce</a> in 1887. By giving the workers a stake in the company, he correctly assumed that they would be less likely to go on strike.
</p><p>The company began to build factories in other locations in the United States because the demand for products had outgrown the capacity of the Cincinnati facilities. The company's leaders began to diversify its products, as well, and in 1911, began producing <a href="/wiki/Crisco" title="Crisco">Crisco</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Shortening" title="Shortening">shortening</a> made of <a href="/wiki/Vegetable_oils" class="mw-redirect" title="Vegetable oils">vegetable oils</a> rather than <a href="/wiki/Animal_fat" title="Animal fat">animal fats</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_8-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-8">[8]</a></sup> As radio became more popular in the 1920s and 1930s, the company sponsored a number of radio programs.
</p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="International_expansion">International expansion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Procter_%26_Gamble&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: International expansion" data-section="3" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>The company moved into other countries, both in terms of manufacturing and product sales, becoming an international corporation with its 1930 acquisition of the <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Hedley_Co." title="Thomas Hedley Co.">Thomas Hedley Co.</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-:0_8-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-8">[8]</a></sup> based in <a href="/wiki/Newcastle_upon_Tyne" title="Newcastle upon Tyne">Newcastle upon Tyne</a>, England. After this acquisition, Procter &amp; Gamble had their UK Headquarters at 'Hedley House' in <a href="/wiki/Newcastle_upon_Tyne" title="Newcastle upon Tyne">Newcastle upon Tyne</a>, until quite recently, when they moved to The Heights, <a href="/wiki/Brooklands" title="Brooklands">Brooklands</a>. Numerous new products and brand names were introduced over time, and Procter &amp; Gamble began branching out into new areas. The company introduced <a href="/wiki/Tide_(brand)" title="Tide (brand)">Tide</a> laundry <a href="/wiki/Detergent" title="Detergent">detergent</a> in 1946<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Prell" title="Prell">Prell</a> shampoo in 1947.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> In 1955, Procter &amp; Gamble began selling the first toothpaste to contain <a href="/wiki/Fluoride" title="Fluoride">fluoride</a>, known as <a href="/wiki/Crest_(toothpaste)" title="Crest (toothpaste)">Crest</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_8-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-8">[8]</a></sup> Branching out once again in 1957, the company purchased <a href="/wiki/Charmin" title="Charmin">Charmin</a> <a href="/wiki/Paper_mill" title="Paper mill">paper mills</a> and began manufacturing <a href="/wiki/Toilet_paper" title="Toilet paper">toilet paper</a> and other <a href="/wiki/Tissue_paper" title="Tissue paper">tissue paper</a> products. Once again focusing on laundry, Procter &amp; Gamble began making <a href="/wiki/Downy" title="Downy">Downy</a> <a href="/wiki/Fabric_softener" title="Fabric softener">fabric softener</a> in 1960 and Bounce fabric softener sheets in 1972.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p><p>One of the most revolutionary products to come out on the market was the company's disposable <a href="/wiki/Pampers" title="Pampers">Pampers</a> diaper, first test-marketed in 1961, the same year Procter &amp; Gamble came out with <a href="/wiki/Head_%26_Shoulders" title="Head &amp; Shoulders">Head &amp; Shoulders</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Prior to this point, <a href="/wiki/Disposable_diapers" class="mw-redirect" title="Disposable diapers">disposable diapers</a> were not popular, although <a href="/wiki/Johnson_%26_Johnson" title="Johnson &amp; Johnson">Johnson &amp; Johnson</a> had developed a product called Chux. Babies always wore cloth diapers, which were leaky and labor-intensive to wash. Pampers provided a convenient alternative, albeit at the environmental cost of more waste requiring <a href="/wiki/Landfill" title="Landfill">landfilling</a>. Amid the recent concerns parents have voiced on the ingredients in diapers, Pampers launched Pampers Pure collection in 2018, which is a "natural" diaper alternative.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Further_developments">Further developments</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Procter_%26_Gamble&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Further developments" data-section="4" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>Procter &amp; Gamble acquired a number of other companies that diversified its product line and significantly increased profits. These acquisitions included <a href="/wiki/Folgers_Coffee" class="mw-redirect" title="Folgers Coffee">Folgers Coffee</a>, Norwich Eaton Pharmaceuticals (the makers of <a href="/wiki/Pepto-Bismol" class="mw-redirect" title="Pepto-Bismol">Pepto-Bismol</a>), Richardson-Vicks, Noxell (<a href="/wiki/Noxzema" title="Noxzema">Noxzema</a>), Shulton's <a href="/wiki/Old_Spice" title="Old Spice">Old Spice</a>, <a href="/wiki/Max_Factor" title="Max Factor">Max Factor</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Iams" title="Iams">Iams</a> Company, and <a href="/wiki/Pantene" title="Pantene">Pantene</a>, among others. In 1994, the company made headlines for big losses resulting from levered positions in <a href="/wiki/Interest_rate_derivative" title="Interest rate derivative">interest rate derivatives</a>, and subsequently sued <a href="/wiki/Bankers_Trust" title="Bankers Trust">Bankers Trust</a> for fraud; this placed their management in the unusual position of testifying in court that they had entered into transactions that they were not capable of understanding. In 1996, P&amp;G again made headlines when the <a href="/wiki/Food_and_Drug_Administration" title="Food and Drug Administration">Food and Drug Administration</a> approved a new product developed by the company, <a href="/wiki/Olestra" title="Olestra">Olestra</a>. Also known by its brand name 'Olean', Olestra is a lower-calorie substitute for fat in cooking <a href="/wiki/Potato_chip" title="Potato chip">potato chips</a> and other snacks.
</p><p>In January 2005, P&amp;G announced the acquisition of <a href="/wiki/Gillette" title="Gillette">Gillette</a>, forming the largest consumer goods company and placing <a href="/wiki/Unilever" title="Unilever">Unilever</a> into second place.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> This added brands such as Gillette razors, <a href="/wiki/Duracell" title="Duracell">Duracell</a>, <a href="/wiki/Braun_(company)" title="Braun (company)">Braun</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Oral-B" title="Oral-B">Oral-B</a> to their stable. The acquisition was approved by the <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Federal_Trade_Commission" title="Federal Trade Commission">Federal Trade Commission</a>, with conditions to a spinoff of certain overlapping brands. P&amp;G agreed to sell its SpinBrush battery-operated <a href="/wiki/Electric_toothbrush" title="Electric toothbrush">electric toothbrush</a> business to <a href="/wiki/Church_%26_Dwight" title="Church &amp; Dwight">Church &amp; Dwight</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> and Gillette's <a href="/wiki/Rembrandt_toothpaste" title="Rembrandt toothpaste">Rembrandt toothpaste</a> line to <a href="/wiki/Johnson_%26_Johnson" title="Johnson &amp; Johnson">Johnson &amp; Johnson</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Deodorant" title="Deodorant">deodorant</a> brands <a href="/wiki/Right_Guard" title="Right Guard">Right Guard</a>, Soft and Dri, and Dry Idea were sold to <a href="/wiki/Dial_Corporation" class="mw-redirect" title="Dial Corporation">Dial Corporation</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> The companies officially merged on October 1, 2005. <a href="/wiki/Liquid_Paper" title="Liquid Paper">Liquid Paper</a> and Gillette's stationery division, <a href="/wiki/Paper_Mate" title="Paper Mate">Paper Mate</a>, were sold to <a href="/wiki/Newell_Rubbermaid" class="mw-redirect" title="Newell Rubbermaid">Newell Rubbermaid</a>. In 2008, P&amp;G branched into the record business with its sponsorship of Tag Records, as an endorsement for <a href="/wiki/TAG_Body_Spray" title="TAG Body Spray">TAG Body Spray</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p><p>P&amp;G's dominance in many categories of consumer products makes its <a href="/wiki/Brand_management" title="Brand management">brand management</a> decisions worthy of study.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> For example, P&amp;G's corporate strategists must account for the likelihood of one of their products <a href="/wiki/Cannibalization_(marketing)" title="Cannibalization (marketing)">cannibalizing the sales of another</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p><p>On August 25, 2009, the Ireland-based pharmaceutical company <a href="/wiki/Warner_Chilcott" title="Warner Chilcott">Warner Chilcott</a> announced they had bought P&amp;G's prescription-drug business for $3.1 billion.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p><p>P&amp;G exited the food business in 2012 when it sold its <a href="/wiki/Pringles" title="Pringles">Pringles</a> snack food business to <a href="/wiki/Kellogg%27s" title="Kellogg's">Kellogg's</a> for $2.75 billion after the $2.35 billion deal with former suitor <a href="/wiki/Diamond_Foods" title="Diamond Foods">Diamond Foods</a> fell short.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> The company had previously sold <a href="/wiki/Jif_(peanut_butter)" title="Jif (peanut butter)">Jif peanut butter</a>, Crisco shortening and oils, and <a href="/wiki/Folgers" title="Folgers">Folgers</a> coffee in separate transactions to <a href="/wiki/The_J.M._Smucker_Company" title="The J.M. Smucker Company">Smucker's</a>.
</p><p>In April 2014, the company sold its <a href="/wiki/Iams" title="Iams">Iams</a> pet food business in all markets excluding Europe to <a href="/wiki/Mars,_Incorporated" title="Mars, Incorporated">Mars, Inc.</a> for $2.9 billion.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> It sold the European Iams business to <a href="/wiki/Spectrum_Brands" title="Spectrum Brands">Spectrum Brands</a> in December 2014.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Restructuring">Restructuring</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Procter_%26_Gamble&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Restructuring" data-section="5" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>In August 2014, P&amp;G announced it was streamlining the company, dropping around 100 brands and concentrating on the remaining 65, which were producing 95% of the company's profits.<sup id="cite_ref-Coolidge_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Coolidge-5">[5]</a></sup></p><p>In March 2015, the company divested its <a href="/wiki/Vicks" title="Vicks">Vicks</a> VapoSteam U.S. liquid inhalant business to <a href="/wiki/Helen_of_Troy_Limited" title="Helen of Troy Limited">Helen of Troy</a>, part of a brand-restructuring operation. This deal was the first health-related divestiture under the brand-restructuring operation.The deal included fully paid-up license to the Vicks VapoSteam trademarks and the U.S. license of P&amp;G's Vicks VapoPad trademarks for scent pads. Most Vicks VapoSteam and VapoPads are used in Vicks humidifiers, vaporizers and other health care devices already marketed by Helen of Troy.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p><p>Later that same year in July, the company announced the sale of 43 of its beauty brands to <a href="/wiki/Coty,_Inc." title="Coty, Inc.">Coty</a>, a beauty-product manufacturer, in a US$13 billion deal. It cited sluggish growth of its beauty division as the reason for the divestiture.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> The sale was completed on October 3, 2016.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p><p>In February 2016, P&amp;G completed the transfer of <a href="/wiki/Duracell" title="Duracell">Duracell</a> to <a href="/wiki/Berkshire_Hathaway" title="Berkshire Hathaway">Berkshire Hathaway</a> through an exchange of shares.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p><p>In December 2018, Procter &amp; Gamble completed the acquisition of the consumer health division of <a href="/wiki/Merck_Group" title="Merck Group">Merck Group</a> (known as EMD Serono in North America) for €3.4 billion ($4.2 billion) and renamed it as Procter &amp; Gamble Health Limited in May 2019.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p><p>In November 2018, P&amp;G unveiled a simpler corporate structure with six business units that will be effective from July 2019.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(2)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Finances">Finances</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Procter_%26_Gamble&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Finances" data-section="6" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-2 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-2">
<p>For the fiscal year 2018, Procter &amp; Gamble reported earnings of US$9.750 billion, with an annual revenue of US$66.832 billion, an increase of 2.7% over the previous fiscal cycle. Procter &amp; Gamble's Shares traded at over $86 per share in 2017, and its market capitalization was valued at over US$221.5 billion in October 2018.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> Procter &amp; Gamble ranked No. 42 on the 2018 <a href="/wiki/Fortune_500" title="Fortune 500">Fortune 500</a> list of the largest United States corporations by total revenue.

<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Employer_recognition">Employer recognition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Procter_%26_Gamble&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Employer recognition" data-section="9" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p><i>Fortune</i> magazine awarded P&amp;G a top spot on its list of "Global Top Companies for Leaders", and ranked the company at 15th place of the "World's Most Admired Companies" list.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> <i>Chief Executive</i> magazine named P&amp;G the best overall company for leadership development in its list of the "40 Best Companies for Leaders".
</p><p>In October 2008, P&amp;G was named one of "<a href="/wiki/Canada%27s_Top_100_Employers" title="Canada's Top 100 Employers">Canada's Top 100 Employers</a>" by Mediacorp Canada Inc. and was featured in <i><a href="/wiki/Maclean%27s" title="Maclean's">Maclean's</a></i> <a href="/wiki/Newsmagazine" class="mw-redirect" title="Newsmagazine">newsmagazine</a>. Later that month, P&amp;G was also named one of <a href="/wiki/Greater_Toronto%27s_Top_Employers" class="mw-redirect" title="Greater Toronto's Top Employers">Greater Toronto's Top Employers</a>, which was announced by the <i><a href="/wiki/Toronto_Star" title="Toronto Star">Toronto Star</a></i> newspaper.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup></p><p>In October 2013, the company was named the fourth-most in-demand employer in the world according to analytic data sourced by Linkedin.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup></p><p>In August 2013, P&amp;G was named the 14th-hardest company to interview for by Glassdoor.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> In November 2013, Glassdoor also named them as a top 25 company for career opportunities.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> In February 2014, Glassdoor placed P&amp;G 34th on their annual Best Places to Work list.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup></p><p>In November 2014, P&amp;G came out publicly in support of same-sex marriage in a statement made by William Gipson, P&amp;G's chief global diversity officer.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup></p><p>In November 2015, P&amp;G was named the Careers in Africa Employer of Choice 2015 following a survey of over 13,000 African professionals from across the globe. P&amp;G was also recognised as the most desirable FMCG business to work for in Africa.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup></p><p>In 2016 and 2017, P&amp;G was recognized as one of <a href="/wiki/Forbes" title="Forbes">Forbes</a> World's Most Reputable Companies.<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Brands">Brands</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Procter_%26_Gamble&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Brands" data-section="10" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/List_of_Procter_%26_Gamble_brands" title="List of Procter &amp; Gamble brands">List of Procter &amp; Gamble brands</a></div>
<p>As of 2015, 21 of P&amp;G's brands have more than a billion dollars in net annual sales.<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> Most of these brands—including Bounty, Crest, and Tide—are global products available on several continents. P&amp;G's products are available in North America, Latin America, Europe, the Middle East, Africa, Asia, Australia, and New Zealand.
</p><p>In 2018, P&amp;G's <a href="/wiki/Fabric" class="mw-redirect" title="Fabric">fabric</a> and home care division accounted for 32% of the company's total net sales, the highest of all its divisions. The division includes <a href="/wiki/Downy" title="Downy">Downy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gain_(detergent)" title="Gain (detergent)">Gain</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tide_(brand)" title="Tide (brand)">Tide</a>, <a href="/wiki/Febreze" title="Febreze">Febreze</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Dawn_(brand)" title="Dawn (brand)">Dawn</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup></p><p>According to <i><a href="/wiki/Advertising_Age" class="mw-redirect" title="Advertising Age">Advertising Age</a></i>, Procter &amp; Gamble spent $4.3 billion advertising their various brands in the United States in 2015, making it the top advertiser in the country.<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup></p><p>Manufacturing operations are based in these countries:

<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Radio_and_television_production">Radio and television production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Procter_%26_Gamble&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Radio and television production" data-section="11" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>Procter &amp; Gamble produced and sponsored the first radio serial dramas in the 1930s. As the company was known for detergents, the serials became known as "soap operas".<sup id="cite_ref-59" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup> With the rise of television in the 1950s and 1960s, most of the new serials were sponsored and produced by the company (including <i><a href="/wiki/The_Guiding_Light" class="mw-redirect" title="The Guiding Light">The Guiding Light</a></i>, which had begun in 1937 as a radio serial, and made the transition to television in 1952). Though the last P&amp;G-produced show, <i><a href="/wiki/As_the_World_Turns" title="As the World Turns">As the World Turns</a>,</i> left the air in 2010, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Young_and_the_Restless" title="The Young and the Restless">The Young and the Restless</a></i>, produced by <a href="/wiki/Sony_Pictures_Television" title="Sony Pictures Television">Sony Pictures Television</a> and broadcast on CBS, is still partially sponsored by Procter &amp; Gamble; as of 2017, it is the only remaining daytime drama that is partially sponsored by Procter &amp; Gamble.
</p><p>These past serials were produced by Procter &amp; Gamble:
</p>

<p>Procter &amp; Gamble also was the first company to produce and sponsor a prime-time serial, a 1965 spin-off of <i>As the World Turns</i> called <i>Our Private World</i>. In 1979, PGP produced <i>Shirley</i>, a prime-time <a href="/wiki/NBC" title="NBC">NBC</a> series starring <a href="/wiki/Shirley_Jones" title="Shirley Jones">Shirley Jones</a>, which lasted 13 episodes. They also produced <a href="/wiki/TBS_(TV_network)" class="mw-redirect" title="TBS (TV network)">TBS</a>' first original comedy series, <i><a href="/wiki/Down_to_Earth_(US_TV_series)" class="mw-redirect" title="Down to Earth (US TV series)">Down to Earth</a></i>, which ran from 1984 to 1987 (110 episodes were produced). They also distributed the syndicated comedy series <i><a href="/wiki/Throb" title="Throb">Throb</a></i>. In 1985, they produced a game-show pilot called <i>The Buck Stops Here</i> with <a href="/wiki/Taft_Broadcasting" title="Taft Broadcasting">Taft Entertainment Television</a> in 1985, hosted by <a href="/wiki/Jim_Peck" title="Jim Peck">Jim Peck</a>; it was not picked up. Procter &amp; Gamble Productions originally co-produced <i><a href="/wiki/Dawson%27s_Creek" title="Dawson's Creek">Dawson's Creek</a></i> with <a href="/wiki/Sony_Pictures_Television" title="Sony Pictures Television">Sony Pictures Television</a> but withdrew before the series premiere due to early press reviews. They also produced the 1991 TV movie <i>A Triumph of the Heart: The Ricky Bell Story</i>, which was co-produced by <a href="/wiki/Alan_Landsburg_Productions" title="Alan Landsburg Productions">The Landsburg Company</a>, and continue to produce the <a href="/wiki/People%27s_Choice_Awards" title="People's Choice Awards">People's Choice Awards</a>. In 2007, PGP teamed up with the now-defunct <a href="/wiki/Cookie_Jar_Group" title="Cookie Jar Group">Cookie Jar Group</a> to produce the Flash-animated children's series <i><a href="/wiki/Will_and_Dewitt" title="Will and Dewitt">Will and Dewitt</a></i>, which features the character Dewitt, the mascot for the Pampers baby product line's former sub-brand, Kandoo. 
</p><p>In 2013, PGP rebranded itself as Procter &amp; Gamble Entertainment (PGE) with a new logo and an emphasis on multiple-platform entertainment production.

<p>In addition to its self-produced items through PGE, Procter &amp; Gamble also supports many Spanish-language novellas through advertising on all networks: <a href="/wiki/Azteca_Am%C3%A9rica" title="Azteca América">Azteca América</a>, <a href="/wiki/Estrella_TV" title="Estrella TV">Estrella TV</a>, <a href="/wiki/Galavisi%C3%B3n" title="Galavisión">Galavisión</a>, <a href="/wiki/Telemundo" title="Telemundo">Telemundo</a>, <a href="/wiki/UniM%C3%A1s" title="UniMás">UniMás</a> &amp; <a href="/wiki/Univisi%C3%B3n" class="mw-redirect" title="Univisión">Univisión</a>. P&amp;G was one of the first mainstream advertisers on Spanish-language TV during the mid-1980s.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2007)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p><p>In 2008, P&amp;G expanded into music sponsorship when it joined Island Def Jam to create Tag Records, named after a body spray that P&amp;G acquired from Gillette. In April 2010, after the cancellation of <i>As the World Turns</i>, PGP announced they were phasing out soap opera production and expanding into more family-appropriate programming.<sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-61" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup></p><p>Procter &amp; Gamble also gave a $100,000 contract to the winners of Cycles 1 through 3 of <i><a href="/wiki/Canada%27s_Next_Top_Model" title="Canada's Next Top Model">Canada's Next Top Model</a></i>, wherein <a href="/wiki/Andrea_Muizelaar" class="mw-redirect" title="Andrea Muizelaar">Andrea Muizelaar</a>, Rebecca Hardy, and <a href="/wiki/Meaghan_Waller" title="Meaghan Waller">Meaghan Waller</a> won the prize.
</p><p>Procter &amp; Gamble has been a major sponsor of the <a href="/wiki/Summer_Olympics" class="mw-redirect" title="Summer Olympics">Summer Olympics</a> since 2012. It sponsored 150 athletes at the London games that year.<sup id="cite_ref-62" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup> They have also sponsored the <a href="/wiki/Winter_Olympics" class="mw-redirect" title="Winter Olympics">Winter Olympics</a> since <a href="/wiki/2014_Winter_Olympics" title="2014 Winter Olympics">2014</a>. It'll do so at the <a href="/wiki/2020_Summer_Olympics" title="2020 Summer Olympics">2020 Summer Olympics</a> in Tokyo besides the <a href="/wiki/2022_Winter_Olympics" title="2022 Winter Olympics">2022 Winter Olympics</a> in Beijing. The company's sponsorship includes television ads in which Olympic athletes are portrayed as children to convey the sense that the mothers of these athletes still remember them as infants; other ads stress how Olympic mothers stood by their children through years of training all the way through to Olympic success. 2016's ad for the Rio Games notes upheavals as youths by an American gymnast, Chinese swimmer, Brazilian volleyballer, and German distance runner.  The ads all make prominent use of the <a href="/wiki/Ludovico_Einaudi" title="Ludovico Einaudi">Ludovico Einaudi</a> orchestral track "<a href="/wiki/Divenire" title="Divenire">Divenire</a>" and related such instrumentals.
</p><p>The company has actively developed or sponsored numerous <a href="/wiki/Online_community" title="Online community">online communities</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-Shaw2002_63-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Shaw2002-63">[63]</a></sup> e.g. <a href="/wiki/BeingGirl.com" class="mw-redirect" title="BeingGirl.com">BeingGirl.com</a> (launched in 2000)<sup id="cite_ref-wired_2000_64-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wired_2000-64">[64]</a></sup> and Women.com.<sup id="cite_ref-Shaw2002_63-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Shaw2002-63">[63]</a></sup> As of 2000<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Procter_%26_Gamble&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, the company had 72 "highly stylized destination sites".<sup id="cite_ref-Business_2.0_65-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Business_2.0-65">[65]</a></sup></p>

<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Price_fixing">Price fixing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Procter_%26_Gamble&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Price fixing" data-section="14" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>In April 2011, P&amp;G was fined €211.2 million by the <a href="/wiki/European_Commission" title="European Commission">European Commission</a> for establishing a price-fixing <a href="/wiki/Cartel" title="Cartel">cartel</a> for washing powder in Europe along with <a href="/wiki/Unilever" title="Unilever">Unilever</a>, who was fined €104 million, and <a href="/wiki/Henkel" title="Henkel">Henkel</a>. Though the fine was set higher at first, it was discounted by 10% after P&amp;G and Unilever admitted running the cartel. As the provider of the tip-off leading to investigations, Henkel was not fined.<sup id="cite_ref-66" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Toxic_shock_syndrome_and_tampons">Toxic shock syndrome and tampons</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Procter_%26_Gamble&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Toxic shock syndrome and tampons" data-section="15" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Toxic_shock_syndrome" title="Toxic shock syndrome">Toxic shock syndrome</a> (TSS) is a disease caused by strains of the bacteria <i><a href="/wiki/Staphylococcus_aureus" title="Staphylococcus aureus">Staphylococcus aureus</a></i>. Most people have these bacteria living in their bodies as harmless <a href="/wiki/Commensal" class="mw-redirect" title="Commensal">commensals</a> in places such as the nose, skin, and vagina. The disease can strike anyone, not only women, but the disease is often associated with <a href="/wiki/Tampon" title="Tampon">tampons</a>.
In 1980, 814 menstrual-related TSS cases were reported; 38 deaths resulted from the disease. The majority of women in these cases were documented as using super-absorbent synthetic tampons, particularly the <a href="/wiki/Rely_(brand)" title="Rely (brand)">Rely</a> tampon created by Procter &amp; Gamble.<sup id="cite_ref-67" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup> The Rely tampon was so super-absorbent that one by itself could, in fact, hold one woman's entire menstrual period flow.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Unlike other tampons made of cotton and rayon, Rely used carboxymethylcellulose and compressed beads of <a href="/wiki/Polyester" title="Polyester">polyester</a> for absorption.
</p><p>In the summer of 1980, the <a href="/wiki/Centers_for_Disease_Control" class="mw-redirect" title="Centers for Disease Control">Centers for Disease Control</a> released a report explaining how these bacterial mechanisms were leading to TSS. They also stated that the Rely tampon was associated with TSS more than any other brand of tampon. In September 1980, Procter &amp; Gamble voluntarily recalled its Rely brand of tampons from the market and agreed to provide for a program to notify consumers. Since the 1980s, reported cases of TSS have dramatically decreased.<sup id="cite_ref-68" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Child_labor_and_forced_labor">Child labor and forced labor</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Procter_%26_Gamble&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Child labor and forced labor" data-section="16" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>According to a 2016 report by <a href="/wiki/Amnesty_International" title="Amnesty International">Amnesty International</a>, palm oil provider <a href="/wiki/Wilmar_International" title="Wilmar International">Wilmar International</a>, the world's biggest <a href="/wiki/Palm_oil" title="Palm oil">palm oil</a> grower in 2016 and supplier of raw materials to Procter &amp; Gamble, profited from 8 to 14-year-old <a href="/wiki/Child_labor" class="mw-redirect" title="Child labor">child labor</a> and <a href="/wiki/Forced_labor" class="mw-redirect" title="Forced labor">forced labor</a>. Some workers were extorted, threatened, or not paid for work. Some workers also suffered severe injuries from toxic banned chemicals.<sup id="cite_ref-69" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Animal_testing">Animal testing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Procter_%26_Gamble&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Animal testing" data-section="17" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>Procter &amp; Gamble has received criticism from animal advocacy group PETA for the practice of <a href="/wiki/Animal_testing" title="Animal testing">testing on animals</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-70" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup></p><p>On June 30, 1999, Procter &amp; Gamble announced that it would limit its animal testing practices to its food and drug products which represented less than 20% of its product portfolio.<sup id="cite_ref-71" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup> The company invested more than $275 million in the development of alternative testing methods.<sup id="cite_ref-72" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-72">[72]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_products">Other products</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Procter_%26_Gamble&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Other products" data-section="18" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>In 2002, P&amp;G was sued for its ads falsely suggesting to the consumers that the drug Prilosec could cure heartburn in a day.<sup id="cite_ref-73" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-73">[73]</a></sup> In December 2005, the Pharmaceutical division of P&amp;G was involved in a dispute over research involving its osteoporosis drug <a href="/wiki/Risedronic_acid" title="Risedronic acid">Actonel</a>. The case was discussed in the media.<sup id="cite_ref-74" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-74">[74]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Logo_myth">Logo myth</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Procter_%26_Gamble&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Logo myth" data-section="19" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:P%26G_logo.jpg" class="image"><noscript><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/c/c5/P%26G_logo.jpg/220px-P%26G_logo.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="221" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="283" data-file-height="284"></noscript><span class="lazy-image-placeholder" style="width: 220px;height: 221px;" data-src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/c/c5/P%26G_logo.jpg/220px-P%26G_logo.jpg" data-alt="" data-width="220" data-height="221" data-class="thumbimage"> </span></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:P%26G_logo.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Former P&amp;G logo</div></div></div>
<p>P&amp;G's former logo originated in 1851 as a crude cross that barge workers on the <a href="/wiki/Ohio_River" title="Ohio River">Ohio River</a> painted on cases of P&amp;G star candles to identify them. P&amp;G later changed this symbol into a trademark that showed a man in the moon overlooking 13 stars, said to commemorate the original <a href="/wiki/13_colonies" class="mw-redirect" title="13 colonies">13 colonies</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-75" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-75">[75]</a></sup></p><p>The company received unwanted media publicity in the 1980s due to rumors, spread largely by <a href="/wiki/Amway" title="Amway">Amway</a> distributors, that the moon-and-stars logo was a <a href="/wiki/Satanism" title="Satanism">satanic</a> symbol. The accusation was based on a particular passage in <a href="/wiki/The_Bible" class="mw-redirect" title="The Bible">the Bible</a>, specifically <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Revelation" title="Book of Revelation">Revelation</a> 12:1, which states: "And there appeared a great wonder in heaven; <a href="/wiki/Woman_of_the_Apocalypse" title="Woman of the Apocalypse">a woman</a> clothed with the sun, the moon under her feet and upon her head a crown of 12 stars." P&amp;G's logo consisted of a man's face on the moon surrounded by 13 stars. Some claimed that the logo was a mockery of the heavenly symbol alluded to in the aforementioned verse, thus construing the logo to be satanic. Where the flowing beard meets the surrounding circle, three curls were said to be a mirror image of the number <i><a href="/wiki/666_(number)" title="666 (number)">666</a></i>, or the reflected <a href="/wiki/Number_of_the_beast" class="mw-redirect" title="Number of the beast">number of the beast</a>. At the top and bottom, the hair curls in on itself and was said to be the two horns like those of a ram. The moon-and-stars logo was discontinued in 1985 in a failed attempt to quash the rumors.<sup id="cite_ref-76" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-76">[76]</a></sup></p><p>These interpretations have been denied by company officials and no evidence linking the company to the <a href="/wiki/Church_of_Satan" title="Church of Satan">Church of Satan</a> or any other occult organization has ever been presented. The company unsuccessfully sued Amway from 1995 to 2003 over rumors forwarded through a company voice-mail system in 1995. In 2007, the company successfully sued individual Amway distributors for reviving and propagating the false rumors.<sup id="cite_ref-77" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-77">[77]</a></sup> The Church of Satan denies being supported by Procter &amp; Gamble.<sup id="cite_ref-78" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-78">[78]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span id=".22The_Talk.22"></span><span class="mw-headline" id='"The_Talk"'>"The Talk"</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Procter_%26_Gamble&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title='Edit section: "The Talk"' data-section="20" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>In 2017, as part of the "My Black is Beautiful" platform, P&amp;G released an advertisement called "The Talk".<sup id="cite_ref-CBS_News_79-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CBS_News-79">[79]</a></sup> The advertisement shows <a href="/wiki/African_Americans" title="African Americans">African American</a> parents throughout the decades giving their children "the talk" about <a href="/wiki/Racism" title="Racism">racism</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-The_Washington_Post_80-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-The_Washington_Post-80">[80]</a></sup> The advertisement garnered controversy for several different reasons.<sup id="cite_ref-Fast_Company_81-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fast_Company-81">[81]</a></sup> Some people criticized the advertisement for not showing any fathers giving "the talk" while others accused it of being anti-white.<sup id="cite_ref-Daily_News_82-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Daily_News-82">[82]</a></sup> One scene shows a mother warning her daughter about being <a href="/wiki/Traffic_stop" title="Traffic stop">pulled over</a> by the police. The daughter responds by saying that she is a good driver so her mother doesn't need to worry about her getting a ticket. The mother then implies that she might experience <a href="/wiki/Police_brutality" title="Police brutality">police brutality</a> by being <a href="/wiki/Racial_profiling" title="Racial profiling">racially profiled</a> and killed.<sup id="cite_ref-SHOOT_83-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SHOOT-83">[83]</a></sup> Several police officers and police groups accused that part of the advertisement of being anti-cop.<sup id="cite_ref-Daily_News_82-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Daily_News-82">[82]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-US_Campaign_84-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-US_Campaign-84">[84]</a></sup> "The Talk" was accused by <a href="/wiki/Michelle_Malkin" title="Michelle Malkin">Michelle Malkin</a> of the right-leaning <i><a href="/wiki/National_Review" title="National Review">National Review</a></i> of being "liberal advertising". Malkin also called the advertisement "<a href="/wiki/Black_Lives_Matter" title="Black Lives Matter">Black Lives Matter</a> propaganda" and accused it of <a href="/wiki/Pandering_(politics)" title="Pandering (politics)">pandering</a> and using <a href="/wiki/Identity_politics" title="Identity politics">identity politics</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-National_Review_85-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-National_Review-85">[85]</a></sup> Despite the criticism, the advertisement also received a lot of positive reception and praise with some calling it "powerful"<sup id="cite_ref-Adweek_86-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Adweek-86">[86]</a></sup> and "thought-provoking".<sup id="cite_ref-CBS_News_79-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CBS_News-79">[79]</a></sup> The advertisement has also won several awards including the 2018 <a href="/wiki/Cannes_Lions_International_Festival_of_Creativity" title="Cannes Lions International Festival of Creativity">Cannes Lions International Festival of Creativity</a> Grand Prix<sup id="cite_ref-AdAge_87-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AdAge-87">[87]</a></sup> and the 2018 <a href="/wiki/Primetime_Emmy_Award_for_Outstanding_Commercial" title="Primetime Emmy Award for Outstanding Commercial">Primetime Emmy Award for Outstanding Commercial</a> during the <a href="/wiki/70th_Primetime_Creative_Arts_Emmy_Awards" title="70th Primetime Creative Arts Emmy Awards">70th Primetime Creative Arts Emmy Awards</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-MediaPost_88-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MediaPost-88">[88]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Gillette_ad">Gillette ad</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Procter_%26_Gamble&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Gillette ad" data-section="21" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>On January 14, 2019, P&amp;G subsidiary <a href="/wiki/Gillette" title="Gillette">Gillette</a> put out a controversial advertisement called "<a href="/wiki/The_Best_Men_Can_Be" title="The Best Men Can Be">The Best Men Can Be</a>", criticising toxic masculinity, bullying, and sexual assault, challenging men to be the best they can be. This advert was felt by some viewers and media personalities to include wide generalisations and prejudice toward men. The film notably received strong criticism by political commentators at the news outlet <a href="/wiki/Fox_News" title="Fox News">Fox News</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-89" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-89">[89]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-90" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-90">[90]</a></sup></p>

<p>In January 2019, CEO David Taylor said in Switzerland: "The world would be a better place if my board of directors on down is represented by 50% of the women. We sell our products to more than 50% of the women." <i><a href="/wiki/The_Wall_Street_Journal" title="The Wall Street Journal">The Wall Street Journal</a></i> noted the company's board of directors has more than twice as many men as it does women.<sup id="cite_ref-91" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-91">[91]</a></sup></p>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(6)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="CEO-to-worker_pay_ratio">CEO-to-worker pay ratio</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Procter_%26_Gamble&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: CEO-to-worker pay ratio" data-section="23" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-6 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-6">
<p>For the first time in 2018, a new <a href="/wiki/Securities_and_Exchange_Commission" class="mw-redirect" title="Securities and Exchange Commission">Securities and Exchange Commission</a> rule mandated under the 2010 <a href="/wiki/Dodd-Frank" class="mw-redirect" title="Dodd-Frank">Dodd-Frank</a> financial reform requires publicly traded companies to disclose how their CEOs are compensated in comparison with their employees. In public filings, companies have to disclose their “Pay Ratios,” or the CEO's compensation divided by the median employee's.<sup id="cite_ref-92" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-92">[92]</a></sup> According to SEC filings, Procter and Gamble Corporation paid its CEO $17,354,256 in 2017.  The average worker employed by Procter &amp; Gamble Corporation was paid $60,412 in 2017; thus marking a CEO-to-worker Pay Ratio of 287 to 1.<sup id="cite_ref-93" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-93">[93]</a></sup> As of April 2018, steelmaker <a href="/wiki/Nucor" title="Nucor">Nucor</a> represented the median CEO-to-worker Pay Ratio from SEC filings with values of 133 to 1.<sup id="cite_ref-94" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-94">[94]</a></sup></p>
