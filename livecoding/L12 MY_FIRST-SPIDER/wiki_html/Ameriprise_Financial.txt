</p>

</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(1)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Current_operations">Current operations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ameriprise_Financial&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Current operations" data-section="1" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-1 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-1">
<p>The company specializes in <a href="/wiki/Retirement_plans_in_the_United_States" title="Retirement plans in the United States">retirement</a>-related financial planning for relatively well-off clients. The company offers variable annuities and life and disability insurance. It also operates Ameriprise Bank, FSB which offers a variety of consumer banking and lending products and personal trust and related services. Since 2015, its asset management arm operates under the name Columbia Threadneedle Investments. The company uses three principal brands for its businesses in the United States: Ameriprise Financial, <a href="/wiki/Columbia_Management" class="mw-redirect" title="Columbia Management">Columbia Management</a> and <a href="/wiki/RiverSource" title="RiverSource">RiverSource</a>. The Threadneedle brand is used for its international asset manager's products. Ameriprise Financial Services, a financial planning and retail distribution subsidiary, is a registered broker-dealer and registered investment adviser. It has an integrated model of comprehensive and personalized financial planning, diversified product manufacturing and affiliated and unaffiliated distribution through its network of financial advisors and registered representatives. The retail products and services that use the Ameriprise Financial brand include those that the company provides through its affiliated advisors (e.g., financial planning, investment advisory accounts, retail brokerage services and banking products) and products and services that it markets directly to consumers).<sup id="cite_ref-10K_1-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10K-1">[1]</a></sup></p><p>Columbia Threadneedle Investments is the primary brand for the company's U.S. asset management products and services, including retail and institutional asset management products. Columbia Threadneedle Investments provides <a href="/wiki/Investment_management" title="Investment management">investment management</a> products and services to clients in the United Kingdom, Europe, the Middle East and the Asia-Pacific region. Acquired by Ameriprise Financial in 2003, Threadneedle initially operated as an independent international asset manager, under its own brand name, management organization and operating, compliance and technology infrastructure. Since 2015 it has been merged with Columbia Management to create the combined Columbia Threadneedle Investments brand.<sup id="cite_ref-citywire_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-citywire-4">[4]</a></sup></p><p><a href="/wiki/RiverSource" title="RiverSource">RiverSource</a> is the brand for the company's annuities products and for the protection products issued by the RiverSource Life companies, including life and disability income insurance products.<sup id="cite_ref-10K_1-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10K-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Ameriprise_advisors">Ameriprise advisors</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ameriprise_Financial&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Ameriprise advisors" data-section="2" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>Ameriprise Financial has more than 9,700 branded advisors, the 5th largest branded advisor force in the U.S.<sup id="cite_ref-10K_1-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10K-1">[1]</a></sup> Ameriprise Financial advisors recommend actions and a broad range of products consisting of investment, annuity, insurance, banking and other financial products to clients based on an appropriate range and level of risk. The financial advisors charge clients for financial advice and selling products. Many Ameriprise advisors are <a href="/wiki/Certified_Financial_Planner" title="Certified Financial Planner">Certified Financial Planners</a>, and the company actively encourages attaining this designation. Many advisors also hold similar designations such as the <a href="/wiki/Chartered_Life_Underwriter" class="mw-redirect" title="Chartered Life Underwriter">CLU</a> or <a href="/wiki/Financial_planner#History_of_certifications_in_financial_planning_across_the_globe" title="Financial planner">ChFC</a>.
</p><p>There are four ways the financial advisors affiliate with Ameriprise Financial:
</p>
<ul><li>Approximately 60% of Ameriprise's financial advisors are <a href="/wiki/Independent_contractor" title="Independent contractor">independent contractor</a> <a href="/wiki/Franchising" title="Franchising">franchisees</a> — they are not employed by Ameriprise, but are licensed registered representatives of Ameriprise and do not receive a salary from the parent company.</li>
<li>Approximately 25% of financial advisors are employed by Ameriprise ("employee financial advisors"). Ameriprise Financial Services, Inc. has the largest number of these professionals among any retail advisory force.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The majority of the employee advisors work in local offices like their franchisee counterparts.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></li>
<li>Over 120 of the employee advisors work within the corporate office in <a href="/wiki/Minneapolis" title="Minneapolis">Minneapolis</a> as part of the Ameriprise Advisor Center (AAC). These Advisors are fully licensed Securities and Insurance professionals that have received additional training which allows them to assist clients exclusively by telephone. They are required to hold the <a href="/wiki/General_Securities_Representative_Exam" class="mw-redirect" title="General Securities Representative Exam">Series 7</a> and <a href="/wiki/Series_63" class="mw-redirect" title="Series 63">Series 63</a> <a href="/wiki/Financial_Industry_Regulatory_Authority" title="Financial Industry Regulatory Authority">Financial Industry Regulatory Authority</a> (FINRA) licenses, along with insurance licenses in all states they serve, and are encouraged to acquire their Chartered Retirement Planning Counselor (CRPC) professional designation from the College of Financial Planning, and are reimbursed for the cost.</li>
<li>The company also has associate Financial Advisors, employed by the independent contractor franchisees.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></li></ul></section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(2)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ameriprise_Financial&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: History" data-section="3" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-2 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-2">
<p>In 1894, John Tappan founded Investors Syndicate in <a href="/wiki/Minneapolis" title="Minneapolis">Minneapolis</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-history_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-history-6">[6]</a></sup></p><p>In 1925, <a href="/wiki/United_States_West_Coast" class="mw-redirect" title="United States West Coast">West Coast</a> businessman J. R. Ridgway merged his investment firm with Investors Syndicate, and took over as president.<sup id="cite_ref-history_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-history-6">[6]</a></sup></p><p>In 1937, upon the death of then 50-year-old J. R. Ridgway from <a href="/wiki/Leukemia" title="Leukemia">leukemia</a>, 23-year-old J. R. Ridgway, Sr. was appointed president.<sup id="cite_ref-history_6-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-history-6">[6]</a></sup></p><p>In 1940, Investors Syndicate introduced one of the first <a href="/wiki/Mutual_fund" title="Mutual fund">mutual funds</a>, the Investors Mutual Fund, giving clients new investing options and two advantages: diversification and professional management. By the 1960s, Investors Mutual Fund was the largest balanced mutual fund in the world.<sup id="cite_ref-history_6-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-history-6">[6]</a></sup></p><p>In 1949, Investors Syndicate changed its name to Investors Diversified Services, Inc. (IDS).<sup id="cite_ref-history_6-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-history-6">[6]</a></sup></p><p>In 1958, IDS founded the Investors Syndicate Life Insurance and Annuity Company (now known as <a href="/wiki/RiverSource" title="RiverSource">RiverSource</a> Life Insurance Company).<sup id="cite_ref-history_6-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-history-6">[6]</a></sup></p><p>In 1974, the IDS Centre (now <a href="/wiki/IDS_Center" title="IDS Center">IDS Center</a>) is opened in downtown Minneapolis as the company's headquarters.<sup id="cite_ref-history_6-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-history-6">[6]</a></sup></p><p>In 1979, the Ridgway family sold the last of its ownership interest. IDS became a wholly owned subsidiary of <a href="/wiki/Alleghany_Corporation" title="Alleghany Corporation">Alleghany Corporation</a> pursuant to a <a href="/wiki/Merger" class="mw-redirect" title="Merger">merger</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-history_6-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-history-6">[6]</a></sup></p><p>In 1984, <a href="/wiki/American_Express" title="American Express">American Express</a> acquired IDS Financial Services from Alleghany Corporation.<sup id="cite_ref-history_6-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-history-6">[6]</a></sup></p><p>In 1986, IDS acquired Wisconsin Employers Casualty Company of <a href="/wiki/Green_Bay,_Wisconsin" title="Green Bay, Wisconsin">Green Bay, Wisconsin</a> and renamed it IDS Property Casualty Insurance Company.<sup id="cite_ref-history_6-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-history-6">[6]</a></sup></p><p>In 1994, IDS reached $100 billion in assets.<sup id="cite_ref-history_6-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-history-6">[6]</a></sup></p><p>Effective January 1, 1995, IDS changed its name to American Express Financial Corporation, doing business as American Express Financial Advisors (AEFA).<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p><p>In October 2003, AEFA acquired London-based <a href="/wiki/Threadneedle_Asset_Management" class="mw-redirect" title="Threadneedle Asset Management">Threadneedle Asset Management</a> Holdings.<sup id="cite_ref-history_6-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-history-6">[6]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p><p>In September 2005, American Express completed the <a href="/wiki/Corporate_spin-off" title="Corporate spin-off">corporate spin-off</a> of AEFA as Ameriprise Financial, Inc., a <a href="/wiki/Public_company" title="Public company">public company</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-history_6-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-history-6">[6]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p><p>In September 2006, the company launched Ameriprise Bank, FSB.<sup id="cite_ref-history_6-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-history-6">[6]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p><p>In February 2008, Threadneedle acquired Invesco Perpetual's full service defined contribution pension business with total assets of £470 million.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p><p>In 2009, Ameriprise acquired Standard Chartered Bank's World Express Funds investment funds business, providing Threadneedle with an established Luxembourg-based SICAV platform with over US$2.38 billion worth assets under management.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p><p>In August 2011, Threadneedle announced the acquisition of the £8 billion in investment assets of <a href="/wiki/LV%3D" class="mw-redirect" title="LV=">LV=</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p><p>In November 2008, the company acquired <a href="/wiki/H%26R_Block" title="H&amp;R Block">H&amp;R Block</a> Financial Advisors for $315 million, and the asset management firm <a href="/wiki/J._%26_W._Seligman_%26_Co." title="J. &amp; W. Seligman &amp; Co.">J. &amp; W. Seligman &amp; Co.</a> for $400 million.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p><p>During the <a href="/wiki/Great_Recession" title="Great Recession">Great Recession</a>, the company declined an investment by the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_the_Treasury" title="United States Department of the Treasury">United States Department of the Treasury</a> under the <a href="/wiki/Troubled_Asset_Relief_Program" title="Troubled Asset Relief Program">Troubled Asset Relief Program</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-history_6-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-history-6">[6]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p><p>In May 2010, Ameriprise Financial acquired <a href="/wiki/Columbia_Management" class="mw-redirect" title="Columbia Management">Columbia Management</a>, the long-term asset management business of <a href="/wiki/Bank_of_America" title="Bank of America">Bank of America</a>, for $1 billion.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p><p>On April 25, 2011, Ameriprise announced that it was trying to find an "appropriate buyer" for Securities America Financial Corporation to allow its registered representatives to focus on growth opportunities.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> In November 2011, Ameriprise completed the sale of Securities America to <a href="/wiki/Ladenburg_Thalmann_Financial_Services" class="mw-redirect" title="Ladenburg Thalmann Financial Services">Ladenburg Thalmann Financial Services</a> for $150 million in cash and potential future payments.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> Securities America traced its roots to Financial Dynamics, an insurance marketing organization founded in 1984 by Steve Wild that had between 10,000 and 12,000 affiliated independent insurance agents. In 1993, Financial Dynamics created Securities America, an independent securities broker/dealer, to use its insurance sales force to offer investment management, financial advice and financial planning through a national network of independent financial advisors. In 1998, Securities America was acquired by American Express and was transferred to Ameriprise as part of the <a href="/wiki/Corporate_spin-off" title="Corporate spin-off">corporate spin-off</a> in September 2005. In 2009, Securities America acquired Brecek &amp; Young Advisors, a California-based broker/dealer.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> Securities America received the inaugural Practitioner Thought Leadership Award from the Retirement Income Industry Association in 2011.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p><p>In January 2012, the company opened services to individuals in India with US$40,000 equivalent minimum income.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> Ameriprise also established an insurance brokerage entity in India that
was licensed to deal in insurance products by India's <a href="/wiki/Insurance_Regulatory_and_Development_Authority" title="Insurance Regulatory and Development Authority">Insurance Regulatory and Development Authority</a> (IRDA). Ameriprise India established offices in Delhi, Gurgaon, Greater Noida, Mumbai &amp; Pune. In May 2014, Ameriprise shut down the nascent financial planning division in India, citing poor prospects for fee-based advisory services.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p><p>In January 2013, the company completed the conversion of its federal savings bank subsidiary, Ameriprise Bank, FSB, to a limited powers national trust bank, which conversion included changing the name of this subsidiary to Ameriprise National Trust Bank.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p><p>In 2015, Threadneedle rebranded as Columbia Threadneedle.<sup id="cite_ref-citywire_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-citywire-4">[4]</a></sup></p><p>In 2017, the company acquired Investment Professionals, Inc.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p><p>In 2019, the company sold <a href="/wiki/Ameriprise_Auto_%26_Home_Insurance" title="Ameriprise Auto &amp; Home Insurance">Ameriprise Auto &amp; Home Insurance</a> to <a href="/wiki/American_Family_Insurance" title="American Family Insurance">American Family Insurance</a> for $1.05 billion.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>

<p>In 2003, Securities America was fined $5.4 million for letting a broker work under a false name in its <a href="/wiki/Orlando" class="mw-redirect" title="Orlando">Orlando</a> office and allegedly make bogus investments.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p><p>In July 2005, <a href="/wiki/New_Hampshire" title="New Hampshire">New Hampshire</a> reached a $7.4 million settlement with American Express Financial Advisors, alleging the company had violated the law by rewarding their financial advisers for recommending underperforming in-house mutual funds to clients.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p><p>In December 2005, Ameriprise agreed to pay a $12.3 million to settle <a href="/wiki/National_Association_of_Securities_Dealers" class="mw-redirect" title="National Association of Securities Dealers">NASD</a> charges relating to favorable treatment allegedly given to some mutual funds in exchange for brokerage business.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p><p>In December 2005, the company agreed to pay $15 million to settle charges of <a href="/wiki/Market_timing" title="Market timing">market timing</a> by the <a href="/wiki/U.S._Securities_and_Exchange_Commission" title="U.S. Securities and Exchange Commission">U.S. Securities and Exchange Commission</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> The Minnesota Department of Commerce levied $2 million in fines for similar market timing violations and the <a href="/wiki/National_Association_of_Securities_Dealers" class="mw-redirect" title="National Association of Securities Dealers">National Association of Securities Dealers</a> fined Ameriprise an additional $12.3 million for unsuitable share sales.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> Ameriprise had not revealed which funds were timed, or the names of the people involved and the exact nature of the disciplinary action taken. <a href="/wiki/Morningstar,_Inc." title="Morningstar, Inc.">Morningstar, Inc.</a> temporarily reduced the stewardship grade for Ameriprise's funds, although it did not impact the fund's overall star ratings from that firm.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p><p>In September 2006, Securities America reached a $16.3 million settlement with a group of <a href="/wiki/ExxonMobil" title="ExxonMobil">ExxonMobil</a> retirees for failing to supervise an associated broker.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></p><p>In December 2006, a NASD arbitration panel awarded $9.3 million to three retired <a href="/wiki/American_Airlines" title="American Airlines">American Airlines</a> pilots against Securities America and a formerly associated broker for allegedly mishandling their savings.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></p><p>Also in December 2006, the company settled charges regarding a lost laptop containing customer data.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></p><p>On July 11, 2007, the NASD fined Securities America $375,000 for improperly sharing directed brokerage commissions from a mutual fund company with a former Securities America broker.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></p><p>In September 2008, the company agreed to pay as much as $33 million to cover investor losses in the <a href="/wiki/Reserve_Primary_Fund" title="Reserve Primary Fund">Reserve Primary Fund</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup></p><p>On July 10, 2009, the company agreed to pay $17.3 million after the <a href="/wiki/Securities_and_Exchange_Commission" class="mw-redirect" title="Securities and Exchange Commission">Securities and Exchange Commission</a> (SEC) announced an enforcement action against the company for receiving millions of dollars in undisclosed revenue sharing as a condition for selling certain <a href="/wiki/Real_estate_investment_trust" title="Real estate investment trust">real estate investment trusts</a> (REITs) to its brokerage customers before the spinoff from American Express.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup></p><p>On April 15, 2011, Securities America, Inc. (SAI) and its holding company, Securities America Financial Corporation, entered into settlement agreements related to the sale of <a href="/wiki/Private_placement" title="Private placement">private placement</a> securities issued by Medical Capital and Provident Royalties that resulted in a $118 million pre-tax charge in the first quarter of 2011. The charge is in addition to a $40 million pre-tax charge in the fourth quarter of 2010.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup></p><p>In April 2008, the company agreed to pay <a href="/wiki/New_Hampshire" title="New Hampshire">New Hampshire</a> $3.8 million to settle allegations that its Portsmouth agents forged clients’ signatures to cut corners and increase their income.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup></p><p>On July 12, 2009, an advisor filed a complaint under 18 USC 1514(a) the whistleblower provisions of the <a href="/wiki/Sarbanes_Oxley_Act" class="mw-redirect" title="Sarbanes Oxley Act">Sarbanes Oxley Act</a>. The case was filed with OSHA. OSHA determined the advisor was an independent contractor and not an employee. The advisor was referred to FINRA Arbitration. The Finra arbitration panel dismissed the case without a hearing 4 years later. All of the advisors clients were transferred to other advisors and his practice was destroyed. He had no recourse to sell the equity in his practice in spite of a franchise agreement. The case is set to go before a Federal Appeals judge next month. See: DOL-ALJ website, case lookup, Ameriprise Financial. 2015 case dismissed. 2016 case set for hearings.
</p><p>In October 2010, Michael Loscalso, a branch manager in <a href="/wiki/West_Conshohocken,_Pennsylvania" title="West Conshohocken, Pennsylvania">West Conshohocken, Pennsylvania</a> who earned $260,000 per year, sued the company after being fired, allegedly for raising serious allegations about the firm’s oversight of brokers.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup></p><p>In October 2014, a former Ameriprise adviser in <a href="/wiki/Plymouth,_Minnesota" title="Plymouth, Minnesota">Plymouth, Minnesota</a> admitted to stealing $1 million from client accounts, which she spent at <a href="/wiki/Victoria%27s_Secret" title="Victoria's Secret">Victoria's Secret</a>, on Caribbean cruises, salon visits and donations to a local Catholic Church.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup></p><p>In March 2015, the company paid $27.5 million to settle a lawsuit that it charged its own employees high fees in funds in its <a href="/wiki/401(k)" title="401(k)">401(k)</a> program.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup></p><p>In December 2016, the company settled with the <a href="/wiki/U.S._Department_of_Labor" class="mw-redirect" title="U.S. Department of Labor">U.S. Department of Labor</a>, agreeing to pay $128,200 in back wages for discriminating against 20 black employees by paying them less than similarly situated white workers.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup></p><p>In May 2017, <a href="/wiki/NFL" class="mw-redirect" title="NFL">NFL</a> <a href="/wiki/Running_back" title="Running back">running back</a> <a href="/wiki/Darren_McFadden" title="Darren McFadden">Darren McFadden</a> sued the company for $15 million, claiming the company kept quiet when it found out one of its financial advisers was looting his account.<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup></p><p>In February 2018, the company paid $230,000 to settle allegations by the <a href="/wiki/U.S._Securities_and_Exchange_Commission" title="U.S. Securities and Exchange Commission">U.S. Securities and Exchange Commission</a> that it put customers into higher fee funds.<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup></p><p>In August 2018, the company paid $4.5 million to settle charges that it failed to safeguard retail investor assets from theft by its representatives after 5 Ameriprise representatives committed fraudulent acts, including forging client documents and stole more than $1 million in client funds over a four-year period.<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup></p><p>In 2019, a <a href="/wiki/Class_action" title="Class action">class action</a> lawsuit against the company alleging <a href="/wiki/Securities_fraud" title="Securities fraud">securities fraud</a> and <a href="/wiki/Breach_of_fiduciary_duty" class="mw-redirect" title="Breach of fiduciary duty">breach of fiduciary duty</a> moved to trial. The class-action suit was brought in December 2018 by a 56-year-old grocery store stocker in <a href="/wiki/Clay_County,_Kansas" title="Clay County, Kansas">Clay County, Kansas</a> who lost $900,000 in his retirement account.<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup></p>
