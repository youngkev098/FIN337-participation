<p><b>CVS Health</b> (previously <b>CVS Corporation</b> or <b>CVS Caremark Corporation</b>) is an American healthcare company that owns <a href="/wiki/CVS_Pharmacy" title="CVS Pharmacy">CVS Pharmacy</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Retail" title="Retail">retail</a> pharmacy chain, <a href="/wiki/CVS_Caremark" title="CVS Caremark">CVS Caremark</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Pharmacy_benefit_management" title="Pharmacy benefit management">pharmacy benefits manager</a>, <a href="/wiki/Aetna" title="Aetna">Aetna</a>, a health insurance provider, among many other brands. The company's headquarter is in <a href="/wiki/Woonsocket,_Rhode_Island" title="Woonsocket, Rhode Island">Woonsocket, Rhode Island</a>.

To facilitate growth and expansion, the company joined the <a href="/wiki/Melville_Corporation" title="Melville Corporation">Melville Corporation</a>, which managed a string of retail businesses. Following a period of growth in the 1980s and 1990s, CVS Corporation spun off from Melville in 1996, becoming a standalone company trading on the <a href="/wiki/New_York_Stock_Exchange" title="New York Stock Exchange">New York Stock Exchange</a> as <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://www.nyse.com/quote/XNYS:CVS">CVS</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p><p>It later completed a merger with the <a href="/wiki/Pharmacy_benefit_management" title="Pharmacy benefit management">pharmacy benefit management</a> company Caremark Rx in 2007, and was renamed CVS Caremark Corporation. The company was renamed CVS Health in 2014, following its decision to remove tobacco products from CVS Pharmacy store shelves. CVS Health's assets include <a href="/wiki/CVS_Pharmacy" title="CVS Pharmacy">CVS Pharmacy</a>, <a href="/wiki/CVS_Caremark" title="CVS Caremark">CVS Caremark</a>, CVS Specialty, and the <a href="/wiki/Retail_clinic" class="mw-redirect" title="Retail clinic">retail clinic</a> <a href="/wiki/MinuteClinic" title="MinuteClinic">MinuteClinic</a>.
</p><p>In 2019, it ranked eighth<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> on the <a href="/wiki/Fortune_500" title="Fortune 500">Fortune 500</a> and 19th on the <a href="/wiki/Fortune_Global_500" title="Fortune Global 500">Fortune Global 500</a> list with $194.58<span class="nowrap"> </span>billion in annual revenue.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> In December 2017, CVS agreed to acquire <a href="/wiki/Aetna" title="Aetna">Aetna</a> for $69<span class="nowrap"> </span>billion<sup id="cite_ref-WP_Johnson_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WP_Johnson-9">[9]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-NYT_Michael_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NYT_Michael-10">[10]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> and completed the acquisition in November 2018.<sup id="cite_ref-Pharmacy_Times_2018_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pharmacy_Times_2018-12">[12]</a></sup> Legal issues related to the merger were resolved in September 2019.<sup id="cite_ref-Martin_2019_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Martin_2019-13">[13]</a></sup></p><p>In February 2020, CVS Health announced changes to its Board of Directors. Directors Richard "Dick" Swift, Richard Bracken and Mark Bertolini will no longer stand for re-election at the company's 2020 Annual Meeting and the Board will be reduced from 16 to 13 directors.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>

</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(1)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Company_name">Company name</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=CVS_Health&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Company name" data-section="1" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-1 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-1">
<p>CVS began in <a href="/wiki/Lowell,_Massachusetts" title="Lowell, Massachusetts">Lowell, Massachusetts</a> by the Goldstein brothers and their partner Ralph, later had to sell to the <a href="/wiki/Melville_Corporation" title="Melville Corporation">Melville Corporation</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> formerly based in <a href="/wiki/Rye,_New_York" title="Rye, New York">Rye</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_York_(state)" title="New York (state)">New York</a>. The CVS name once stood for Consumer Value Stores.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p><p>Caremark was established by James M. Sweeney in 1979, as Home Health Care of America (HHCA), incorporated in Delaware, with corporate headquarters in Irvine, California. The first office was opened in Beachwood, Ohio, with four employees, in conjunction with Ezra Steiger, of the Cleveland Clinic Foundation. Steiger's Hyperalimentation Team worked closely to provide supplies at home for their <a href="/wiki/Parenteral_nutrition" title="Parenteral nutrition">parenteral therapy</a> patients.  Satellite offices were subsequently opened in Atlanta, Philadelphia, Houston, Chicago, and Irvine.  HHCA changed its name to Caremark in 1985. In 1987, Caremark was acquired by <a href="/wiki/Baxter_International" title="Baxter International">Baxter International</a>. In 1991, when Caremark was <a href="/wiki/Baxter_International" title="Baxter International">Baxter International</a>'s home infusion subsidiary, Caremark was accused by the United States government of "paying doctors to steer patients to its intravenous drug service".<sup id="cite_ref-chicagotribune_1995_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-chicagotribune_1995-18">[18]</a></sup> Caremark was fined $160<span class="nowrap"> </span>million for the "four-year-long federal mail-fraud and kickback" scheme in which the "home-infusion business unit made weekly payments to scores of doctors that averaged about $75 per patient for referring those patients to its services. Some doctors earned as much as $80,000 a year from the kickbacks, according to government documents."<sup id="cite_ref-chicagotribune_1995_18-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-chicagotribune_1995-18">[18]</a></sup> In 1992, Baxter spun off Caremark as a public company. Caremark sold its home infusion service and successfully branched out to four units, "a physician practice management unit, a prescription benefits management unit, a disease state management service aimed at treating high-cost, chronic diseases and an international division."<sup id="cite_ref-chicagotribune_1995_18-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-chicagotribune_1995-18">[18]</a></sup> In 1996, Caremark merged with <a href="/wiki/Birmingham,_Alabama" title="Birmingham, Alabama">Birmingham</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alabama" title="Alabama">Alabama</a>, based MedPartners/Mullikin, Inc., with the combined company being called MedPartners, Inc. In 1998, MedPartners changed its name to <a href="/wiki/Caremark_Rx" class="mw-redirect" title="Caremark Rx">Caremark Rx</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p><p>On September 3, 2014, it was announced that CVS, as of midnight on Tuesday September 2, 2014, will no longer sell tobacco at all of its 7,700 locations nationwide, which is a month earlier than previously planned. The company also announced that it would change its corporate name to CVS Health, in order to reflect "its broader health care commitment", and also a desire to change the future health of Americans, although all retail stores will continue to be called "CVS/pharmacy".<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-NBC_News_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NBC_News-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(2)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=CVS_Health&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History" data-section="2" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-2 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-2">
<table class="box-Prose plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-style ambox-Prose" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">This section <b>is in <a href="/wiki/MOS:LIST" class="mw-redirect" title="MOS:LIST">list</a> format, but may read better as <a href="/wiki/MOS:PROSE" class="mw-redirect" title="MOS:PROSE">prose</a></b>.<span class="hide-when-compact"> You can help by <a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=CVS_Health&amp;action=edit">converting this section</a>, if appropriate. <a href="/wiki/Help:Editing" title="Help:Editing">Editing help</a> is available.</span>  <small class="date-container"><i>(<span class="date">December 2017</span>)</i></small></div></td></tr></tbody></table><h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="1960s">1960s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=CVS_Health&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: 1960s" data-section="3" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>The first Consumer Value Store (CVS), selling health and beauty products, was founded in 1963, in <a href="/wiki/Lowell,_Massachusetts" title="Lowell, Massachusetts">Lowell, Massachusetts</a> by brothers Stanley and Sidney Goldstein and Scandinavian American Ralph Hoagland. By 1964, CVS had 17 stores that sold primarily beauty products. In 1967, CVS began operation of its first stores with pharmacy departments, opening locations in <a href="/wiki/Warwick,_Rhode_Island" title="Warwick, Rhode Island">Warwick, Rhode Island</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cumberland,_Rhode_Island" title="Cumberland, Rhode Island">Cumberland, Rhode Island</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-23">[23]</a></sup></p><p>CVS was sold to Melville Corporation in 1969.<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_23-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="1970s">1970s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=CVS_Health&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: 1970s" data-section="4" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>By 1970, CVS was operating 100 stores in <a href="/wiki/New_England" title="New England">New England</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Northeastern_United_States" title="Northeastern United States">Northeast</a>. In 1972, CVS acquired 84 Clinton Drug and Discount Stores. The purchase introduced CVS to the Midwest with stores in <a href="/wiki/Indiana" title="Indiana">Indiana</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_23-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-23">[23]</a></sup></p><p>During 1977, CVS acquired 36 <a href="/wiki/New_Jersey" title="New Jersey">New Jersey</a>–based Mack Drug stores.<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_23-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="1980s">1980s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=CVS_Health&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: 1980s" data-section="5" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<ul><li>1980: CVS became the 15th largest pharmacy chain in the U.S. with 408 stores and $414<span class="nowrap"> </span>million in sales.<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_23-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-23">[23]</a></sup></li>
<li>1983: Hemophilia patient home health care is launched.<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_23-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-23">[23]</a></sup></li>
<li>1988: CVS Pharmacy celebrated its 25th anniversary, finishing the year with nearly 750 stores and sales of about $1.6<span class="nowrap"> </span>billion.<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_23-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-23">[23]</a></sup></li>

<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="CVS_Pharmacy">CVS Pharmacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=CVS_Health&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: CVS Pharmacy" data-section="12" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/CVS_Pharmacy" title="CVS Pharmacy">CVS Pharmacy</a></div>
<p>CVS Pharmacy is one of the largest retail pharmacy chains in the United States, with 9,600 stores located in all 50 states <sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup>, the District of Columbia, and <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rico" title="Puerto Rico">Puerto Rico</a>, operating primarily under the CVS Pharmacy, CVS, Longs Drugs, Navarro Discount Pharmacy and Drogaria Onofre names.<sup id="cite_ref-cvshealth.com_49-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cvshealth.com-49">[49]</a></sup> CVS Pharmacy fills more than one of every five prescriptions in the United States, and 76% of the U.S. population now lives within 5 miles of a CVS Pharmacy. The ExtraCare loyalty program boasts over 70<span class="nowrap"> </span>million cardholders, making it the largest retail loyalty program in the country.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_50-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-50">[50]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="MinuteClinic">MinuteClinic</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=CVS_Health&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: MinuteClinic" data-section="13" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/MinuteClinic" title="MinuteClinic">MinuteClinic</a></div>
<p>MinuteClinic retail medical clinics operate inside CVS Pharmacy locations within the United States. It is the largest walk-in medical clinic in the United States, with over 1,100 locations in 33 states and the <a href="/wiki/District_of_Columbia" class="mw-redirect" title="District of Columbia">District of Columbia</a>. More than 50 percent of the U.S. population now lives within 10 miles of a MinuteClinic.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_50-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-50">[50]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="CVS_Caremark">CVS Caremark</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=CVS_Health&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: CVS Caremark" data-section="14" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/CVS_Caremark" title="CVS Caremark">CVS Caremark</a> provides comprehensive <a href="/wiki/Pharmacy_benefit_management" title="Pharmacy benefit management">prescription benefit management</a> services including <a href="/wiki/Online_pharmacy" title="Online pharmacy">mail order pharmacy services</a>, <a href="/wiki/Specialty_pharmacy" title="Specialty pharmacy">specialty pharmacy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Intravenous_therapy" title="Intravenous therapy">infusion services</a>, plan design and administration, <a href="/wiki/Formulary_(pharmacy)" title="Formulary (pharmacy)">formulary management</a> and claims processing. The company's clients are primarily employers, insurance companies, unions, government employee groups, health plans, Managed Medicaid plans and other sponsors of health benefit plans and individuals throughout the United States. CVS Caremark manages the dispensing of prescription drugs for more than 75<span class="nowrap"> </span>million plan members through five mail order pharmacies, specialty pharmacies, long-term care pharmacies and national network of more than 68,000 retail pharmacies, consisting of approximately 41,000 chain pharmacies and 27,000 independent pharmacies.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_50-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-50">[50]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="CVS_Specialty">CVS Specialty</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=CVS_Health&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: CVS Specialty" data-section="15" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>CVS Specialty is the specialty pharmacy division that provides specialty pharmacy services for individuals with chronic or genetic diseases who require complex and expensive drug therapies. CVS Health operate 24 retail specialty pharmacy stores and 11 specialty mail order pharmacies, making them the largest specialty pharmacy in the United States.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_50-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-50">[50]</a></sup></p><p><br></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Longs_Drugs">Longs Drugs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=CVS_Health&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Longs Drugs" data-section="16" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Longs_Drugs" title="Longs Drugs">Longs Drugs</a> is a retail pharmacy chain with approximately 40 drug stores throughout the state of Hawaii. <h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Navarro_Discount_Pharmacies">Navarro Discount Pharmacies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=CVS_Health&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Navarro Discount Pharmacies" data-section="17" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Navarro_Discount_Pharmacies" title="Navarro Discount Pharmacies">Navarro Discount Pharmacies</a> is a pharmacy chain, photo service, and pharmacy benefit manager in the United States. The company was acquired by CVS Health in September 2014, and is operated as a separate brand of CVS Health. The company mainly operates in Miami-Dade and Broward Counties and currently has 33 stores.<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup></p>

Clients are primarily health plans, employers, and third party administrators (TPAs). The company is operated as a wholly owned subsidiary of CVS Health Corporation.<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup></p>

<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Elensys">Elensys</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=CVS_Health&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Elensys" data-section="25" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>In 1998, the <i><a href="/wiki/Washington_Post" class="mw-redirect" title="Washington Post">Washington Post</a></i> reported that CVS Corporation appeared to be sharing <a href="/wiki/Prescription_drug" title="Prescription drug">prescription drug</a> information with the <a href="/wiki/Woburn,_Massachusetts" title="Woburn, Massachusetts">Woburn</a>, Massachusetts, based marketing company, Elensys. According to the <i>Post</i>, Elensys received information on specific prescription drugs that individual CVS customers had purchased and used this information to send targeted direct mailings urging customers to renew prescriptions and promoting other products in which they might be interested. CVS and Elensys argued that there were no privacy issues because Elensys was acting solely as a <a href="/wiki/Independent_contractor" title="Independent contractor">contractor</a> to CVS, and because the purpose of the mailings was to educate consumers. CVS claimed that it never shared customers' <a href="/wiki/Medical_history" title="Medical history">medical histories</a> with Elensys (despite the Washington Post's indirect evidence that they had). George D. Lundberg, editor of the <i><a href="/wiki/Journal_of_the_American_Medical_Association" class="mw-redirect" title="Journal of the American Medical Association">Journal of the American Medical Association</a></i>, called the practice "a gross invasion" of <a href="/wiki/Privacy" title="Privacy">privacy</a>. Following a firestorm of criticism and complaints by consumers, CVS discontinued the relationship with Elensys, and moved the practice in-house.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Boston_prescriptions">Boston prescriptions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=CVS_Health&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Boston prescriptions" data-section="26" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>During 2005, a series of prescription mistakes came to light in some of CVS Corporation's <a href="/wiki/Boston" title="Boston">Boston</a>-area stores. An investigation confirmed 62 errors or quality problems going back to 2002. In February 2006, the state Board of Pharmacy announced that the non-profit Institute for Safe Medication Practices (ISMP) would monitor all Massachusetts stores for the next two years.<sup id="cite_ref-61" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Health_and_Medicare_fraud">Health and Medicare fraud</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=CVS_Health&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: Health and Medicare fraud" data-section="27" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>In the late 1980s and early 1990s Caremark RX was involved in a number of health fraud and <a href="/wiki/Medicare_(United_States)" title="Medicare (United States)">Medicare</a> fraud scandals.<sup id="cite_ref-62" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-63" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup>
The combined price to settle this dispute with the U.S. Government cost the company over $250<span class="nowrap"> </span>million.<sup id="cite_ref-64" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Pharmaceutical_kickbacks">Pharmaceutical kickbacks</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=CVS_Health&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: Pharmaceutical kickbacks" data-section="28" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>In 2005, Caremark Rx paid $137.5<span class="nowrap"> </span>million to settle federal lawsuits filed by whistle-blowers that accused a company it acquired in 2003, of improper dealings with pharmaceutical manufacturers.
</p><p>The lawsuits said that the acquired company, <a href="/wiki/AdvancePCS" title="AdvancePCS">AdvancePCS</a>, accepted kickbacks from drug makers to promote their products over those of rivals under contracts with government programs including the Federal Employees Health Benefit Program, the Mail Handlers Health Benefit Program and Medicare health maintenance plans.
</p><p>There was no admission of wrongdoing by Caremark or AdvancePCS.
</p><p>CVS Caremark Corp. has changed their practices. The formulary revision process considers manufacturer rebates, payments from drug manufacturers for low placement on PBM formularies, along with average wholesale price (AWP), drug availability, and bulk discounts when choosing at which co-pay a brand name drug should be placed.<sup id="cite_ref-65" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Deceptive_business_practices">Deceptive business practices</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=CVS_Health&amp;action=edit&amp;section=29" title="Edit section: Deceptive business practices" data-section="29" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>In February 2008, CVS settled a large civil lawsuit for deceptive business practices. The <a href="/wiki/Kaiser_Family_Foundation" title="Kaiser Family Foundation">Kaiser Family Foundation</a> reported:<sup id="cite_ref-66" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup></p>
<blockquote><p>CVS has agreed to a $38.5<span class="nowrap"> </span>million settlement in a multi-state civil deceptive-practices lawsuit against pharmacy benefit manager Caremark filed by 28 attorneys general, the <i><a href="/wiki/Chicago_Tribune" title="Chicago Tribune">Chicago Tribune</a></i> reports.<sup id="cite_ref-miller_67-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-miller-67">[67]</a></sup> The attorneys general, led by Lisa Madigan (D) of Illinois and Douglas Ganslar (D) of Maryland, allege that Caremark "engaged in deceptive business practices" by informing physicians that patients or health plans could save money if patients were switched to certain brand-name prescription drugs (Miller, <i>Chicago Tribune</i>, 2/14).<sup id="cite_ref-miller_67-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-miller-67">[67]</a></sup></p></blockquote>
<blockquote><p>However, the switch often saved patients and health plans only small amounts or increased their costs, while increasing Caremark's profits, Connecticut Attorney General Richard Blumenthal (D) said (Levick, <i><a href="/wiki/Hartford_Courant" title="Hartford Courant">Hartford Courant</a></i>, 2/15).<sup id="cite_ref-levick_68-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-levick-68">[68]</a></sup> Pennsylvania Attorney General Tom Corbett (R) said the PBM<sup id="cite_ref-PBM_69-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PBM-69">[69]</a></sup> kept discounts and rebates that should have been passed on to employers and patients (Levy, <i><a href="/wiki/Associated_Press" title="Associated Press">AP</a>/<a href="/wiki/San_Francisco_Chronicle" title="San Francisco Chronicle">San Francisco Chronicle</a></i>, 2/14).<sup id="cite_ref-levy_2008_70-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-levy_2008-70">[70]</a></sup> In addition, Caremark did not "adequately inform doctors" of the full financial effect of the switch and did not disclose that the switch would increase Caremark's profits, the lawsuit alleges (<i>Chicago Tribune</i>, 2/14).<sup id="cite_ref-miller_67-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-miller-67">[67]</a></sup></p></blockquote>
<blockquote><p>...The settlement prohibits CVS from requesting prescription drug switches in certain cases, such as when the cost to the patient would be higher with the new prescription drug; when the original prescription drug's patent will expire within six months; and when patients were switched from a similar prescription drug within the previous two years (<i>Hartford Courant</i>, 2/15).<sup id="cite_ref-levick_68-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-levick-68">[68]</a></sup> Patients also have the ability to decline a switch from the prescribed treatment to the prescription offered by the pharmacy under the settlement, Madigan said (<i><a href="/wiki/Bloomberg_News" title="Bloomberg News">Bloomberg News</a>/<a href="/wiki/Philadelphia_Inquirer" class="mw-redirect" title="Philadelphia Inquirer">Philadelphia Inquirer</a></i>, 2/15).<sup id="cite_ref-levy_harris_71-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-levy_harris-71">[71]</a></sup></p></blockquote>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Rhode_Island_Senate_corruption_case">Rhode Island Senate corruption case</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=CVS_Health&amp;action=edit&amp;section=30" title="Edit section: Rhode Island Senate corruption case" data-section="30" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>In 2008, two former CVS executives, John R. "Jack" Kramer and Carlos Ortiz, were charged with 20 counts of mail fraud, bribery and conspiracy in relation to Operation Dollar Bill, a probe of corruption in the Rhode Island General Assembly. Kramer and Ortiz hired former State Senator John Celona, who currently is serving 2½ years on corruption charges involving CVS and other companies, as a media consultant for $12,000 a year. Celona was known for walking out on a pharmacy choice vote in the state senate while on the CVS payroll. Despite originally claiming CVS never bought any favors in his own trial, he testified against Kramer and Ortiz as the prosecution's star witness. On May 31, 2008, Kramer and Ortiz were acquitted on all counts. One juror went on the record as saying "My perception living in Rhode Island all my life is, 'Yeah, this probably did go on', but I didn't see any proof beyond a reasonable doubt that CVS did this."<sup id="cite_ref-72" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-72">[72]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Business_practices_under_investigation">Business practices under investigation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=CVS_Health&amp;action=edit&amp;section=31" title="Edit section: Business practices under investigation" data-section="31" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>On May 4, 2010, CVS Caremark Corp. announced that its business practices were being investigated by a group of 24 states, along with the District of Columbia and Los Angeles County. At issue is the post-merger relationship between CVS and Caremark. In addition, the company had earlier acknowledged in a filing with the Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC) that it had received a subpoena from the Office of Inspector General of the United States Department of Health and Human Services, requiring the company to provide information regarding the incentives the company provides to customers who transfer their prescriptions to CVS, including gift cards, goods and other incentives.<sup id="cite_ref-73" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-73">[73]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="FTC_charges_of_privacy_violations">FTC charges of privacy violations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=CVS_Health&amp;action=edit&amp;section=32" title="Edit section: FTC charges of privacy violations" data-section="32" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>On February 18, 2009, CVS Caremark agreed to settle <a href="/wiki/Federal_Trade_Commission" title="Federal Trade Commission">Federal Trade Commission</a> charges that it failed to take reasonable and appropriate security measures to protect the sensitive financial and medical information of its customers and employees, in violation of federal law. In a separate but related agreement, the company's pharmacy chain also has agreed to pay $2.25<span class="nowrap"> </span>million to resolve Department of Health and Human Services allegations that it violated the Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act (HIPAA).<sup id="cite_ref-74" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-74">[74]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="FTC_charges_of_deceptive_pricing">FTC charges of deceptive pricing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=CVS_Health&amp;action=edit&amp;section=33" title="Edit section: FTC charges of deceptive pricing" data-section="33" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>On January 12, 2012, CVS Caremark paid $5<span class="nowrap"> </span>million to settle Federal Trade Commission charges that it misrepresented the prices of certain Medicare Part D prescription drugs – including drugs used to treat breast cancer symptoms and epilepsy – at CVS and <a href="/wiki/Walgreens" title="Walgreens">Walgreens</a> pharmacies.<sup id="cite_ref-75" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-75">[75]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="DEA_investigation_into_oxycodone_diversion">DEA investigation into oxycodone diversion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=CVS_Health&amp;action=edit&amp;section=34" title="Edit section: DEA investigation into oxycodone diversion" data-section="34" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Drug_diversion" title="Drug diversion">Drug diversion</a></div>
<p>According to the U.S. Justice Department, in 2011, CVS pharmacies in Sanford, Florida, ordered enough painkillers to supply a population eight times its size. Sanford has a population of 53,000 but the supply would support 400,000.<sup id="cite_ref-76" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-76">[76]</a></sup> According to the <a href="/wiki/Drug_Enforcement_Administration" title="Drug Enforcement Administration">Drug Enforcement Administration</a>, in 2010, a single CVS pharmacy in Sanford ordered 1.8<span class="nowrap"> </span>million Oxycodone pills, an average of 137,994 pills a month. Other pharmacy customers in Florida averaged 5,364 oxycodone pills a month. DEA investigators serving a warrant to a CVS pharmacy in Sanford on October 18, 2011, noted that "approximately every third car that came through the drive-thru lane had prescriptions for oxycodone or hydrocodone". According to the DEA, a pharmacist at that location stated to investigators that "her customers often requested certain brands of oxycodone using street slang", an indicator that the drugs were being diverted and not used for legitimate pain management. In response, CVS in a statement issued February 17 in response to opioid trafficking questions from <i><a href="/wiki/USA_Today" title="USA Today">USA Today</a></i> said the company is committed to working with the DEA and had taken "significant actions to ensure appropriate dispensing of painkillers in Florida".<sup id="cite_ref-77" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-77">[77]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Restatements">Restatements</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=CVS_Health&amp;action=edit&amp;section=35" title="Edit section: Restatements" data-section="35" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>On November 15, 1999, CVS announced a restatement of its financial results for 1997, and 1998, following a Securities and Exchange Commission review of acquisition-related charges.<sup id="cite_ref-78" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-78">[78]</a></sup></p><p>On February 25, 2005, CVS said it was reducing its previously announced fourth-quarter earnings by $40.5<span class="nowrap"> </span>million, to reflect the way it accounted for leased properties in its results.<sup id="cite_ref-79" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-79">[79]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Opioid_crisis">Opioid crisis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=CVS_Health&amp;action=edit&amp;section=36" title="Edit section: Opioid crisis" data-section="36" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>In September 2016, Massachusetts Attorney General <a href="/wiki/Maura_Healey" title="Maura Healey">Maura Healey</a> announced a $795,000 settlement where CVS agrees to check a state database before filling the prescription for addictive <a href="/wiki/Opioids" class="mw-redirect" title="Opioids">opioids</a> and other controlled substances. The settlement resolves previous allegations that the drugstore chain failed to provide pharmacists with access to the state of Massachusetts Prescription Monitoring Program (PMP).<sup id="cite_ref-80" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-80">[80]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="CFI_lawsuit_for_consumer_fraud">CFI lawsuit for consumer fraud</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=CVS_Health&amp;action=edit&amp;section=37" title="Edit section: CFI lawsuit for consumer fraud" data-section="37" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>In July 2018, the <a href="/wiki/Center_for_Inquiry" title="Center for Inquiry">Center for Inquiry</a> filed a lawsuit against CVS for consumer fraud over its sale of homeopathic medicines. According to Nicholas Little, CFI's Vice President and General Counsel: "Homeopathy is a total sham, and CVS knows it. Yet the company persists in deceiving its customers about the effectiveness of homeopathic products. Homeopathics are shelved right alongside scientifically-proven medicines, under the same signs for cold and flu, pain relief, sleep aids, and so on. If you search for 'flu treatment' on their website, it even suggests homeopathics to you, CVS is making no distinction between those products that have been vetted and tested by science, and those that are nothing but snake oil." The filing in part contends that apart from being a waste of money, choosing homeopathic treatments to the exclusion of evidence-based medicines can result in worsened or prolonged symptoms, and in some cases, even death.<sup id="cite_ref-81" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-81">[81]</a></sup></p>
