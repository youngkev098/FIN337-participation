message</a>)</i></small></div></td></tr></tbody></table><p><b>Dover Corporation</b> is an American <a href="/wiki/Conglomerate_(company)" title="Conglomerate (company)">conglomerate</a> manufacturer of industrial products.<sup id="cite_ref-SEC_Filing_for_Year_2014_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SEC_Filing_for_Year_2014-6">[6]</a></sup> Founded in 1955 in <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-NASDAQ_Stock_Report_for_Dover_Corporation_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NASDAQ_Stock_Report_for_Dover_Corporation-1">[1]</a></sup> Dover is now based in <a href="/wiki/Downers_Grove,_Illinois" title="Downers Grove, Illinois">Downers Grove, Illinois</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-SEC_Filing_for_Year_2014_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SEC_Filing_for_Year_2014-6">[6]</a></sup> and employs more than 26,000 people worldwide.<sup id="cite_ref-NASDAQ_Stock_Report_for_Dover_Corporation_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NASDAQ_Stock_Report_for_Dover_Corporation-1">[1]</a></sup> Dover's business is divided into three segments; <a href="/wiki/Systems_engineering" title="Systems engineering">Engineered Systems</a>, Fluids, and Refrigeration &amp; Food Equipment.<sup id="cite_ref-SEC_Filing_for_Year_2014_6-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SEC_Filing_for_Year_2014-6">[6]</a></sup> Each segment holds operating companies that are run like independent companies. Dover is a constituent of the <a href="/wiki/S%26P_500" class="mw-redirect" title="S&amp;P 500">S&amp;P 500</a> index<sup id="cite_ref-S&amp;P_Dow_Jones_Indices_Press_Release_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-S&amp;P_Dow_Jones_Indices_Press_Release-7">[7]</a></sup> and trades on the New York Stock Exchange under "DOV". Dover is ranked 360th on the <a href="/wiki/Fortune_500" title="Fortune 500">Fortune 500</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Dover_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dover-5">[5]</a></sup> The company relocated its headquarters to Illinois from New York in mid-2010.

</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(1)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dover_Corporation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History" data-section="1" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-1 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-1">
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Founding">Founding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dover_Corporation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Founding" data-section="2" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>In the 1930s and 1940s, George Ohrstrom Sr., a <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a> stockbroker, bought four manufacturing companies: C. Lee Cook Company (seals and piston rings), Rotary Lift (automotive lifts), C. Norris (oil-well pump-sucker rods),<sup id="cite_ref-CohenSalamieWeaver_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CohenSalamieWeaver-8">[8]</a></sup> and Peerless (<a href="/wiki/Space_heater" title="Space heater">space heaters</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-DoverCachedDocs_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DoverCachedDocs-9">[9]</a></sup> Dover Corporation was incorporated in 1947,<sup id="cite_ref-Reuters_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Reuters-10">[10]</a></sup> and in 1955, Ohrstrom brought in the former owner and president of C. Lee Cook Company, Fred D. Durham, to manage his four companies. Later that same year, the Dover Corporate offices opened in <a href="/wiki/Washington,_D.C." title="Washington, D.C.">Washington, D.C.</a>, and Dover Corporation went public on the <a href="/wiki/New_York_Stock_Exchange" title="New York Stock Exchange">New York Stock Exchange</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-CohenSalamieWeaver_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CohenSalamieWeaver-8">[8]</a></sup> As such, the year 1955 marks the company's official founding.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:No_original_research" title="Wikipedia:No original research"><span title="The material near this tag possibly contains original research. (March 2016)">original research?</span></a></i>]</sup></p><p>Fred Durham influenced Dover's corporate culture, emphasizing autonomy, decentralization, and few corporate staff members. As a result, divisions were run in an independent fashion, each with its own president.<sup id="cite_ref-CohenSalamieWeaver_8-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CohenSalamieWeaver-8">[8]</a></sup> Today, Dover is still known for its decentralized management structure, in its governance of operating companies, and for its acquisitive approach.<sup id="cite_ref-warrenbuffet.com_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-warrenbuffet.com-11">[11]</a></sup></p><p>Between 1955 and 1979, Dover acquired fourteen companies.<sup id="cite_ref-CohenSalamieWeaver_8-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CohenSalamieWeaver-8">[8]</a></sup> A great deal of this acquisition activity served to build the Dover Elevator business. This elevator growth began in 1955, as Dover Elevator split from Rotary Lift and became an independent operating company within Dover.<sup id="cite_ref-DoverCachedDocs_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DoverCachedDocs-9">[9]</a></sup> Dover's purchase of the Shepard Warner Elevator Company in 1958 marked the beginning of an effort to grow the elevator business.<sup id="cite_ref-HooversDoverProfile_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HooversDoverProfile-12">[12]</a></sup> In 1963, Dover acquired Acme Elevator, and then Elevator Service,<sup id="cite_ref-DoverCachedDocs_9-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DoverCachedDocs-9">[9]</a></sup> Reddy Elevator Company in 1964, and Hunter-Hayes Elevator Company in 1970. With these purchases, Dover soon became the third largest elevator company in the U.S. and remained so for many years.<sup id="cite_ref-CohenSalamieWeaver_8-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CohenSalamieWeaver-8">[8]</a></sup> Dover continued to expand its elevator division throughout the 1960s and 1970s with the purchases of Moody &amp; Rowe, Burch, Turnbull, Burlington, Hammond &amp; Champness, Louisiana Elevator, and W.W. Moore.<sup id="cite_ref-DoverCachedDocs_9-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DoverCachedDocs-9">[9]</a></sup> Ultimately, Dover sold its elevator business in 1999 to <a href="/wiki/Thyssen_AG" title="Thyssen AG">Thyssen AG</a> for $1.1 billion.<sup id="cite_ref-Dover_Sells_Elevator_Business_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dover_Sells_Elevator_Business-13">[13]</a></sup> Dover Elevator had a pretax operating profit of $93 million in 1997<sup id="cite_ref-Dover_Will_Spin_Off_Elevators_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dover_Will_Spin_Off_Elevators-14">[14]</a></sup> and the sale was mutually beneficial: it allowed Dover to focus on building its other businesses and moved <a href="/wiki/ThyssenKrupp" title="ThyssenKrupp">ThyssenKrupp</a> Elevator Americas to the number three spot worldwide in the elevator and escalator industry.<sup id="cite_ref-Thyssen_and_Dover_in_$1.1_Billion_Deal_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Thyssen_and_Dover_in_%241.1_Billion_Deal-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="What events led Dover to sell their elevator business to Thyssen AG, etc.? Did the board of directors have new business activities in mind to diversify into new companies in 1998? This article should elaborate on this point about selling off its main product line - elevators? (May 2016)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Acquisitions">Acquisitions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dover_Corporation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Acquisitions" data-section="3" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>Dover's acquisition history reaches beyond the elevator industry. With a focus on diversification in the 1960s,<sup id="cite_ref-HooversDoverProfile_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HooversDoverProfile-12">[12]</a></sup> such as the acquisition of Ohio Pattern Works &amp; Foundry Company in 1961, came notable purchases of companies that continue to be a significant part of Dover Corporation today. The company was shorthanded to OPW, and in 1949 one of its engineers, Leonard H. Duerr, invented the automatic shutoff fuel-dispensing nozzle valve.<sup id="cite_ref-U.S._Patent_Doc_-_Duerr_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-U.S._Patent_Doc_-_Duerr-16">[16]</a></sup> Today, the basic technology of Duerr's invention continues to exist in automatic shutoff nozzles,<sup id="cite_ref-OPW_Blog_Post_-_Evolution_of_the_Fuel_Nozzle_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OPW_Blog_Post_-_Evolution_of_the_Fuel_Nozzle-17">[17]</a></sup> and OPW is still a part of Dover, specializing in the design and manufacture of commercial and retail-fueling solutions.<sup id="cite_ref-History_of_OPW_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-History_of_OPW-18">[18]</a></sup></p><p>In 1962, Dover made two notable acquisitions: Detroit Stamping Company, now DE-STA-CO, specializing in the design and manufacture of clamping, gripping, transferring, and robotic tooling solutions;<sup id="cite_ref-DE-STA-CO_Company_History_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DE-STA-CO_Company_History-19">[19]</a></sup> and Alberta Oil Tool, now part of <a href="/wiki/Dover_Artificial_Lift" class="mw-redirect" title="Dover Artificial Lift">Dover Artificial Lift</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Brand_List_for_Dover_ALS_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brand_List_for_Dover_ALS-20">[20]</a></sup> Today, Alberta Oil Tool produces specialty drive rods, Norris tubular fittings, Norriseal control valves, and other products.<sup id="cite_ref-Welcome_Page_of_Alberta_Oil_Tool_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Welcome_Page_of_Alberta_Oil_Tool-21">[21]</a></sup></p><p>1964 marked a significant year, as OPW's former leader Thomas Sutton became president of Dover and corporate headquarters were relocated to New York City. The 1970s were characterized by Dover's intent to expand beyond its principle industries of building materials, equipment, and industrial components. A result of this effort included the acquisition of Dieterich Standard, which manufactured liquid-measurement instruments and whose president, Gary Roubos, went on to become Dover's <a href="/wiki/Chief_operating_officer" title="Chief operating officer">chief operating officer</a> (COO) and president in 1977. Later, Roubos became Dover's <a href="/wiki/Chief_executive_officer" title="Chief executive officer">chief executive officer</a> (CEO) in 1981.<sup id="cite_ref-HooversDoverProfile_12-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HooversDoverProfile-12">[12]</a></sup></p><p>Another significant acquisition included <a href="/wiki/Sargent_Industries" class="mw-redirect" title="Sargent Industries">Sargent Industries</a>, a manufacturer of control devices for aerospace and industrial end-markets, purchased in 1984 for $68 million.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> The Sargent Aerospace &amp; Defense unit persisted as a global supplier of precision-engineered components and aftermarket services, performing critical functions on a variety of commercial and military aircraft, submarines, and land-based vehicles.<sup id="cite_ref-Landing_Page_for_Sargent_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Landing_Page_for_Sargent-23">[23]</a></sup></p><p>In the 1980s, Dover also began to focus more on electronics. This period was marked by the acquisitions of K&amp;L Microwave in 1983, Dielectric Laboratories in 1985, and NURAD in 1986.<sup id="cite_ref-HooversDoverProfile_12-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HooversDoverProfile-12">[12]</a></sup> Dover later chose to spin off DOVatron in 1993 to shareholders,<sup id="cite_ref-DOVatron_Press_Release_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DOVatron_Press_Release-24">[24]</a></sup> as the company specialized in circuit-board assembly and had become a source of competition for Dover customers.<sup id="cite_ref-HooversDoverProfile_12-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HooversDoverProfile-12">[12]</a></sup></p><p>That same year, Dover acquired Phoenix Refrigeration Systems, which then acquired Hill Refrigeration in 1994 to form Hillphoenix. Hillphoenix now manufactures commercial refrigeration systems and mechanical centers, electrical distribution products, walk-in coolers and freezers, and specialty display cases and fixtures.<sup id="cite_ref-About_Us_Page_for_Hillphoenix_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-About_Us_Page_for_Hillphoenix-25">[25]</a></sup> The company is a partner of the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Environmental_Protection_Agency" title="United States Environmental Protection Agency">EPA</a>'s GreenChill initiative, which focuses on reducing refrigerant emissions and climate-change impact.<sup id="cite_ref-Listing_of_GreenChill_Partners_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Listing_of_GreenChill_Partners-26">[26]</a></sup> Hillphoenix has more equipment installed at GreenChill Certified retail stores than any other refrigeration manufacturer.<sup id="cite_ref-Hillphoenix_Sustainability_Center_Overview_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hillphoenix_Sustainability_Center_Overview-27">[27]</a></sup></p><p>Also in 1994, Dover's <a href="/wiki/COO_(chief_operating_officer)" class="mw-redirect" title="COO (chief operating officer)">COO</a> and President, Thomas Reece, became CEO of the company.<sup id="cite_ref-HooversDoverProfile_12-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HooversDoverProfile-12">[12]</a></sup> The following year, Dover purchased an 88 percent interest in the French company Imaje Printing Products for $200 million;<sup id="cite_ref-CohenSalamieWeaver_8-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CohenSalamieWeaver-8">[8]</a></sup> this acquisition marks the largest in Dover's corporate history.<sup id="cite_ref-HooversDoverProfile_12-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HooversDoverProfile-12">[12]</a></sup> In 2006, Dover acquired Markem Corporation which specializes in identification solutions.<sup id="cite_ref-Press_Release_Announcing_MARKEM_Acquisition_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Press_Release_Announcing_MARKEM_Acquisition-28">[28]</a></sup> One year later, the companies merged and were renamed <a href="/wiki/Markem-Imaje" title="Markem-Imaje">Markem-Imaje</a>, and have since focused on product coding, labeling, and traceability solutions.<sup id="cite_ref-Overview_of_Markem-Imaje_Products_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Overview_of_Markem-Imaje_Products-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="2000s">2000s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dover_Corporation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: 2000s" data-section="4" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>While Dover acquired more than seventy companies between 1998 and 2002, the company's acquisition rate slowed in the early 2000s. Dover sold eight companies in 2001 for a total of $400 million. The early 2000s also marked a leadership change: Ronald Hoffman, Dover's vice president, and former president and CEO of Dover Resources, become president and COO of Dover Corporation in 2003. In 2005, he was appointed to the position of CEO.<sup id="cite_ref-HooversDoverProfile_12-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HooversDoverProfile-12">[12]</a></sup> The financial crisis hit Dover hard, but the company recovered quickly.<sup id="cite_ref-Alden2011_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Alden2011-30">[30]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="IN SUM THIS SENTENCE NEEDS TO BE MORE SPECIFIC AND PROVIDE QUANTITY OF EFFECTS OF 2007/08 RECESSION. (May 2016)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p><p>Dover focused on growing target areas from 2007 to 2009, including electronic communication, energy and fluids, product identification, and refrigeration.<sup id="cite_ref-HooversDoverProfile_12-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HooversDoverProfile-12">[12]</a></sup> Dover continued to grow its refrigeration business with the 2012 purchase of Anthony International for $602.5 million. Anthony designs and manufactures specialty glass, commercial-glass refrigeration and freezer doors, lighting systems, and display equipment.<sup id="cite_ref-Anthony_Acquisition_Announcement_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Anthony_Acquisition_Announcement-31">[31]</a></sup></p><p>In 2008, Robert Livingston was appointed Dover's new CEO and president. Livingston's Dover career began twenty-nine years earlier with the acquisition of K&amp;L Microwave, where he was a vice president.<sup id="cite_ref-Dover_CEO_and_Company_Profile_in_Dow_Jones_Newswires_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dover_CEO_and_Company_Profile_in_Dow_Jones_Newswires-32">[32]</a></sup> After joining Dover, Livingston also served as COO and vice president of Dover Corporation, president and CEO of Dover Engineered Systems, and president and CEO of Dover Electronics.<sup id="cite_ref-Announcement_of_Livingston_as_CEO_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Announcement_of_Livingston_as_CEO-33">[33]</a></sup> Under Livingston's leadership, Dover moved its corporate headquarters from New York City to the <a href="/wiki/Chicago" title="Chicago">Chicago</a> area in 2010. Factors at play in this decision included Chicago's central location, wide variety of housing options for employees, quality of life, and nearby air service to national and global destinations.<sup id="cite_ref-Dover_Moving_to_Illinois_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dover_Moving_to_Illinois-34">[34]</a></sup> The choice to move to <a href="/wiki/Downers_Grove,_Illinois" title="Downers Grove, Illinois">Downers Grove, Illinois</a>, was also partially impacted by an effort to consolidate operations, and reduce administration costs,<sup id="cite_ref-Corfman_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Corfman-35">[35]</a></sup> as the move brought all four segment headquarters under one roof.<sup id="cite_ref-Dover_Moving_to_Illinois_34-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dover_Moving_to_Illinois-34">[34]</a></sup></p><p>From 2008 to 2012, Dover acquired almost twenty-five companies. Livingston's corporate development strategy sought to strengthen promising businesses and divest those that were either growing slowly or had significant exposure to highly volatile industries. As a result, Dover was able to apply greater focus to growing four or five targeted end-markets. Livingston also worked to reduced expenses by consolidating back office operations, metal purchases, and freight transportation.<sup id="cite_ref-Dover_CEO_and_Company_Profile_in_Dow_Jones_Newswires_32-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dover_CEO_and_Company_Profile_in_Dow_Jones_Newswires-32">[32]</a></sup> Inpro/Seal, a producer of bearing isolators, was purchased by Waukesha Bearings Corporation in 2009, which included 150,991 shares of Dover's common stock being issued to Inpro/Seal shareholders.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> In 2010, KMC Inc. and Bearings Plus Inc. were acquired by Waukesha Bearings Corporation with the expectation of synergizing their bearing seal technologies with Dover.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> In addition, between 2008 and 2010, Dover spent approximately $436 million to purchase a total of sixteen businesses, while only $100 million was earned from the sale of eight businesses.<sup id="cite_ref-Alden2011_30-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Alden2011-30">[30]</a></sup></p><p>In 2011, Dover expanded its acoustic components business with the purchase of Sound Solutions, a manufacturer of dynamic speakers and receivers for mobile phones and other consumer electronics, for $855 million.<sup id="cite_ref-Press_Release_Acquiring_Sound_Solutions_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Press_Release_Acquiring_Sound_Solutions-38">[38]</a></sup> Dover had acquired Knowles, a designer and manufacturer of hearing-aid technology and other acoustical componentry, six years earlier for $750 million.<sup id="cite_ref-Dover_to_Buy_Knowles_39-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dover_to_Buy_Knowles-39">[39]</a></sup> Together, Sound Solutions and Knowles gave Dover the necessary scale to develop integrated solutions for the rapidly evolving mobile-electronics industry.<sup id="cite_ref-Dover_CEO_and_Company_Profile_in_Dow_Jones_Newswires_32-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dover_CEO_and_Company_Profile_in_Dow_Jones_Newswires-32">[32]</a></sup> In 2014, Dover spun off Knowles,<sup id="cite_ref-Dover_Spin_Off_Of_Knowles_40-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dover_Spin_Off_Of_Knowles-40">[40]</a></sup> which had a $2.6 billion market cap post-spin.<sup id="cite_ref-Knowles_Financials_Post_Spin_Off_41-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Knowles_Financials_Post_Spin_Off-41">[41]</a></sup> Today, Knowles continues to be a global supplier of communication-technology components, such as <a href="/wiki/Microelectromechanical_systems" title="Microelectromechanical systems">MEMS</a> (micro-electro-mechanical systems), <a href="/wiki/Microphone" title="Microphone">microphones</a>, <a href="/wiki/Speaker_(electronics)" class="mw-redirect" title="Speaker (electronics)">speakers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Radio_receiver" title="Radio receiver">receivers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Transducer" title="Transducer">transducers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Capacitor" title="Capacitor">capacitors</a> and <a href="/wiki/Oscillation" title="Oscillation">oscillators</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Overview_of_Knowles_42-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Overview_of_Knowles-42">[42]</a></sup> Dover CEO and president Robert Livingston described the spinoff as allowing both Knowles and Dover to more aggressively pursue their individual growth strategies.<sup id="cite_ref-Dover_Board_Approves_Spin-Off_43-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dover_Board_Approves_Spin-Off-43">[43]</a></sup></p><p>In 2015, Dover sold Sargent Aerospace &amp; Defense to RBC Bearings.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup></p>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(2)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Segments_and_operating_companies">Segments and operating companies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dover_Corporation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Segments and operating companies" data-section="5" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-2 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-2">
<p>Dover's organization and segment structure has changed over its history. For example, in 1985 Dover restructured into five major subsidiaries to reflect its key markets of interest. For reporting purposes, these five major subsidies were represented in four sections: building industries (Dover Elevator International); electronic products (Dover Technologies); petroleum (Dover Resources); and industrial and aerospace products (Dover Industries, Dover Sargent). In 1989, Dover re-segmented into six sectors based on market activity changes: Dover Elevator International, Dover Technology, Dover Resources, Dover Industries, Dover Diversified, and Corporate Companies. After selling Dover Elevator, these segments became Diversified, Electronics, Industries, Resources, Systems and Technologies.<sup id="cite_ref-10-K_Annual_Report_2005_45-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10-K_Annual_Report_2005-45">[45]</a></sup> In 2007, Dover reported four segments: Industrial Products, Engineered Systems, Fluid Management, and Electronic Technologies.<sup id="cite_ref-2007_Annual_Report_46-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2007_Annual_Report-46">[46]</a></sup> By 2012, Dover had re-segmented again into Dover Communication Technologies, Dover Energy, Dover Engineered Systems, and Dover Printing &amp; Identification.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p><p>Since 2014, Dover Corporation has operated with four segments: Energy, Engineered Systems, Fluids, and Refrigeration &amp; Food Equipment.<sup id="cite_ref-Dover_2014_Quarterly_Report_47-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dover_2014_Quarterly_Report-47">[47]</a></sup> The operating companies that reside within each segment are run like independent companies.<sup id="cite_ref-warrenbuffet.com_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-warrenbuffet.com-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Energy">Energy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dover_Corporation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Energy" data-section="6" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>Today, the Energy segment works in the drilling and production markets to develop solutions that advance the efficiency and safety of extracting oil and gas. It is composed of seven operating companies: Cook Compression, <a href="/wiki/Dover_Artificial_Lift" class="mw-redirect" title="Dover Artificial Lift">Dover Artificial Lift</a>, Dover Energy Automation (DEA), Accelerated Process Systems, TWG, US Synthetic, and Waukesha Bearings.<sup id="cite_ref-Dover_Energy_Market_Overview_48-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dover_Energy_Market_Overview-48">[48]</a></sup> Cook Compression develops engineered compressor solutions,<sup id="cite_ref-Homepage_of_Cook_Compression_49-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Homepage_of_Cook_Compression-49">[49]</a></sup> while Dover Artificial Lift develops solutions for oil-and-gas production, such as rod lifts, progressing cavity pumps, gas lifts, and plunger-lift systems.<sup id="cite_ref-Homepage_of_Dover_ALS_50-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Homepage_of_Dover_ALS-50">[50]</a></sup> DEA includes Quartzdyne, which designs, develops, and manufactures resonating quartz pressure transducers for the downhole oil-and-gas industry.<sup id="cite_ref-Homepage_of_Quartzdyne_51-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Homepage_of_Quartzdyne-51">[51]</a></sup> TWG is made up of six companies, all of which specialize in industry-specific winches and supporting electronic systems.<sup id="cite_ref-TWG_Brands_52-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TWG_Brands-52">[52]</a></sup> US Synthetic produces long-lasting diamond inserts for downhole drilling-tool applications.<sup id="cite_ref-US_Synthetic_Overview_53-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-US_Synthetic_Overview-53">[53]</a></sup> Finally, Waukesha Bearings develops custom-engineered fluid-film bearings and magnet-bearing systems for high-performance turbomachinery.<sup id="cite_ref-Waukesha_Bearings_Overview_54-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Waukesha_Bearings_Overview-54">[54]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Engineered_Systems">Engineered Systems</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dover_Corporation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Engineered Systems" data-section="7" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>The Engineered Systems segment develops products and systems for a variety of industries, from printing to waste handling. JK Group, <a href="/wiki/Markem-Imaje" title="Markem-Imaje">Markem-Imaje</a>, MS Printing Solutions, and OK International all serve the printing and identification markets. Environmental Solutions Group develops solutions for the transportation and transformation of solid waste. De-Sta-Co manufactures work-holding and flexible-automation solutions, such as clamps and grippers. Microwave Products Group serves defense, aerospace, and telecommunications markets. Lastly, Performance Motorsports Inc., Texas Hydraulics, Vehicle Service Group, and Warn each develop solutions for the automobile and vehicle-service industry.<sup id="cite_ref-Dover_Engineered_Systems_55-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dover_Engineered_Systems-55">[55]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Fluids">Fluids</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dover_Corporation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Fluids" data-section="8" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>The Fluids segment designs and manufactures products and systems that safely handle critical fluids for the following end markets: oil-and-gas, retail-fueling, vehicle-wash, chemical, hygienic (food and pharmaceutical), and industrial. This segment is composed of: Colder Products Company, Finder, Hydro Systems Company, Maag, OPW, Tokheim, Wayne Fueling Systems and Pump Solutions Group.<sup id="cite_ref-Overview_of_Dover_Fluids_Market_56-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Overview_of_Dover_Fluids_Market-56">[56]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span id="Refrigeration_.26_Food_Equipment"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Refrigeration_&amp;_Food_Equipment">Refrigeration &amp; Food Equipment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dover_Corporation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Refrigeration &amp; Food Equipment" data-section="9" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>Dover's fourth segment, Refrigeration &amp; Food Equipment, develops equipment and systems for the commercial-refrigeration, heating and cooling, and food and beverage packaging, and food service industries.<sup id="cite_ref-Overview_of_Refrigeration_and_Food_Equipment_Market_57-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Overview_of_Refrigeration_and_Food_Equipment_Market-57">[57]</a></sup> Anthony and Hill Phoenix design solutions for grocery stores: Anthony manufactures glass refrigerator and freezer doors, lighting systems and display equipment,<sup id="cite_ref-Products_of_Anthony_58-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Products_of_Anthony-58">[58]</a></sup> and Hillphoenix manufactures display cases, walk-in coolers/freezers, and refrigeration and power systems.<sup id="cite_ref-Hillphoenix_Landing_Page_59-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hillphoenix_Landing_Page-59">[59]</a></sup> The packaging industry is served by Belvac Production Machinery, Inc., which offers solutions for the beverage can-making industry.<sup id="cite_ref-Homepage_of_Belvac_60-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Homepage_of_Belvac-60">[60]</a></sup> SWEP specializes in brazed-plate heat exchangers, with applications in air conditioning, refrigeration, heating, and industry and district energy.<sup id="cite_ref-Company_Overview_of_SWEP_61-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Company_Overview_of_SWEP-61">[61]</a></sup> Unified Brands produces commercial kitchen equipment.<sup id="cite_ref-Homepage_of_unified_brands_62-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Homepage_of_unified_brands-62">[62]</a></sup></p>
