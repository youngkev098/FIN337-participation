</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(1)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Comerica&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History" data-section="1" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-1 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-1">
<p>In 1849, the company was founded in Detroit by <a href="/wiki/Elon_Farnsworth_(Michigan_Attorney_General)" title="Elon Farnsworth (Michigan Attorney General)">Elon Farnsworth</a> as the <b>Detroit Savings Fund Institute</b>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p><p>Its name changed to <b>The Detroit Savings Bank</b> in 1871 and to <b>The Detroit Bank</b> in 1936, being one of the few area banks to survive the <a href="/wiki/Great_Depression" title="Great Depression">Great Depression</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-158detroit_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-158detroit-3">[3]</a></sup> In 1956, the company merged with Birmingham National Bank, Ferndale National Bank and Detroit Wabeek Bank and Trust Company to form <b>The Detroit Bank &amp; Trust Company</b>.<sup id="cite_ref-158detroit_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-158detroit-3">[3]</a></sup> In 1973, it formed a holding company, DetroitBank Corporation.<sup id="cite_ref-158detroit_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-158detroit-3">[3]</a></sup> The current name was adopted in 1982.<sup id="cite_ref-158detroit_3-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-158detroit-3">[3]</a></sup></p><p>In 1982, Comerica entered the Florida market. In 1983, it acquired its hometown rival, Bank of the Commonwealth of Michigan. It entered the Texas market in 1988 when it acquired Grand Bancshares.
</p><p>In 1990, Comerica received approval to construct a new headquarters building, <a href="/wiki/One_Detroit_Center" class="mw-redirect" title="One Detroit Center">One Detroit Center</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p><p>In 1991, the bank expanded to California by acquiring Plaza Commerce Bancorp and InBancshares.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p><p>In 1992, the bank merged with a similarly-sized Detroit-based bank, Manufacturers National Corporation.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p><p>In 1996, the bank sold its Illinois operation to <a href="/wiki/LaSalle_Bank" title="LaSalle Bank">LaSalle Bank</a> parent <a href="/wiki/ABN_Amro" class="mw-redirect" title="ABN Amro">ABN Amro</a> for $190 million.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p><p>In 1998, the bank signed a 30-year $66 million agreement for the naming rights to <a href="/wiki/Comerica_Park" title="Comerica Park">Comerica Park</a> in downtown Detroit, home to the <a href="/wiki/Detroit_Tigers" title="Detroit Tigers">Detroit Tigers</a> of <a href="/wiki/Major_League_Baseball" title="Major League Baseball">Major League Baseball</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p><p>In 2000, the bank sold its credit card division to <a href="/wiki/MBNA" title="MBNA">MBNA</a> and formed an alliance with the company.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p><p>In 2001, the bank acquired Imperial Bank of California, which also had branches in Arizona.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p><p>On March 6, 2007, the company announced its decision to relocate its corporate headquarters to Dallas to move closer to its customer base in the Sun Belt.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> In August, the company announced that it selected 1717 Main Street in <a href="/wiki/Downtown_Dallas" title="Downtown Dallas">Downtown Dallas</a>. The company executives began moving into the new location in November 2007 and the building was renamed Comerica Tower.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p><p>In January 2008, the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_the_Treasury" title="United States Department of the Treasury">United States Department of the Treasury</a> selected the company as the issuing bank for its Direct Express <a href="/wiki/Debit_card" title="Debit card">debit card</a> program. The federal government uses the Express Debit product to issue electronic payments, such as <a href="/wiki/Social_Security_(United_States)" title="Social Security (United States)">Social Security</a> benefits, to people who do not have bank accounts (<a href="/wiki/Unbanked" title="Unbanked">unbanked</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p><p>In July 2011, the bank acquired <a href="/wiki/Sterling_Bank_(Texas)" title="Sterling Bank (Texas)">Sterling Bank</a> of Texas for $1.03 billion.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p><p>In 2017, the bank announced plans to reduce its office space by 500,000 square feet, saving $7 million in 2018.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p><p>In March 2020, Comerica appointed a new executive vice president and chief enterprise technology and operations services officer, Megan Crespi.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
