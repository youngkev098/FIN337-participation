<p><b>FedEx Corporation</b> is an American <a href="/wiki/Multinational_corporation" title="Multinational corporation">multinational</a> delivery services company headquartered in <a href="/wiki/Memphis,_Tennessee" title="Memphis, Tennessee">Memphis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tennessee" title="Tennessee">Tennessee</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> The name "FedEx" is a <a href="/wiki/Syllabic_abbreviation" class="mw-redirect" title="Syllabic abbreviation">syllabic abbreviation</a> of the name of the company's original air division, <b>Fed</b>eral <b>Ex</b>press (now <a href="/wiki/FedEx_Express" title="FedEx Express">FedEx Express</a>), which was used from 1973 until 2000.  The company is known for its overnight shipping service and pioneering a system that could track packages and provide real-time updates on package location, a feature that has now been implemented by most other carrier services.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> FedEx is also <a href="/wiki/Top_100_Contractors_of_the_U.S._federal_government" title="Top 100 Contractors of the U.S. federal government">one of the top contractors of the US government</a>.

</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(1)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=FedEx&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History" data-section="1" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-1 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-1">
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">For the history of Federal Express, see <a href="/wiki/FedEx_Express" title="FedEx Express">FedEx Express</a>.</div>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:First_FedEx_van_Memphis_TN_2013-05-17_002.jpg" class="image"><noscript><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/56/First_FedEx_van_Memphis_TN_2013-05-17_002.jpg/220px-First_FedEx_van_Memphis_TN_2013-05-17_002.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="1024" data-file-height="768"></noscript><span class="lazy-image-placeholder" style="width: 220px;height: 165px;" data-src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/56/First_FedEx_van_Memphis_TN_2013-05-17_002.jpg/220px-First_FedEx_van_Memphis_TN_2013-05-17_002.jpg" data-alt="" data-width="220" data-height="165" data-class="thumbimage"> </span></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:First_FedEx_van_Memphis_TN_2013-05-17_002.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Fedex's first van displayed at the FedEx World Headquarters</div></div></div>
<p>The company was founded in 1971 as <b>Federal Express Corporation</b> by <a href="/wiki/Frederick_W._Smith" title="Frederick W. Smith">Frederick W. Smith</a>, a graduate of <a href="/wiki/Yale_Business_School" class="mw-redirect" title="Yale Business School">Yale Business School</a>. He drew up the company's concept in a term paper at Yale, in which he called for a system specifically designed for urgent deliveries. While his professor didn't think much of the idea, Smith pressed on. He began formal operations in 1973, when he moved operations to Memphis. Besides its location near the center of the country, Memphis International Airport almost always stayed open in inclement weather.<sup id="cite_ref-AboutFedEx_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AboutFedEx-4">[4]</a></sup></p><p>The company grew rapidly, and by 1983 had a billion dollars in revenues, a rarity for a startup company that had never taken part in mergers or acquisitions in its first decade. It expanded to Europe and Asia in 1984. In 1988, it acquired one of its major competitors, <a href="/wiki/Flying_Tiger_Line" title="Flying Tiger Line">Flying Tiger Line</a>, creating the largest full-service cargo airline in the world. In 1994, Federal Express shortened its name to "FedEx" for marketing purposes, officially adopting a nickname that had been used for years.<sup id="cite_ref-AboutFedEx_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AboutFedEx-4">[4]</a></sup></p><p>On October 2, 1997, FedEx reorganized as a holding company, FDX Corporation, a <a href="/wiki/Delaware" title="Delaware">Delaware</a> corporation.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> The new holding company began operations in January 1998 with the acquisition of <a href="/wiki/Roadway_Services" title="Roadway Services">Caliber System Inc.</a> by Federal Express. With the purchase of Caliber, FedEx started offering other services besides express shipping. Caliber subsidiaries included RPS, a small-package ground service; Roberts Express, an expedited shipping provider; Viking Freight, a regional, <a href="/wiki/Less-than-truckload_shipping" title="Less-than-truckload shipping">less-than-truckload</a> freight carrier serving the Western United States; Caribbean Transportation Services, a provider of airfreight forwarding between the United States and the Caribbean; and Caliber Logistics and Caliber Technology, providers of logistics and technology services. FDX Corporation was founded to oversee all of the operations of those companies and its original air division, Federal Express.<sup id="cite_ref-AboutFedEx_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AboutFedEx-4">[4]</a></sup></p><p>In the 1990s, FedEx Ground planned, but later abandoned, a joint service with <a href="/wiki/British_Airways" title="British Airways">British Airways</a> to have BA fly a <a href="/wiki/Concorde" title="Concorde">Concorde</a> <a href="/wiki/Supersonic_airliner" class="mw-redirect" title="Supersonic airliner">supersonic</a> <a href="/wiki/Jet_airliner" title="Jet airliner">jet airliner</a> to <a href="/wiki/Shannon_Airport" title="Shannon Airport">Shannon Airport</a> in Ireland with FedEx packages on board, and then FedEx would have flown the packages <a href="/wiki/Subsonic_flight" class="mw-redirect" title="Subsonic flight">subsonically</a> to their delivery points in Europe. <a href="/wiki/Ron_Ponder" title="Ron Ponder">Ron Ponder</a>, a vice president at the time, was in charge of this proposed venture.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:FEDEX_MD-11F(AF)_(N612FE48605555).jpg" class="image"><noscript><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e9/FEDEX_MD-11F%28AF%29_%28N612FE48605555%29.jpg/220px-FEDEX_MD-11F%28AF%29_%28N612FE48605555%29.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="103" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="4564" data-file-height="2140"></noscript><span class="lazy-image-placeholder" style="width: 220px;height: 103px;" data-src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e9/FEDEX_MD-11F%28AF%29_%28N612FE48605555%29.jpg/220px-FEDEX_MD-11F%28AF%29_%28N612FE48605555%29.jpg" data-alt="" data-width="220" data-height="103" data-class="thumbimage"> </span></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:FEDEX_MD-11F(AF)_(N612FE48605555).jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A Federal Express <a href="/wiki/McDonnell_Douglas_MD-11" title="McDonnell Douglas MD-11">McDonnell Douglas MD-11</a> in 1995</div></div></div>
<p>In January 2000, FDX Corporation changed its name to FedEx Corporation and re-branded all of its subsidiaries. Federal Express became FedEx Express, RPS became <a href="/wiki/FedEx_Ground" title="FedEx Ground">FedEx Ground</a>, Roberts Express became FedEx Custom Critical, and Caliber Logistics and Caliber Technology were combined to comprise FedEx Global Logistics. A new subsidiary, called FedEx Corporate Services, was formed to centralize the sales, marketing, and customer service for all of the subsidiaries. In February 2000, FedEx acquired Tower Group International, an international logistics company. FedEx also acquired WorldTariff, a <a href="/wiki/Customs_duty" class="mw-redirect" title="Customs duty">customs duty</a> and tax information company; TowerGroup and WorldTariff were re-branded to form FedEx Trade Networks.<sup id="cite_ref-about.fedex.designcdt.com_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-about.fedex.designcdt.com-6">[6]</a></sup></p><p>FedEx Corp. acquired privately held <a href="/wiki/Kinko%27s" class="mw-redirect" title="Kinko's">Kinko's</a>, Inc. in February 2004 and re-branded it FedEx Kinko's. The acquisition was made to expand FedEx's retail access to the general public. After the acquisition, all FedEx Kinko's locations offered only FedEx shipping.<sup id="cite_ref-about.fedex.designcdt.com_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-about.fedex.designcdt.com-6">[6]</a></sup> In June 2008, FedEx announced that they would be dropping the Kinko's name from their ship centers; FedEx Kinko's would now be called <a href="/wiki/FedEx_Office" title="FedEx Office">FedEx Office</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> In September 2004, FedEx acquired Parcel Direct, a parcel consolidator, and re-branded it FedEx SmartPost.<sup id="cite_ref-about.fedex.designcdt.com_6-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-about.fedex.designcdt.com-6">[6]</a></sup></p><p>In December 2007, the U.S. <a href="/wiki/Internal_Revenue_Service" title="Internal Revenue Service">Internal Revenue Service</a> "tentatively decided" the FedEx Ground Division might be facing a tax liability of $319 million for 2002, due to misclassification of its operatives as independent contractors. Reversing a 1994 decision which allowed FedEx to classify its operatives that own their own vehicles as independent contractors, the IRS audited the years 2003 to 2006, with a view to assessing whether similar misclassification of operatives had taken place. FedEx denied that any irregularities in classification had occurred, but faced legal action from operatives claiming benefits that would have accrued had they been classified as employees.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p><p>In June 2009, FedEx began a campaign against <a href="/wiki/United_Parcel_Service" title="United Parcel Service">United Parcel Service</a> (UPS) and the <a href="/wiki/Teamsters" class="mw-redirect" title="Teamsters">Teamsters</a> union, accusing its competitor of receiving a <a href="/wiki/Bailout" title="Bailout">bailout</a> in an advertising campaign called "<a href="/wiki/Brown_Bailout" class="mw-redirect" title="Brown Bailout">Brown Bailout</a>". FedEx claimed that signing the <a href="/wiki/Federal_Aviation_Administration" title="Federal Aviation Administration">Federal Aviation Administration</a> re-authorization bill, which would let some of its workers <a href="/wiki/Trade_union" title="Trade union">unionize</a> more easily (and, according to the Memphis-based company, "could expose [its] customers at any time to local <a href="/wiki/Work_stoppage" class="mw-redirect" title="Work stoppage">work stoppages</a> that interrupted the flow of their time-sensitive, high-value shipments"),<sup id="cite_ref-bailout_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bailout-10">[10]</a></sup> was equivalent to giving UPS a "<a href="/wiki/Bailout" title="Bailout">bailout</a>". Independent observers heavily criticized FedEx's wording,<sup id="cite_ref-bailout_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bailout-10">[10]</a></sup> claiming that it was "an abuse of the term".<sup id="cite_ref-bailout_10-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bailout-10">[10]</a></sup> FedEx Express employees are regulated under the <a href="/wiki/Railway_Labor_Act" title="Railway Labor Act">Railway Labor Act</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p><p>On January 14, 2013, FedEx named Henry Maier CEO and President of FedEx Ground, to take effect after David Rebholz retired on May 31, 2013.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> On July 17, 2014, FedEx was indicted for conspiracy to distribute controlled substances in cooperation with the Chhabra-Smoley Organization and Superior Drugs.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> According to the U.S. <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Justice" title="United States Department of Justice">Department of Justice</a>, "FedEx is alleged to have knowingly and intentionally conspired to distribute controlled substances and prescription drugs, including <a href="/wiki/Phendimetrazine" title="Phendimetrazine">Phendimetrazine</a> (Schedule III); <a href="/wiki/Ambien" class="mw-redirect" title="Ambien">Ambien</a>, <a href="/wiki/Phentermine" title="Phentermine">Phentermine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Diazepam" title="Diazepam">Diazepam</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Alprazolam" title="Alprazolam">Alprazolam</a> (Schedule IV), to customers who had no legitimate medical need for them based on invalid prescriptions issued by doctors who were acting outside the usual course of professional practice."<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> A representative for the company contested these claims, stating that it would violate personal rights of customers to deny service and that "We are a transportation company — we are not law enforcement".<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>  On July 17, 2016 the Department of Justice U.S. Attorney's Office confirmed in a statement that it had asked U.S. District Court Judge Charles Breyer to dismiss the indictment but also did not say why.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p><p>In April 2015, FedEx acquired their rival firm <a href="/wiki/TNT_Express" title="TNT Express">TNT Express</a> for €4.4 billion ($4.8 billion; £3.2 billion) as it looked to expand their operations in Europe.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p><p>In February 2016, FedEx announced the launch of FedEx Cares, a global giving platform, and committed to invest $200 million to strengthen more than 200 communities by 2020.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p><p>In March 2018, FedEx announced the acquisition of P2P Mailing Limited, a last-mile delivery service, for £92 million to expand their portfolio.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p><p>In September 2018, FedEx expanded FedEx Ground U.S. operations to six days per week due to the rise in demand for e-commerce<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p><p>In January 2019, FedEx Trade Networks was re-branded to FedEx Logistics.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p><p>In May 2019, FedEx announced the expansion of FedEx Ground U.S. operations to seven days per week during the holiday peak season. The service will continue year-round beginning in January 2020 for the majority of the U.S. population.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p><p>On June 1, 2019, the authorities in China filed a case against FedEx for allegedly undermining the rights of Chinese clients.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> The investigation stemmed from allegations by <a href="/wiki/Huawei" title="Huawei">Huawei</a> that FedEx attempted to divert the shipping route of its packages without the company's prior authorization.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-28">[28]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:1_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-29">[29]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> which in turn have been denied by FedEx.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_28-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-28">[28]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:1_29-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-29">[29]</a></sup> It has been reported that FedEx refused to deliver a used <a href="/wiki/Huawei" title="Huawei">Huawei</a> phone into the US. Writers at <a href="/wiki/PC_Magazine" title="PC Magazine">PC Magazine</a> tried to ship a <a href="/wiki/Huawei_P30" title="Huawei P30">Huawei P30</a> from a UK office to a US one to find it sent back a few days later.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p><p>In June 2019, FedEx announced they would not be renewing their $850 million contract with Amazon for the company's U.S. domestic express delivery business. Amazon accounted for 1.3 percent of 2018 revenues.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> In August 2019, FedEx announced the termination of ground deliveries for Amazon as well.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></p><p>In July 2019, China accused FedEx of holding back more than 100 packages that Huawei was trying to deliver to China. The Chinese state media said that the company committed "violations" when it diverted Huawei parcels.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(2)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Finances">Finances</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=FedEx&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Finances" data-section="2" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-2 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-2">

FedEx's shares traded at over $244 per share, and its market capitalization was valued at over US$55.5 billion in October 2018.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> FedEx ranked No. 50 in the 2018 <a href="/wiki/Fortune_500" title="Fortune 500">Fortune 500</a> list of the largest United States corporations by total revenue.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> In June, 2019, investor Michael Burry of Scion Asset Management (from "The Big Short") reported taking a position in the company.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></p>

<p>The FedEx logo is a <a href="/wiki/Wordmark_(graphic_identity)" class="mw-redirect" title="Wordmark (graphic identity)">wordmark</a> designed in 1994 by Lindon Leader of Landor Associates, of San Francisco.<sup id="cite_ref-logo_39-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-logo-39">[39]</a></sup> It consists of <i>Fed</i> in purple and <i>Ex</i> in orange. The FedEx wordmark is notable for containing a subliminal right-pointing arrow in the <a href="/wiki/Negative_space" title="Negative space">negative space</a> between the "E" and the "X", which was achieved by designing a proprietary font, based on <a href="/wiki/Univers" title="Univers">Univers</a> and <a href="/wiki/Futura_(typeface)" title="Futura (typeface)">Futura</a>, to emphasize the arrow shape.<sup id="cite_ref-logo_39-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-logo-39">[39]</a></sup> Previously, the <i>Ex</i> was in a different color for each <a href="/wiki/Division_(organisation)" class="mw-redirect" title="Division (organisation)">division</a> and platinum for the overall corporation use. However, in August 2016, FedEx announced that all operating units will adopt the purple and orange color logo over the next five years (the same as the original FedEx logo, and later used by FedEx Express).<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup></p>

<p>FedEx is divided into the following operating units:
</p>
<ul><li><b><a href="/wiki/FedEx_Express" title="FedEx Express">FedEx Express</a></b> (Orange "Ex"): The original overnight <a href="/wiki/Courier" title="Courier">courier</a> services, providing next day air service within the United States and time-definite international service. FedEx Express operates one of the largest civil aircraft fleets in the world and the largest fleet of wide bodied civil aircraft; it also carries more freight than any other airline.<sup id="cite_ref-WATS_freight_41-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WATS_freight-41">[41]</a></sup><ul><li><b>Caribbean Transport Services</b>: Until 2008, a part of <i>FedEx Freight</i>. Provides airfreight forwarding services between the US mainland, <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rico" title="Puerto Rico">Puerto Rico</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Dominican_Republic" title="Dominican Republic">Dominican Republic</a>, and other <a href="/wiki/Caribbean" title="Caribbean">Caribbean</a> islands.</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/TNT_Express" title="TNT Express">TNT Express</a></b>: An international courier delivery services company, now a subsidiary of FedEx, with its headquarters in Hoofddorp, Netherlands. The firm has fully owned operations in 61 countries, and delivers documents, parcels and pieces of freight to over two hundred countries.</li></ul></li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/FedEx_Ground" title="FedEx Ground">FedEx Ground</a></b> (Orange "Ex", formerly Green): Guaranteed day-definite delivery within Canada and the United States at a <a href="/wiki/Cost" title="Cost">cost</a> savings as compared to time-definite FedEx Express. Uses a large fleet of trucks which are owned by the independent <a href="/wiki/Owner/operator" class="mw-redirect" title="Owner/operator">owner/operators</a> and drivers are <a href="/wiki/Independent_contractors" class="mw-redirect" title="Independent contractors">independent contractors</a> who control individual delivery routes and territories. Formerly <a href="/wiki/Roadway_Services" title="Roadway Services">Roadway Package System</a> (RPS).<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup><ul><li><b>FedEx Home Delivery</b>: Specializes in residential delivery Tuesday through Saturday and offers delivery options to provide more flexibility for residential recipients. The logo includes a drawing of a dog carrying a <a href="/wiki/Mail" title="Mail">package</a> . FedEx Home Delivery only operates in the United States. In the US it is not uncommon for Home Delivery packages to be delivered by standard Ground trucks. To make up the difference, <i>FedEx Ground</i> in Canada performs the business deliveries and residential deliveries.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup></li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/FedEx_SmartPost" class="mw-redirect" title="FedEx SmartPost">FedEx SmartPost</a></b>: Consolidates parcels from merchants such as e-commerce and catalog companies and uses the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Postal_Service" title="United States Postal Service">United States Postal Service</a> for the final delivery. Formerly the independent company Parcel Direct until FedEx acquired it for $120 million in 2004.<sup id="cite_ref-Parcel_Direct_Acquisition_44-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Parcel_Direct_Acquisition-44">[44]</a></sup></li></ul></li>
<li><b>FedEx Freight</b> (Orange "Ex", formerly Red): LTL and other <a href="/wiki/Freight" class="mw-redirect" title="Freight">freight</a> services. The largest LTL carrier in the United States, with $4.5 billion in revenue for 2008.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup>  Formerly <a href="/wiki/American_Freightways" title="American Freightways">American Freightways</a>, Viking Freight, and Watkins Motor Lines.
<ul><li><b>FedEx Freight Canada</b>: Formerly Watkins Canada Express.</li></ul></li>
<li><b>FedEx Logistics</b> (Orange "Ex", formerly Platinum): Known as FedEx Trade Networks until 2019, FedEx Logistics provides supply chain solutions, specialty transportation, cross border e-commerce technology services, customs brokerage, and trade management tools and data.  Formerly C.J. Tower &amp; Sons, then Tower Group International.
<ul><li><b>FedEx Air &amp; Ocean Cargo Networks</b>: International air and ocean freight forwarding.</li>
<li><b>FedEx Cross Border</b>: Provides cross border enablement technologies and solutions that help retailers and e-tailers reach international e-commerce consumers. Its capabilities include duty and tax calculations, export compliance management, HS classification, currency conversions, shopping cart management, and protection against credit card fraud. Formerly Bongo International.</li>
<li><b>FedEx Custom Critical</b> (Orange "Ex", formerly Blue and then Red): Delivers urgent, valuable, or hazardous items using trucks and chartered aircraft. Freight not accepted for transport includes <a href="/wiki/Perishable" class="mw-redirect" title="Perishable">perishable</a> food, alcohol, medication, livestock, household goods, <a href="/wiki/Hazardous_waste" title="Hazardous waste">hazardous waste</a>, and money.<sup id="cite_ref-prohibited_46-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-prohibited-46">[46]</a></sup> Drivers are independent contractors who own their vehicles. Service in Mexico uses <a href="/wiki/Freight_interline_system" title="Freight interline system">interline carriers</a>. Formerly Roberts Cartage and then Roberts Express.</li>
<li><b>FedEx Customs Brokerage</b>: Services related to <a href="/wiki/Customs" title="Customs">customs</a> and international trade compliance.</li>
<li><b>FedEx Forward Depots</b>: Critical inventory and service parts logistics, TechConnect repair and refurbishment of business technology equipment, 3-D printing and the FedEx Packaging Lab.</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/FedEx_Supply_Chain" title="FedEx Supply Chain">FedEx Supply Chain</a></b>: Third-party logistics including transportation management, warehousing, fulfillment, and returns. Formerly GENCO, Roadway Logistics System, and Caliber Logistics.</li></ul></li>
<li><b>FedEx Services</b> (Orange "Ex", formerly Platinum): Provides global marketing, <a href="/wiki/Planning" title="Planning">planning</a>, and information technology (IT) services for the other FedEx operating companies.
<ul><li><b>FedEx Customer Relations</b>: Offering a customer service toll-free telephone line for customer questions. It is operated by an automated operator then will prompt the user to a live agent for uses of tracking, claims, scheduling pick-ups (Express, Ground, Same Day, Custom Critical, Freight Express, and Freight LTL), compliments and complaints, locations (both staffed counter locations and drop-boxes), ordering supplies, setting up FedEx accounts, billing etc. Formerly FCIS or FedEx Customer Information Services.</li>
<li><b>FedEx Delivery Manager</b>: Provides U.S. customers with options to schedule dates, locations, and times of delivery. Customers can also track and manage deliveries en route to or from their home, without a tracking number or FedEx account.</li></ul></li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/FedEx_Office" title="FedEx Office">FedEx Office</a></b> (Orange "Ex", formerly Blue): The retail arm of the corporation, offers copying and digital printing, professional finishing, document creation, Internet access, computer rentals, signs and graphics, direct mail, Web-based printing, and FedEx shipping. Formerly an independent company, known as Kinko's until it was acquired by FedEx in 2004 and rebranded to FedEx Kinko's. In June 2008 the company was finally rebranded as FedEx Office.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup><ul><li><b>FedEx Office Print and Ship Centers</b>: Provides services such as copying, printing, Internet access, and shipping.  They are a central location for FedEx customers to deposit their packages for shipping, offering self-service photocopy and fax machines, office products for packing and shipping, boxes, and packaging services. They also offer "Hold at Locations" for FedEx Ground &amp; FedEx Express shipments for easy pick up. Transfer to Office/Ship centers takes 1 to 2 business days (example: calling the customer service line one day prior to pick up. This ensures package is put with proper route of courier that services that area). FedEx Office counts with its own FedEx <a href="/wiki/Couriers" class="mw-redirect" title="Couriers">Couriers</a> for Center to Center and local customer deliveries. Formerly, these locations were called FedEx World Service Centers.</li>
<li><b>FedEx SameDay City</b> (Orange "Ex", formerly Platinum): Offers a delivery service between select ZIP codes in as little as two hours.  Services include Standard, providing pickup by noon and delivery by the end of the day, or Priority, providing delivery within two hours.  FedEx SameDay City is currently expanding in all major cities across the country and is planning on becoming its own operating unit in the next five years.</li></ul></li></ul><h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="SCAC_codes">SCAC codes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=FedEx&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: SCAC codes" data-section="4" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Standard_Carrier_Alpha_Codes" class="mw-redirect" title="Standard Carrier Alpha Codes">Standard Carrier Alpha Code (SCAC)</a> is a unique code used to identify transportation companies. It is typically two to four alphabetic letters long. It was developed by the <a href="/wiki/National_Motor_Freight_Traffic_Association" title="National Motor Freight Traffic Association">National Motor Freight Traffic Association</a> in the 1960s to help the transportation industry for computerizing data and records. FedEx's codes include:
</p>
<ul><li>FXE – FedEx Express</li>
<li>FXSP – FedEx SmartPost</li>
<li>FXG – FedEx Ground</li>
<li>FXFE – FedEx Freight</li>
<li>FDCC – FedEx Custom Critical</li>
<li>FXO – FedEx Office</li>
<li>FSDC – FedEx Same Day City</li></ul></section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(4)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Political_donations_and_lobbying">Political donations and lobbying</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=FedEx&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Political donations and lobbying" data-section="5" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-4 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-4">
<p>According to the <a href="/wiki/Center_for_Responsive_Politics" title="Center for Responsive Politics">Center for Responsive Politics</a>, FedEx Corp is the 79th largest campaign contributor in the United States, having donated over $29.8 million to federal candidates and committees since 1989, 37% of which went to Democrats and 63% to Republicans.<sup id="cite_ref-osfedex_48-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-osfedex-48">[48]</a></sup> Strong ties to the White House and members of Congress allow access to international trade and tax cut rebates as well as the rules of the business practices of the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Postal_Service" title="United States Postal Service">United States Postal Service</a>. In 2001, FedEx sealed a $9 billion deal with the <a href="/wiki/USPS" class="mw-redirect" title="USPS">USPS</a> to transport all of the post office's overnight and express deliveries.
</p><p>In 2005, FedEx was among 53 entities that contributed the maximum of $250,000 to sponsor the second inauguration of President <a href="/wiki/George_W._Bush" title="George W. Bush">George W. Bush</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup></p><p>During the 2018 calendar year, FedEx spent nearly $10.2 million <a href="/wiki/Lobbying" title="Lobbying">lobbying</a> the <a href="/wiki/Government_of_the_United_States" class="mw-redirect" title="Government of the United States">federal government</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-oslobby1_52-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-oslobby1-52">[52]</a></sup> its lowest total since 2008 but more than any other company in the air transport industry.<sup id="cite_ref-oslobby2_53-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-oslobby2-53">[53]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Tax_avoidance">Tax avoidance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=FedEx&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Tax avoidance" data-section="6" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>In December 2019, <a href="/wiki/CNBC" title="CNBC">CNBC</a> listed FedEx along with 378 additional <a href="/wiki/Fortune_500" title="Fortune 500"><i>Fortune</i> 500</a> companies that "paid an effective federal tax rate of 0% or less" as a result of the <a href="/wiki/Tax_Cuts_and_Jobs_Act_of_2017" title="Tax Cuts and Jobs Act of 2017">Tax Cuts and Jobs Act of 2017</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times" title="The New York Times">The New York Times</a></i> reported that FedEx paid $1.5 billion in taxes after the 2017 fiscal year (effective tax rate of 34%) and then $0 after the 2018 fiscal year (effective tax rate of 0%) as a result of lobbying done by the company.<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup></p>

<ul><li>From 1997 to 2002, FedEx was the title sponsor of <a href="/wiki/Champ_Car_World_Series" title="Champ Car World Series">Champ Car World Series</a> when it was known as <a href="/wiki/Champ_Car" class="mw-redirect" title="Champ Car">CART</a>. The car has been driven by <a href="/wiki/Denny_Hamlin" title="Denny Hamlin">Denny Hamlin</a> since 2006.</li>

<li><a href="/wiki/FedEx_Field" class="mw-redirect" title="FedEx Field">FedEx Field</a>, home of the National Football League's <a href="/wiki/Washington_Redskins" title="Washington Redskins">Washington Redskins</a> in <a href="/wiki/Landover,_Maryland" title="Landover, Maryland">Landover, Maryland</a><sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup></li></ul><h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_sports">Other sports</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=FedEx&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Other sports" data-section="12" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<ul><li>FedEx sponsors <a href="/wiki/FedExForum" title="FedExForum">FedExForum</a>, home of the <a href="/wiki/NBA" class="mw-redirect" title="NBA">NBA</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Memphis_Grizzlies" title="Memphis Grizzlies">Memphis Grizzlies</a> and the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Memphis" title="University of Memphis">University of Memphis</a> men's basketball team.<sup id="cite_ref-61" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup></li>
<li>FedEx sponsors the <a href="/wiki/Heineken_Cup" class="mw-redirect" title="Heineken Cup">Heineken Cup</a> in Rugby.<sup id="cite_ref-62" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup></li>
<li>Beginning in 2007, FedEx became the title sponsor of the <a href="/wiki/FedEx_Cup" title="FedEx Cup">FedEx Cup</a>, a championship trophy for the <a href="/wiki/PGA_Tour" title="PGA Tour">PGA Tour</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-63" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup></li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/St._Jude_Classic" title="St. Jude Classic">St. Jude Classic</a>, a PGA Tour golf tournament held in Memphis, has been sponsored by FedEx from 1986 to 2006, in 2009, and currently since 2011.</li>
