</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(1)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Marsh_%26_McLennan_Companies&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History" data-section="1" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-1 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-1">
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Marsh_%26_McLennan_Companies_logo.svg" class="image"><noscript><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/3/39/Marsh_%26_McLennan_Companies_logo.svg/150px-Marsh_%26_McLennan_Companies_logo.svg.png" decoding="async" width="150" height="122" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="169" data-file-height="137"></noscript><span class="lazy-image-placeholder" style="width: 150px;height: 122px;" data-src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/3/39/Marsh_%26_McLennan_Companies_logo.svg/150px-Marsh_%26_McLennan_Companies_logo.svg.png" data-alt="" data-width="150" data-height="122" data-srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/3/39/Marsh_%26_McLennan_Companies_logo.svg/225px-Marsh_%26_McLennan_Companies_logo.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/3/39/Marsh_%26_McLennan_Companies_logo.svg/300px-Marsh_%26_McLennan_Companies_logo.svg.png 2x" data-class="thumbimage"> </span></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Marsh_%26_McLennan_Companies_logo.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Marsh &amp; McLennan Companies previous logo</div></div></div>
<p>Burroughs, Marsh &amp; McLennan was formed by Henry W. Marsh and <a href="/wiki/Donald_R._McLennan" title="Donald R. McLennan">Donald R. McLennan</a> in <a href="/wiki/Chicago" title="Chicago">Chicago</a> in 1905.  It was renamed as Marsh &amp; McLennan in 1906.
</p><p>The reinsurance firm Guy Carpenter &amp; Company was acquired in 1923, a year after it was founded by Guy Carpenter.<sup id="cite_ref-intl_dir_company_history_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-intl_dir_company_history-8">[8]</a></sup>  In 1959, it acquired the <a href="/wiki/Human_resources" title="Human resources">human resources</a> consulting firm <a href="/wiki/Mercer_(consulting_firm)" title="Mercer (consulting firm)">Mercer</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-founding_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-founding-1">[1]</a></sup></p><p>The 1960s were particularly notable for the company's development, including an <a href="/wiki/Initial_public_offering" title="Initial public offering">initial public offering</a> in 1962 and a 1969 reorganization that introduced a <a href="/wiki/Holding_company" title="Holding company">holding company</a> configuration, with the company offering clients its services under the banners of separately managed companies.<sup id="cite_ref-intl_dir_company_history_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-intl_dir_company_history-8">[8]</a></sup></p><p>In 1970, the company purchased <a href="/wiki/Putnam_Investments" title="Putnam Investments">Putnam Investments</a>, adding a mutual fund business to its portfolio (and sold it in 2007).<sup id="cite_ref-putnam_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-putnam-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Developments_leading_to_current_structure">Developments leading to current structure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Marsh_%26_McLennan_Companies&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Developments leading to current structure" data-section="2" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>In 1997, the company significantly boosted its insurance brokerage business with a $1.8 billion acquisition of <a href="/wiki/Johnson_%26_Higgins" title="Johnson &amp; Higgins">Johnson &amp; Higgins</a>, which, at the time, was one of MMC's biggest competitors in its brokerage business. The purchase occurred during a time of consolidation in the industry, and pushed Marsh &amp; McLennan back above Aon as the world's largest insurance broker.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p><p>Throughout the 2000s, the company further transformed and focused its operating strategy through various acquisitions and <a href="/wiki/Divestment" title="Divestment">divestment</a> at its subsidiaries, including:
</p>
<ul><li>In 2000, Marsh &amp; McLennan’s HR consulting unit, <a href="/wiki/Mercer_(consulting_firm)" title="Mercer (consulting firm)">Mercer</a>, acquired Delta Consulting Group for its organizational development and change management expertise.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></li>
<li>In 2003, the company acquired <a href="/wiki/Oliver_Wyman" title="Oliver Wyman">Oliver Wyman</a>, a management consultancy with a large <a href="/wiki/Financial_services_industry" class="mw-redirect" title="Financial services industry">financial services industry</a> clientele.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> The acquisition served to transform MMC from a company known mostly for its insurance brokerage services into one with a full-fledged management consulting practice that competes with <a href="/wiki/McKinsey_%26_Company" title="McKinsey &amp; Company">McKinsey</a>, <a href="/wiki/Boston_Consulting_Group" title="Boston Consulting Group">Boston Consulting Group</a>, and others.<sup id="cite_ref-founding_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-founding-1">[1]</a></sup></li>
<li>In 2007, Marsh &amp; McLennan sold its <a href="/wiki/Putnam_Investments" title="Putnam Investments">Putnam Investments</a> mutual fund business to <a href="/wiki/Power_financial_corp" class="mw-redirect" title="Power financial corp">Power Financial Corp</a>. for $3.9 billion in a divestment meant to focus the parent company on its risk and human capital businesses.<sup id="cite_ref-putnam_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-putnam-9">[9]</a></sup></li>
<li>Also in 2007, the company announced that its insurance brokerage unit, <a href="/wiki/Marsh_(company)" title="Marsh (company)">Marsh</a>, had received the first license for a wholly owned foreign company to operate an insurance brokerage business in China.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></li>
<li>In 2010, the company sold <a href="/wiki/Kroll_Inc." title="Kroll Inc.">Kroll</a>, its corporate intelligence and investigative unit, to Altegrity Inc. for $1.13 billion. Prior to this final deal and divestiture, Marsh &amp; McLennan had been selling off smaller divisions within Kroll to further focus on its core risk and consulting businesses.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></li>
<li>In September 2018 the company agreed to acquire London-based <a href="/wiki/Jardine_Lloyd_Thompson" title="Jardine Lloyd Thompson">Jardine Lloyd Thompson</a> for £4.3 billion.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> The transaction was completed on 1 April 2019.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></li></ul><p>In July 2017, Marsh &amp; McLennan Cos. Inc. was ranked first in <i>Business Insurance'</i>s world's largest brokers list.<sup id="cite_ref-BI2017_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BI2017-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="2004_bid-rigging_investigation">2004 bid-rigging investigation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Marsh_%26_McLennan_Companies&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: 2004 bid-rigging investigation" data-section="3" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>In 2004, <a href="/wiki/Marsh_(company)" title="Marsh (company)">Marsh</a>, the company's insurance brokerage unit, was embroiled in a <a href="/wiki/Bid_rigging" title="Bid rigging">bid rigging</a> scandal that plagued much of the insurance industry, including brokerage rivals <a href="/wiki/Aon_(company)" title="Aon (company)">Aon</a> and <a href="/wiki/Willis_Group" title="Willis Group">Willis Group</a>, and insurer <a href="/wiki/AIG" class="mw-redirect" title="AIG">AIG</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> In a lawsuit, <a href="/wiki/Eliot_Spitzer" title="Eliot Spitzer">Eliot Spitzer</a>, then <a href="/wiki/New_York_State" class="mw-redirect" title="New York State">New York State</a>’s attorney general, accused Marsh of not serving as an unbiased broker, leading to increased costs for clients and higher revenues for Marsh. In early 2005, Marsh agreed to pay $850 million to settle the lawsuit and compensate clients whose commercial insurance it arranged from 2001 to 2004.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p><p>Much of Marsh &amp; McLennan's corporate strategy since 2005 stemmed from an effort to recover from this tumultuous period, eventually leading to the firm's current organization and simplified focus on insurance services and consulting.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="September_11_attacks">September 11 attacks</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Marsh_%26_McLennan_Companies&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: September 11 attacks" data-section="4" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>At the time of the 2001 <a href="/wiki/September_11_attacks" title="September 11 attacks">September 11 attacks</a> in the United States, the corporation held offices on eight floors, 93 to 100, of the North Tower of the <a href="/wiki/World_Trade_Center_(1973%E2%80%932001)" title="World Trade Center (1973–2001)">World Trade Center</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> When <a href="/wiki/American_Airlines_Flight_11" title="American Airlines Flight 11">American Airlines Flight 11</a> crashed into the building, its offices spanned the entire impact zone, floors 93 to 99.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> Everyone present in the company's offices on the day of the attack died as all stairwells and elevators passing through the impact zone were destroyed or blocked by the crash; the firm lost 295 employees and 66 contractors.<sup id="cite_ref-founding_1-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-founding-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
