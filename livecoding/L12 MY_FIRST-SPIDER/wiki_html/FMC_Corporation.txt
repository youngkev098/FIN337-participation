For other companies, see <a href="/wiki/FMC_(disambiguation)" class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" title="FMC (disambiguation)">FMC</a>.</div>
<p><b>FMC Corporation</b> (Food Machinery Corporation) is an American <a href="/wiki/Chemical_substance" title="Chemical substance">chemical</a> manufacturing company headquartered in <a href="/wiki/Philadelphia,_Pennsylvania" class="mw-redirect" title="Philadelphia, Pennsylvania">Philadelphia, Pennsylvania</a>, which originated as an insecticide producer and later diversified into other industries. The company was founded by chemist John Bean in 1883 as the <b>Bean Spray Pump Company</b> in <a href="/wiki/Los_Gatos,_California" title="Los Gatos, California">Los Gatos, California</a>, producing <a href="/wiki/Piston_pump" title="Piston pump">piston pumps</a> for <a href="/wiki/Insecticide" title="Insecticide">insecticides</a>. In 1928, Bean Spray Pump purchased two companies: the Anderson-Barngrover Co. and Sprague-Sells Co. At this time the company changed its name to <b>Food Machinery Corporation</b>, but in 1948 changed that to <b>Food Machinery &amp; Chemical Corporation</b>. In 1961, the name was changed again to <b>FMC</b>. In 1941 the company received a contract to design and build amphibious <a href="/wiki/Landing_Vehicle_Tracked" title="Landing Vehicle Tracked">tracked landing vehicles</a> for the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_War" title="United States Department of War">United States Department of War</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-amphibiouxous_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-amphibiouxous-3">[3]</a></sup> and afterwards the company continued to diversify its products. Later FMC produced the <a href="/wiki/M113_armored_personnel_carrier" title="M113 armored personnel carrier">M113</a>, one of the most widely used <a href="/wiki/Armoured_fighting_vehicle" title="Armoured fighting vehicle">AFVs</a> ever. FMC employs 7,000 people worldwide, and had gross revenues of <a href="/wiki/United_States_dollar" title="United States dollar">US$</a>2.8 billion in 2017.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a </section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(1)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=FMC_Corporation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History" data-section="1" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-1 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-1">
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a href="/wiki/File:FMCTowers.jpg" class="image"><noscript><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/f/f0/FMCTowers.jpg/250px-FMCTowers.jpg" decoding="async" width="250" height="333" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="2448" data-file-height="3264"></noscript><span class="lazy-image-placeholder" style="width: 250px;height: 333px;" data-src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/f/f0/FMCTowers.jpg/250px-FMCTowers.jpg" data-alt="" data-width="250" data-height="333" data-class="thumbimage"> </span></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:FMCTowers.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>FMC Tower headquarters in Philadelphia</div></div></div>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Bean_Spray_Pump_Company">The Bean Spray Pump Company</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=FMC_Corporation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: The Bean Spray Pump Company" data-section="2" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>Founded in 1883 as the Bean Spray Pump Company in <a href="/wiki/Los_Gatos,_California" title="Los Gatos, California">Los Gatos, California</a><sup id="cite_ref-lostgatos_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-lostgatos-7">[7]</a></sup> by chemist John Bean. The company's first product was a <a href="/wiki/Piston_pump" title="Piston pump">piston pump</a>. Bean invented the pump to spray <a href="/wiki/Insecticide" title="Insecticide">insecticide</a> on the many fruit orchards in the area. A Bean sprayer was on display at the <a href="/wiki/Forbes_Mill" title="Forbes Mill">Forbes Mill</a> museum in Los Gatos until its closure in 2014.<sup id="cite_ref-spray_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-spray-8">[8]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-newmuseum_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-newmuseum-9">[9]</a></sup> Bean Avenue in downtown Los Gatos is named after John Bean.
</p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="FMC">FMC</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=FMC_Corporation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: FMC" data-section="3" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>In 1928, Bean Spray Pump purchased two companies: the Anderson-Barngrover Co. and Sprague-Sells Co. The Anderson-Barngrover Co. manufactured a sealed can rotary pressure sterilizer<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> and the Sprague-Sells Co. manufactured canning machinery. At this time the company changed its name to <b>Food Machinery Corporation</b>, and began using the initials <b>FMC</b>.  In 1948, the company name changed again, to <b>Food Machinery &amp; Chemical Corporation</b>. In 1961, Food Machinery &amp; Chemical Corporation was changed to simply <b>FMC</b>.
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:262px;"><a href="/wiki/File:LVT-1_3.jpg" class="image"><noscript><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/32/LVT-1_3.jpg/260px-LVT-1_3.jpg" decoding="async" width="260" height="208" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="576" data-file-height="460"></noscript><span class="lazy-image-placeholder" style="width: 260px;height: 208px;" data-src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/32/LVT-1_3.jpg/260px-LVT-1_3.jpg" data-alt="" data-width="260" data-height="208" data-class="thumbimage"> </span></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:LVT-1_3.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>LVT-1 exhibited by manufacturer (FMC) in 1941 parade, <a href="/wiki/Lakeland,_Florida" title="Lakeland, Florida">Lakeland, Florida</a></div></div></div>
<p>FMC was awarded a contract to design and build amphibious <a href="/wiki/Landing_Vehicle_Tracked" title="Landing Vehicle Tracked">tracked landing vehicles</a> for the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_War" title="United States Department of War">United States Department of War</a> in 1941. FMC ranked 64th among United States corporations in the value of <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a> military production contracts.<sup id="cite_ref-amphibiouxous_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-amphibiouxous-3">[3]</a></sup></p><p>In 1961, the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Navy" title="United States Navy">United States Navy</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Bureau_of_Ships" title="Bureau of Ships">Bureau of Ships</a> issued bids for a high performance amphibious ship-to-shore cargo carrier capable of moving over water at 35 knots (approx. 40 mph) and over ground at the same speed. It had to carry five tons of cargo across water, through the surf, across the beach, and inland. The vehicle also had to be quickly loaded and unloaded under combat conditions. FMC's Ordnance Division in San José, California built and tested two prototypes named "LVHX2 Landing Vehicle, Hydrofoil" for the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Marine_Corps" title="United States Marine Corps">United States Marine Corps</a>. These were the first amphibious landing vehicles to make use of <a href="/wiki/Hydrofoil" title="Hydrofoil">hydrofoils</a> for high speed ship-to-shore operation. Although the LVHX2 never went into production, the Marine Corps used the prototypes in their continuous research and development program to develop better equipment for amphibious assault operations.
</p><p>FMC later built the <a href="/wiki/M113_armored_personnel_carrier" title="M113 armored personnel carrier">M113</a> Armored Personnel Carrier (APC), the <a href="/wiki/M2_Bradley" title="M2 Bradley">Bradley Fighting Vehicle</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/FMC_XR311" title="FMC XR311">XR311</a> at its former facility in <a href="/wiki/Santa_Clara,_California" title="Santa Clara, California">Santa Clara, California</a>. It also purchased the rights to manufacture some foreign military hardware, including the <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">Brazilian</a> <a href="/wiki/EE-9_Cascavel" title="EE-9 Cascavel">EE-9 Cascavel</a>, under license.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Bean also manufactured firefighting equipment in the 1960s through the 1980s under the FMC and the Bean names.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:T113_APC.jpg" class="image"><noscript><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e5/T113_APC.jpg/220px-T113_APC.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="178" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="1052" data-file-height="852"></noscript><span class="lazy-image-placeholder" style="width: 220px;height: 178px;" data-src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e5/T113_APC.jpg/220px-T113_APC.jpg" data-alt="" data-width="220" data-height="178" data-class="thumbimage"> </span></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:T113_APC.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>FMC T113 proposal</div></div></div>
<p>In 1972, personnel were transferred from ordnance to designing and building <a href="/wiki/Recreational_vehicle" title="Recreational vehicle">recreational vehicles</a> (motorcoaches). The <a href="/wiki/1973_oil_crisis" title="1973 oil crisis">oil crisis</a> and high prices led production to end after five years.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p><p>FMC also produced <a href="/wiki/Fire_engine" title="Fire engine">fire truck</a> fire pumps and pumper bodies. It had an <a href="/wiki/Original_equipment_manufacturer" title="Original equipment manufacturer">original equipment manufacturer</a> (OEM) arrangement with <a href="/wiki/American_LaFrance" title="American LaFrance">Ladder Towers Inc.</a> (LTI) to market aerial ladders. In the early 1980s the <a href="/wiki/Firefighting_apparatus" title="Firefighting apparatus">firefighting apparatus</a> division of FMC tried to expand its role in <a href="/wiki/Firefighting_apparatus#Aerial_apparatus" title="Firefighting apparatus">aerial ladders</a> on fire trucks, leveraging the <a href="/wiki/Link-Belt_Cranes" title="Link-Belt Cranes">Link-Belt crane division</a>. FMC's expansion into production of aerial ladders failed: the FMC Fire Apparatus division was shut down in 1990.
</p><p>FMC sells chemical products used by beef and poultry <a href="/wiki/Meat_packing_industry" title="Meat packing industry">processors</a> to reduce pathogens, such as <i><a href="/wiki/Escherichia_coli" title="Escherichia coli">E. coli</a></i> and <a href="/wiki/Salmonella" title="Salmonella">salmonella</a>, on uncooked beef and poultry.<sup id="cite_ref-cafc.uscourts.gov_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cafc.uscourts.gov-13">[13]</a></sup> FMC obtained a patent on a method for sanitizing fowl that have been killed, plucked and eviscerated by contacting the fowl with an aqueous acid solution and maintaining that contact for a time sufficient to sanitize the fowl.<sup id="cite_ref-cafc.uscourts.gov_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cafc.uscourts.gov-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Spinoffs">Spinoffs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=FMC_Corporation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Spinoffs" data-section="4" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a href="/wiki/File:THE_FMC_CORPORATION%27S_SOUTH_CHARLESTON_PLANT_-_NARA_-_550957.tif" class="image"><noscript><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/66/THE_FMC_CORPORATION%27S_SOUTH_CHARLESTON_PLANT_-_NARA_-_550957.tif/lossy-page1-200px-THE_FMC_CORPORATION%27S_SOUTH_CHARLESTON_PLANT_-_NARA_-_550957.tif.jpg" decoding="async" width="200" height="297" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="2018" data-file-height="3000"></noscript><span class="lazy-image-placeholder" style="width: 200px;height: 297px;" data-src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/66/THE_FMC_CORPORATION%27S_SOUTH_CHARLESTON_PLANT_-_NARA_-_550957.tif/lossy-page1-200px-THE_FMC_CORPORATION%27S_SOUTH_CHARLESTON_PLANT_-_NARA_-_550957.tif.jpg" data-alt="" data-width="200" data-height="297" data-class="thumbimage"> </span></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:THE_FMC_CORPORATION%27S_SOUTH_CHARLESTON_PLANT_-_NARA_-_550957.tif" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>FMC plant in <a href="/wiki/South_Charleston,_West_Virginia" title="South Charleston, West Virginia">South Charleston, West Virginia</a>, 1970</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:262px;"><a href="/wiki/File:FMC_210_Skidder.jpg" class="image"><noscript><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2c/FMC_210_Skidder.jpg/260px-FMC_210_Skidder.jpg" decoding="async" width="260" height="195" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="1600" data-file-height="1200"></noscript><span class="lazy-image-placeholder" style="width: 260px;height: 195px;" data-src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2c/FMC_210_Skidder.jpg/260px-FMC_210_Skidder.jpg" data-alt="" data-width="260" data-height="195" data-class="thumbimage"> </span></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:FMC_210_Skidder.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>FMC 210 Skidder made by FMC</div></div></div>
<p>In 1946, FMC bought Bolens Lawn and Garden Equipment. FMC changed its name again in 1948, becoming <b>F</b>ood <b>M</b>achinery and <b>C</b>hemical Corporation. In 1961 the name was changed to FMC Corporation.
</p><p>In 1967, the FMC Corporation merged with the Link-Belt Company. The company produced FMC Link-Belt branded cranes and excavators. In 1986, the <a href="/wiki/Link-Belt_Cranes" title="Link-Belt Cranes">Link-Belt Construction Equipment Company</a> was formed as a joint venture between FMC Corporation and <a href="/wiki/Sumitomo_Heavy_Industries" title="Sumitomo Heavy Industries">Sumitomo Heavy Industries</a>.
</p><p>Between 1965 and 1985 FMC was the owner of the Gunderson metal works in Springfield, Oregon, during that period it was known as the "Marine and Rail Equipment Division of FMC" (MRED), it was sold in 1985 to <a href="/wiki/The_Greenbrier_Companies" title="The Greenbrier Companies">The Greenbrier Companies</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p><p>In the 1980s, 1990s, and 2000s, FMC Corporation began spinning several of its divisions into separate companies, including <a href="/wiki/United_Defense" title="United Defense">United Defense</a> and <a href="/wiki/FMC_Technologies" title="FMC Technologies">FMC Technologies</a>, and selling its divisions, including its automotive division to Snap-on Equipment, a division of <a href="/wiki/Snap-on" title="Snap-on">Snap-on</a>, in 1996. Snap-on renamed the division the "John Bean Company".<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Bolens was sold to Troy-Bilt in 1988.
</p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="21st_century">21st century</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=FMC_Corporation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: 21st century" data-section="5" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:FMC_Anchorage.jpg" class="image"><noscript><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/91/FMC_Anchorage.jpg/220px-FMC_Anchorage.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="3264" data-file-height="2448"></noscript><span class="lazy-image-placeholder" style="width: 220px;height: 165px;" data-src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/91/FMC_Anchorage.jpg/220px-FMC_Anchorage.jpg" data-alt="" data-width="220" data-height="165" data-class="thumbimage"> </span></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:FMC_Anchorage.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>FMC in <a href="/wiki/Anchorage,_Alaska" title="Anchorage, Alaska">Anchorage, Alaska</a></div></div></div>
<ul><li>In 2001, FMC spun off its energy, airport, and food equipment businesses into a separate company named <a href="/wiki/FMC_Technologies" title="FMC Technologies">FMC Technologies</a>.</li>
<li>In 2006 FMC Corporation celebrated 75 years being listed on the <a href="/wiki/New_York_Stock_Exchange" title="New York Stock Exchange">New York Stock Exchange</a>.</li>
<li>Pierre Brondeau was named President and Chief Executive Officer succeeding William G. Walter, effective January 1, 2010. Brondeau had been with Dow Chemical and prior to that Rohm &amp; Haas.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></li>
<li>A former FMC site in <a href="/wiki/San_Jose,_California" title="San Jose, California">San Jose, California</a> is the location for <a href="/wiki/Avaya_Stadium" class="mw-redirect" title="Avaya Stadium">Avaya Stadium</a>, a new soccer stadium for the <a href="/wiki/San_Jose_Earthquakes" title="San Jose Earthquakes">San Jose Earthquakes</a>.</li>
<li>In 2015, FMC completed the sale of its Alkali Chemicals business and acquisition of <a href="/wiki/Cheminova" title="Cheminova">Cheminova</a>, a multinational crop protection company, which aligns with the company's corporate strategy to focus its portfolio on agriculture, health and nutrition end markets, and lithium technologies.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></li></ul><h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="FMC_Technologies">FMC Technologies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=FMC_Corporation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: FMC Technologies" data-section="6" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/FMC_Technologies" title="FMC Technologies">FMC Technologies</a> was an American company that produced equipment for exploration and production of hydrocarbons. FMC Technologies was incorporated in 2000 when FMC Corporation divested its machinery businesses. It exists today as a part of TechnipFMC, after a merge with Technip in 2017.
</p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="JBT_Corporation">JBT Corporation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=FMC_Corporation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: JBT Corporation" data-section="7" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/JBT_Corporation" title="JBT Corporation">JBT Corporation</a> is an American food processing machinery and airport equipment company. JBT Corporation was incorporated in 2008 when FMC Technologies divested its non-energy businesses.
</p>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(2)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Controversies">Controversies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=FMC_Corporation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Controversies" data-section="8" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-2 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-2">
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Superfund_sites">Superfund sites</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=FMC_Corporation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Superfund sites" data-section="9" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<h4 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Idaho">Idaho</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=FMC_Corporation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Idaho" data-section="10" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h4>
<p>FMC Corporation operated a phosphate mine and plant in Idaho on the <a href="/wiki/Fort_Hall_Indian_Reservation" title="Fort Hall Indian Reservation">Fort Hall Indian Reservation</a> of the federally recognized Shoshone-Bannock Tribes. Between 1949 and 2001, it produced an estimated 250 million pounds of elemental phosphorus annually. In 1989, as part of what is termed the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Eastern_Michaud_Flats_Contamination&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Eastern Michaud Flats Contamination (page does not exist)">Eastern Michaud Flats Contamination</a>, its 1500-acre plant site was designated by EPA under the Comprehensive Environmental Response, Compensation, and Liability Act of 1980 (CERCLA) as a <a href="/wiki/Superfund" title="Superfund">Superfund</a> site because of extensive water and land pollution caused by these operations. After working to improve operations and air quality, the company abandoned the plant and related mine in 2001 because of much higher electricity costs and the challenge of cheaper phosphate coming from China. Seventeen mines in the area have been designated as Superfund sites because of <a href="/wiki/Selenium" title="Selenium">selenium</a> poisoning.<sup id="cite_ref-fmc_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fmc-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<h4 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Minnesota">Minnesota</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=FMC_Corporation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Minnesota" data-section="11" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h4>
<p>FMC Corporation operated a <a href="/wiki/FMC_Corporation_(Fridley_Plant)" title="FMC Corporation (Fridley Plant)">plant in Fridley Minnesota</a> several hundred feet east of the Mississippi river from the 1940s until 1969 where it disposed of waste such as solvents, paint sludge, and plating wastes in an on-site dump. In the early 1980s, contamination from the on-site dump contaminated the Mississippi River, and nearby drinking water for the city of Minneapolis.
</p>
<h4 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Washington_State">Washington State</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=FMC_Corporation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Washington State" data-section="12" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h4>
<p>FMC Corporation operated a pesticide formulation plant from 1951 until 1986 in <a href="/wiki/Yakima,_Washington" title="Yakima, Washington">Yakima, Washington</a>. From 1952 to 1969, FMC disposed of agricultural pesticides in a pit on site. In 1983 the site was listed in the EPA Superfund list, after groundwater and soil was found to be contaminated with pesticides. After an EPA order, FMC was forced to dispose of 850 tons of contaminated soil between 1988 and 1989. In 1990, EPA required FMC to incinerate soils on-site.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<h4 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="New_York_State">New York State</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=FMC_Corporation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: New York State" data-section="13" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h4>
<p>At its Dublin Road Site, located in north-western New York in Orleans County, FMC Corporation from 1933 to 1968 disposed of coal ash cinders, laboratory wastes consisting of glass bottles and chemical residues, residues from lime sulfur filtration, building debris and residues from pesticide production areas. These materials contained metals in the form of salts and pesticides/insecticides.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="African_lion_program">African lion program</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=FMC_Corporation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: African lion program" data-section="14" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>In 2009, CBS <i><a href="/wiki/60_Minutes" title="60 Minutes">60 Minutes</a></i> ran an exposé on the use by farmers in <a href="/wiki/Kenya" title="Kenya">Kenya</a> of an FMC–produced pesticide, <a href="/wiki/Carbofuran" title="Carbofuran">Furadan</a>, as a poison to kill African lions. The piece suggested that Furadan was a serious threat to the future of the lion population in Africa.<sup id="cite_ref-cbcs_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cbcs-22">[22]</a></sup> FMC has commented extensively on this issue through the media and their websites, including furadanfacts.com.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> They engaged with government officials, <a href="/wiki/Non-governmental_organization" title="Non-governmental organization">non-governmental organizations</a>, and others to try and resolve the illegal use of their pesticides to kill wildlife. The company took action to stop the sale of this product and instituted a buy-back program in East Africa when it determined that the illegal and intentional misuse of chemicals against wildlife could not be controlled by education or stewardship programs alone.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
