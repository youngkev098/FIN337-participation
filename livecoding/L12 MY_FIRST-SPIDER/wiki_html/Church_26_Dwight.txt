</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(1)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Church_%26_Dwight&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History" data-section="1" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-1 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-1">
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Pepsodent-0179c.jpg" class="image"><noscript><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/8/88/Pepsodent-0179c.jpg/220px-Pepsodent-0179c.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="110" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="447" data-file-height="223"></noscript><span class="lazy-image-placeholder" style="width: 220px;height: 110px;" data-src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/8/88/Pepsodent-0179c.jpg/220px-Pepsodent-0179c.jpg" data-alt="" data-width="220" data-height="110" data-class="thumbimage"> </span></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Pepsodent-0179c.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Pepsodent" title="Pepsodent">Pepsodent</a> toothpaste</div></div></div>
<p>The company was founded in 1846 to unify two companies created by <a href="/wiki/John_Dwight_(manufacturer)" title="John Dwight (manufacturer)">John Dwight</a> of <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts" title="Massachusetts">Massachusetts</a> and his brother-in-law, <a href="/wiki/Austin_Church" title="Austin Church">Austin Church</a> of <a href="/wiki/Connecticut" title="Connecticut">Connecticut</a>. Their partnership had begun in 1846 with the two founders selling <a href="/wiki/Sodium_bicarbonate" title="Sodium bicarbonate">sodium bicarbonate</a> (also known as baking soda) that they refined in Dwight's kitchen.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p><p>The Arm &amp; Hammer logo, which dates back to the 1860s,<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> is often incorrectly claimed to have originated with tycoon <a href="/wiki/Armand_Hammer" title="Armand Hammer">Armand Hammer</a>. Hammer was so often asked about the Church &amp; Dwight brand, however, that he attempted to buy the company. While unsuccessful, Hammer's <a href="/wiki/Occidental_Petroleum" title="Occidental Petroleum">Occidental Petroleum</a> in 1986 acquired enough stock for him to join the Church &amp; Dwight board of directors.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p><p>In 1970, the Arm &amp; Hammer brand introduced the market’s first nationally-distributed, phosphate-free detergent: Arm &amp; Hammer Powder Laundry Detergent. Recent findings had shown the harmful effects phosphates could have on the health of lakes, streams and other freshwater bodies. The brand rushed to provide a solution and, in less than a year, was able to bring the product from concept to market. That same year in April, Arm &amp; Hammer was honored to be the sole corporate sponsor of the first annual Earth Day.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p><p>In 2001 the <a href="/wiki/Consumer_product" class="mw-redirect" title="Consumer product">consumer product</a> line of <a href="/wiki/Carter-Wallace" title="Carter-Wallace">Carter-Wallace</a> was sold to Church &amp; Dwight, and <a href="/wiki/MedPointe" class="mw-redirect" title="MedPointe">MedPointe</a> bought the diagnostics and drug businesses.<sup id="cite_ref-sold_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sold-8">[8]</a></sup> The same year, Church &amp; Dwight expanded its business into laundry detergent products by acquiring USA Detergents, the owner of Xtra detergent.
</p><p>In 2006, Church &amp; Dwight expanded its household brand portfolio with the acquisition of <a href="/wiki/Orange_Glo_International" class="mw-redirect" title="Orange Glo International">Orange Glo International</a>, which included such brands as <a href="/wiki/OxiClean" title="OxiClean">OxiClean</a>, a premium-priced leader in the fast-growing laundry pre-wash additive category, Kaboom bathroom cleaners, and Orange Glo household cleaning products. The acquisition provided not only new product categories, but also a different way for Church &amp; Dwight brands to market themselves.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Church &amp; Dwight was ranked 723 in the <a href="/wiki/Fortune_500" title="Fortune 500">Fortune 500</a> listing of companies in 2010.<sup id="cite_ref-rankings_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rankings-10">[10]</a></sup></p><p>In 2008, Church &amp; Dwight acquired the <a href="/wiki/Orajel" class="mw-redirect" title="Orajel">Orajel</a> business from Del Labs, which includes the #1 pain relievers for toothache, mouth sore and teething as well as the #1 brand of non-fluoride toothpaste. Since the acquisition, Orajel has expanded its portfolio with the launch of Single Dose Cold Sore Treatment (recently renamed Touch-Free) and offers a range of kids oral care products featuring best-in-class licensing relationships for characters from Nickelodeon, Sesame, Hasbro and Marvel.
</p><p>In 2010, Church &amp; Dwight acquired two small businesses: Simply Saline, and Feline Pine. Both of these acquisitions leveraged current consumer trends toward use of natural products and positively complemented the company's existing nasal hygiene and cat litter businesses.
</p><p>In 2016, Church &amp; Dwight acquired Spencer Forrest, Inc., the maker of Toppik hair care products.
</p><p>In 2017, <a href="/wiki/MidOcean_Partners" title="MidOcean Partners">MidOcean Partners</a> agreed to sell <a href="/wiki/Water_Pik,_Inc." class="mw-redirect" title="Water Pik, Inc.">Waterpik</a> for $1 billion to Church &amp; Dwight. At the time of the sale announcement, it was reported that Water Pik, Inc. had "$265 million of revenue in the fiscal year ended June 30, about 70% of which came from its water flosser products".<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
