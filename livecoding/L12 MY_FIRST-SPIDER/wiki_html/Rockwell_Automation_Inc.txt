</p><p>In 1904, 19-year-old <a href="/wiki/Harry_Lynde_Bradley" title="Harry Lynde Bradley">Harry Bradley</a> joined his brother in the business.
</p><p>The company's first patented product was a carbon disc compression-type motor controller for industrial cranes.  The crane controller was demonstrated at the St. Louis World's Fair in 1904.
</p><p>In 1909, the company was renamed the Allen-Bradley Company.
</p><p>Allen-Bradley expanded rapidly during <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a> in response to government-contracted work. Its product line grew to include automatic starters and switches, circuit breakers, relays and other electric equipment.
</p><p>In 1914, Fred Loock established the company's first sales office in New York.
</p><p>Upon co-founder Stanton Allen's death in 1916, Lynde Bradley became president. Harry Bradley was appointed vice president and attorney Louis Quarles was named corporate secretary.
</p><p>In 1918 Allen-Bradley hired its first female factory worker, Julia Bizewski Polczynski, who was promoted to foreman the following year.
</p><p>During the 1920s, the company grew its miniature rheostat business to support the burgeoning radio industry. By the middle of this decade, nearly 50 percent of the company's sales were attributed to the radio department. The decade closed with record company sales of $3 million.
</p><p>By 1932, the <a href="/wiki/Great_Depression" title="Great Depression">Great Depression</a> had taken its toll and the company posted record losses. Amid growing economic pressure, Allen-Bradley reduced its workforce from 800 to 550 and cut wages by 50 percent. To lessen the financial burden, Lynde and Harry Bradley implemented a unique program:  the company replaced employees’ lost wages with preferred stock. Eventually, the company bought back all stock at six percent interest.
</p><p>Throughout this period, Lynde Bradley supported an aggressive research and development approach intended to "develop the company out of the Depression." Lynde Bradley's R&amp;D strategy was successful. By 1937, Allen-Bradley employment had rebounded to pre-Depression levels and company sales reached an all-time high of nearly $4 million.
</p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Mid-20th_century">Mid-20th century</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rockwell_Automation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Mid-20th century" data-section="3" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>

The Lynde Bradley Foundation, a charitable trust, was established with Lynde Bradley's assets. The foundation's first gift of $12,500 was made to Milwaukee's Community Fund, predecessor of the <a href="/wiki/United_Way" class="mw-redirect" title="United Way">United Way</a>.
</p><p>World War II fueled unprecedented levels of production, with 80 percent of the company's orders being war-related. Wartime orders were centered on two broad lines of products – industrial controls to speed production and electrical components or "radio parts" used in a wide range of military equipment.
</p><p>Allen-Bradley expanded its facilities numerous times during the 1940s to meet war-time production needs. With Fred Loock serving as president and Harry Bradley as chairman, the company began a major $1 million, two-year expansion project in 1947. The company completed additional expansions at its Milwaukee facilities in the 1950s and 1960s, including the Allen-Bradley clock tower. The clock tower has since been renamed, and is known today as the <a href="/wiki/Allen-Bradley" title="Allen-Bradley">Rockwell Automation clock tower</a>.
</p><p>Harry Bradley died in 1965. Fred Loock retired in 1967 and died in 1973.
</p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Late_20th_century">Late 20th century</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rockwell_Automation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Late 20th century" data-section="4" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>During the 1970s, the company expanded its production facilities and markets and entered the 1980s as a global company. With president J. Tracy O'Rourke (1981–89) at the helm, the company introduced a new line of programmable logic controllers, the PLC in 1981 followed by the PLC-2 Family(1982)(2/30, 2/05/ 2/16&amp;2/17) PLC-3(1982) SLC-100 Family(1986) SLC-500(1986) PLC-5 Family (1985). Earlier PLC developments were the MAC, PLC-4.
</p><p>In 1985 privately owned <a href="/wiki/Allen-Bradley" title="Allen-Bradley">Allen-Bradley</a> set a new fiscal record with sales of $1 billion. On February 20, 1985 <a href="/wiki/Rockwell_International" title="Rockwell International">Rockwell International</a> purchased Allen-Bradley for $1.651 billion; this was the largest acquisition in Wisconsin's history to date.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> For all intents and purposes, Allen-Bradley took over Rockwell's industrial automation division.
</p><p>The 1990s featured continued technology development, including the company's launch of its software business, Rockwell Software (1994), the Logix control platform (1997) and the Integrated Architecture system (1999). Rockwell International developed PowerFlex, a manufacturing software and technology in the 1990s.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p><p>During this decade, Rockwell International also acquired a power systems business, composed of Reliance Electric and Dodge. These two brands, combined with control systems brands Allen-Bradley and Rockwell Software, were marketed as Rockwell Automation.
</p><p>In 1998, <a href="/wiki/Keith_Nosbusch" title="Keith Nosbusch">Keith Nosbusch</a> was named president of Rockwell Automation Control Systems. Rockwell International Corporation headquarters was moved to Milwaukee, Wisconsin the same year.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="21st_century">21st century</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rockwell_Automation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: 21st century" data-section="5" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>In 2001, Rockwell International split into two companies.  The industrial automation division became Rockwell Automation, while the <a href="/wiki/Avionics" title="Avionics">avionics</a> division became <a href="/wiki/Rockwell_Collins" title="Rockwell Collins">Rockwell Collins</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> The split was structured so that Rockwell Automation was the legal successor of Rockwell International, while Rockwell Collins was the spin-off. Rockwell Automation retains Rockwell International's stock price history, and continues to trade on the New York Stock Exchange under the symbol "ROK".
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Keith_Nosbusch" title="Keith Nosbusch">Keith Nosbusch</a> was named chief executive officer in 2004.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p><p>In 2007, Rockwell Automation sold the Power Systems division for $1.8 billion to <a href="/wiki/Baldor_Electric_Company" class="mw-redirect" title="Baldor Electric Company">Baldor Electric Company</a> <sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> to focus on its core competencies in automation and information technology.
</p><p>In April 2016, it was announced that Keith Nosbusch would be replaced by Blake Moret, effective July 1, 2016. Nosbusch would remain with Rockwell Automation as chairman.<sup id="cite_ref-WSJ_CEO_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WSJ_CEO-10">[10]</a></sup> Moret was previously the senior vice president of the Control Products and Solutions segment of the company.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p><p>Effective January 1, 2018, Keith Nosbusch will step down as Chairman. Blake Moret was elected the incoming Chairman by the board of directors.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p><p>On June 11, 2018, Rockwell Automation made a $1bn equity investment in <a href="/wiki/PTC_(software_company)" title="PTC (software company)">PTC</a> acquiring an 8.4% ownership stake.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p><p>On January 8, 2020, Rockwell Automation announced the acquisition of Israeli-based cybersecurity provider Avnet Data Security, LTD.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>

<p>Rockwell Automation operates its business through two segments – Architecture and Software, and Control Products and Solutions.
</p>
<ul><li>The Architecture and Software segment contains key elements of the Rockwell Automation control and information platforms, software applications and automation components.</li>
<li>Control Products and Solutions consists of motor control products and services.</li></ul><h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Products">Products</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rockwell_Automation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Products" data-section="8" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>Rockwell Automation covers control systems, industrial control components, information software, motor control devices, sensing devices, network technology, safety technology, and industrial security.
</p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Services">Services</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rockwell_Automation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Services" data-section="9" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>Rockwell Automation has engineered systems that range from custom-designed, bundled components to large, turnkey system integration projects. Services include repair, asset management consulting and remote support centers and training.
</p>
