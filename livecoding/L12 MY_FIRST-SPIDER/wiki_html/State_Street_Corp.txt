<p><b>State Street Corporation</b> is an American<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Financial_services" title="Financial services">financial services</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bank_holding_company" title="Bank holding company">bank holding company</a> headquartered at <a href="/wiki/One_Lincoln_Street" title="One Lincoln Street">One Lincoln Street</a> in <a href="/wiki/Boston" title="Boston">Boston</a> with operations worldwide. It is the <a href="/wiki/List_of_oldest_banks_in_continuous_operation" title="List of oldest banks in continuous operation">second-oldest</a> continually operating United States bank; its predecessor, Union Bank, was founded in 1792. State Street is ranked 15th on the <a href="/wiki/List_of_largest_banks_in_the_United_States" title="List of largest banks in the United States">list of largest banks in the United States</a> by assets. It is one of the largest asset management companies in the world with US$2.511 trillion under management and US$31.62 trillion under custody and administration. <p>The company is ranked 247th on the <a href="/wiki/Fortune_500" title="Fortune 500">Fortune 500</a> as of 2019.<sup id="cite_ref-Fortune500_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fortune500-1">[1]</a></sup> The company is on the list of the banks that are <a href="/wiki/Too_big_to_fail" title="Too big to fail">too big to fail</a> published by the <a href="/wiki/Financial_Stability_Board" title="Financial Stability Board">Financial Stability Board</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p><p>The company is named after <a href="/wiki/State_Street_(Boston)" title="State Street (Boston)">State Street</a> in Boston, which was known as the "Great Street to the Sea" in the 18th century as Boston became a flourishing maritime capital. The company's logo includes a <a href="/wiki/Clipper" title="Clipper">clipper</a> to reflect the maritime industry in Boston during this time.
</p>

</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(1)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Current_operations">Current operations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=State_Street_Corporation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Current operations" data-section="1" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-1 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-1">
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Investment_servicing:_State_Street_Global_Services">Investment servicing: State Street Global Services</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=State_Street_Corporation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Investment servicing: State Street Global Services" data-section="2" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/State_Street_Bank_and_Trust_Company" title="State Street Bank and Trust Company">State Street Bank and Trust Company</a>, also known as Global Services, is the investment servicing division of State Street. It provides asset owners and managers with <a href="/wiki/Custodian_bank" title="Custodian bank">custodian bank</a> services (safekeeping, corporate actions), fund accounting (pricing and valuation), and administration (financial reporting, tax, compliance, and legal) services. Global Services handles assets from many classes, including <a href="/wiki/Stock" title="Stock">stocks</a>, <a href="/wiki/Derivative_(finance)" title="Derivative (finance)">derivatives</a>, <a href="/wiki/Exchange-traded_fund" title="Exchange-traded fund">exchange-traded funds</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fixed_income" title="Fixed income">fixed income</a> assets, <a href="/wiki/Private_equity" title="Private equity">private equity</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Real_estate" title="Real estate">real estate</a>. State Street administers 40% of the assets under administration in the US <a href="/wiki/Mutual_fund" title="Mutual fund">mutual fund</a> market. Global Services also provides outsourcing for operations activities and handles US$10.2 trillion of middle-office assets.<sup id="cite_ref-10K_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10K-2">[2]</a></sup><a href="/wiki/State_Street_Global_Advisors" title="State Street Global Advisors">State Street Global Advisors</a> dates back to 1978. It provides <a href="/wiki/Asset_management" title="Asset management">asset management</a>, <a href="/wiki/Investment_management" title="Investment management">investment management</a>, research, and advisory services to <a href="/wiki/Corporation" title="Corporation">corporations</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mutual_fund" title="Mutual fund">mutual funds</a>, <a href="/wiki/Insurance" title="Insurance">insurance</a> companies, and other <a href="/wiki/Institutional_investor" title="Institutional investor">institutional investors</a>. Global Advisors develops both <a href="/wiki/Passive_management" title="Passive management">passive management</a> and <a href="/wiki/Active_management" title="Active management">active management</a> strategies using both quantitative and fundamental approaches.<sup id="cite_ref-10K_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10K-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h4 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="1993_SPDR_innovation">1993 SPDR innovation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=State_Street_Corporation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: 1993 SPDR innovation" data-section="3" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h4>
<p>In 1993,<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> the company created the <a href="/wiki/SPDR_S%26P_500_Trust_ETF" title="SPDR S&amp;P 500 Trust ETF">SPDR S&amp;P 500 Trust ETF</a>, the first <a href="/wiki/Exchange-traded_fund" title="Exchange-traded fund">exchange-traded fund</a> (ETF), and is now one of the largest ETF providers worldwide.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p><p>Trading on SPDR began January 29, 1993.
</p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="State_Street_Global_Markets">State Street Global Markets</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=State_Street_Corporation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: State Street Global Markets" data-section="4" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>Global Markets is State Street's securities business. It offers research, <a href="/wiki/Trade_(financial_instrument)" title="Trade (financial instrument)">trading</a>, and securities lending services for <a href="/wiki/Foreign_exchange_market" title="Foreign exchange market">foreign exchange</a>, <a href="/wiki/Stock" title="Stock">stocks</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fixed_income" title="Fixed income">fixed income</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Derivative_(finance)" title="Derivative (finance)">derivatives</a>. To avoid a <a href="/wiki/Conflict_of_interest" title="Conflict of interest">conflict of interest</a>, the company does not run proprietary trading books. Global Markets maintains trading desks in <a href="/wiki/Boston" title="Boston">Boston</a>, <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">London</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sydney" title="Sydney">Sydney</a>, <a href="/wiki/Toronto" title="Toronto">Toronto</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Tokyo" title="Tokyo">Tokyo</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-10K_2-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10K-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(2)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=State_Street_Corporation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: History" data-section="5" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-2 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-2">
<p>The company traces its roots to Union Bank, which received a charter in 1792 from <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts" title="Massachusetts">Massachusetts</a> Governor <a href="/wiki/John_Hancock" title="John Hancock">John Hancock</a>. It was the third bank to be chartered in Boston and its office was at the corner of State and Exchange Streets.<sup id="cite_ref-best_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-best-9">[9]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-History_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-History-10">[10]</a></sup> In 1865, Union Bank received a national charter and became the National Union Bank of Boston. The bank later built a headquarters at Washington and State streets.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p><p>State Street Deposit &amp; Trust Co opened in 1891. It became the custodian of the first U.S. mutual fund in 1924, the Massachusetts Investors Trust (now <a href="/wiki/MFS_Investment_Management" title="MFS Investment Management">MFS Investment Management</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-History_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-History-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="20th_century">20th century</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=State_Street_Corporation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: 20th century" data-section="6" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>State Street and National Union merged in 1925.<sup id="cite_ref-best_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-best-9">[9]</a></sup> The merged bank took the State Street name, but National Union was the nominal survivor, and it operated under National Union's charter, thus giving the current entity its rank among the oldest banks in the United States.
</p><p>The company merged with Second National Bank in 1955 and with the Rockland-Atlas National Bank in 1961.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p><p>In 1966, the company completed construction of the <a href="/wiki/State_Street_Bank_Building" title="State Street Bank Building">State Street Bank Building</a>, a new headquarters building, the first high-rise office tower in downtown Boston.
</p><p>In 1972, the company opened its first international office in <a href="/wiki/Munich" title="Munich">Munich</a>.
</p><p>In 1973, as a 50/50 <a href="/wiki/Joint_venture" title="Joint venture">joint venture</a> with <a href="/wiki/DST_Systems" title="DST Systems">DST Systems</a>, the company formed Boston Financial Data Services, a provider of shareholder record-keeping, intermediary and investor services, and regulatory compliance. More than 100 top staff from <a href="/wiki/IBM" title="IBM">IBM</a> were hired by State Street as it set about implementing IBM <a href="/wiki/Mainframe_computer" title="Mainframe computer">mainframe computer</a> systems.
</p><p>In 1975, William Edgerly became president and chief executive officer of the bank and shifted the company's strategy from commercial banking to investments and securities processing.<sup id="cite_ref-History_10-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-History-10">[10]</a></sup></p><p>During the 1980s and 1990s, the company opened offices in <a href="/wiki/Montreal" title="Montreal">Montreal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Toronto" title="Toronto">Toronto</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dublin" title="Dublin">Dublin</a>, <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">London</a>, <a href="/wiki/Paris" title="Paris">Paris</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dubai" title="Dubai">Dubai</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sydney" title="Sydney">Sydney</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wellington" title="Wellington">Wellington</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hong_Kong" title="Hong Kong">Hong Kong</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Tokyo" title="Tokyo">Tokyo</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-History_10-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-History-10">[10]</a></sup></p><p>By 1992, most of State Street's revenue came from fees for holding securities, settling trades, keeping records, and performing accounting.<sup id="cite_ref-History_10-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-History-10">[10]</a></sup> In 1994, the company formed <a href="/wiki/State_Street_Global_Advisors" title="State Street Global Advisors">State Street Global Advisors</a>, a global asset management business.
</p><p>In 1995, State Street acquired Investors Fiduciary Trust of <a href="/wiki/Kansas_City" class="mw-redirect" title="Kansas City">Kansas City</a> for $162 million from DST Systems and Kemper Financial Services.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> In 1996, Bank of New York acquired the unit investment trust servicing business of Investors Fiduciary Trust Co., Kansas City, Mo.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p><p>In 1999, State Street sold its retail and commercial banking businesses to <a href="/wiki/Citizens_Financial_Group" title="Citizens Financial Group">Citizens Financial Group</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="21st_century">21st century</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=State_Street_Corporation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: 21st century" data-section="7" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>In 1990, State Street Bank Luxembourg was founded, and as of 2018<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=State_Street_Corporation&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup> is the largest player in the country's fund industry by assets.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p><p>In 2003, the company acquired the securities services division of <a href="/wiki/Deutsche_Bank" title="Deutsche Bank">Deutsche Bank</a> for $1.5 billion.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> The company also sold its corporate trust business to <a href="/wiki/U.S._Bancorp" title="U.S. Bancorp">U.S. Bancorp</a> for $725 million.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> Also in 2003, State Street sold its private asset management business to <a href="/wiki/U.S._Trust" class="mw-redirect" title="U.S. Trust">U.S. Trust</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p><p>In July 2007, the company acquired <a href="/wiki/Investors_Bank_%26_Trust" title="Investors Bank &amp; Trust">Investors Bank &amp; Trust</a> for $4.5 billion.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p><p>In October 2008, the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_the_Treasury" title="United States Department of the Treasury">United States Department of the Treasury</a> invested $2 billion in the company as part of the <a href="/wiki/Troubled_Asset_Relief_Program" title="Troubled Asset Relief Program">Troubled Asset Relief Program</a> and in July 2009, the company became the first major financial firm to repay the Treasury.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p><p>In 2010, the company acquired Mourant International Finance Administration.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> It also acquired the securities services group of <a href="/wiki/Intesa_Sanpaolo" title="Intesa Sanpaolo">Intesa Sanpaolo</a> for $1.87 billion.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> In December 2010, the company announced that it would be retrenching 5% of its workforce and effectively reducing the hourly wages of remaining employees by 10% via increased standard work hours.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p><p>In November 2011, the company was named as amongst the world's 29 systemic banks.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p><p>In 2012, the company acquired <a href="/wiki/Goldman_Sachs" title="Goldman Sachs">Goldman Sachs</a> Administration Services, a  hedge fund administrator, for $550 million.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p><p>In November 2014, the company sold SSARIS Advisors, its <a href="/wiki/Hedge_fund" title="Hedge fund">hedge fund</a> unit, to senior management.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p><p>In 2016, State Street launched a program called Beacon, focused on cutting costs and improving reporting technology.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> Their main focus was to shrink their US workforce in order to bolster profits in excess of $2.5 billion (2018 figures).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2019)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Also in 2016, the company acquired the asset management business of <a href="/wiki/General_Electric" title="General Electric">General Electric</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p><p>In 2017, the company announced that Jay Hooley, the chief executive officer of the company, would retire.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p><p>In 2018, State Street completed its acquisition of Charles River Development, a <a href="/wiki/Burlington,_Massachusetts" title="Burlington, Massachusetts">Burlington, Massachusetts</a> provider of investment management software.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> The deal closed October 1, 2018, at a cost of approximately $2.6 billion that will be financed by the suspension of share repurchases and the issuing of common and preferred equity.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> News of the acquisition led to a drop in State Street shares of nearly 10% with share prices remaining flat since the purchase.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></p><p>In January 2019, State Street announced that it planned to lay off 1,500 employees, increasing the number to 2,300 in July.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-36">[36]</a></sup> The company is shifting their workforce from the United States to countries like China, India and Poland and is operating under a hiring freeze.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_36-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-36">[36]</a></sup> Offsetting this is increased overseas hiring, resulting in a 3K+ net gain of employment.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></p>

<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Currency_trade_fraud">Currency trade fraud</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=State_Street_Corporation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Currency trade fraud" data-section="9" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>In 2009, <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a> alleged on behalf of its pension funds <a href="/wiki/CalPERS" title="CalPERS">CalPERS</a> and <a href="/wiki/CalSTRS" title="CalSTRS">CalSTRS</a> that State Street had committed fraud on currency trades handled by the custodian bank.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> In October 2011, two executives from State Street Global Markets left the company following charges over the pricing of a fixed income transaction. In April 2016, they were charged by the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Justice" title="United States Department of Justice">United States Department of Justice</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Non-disclosure_of_short_position">Non-disclosure of short position</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=State_Street_Corporation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Non-disclosure of short position" data-section="10" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>On February 28, 2012, State Street Global Advisors entered into a consent order with the <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts_Secretary_of_the_Commonwealth" title="Massachusetts Secretary of the Commonwealth">Massachusetts Securities Division</a>. The Division was investigating the firm's role as the investment manager of a $1.65 billion (USD) hybrid <a href="/wiki/Collateralized_debt_obligation" title="Collateralized debt obligation">collateralized debt obligation</a>. The investigation resulted in a fine of $5 million (USD) for the non-disclosure of certain initial investors taking a short position on portions of the CDO.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Shareholder_complaints">Shareholder complaints</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=State_Street_Corporation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Shareholder complaints" data-section="11" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>During the May 2012 annual shareholders meeting, chairman and chief executive Jay Hooley was shouted down on numerous occasions by protesters in relation to the outsourcing and other grievances.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Secret_commissions">Secret commissions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=State_Street_Corporation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Secret commissions" data-section="12" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>On January 18, 2017, State Street agreed to pay $64.6 million to resolve U.S. investigations into what prosecutors said was a scheme to defraud six clients through secret commissions on billions of dollars of trades.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup></p><p>In 2018 former employee of State Street, Edward Pennings was sentenced to six months in prison for his role in the scheme.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup>  Employee Ross McLellan was also sentenced in the United States to 18 months in prison.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Fearless_Girl_statue"><i>Fearless Girl</i> statue</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=State_Street_Corporation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Fearless Girl statue" data-section="13" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>In March 2017, State Street Global Advisors commissioned a statue called <i><a href="/wiki/Fearless_Girl" title="Fearless Girl">Fearless Girl</a></i> by <a href="/wiki/Kristen_Visbal" title="Kristen Visbal">Kristen Visbal</a> and placed it temporarily in the <a href="/wiki/Financial_District,_Manhattan" title="Financial District, Manhattan">Financial District, Manhattan</a>, in front of the <a href="/wiki/Wall_Street" title="Wall Street">Wall Street</a> icon <i><a href="/wiki/Charging_Bull" title="Charging Bull">Charging Bull</a></i>. The statue is an advertisement for an <a href="/wiki/Index_fund" title="Index fund">index fund</a> which comprises gender diverse companies that have a higher percentage of women among their senior leadership.<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> While some have seen it as an encouragement of <a href="/wiki/Women_in_business" title="Women in business">women in business</a>, some women criticized the statue as "corporate feminism" that violated their own <a href="/wiki/Feminist" class="mw-redirect" title="Feminist">feminist</a> principles.<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup> In October 2017, the company paid $5 million to settle a lawsuit charging that it had paid certain female and African-American executives less than their male and European-American peers.<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup></p>
