(NGI) in <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles,_California" class="mw-redirect" title="Los Angeles, California">Los Angeles, California</a> which develops PCR testing methods.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p><p>LabCorp's ViroMed facility, originally in <a href="/wiki/Minnetonka,_Minnesota" title="Minnetonka, Minnesota">Minnetonka, Minnesota</a> until closing this site in 2013,<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> is now housed in Burlington, NC<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> and performs <a href="/wiki/Real-Time_PCR" class="mw-redirect" title="Real-Time PCR">real-time PCR</a> microbial testing using laboratory-developed assays.

</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(1)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=LabCorp&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History" data-section="1" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-1 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-1">
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Revlon">Revlon</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=LabCorp&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Revlon" data-section="2" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p><b>National Health Laboratories Incorporated</b> began in 1978.<sup id="cite_ref-ky_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ky-9">[9]</a></sup> The company was a national blood and <a href="/wiki/Pathology" title="Pathology">pathology</a> laboratory owned by the <a href="/wiki/Revlon_Health_Care_Group" class="mw-redirect" title="Revlon Health Care Group">Revlon Health Care Group</a>, and managed by <a href="/w/index.php?title=Michael_E._Lillig&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Michael E. Lillig (page does not exist)">Michael E. Lillig</a> for seven years. Lillig had earlier been with <a href="/wiki/Becton,_Dickinson_and_Company" class="mw-redirect" title="Becton, Dickinson and Company">Becton, Dickinson and Company</a>. At National Health Laboratories, Inc. he grew annual sales of the company each year by 43%, with revenue reaching $12 million by the end of his tenure. Lillig then left to found several health-care companies, including  <a href="/wiki/Syscor" class="mw-redirect" title="Syscor">Syscor</a>, Inc., Intelysis, Inc., <a href="/wiki/Asterion" class="mw-redirect" title="Asterion">Asterion</a>, LLC and <a href="/w/index.php?title=MetaCyte&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="MetaCyte (page does not exist)">MetaCyte</a>'s <a href="/w/index.php?title=3DR_Laboratories&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="3DR Laboratories (page does not exist)">3DR</a> in <a href="/wiki/Louisville,_Kentucky" title="Louisville, Kentucky">Louisville, Kentucky</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-3dr_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3dr-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span id="National_Health_Laboratories.2C_Inc."></span><span class="mw-headline" id="National_Health_Laboratories,_Inc.">National Health Laboratories, Inc.</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=LabCorp&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: National Health Laboratories, Inc." data-section="3" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>In 1988, National Health Laboratories became publicly traded on the <a href="/wiki/NASDAQ" class="mw-redirect" title="NASDAQ">NASDAQ</a> exchange.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-11">[11]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Revlon" title="Revlon">Revlon</a> retained 24% ownership of the common shares,<sup id="cite_ref-:0_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-11">[11]</a></sup> for the next six years.<sup id="cite_ref-stockguide_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-stockguide-12">[12]</a></sup> Revlon had been a publicly traded company since the 1950s, as it was during most of its ownership of National Health Laboratories. But in 1985, Revlon had been taken over by <a href="/wiki/Ronald_Perelman" title="Ronald Perelman">Ronald Perelman</a>.
</p><p>Through early 1995, the National Health Laboratories principal executive offices were located at 4225 Executive Square, Suite 805 in <a href="/wiki/La_Jolla,_California" class="mw-redirect" title="La Jolla, California">La Jolla, California</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-sec940308_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sec940308-13">[13]</a></sup></p><p>In 1989, the company generated revenue of about US$400 million, with about US$70 million in earnings.<sup id="cite_ref-nyt900717_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nyt900717-14">[14]</a></sup></p><p>In 1990, the company's revenues reached US$500 million, with over US$70 million in earnings.<sup id="cite_ref-nyt900717_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nyt900717-14">[14]</a></sup> That year, the company began paying a cash <a href="/wiki/Dividend" title="Dividend">dividend</a> to shareholders.<sup id="cite_ref-stockguide_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-stockguide-12">[12]</a></sup></p><p>In 1991, National Health Laboratories moved from the NASDAQ OTC exchange to the <a href="/wiki/New_York_Stock_Exchange" title="New York Stock Exchange">New York Stock Exchange</a>, where it began to trade under the new ticker symbol NH.<sup id="cite_ref-nyt900717_14-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nyt900717-14">[14]</a></sup> Up until that time the company had performed very well, including through the <a href="/wiki/1990-1991_recession" class="mw-redirect" title="1990-1991 recession">1990-1991 recession</a>. Its earnings peaked that year at almost US$90 million, and its stock price had risen from its low within the prior few years by several-fold.<sup id="cite_ref-stockguide_12-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-stockguide-12">[12]</a></sup></p><p>However, beginning in 1991 the company became embroiled in Operation "<a href="/w/index.php?title=Labscam&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Labscam (page does not exist)">Labscam</a>," a nationwide crackdown on fraud in the health-care system, initiated by the U.S. Attorney's Office in <a href="/wiki/San_Diego,_California" class="mw-redirect" title="San Diego, California">San Diego, California</a>. The charges were that the company and others routinely submitted false claims to the government health-care agencies <a href="/wiki/Medicare_(United_States)" title="Medicare (United States)">Medicare</a> and <a href="/wiki/Medicaid" title="Medicaid">Medicaid</a> for unnecessary tests which physicians had never ordered. In 1992, National Health Laboratories became the first of the companies to be prosecuted in the government operation.<sup id="cite_ref-labscam_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-labscam-15">[15]</a></sup></p><p>In 1992, the company reported revenues of over US$720 million, however with earnings of only US$40 million. The small gain that year reflected a fourth quarter charge of US$80 million, which the company paid in a settlement agreement with state and federal governments related to the LABSCAM investigation.<sup id="cite_ref-nyt940208_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nyt940208-16">[16]</a></sup> The total payments made by National Health Laboratories in the settlement came to US$111 million that year,<sup id="cite_ref-nyt941215_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nyt941215-17">[17]</a></sup> and ultimately reached US$173 million.<sup id="cite_ref-labscam_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-labscam-15">[15]</a></sup></p><p>In 1993, revenues were up to US$761 million, with new peak earnings of nearly US$113 million.<sup id="cite_ref-nyt940208_16-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nyt940208-16">[16]</a></sup> By that time the company had 22 major laboratories.<sup id="cite_ref-National-Health-Laboratories-1994-10-K_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-National-Health-Laboratories-1994-10-K-18">[18]</a></sup> The stock price reached a new all-time high in 1993, which became the peak for the next couple of years, and the company was added to the S&amp;P MidCap 400 list.<sup id="cite_ref-stockguide_12-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-stockguide-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="National_Health_Laboratories_Holdings">National Health Laboratories Holdings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=LabCorp&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: National Health Laboratories Holdings" data-section="4" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>On March 8, 1994 National Health Laboratories Inc. reorganized as a holding company, <b>National Health Laboratories Holdings Inc.</b><sup id="cite_ref-sec940308_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sec940308-13">[13]</a></sup></p><p>By 1994, <a href="/wiki/MacAndrews_%26_Forbes_Holdings" class="mw-redirect" title="MacAndrews &amp; Forbes Holdings">MacAndrews &amp; Forbes Holdings</a> Inc. then owned the Revlon Holdings Group's former 24 percent of National Health Laboratories Holdings.<sup id="cite_ref-National-Health-Laboratories-1994-10-K_18-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-National-Health-Laboratories-1994-10-K-18">[18]</a></sup> That company was a distributor of <a href="/wiki/Licorice" class="mw-redirect" title="Licorice">licorice</a> extract and chocolate, which had previously been taken over, along with Revlon, by <a href="/wiki/Ronald_Perelman" title="Ronald Perelman">Ronald Perelman</a> in the 1980s.<sup id="cite_ref-nyt941215_17-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nyt941215-17">[17]</a></sup></p><p>On May 4, 1994, National Health Laboratories announced that it would acquire <a href="/w/index.php?title=Allied_Clinical_Laboratories&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Allied Clinical Laboratories (page does not exist)">Allied Clinical Laboratories</a>, Inc. of <a href="/wiki/Nashville,_Tennessee" title="Nashville, Tennessee">Nashville, Tennessee</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-nyt941215_17-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nyt941215-17">[17]</a></sup> reducing their takeover offer to $204 million after federal officials issued new subpoenas in an investigation of Medicare billing practices at Allied Clinical Laboratories Inc.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> In 1993, Allied generated revenues of US$163 million. The former President and CEO of Allied Clinical, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Haywood_D._Cochrane,_Jr.&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Haywood D. Cochrane, Jr. (page does not exist)">Haywood D. Cochrane, Jr.</a>, then became Vice Chairman of National Health Laboratories. In order to complete the cash transaction, the company discontinued paying its dividend at that time.<sup id="cite_ref-sec940510_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sec940510-20">[20]</a></sup></p><p>By the end of 1994, the company had run into financial difficulty again, as it struggled through the economic <a href="/wiki/Soft_landing_(economics)" title="Soft landing (economics)">soft landing</a> that year. Its earnings dropped by over two-thirds, to only US$30 million. The stock never traded that year at more than half of its 1993 peak, and at its 1994 low, it was down by nearly two-thirds from the all-time high.<sup id="cite_ref-stockguide_12-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-stockguide-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Laboratory_Corporation_of_America_Holdings">Laboratory Corporation of America Holdings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=LabCorp&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Laboratory Corporation of America Holdings" data-section="5" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>On April 28, 1995 National Health Laboratories Holdings Inc. merged with <a href="/wiki/Hoffmann-La_Roche" title="Hoffmann-La Roche">Roche</a> Biomedical Laboratories, Inc. and changed its name to <b>Laboratory Corporation of America Holdings</b>. It began trading under its new ticker symbol LH.<sup id="cite_ref-sec950515_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sec950515-21">[21]</a></sup> Shareholders of National Health Laboratories received 0.72 shares of the new company, plus $5.60 in cash, for a 50.1 percent interest in the new company. At the time, <a href="/w/index.php?title=James_R._Maher&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="James R. Maher (page does not exist)">James R. Maher</a> was President and <a href="/wiki/Chief_Executive_Officer" class="mw-redirect" title="Chief Executive Officer">Chief Executive Officer</a> of National Health Laboratories. Following the merger, Maher relinquished those positions, and instead became Chairman of the new company, succeeding the financier Ronald O. Perelman in that position. Perelman received about US$100 million from the deal, which made the new company the largest blood-testing company in the United States.<sup id="cite_ref-nyt941215_17-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nyt941215-17">[17]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-National-Health-Laboratories-1994-10-K_18-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-National-Health-Laboratories-1994-10-K-18">[18]</a></sup></p><p>The merged company created revenues of US$1.7 billion.<sup id="cite_ref-National-Health-Laboratories-1994-10-K_18-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-National-Health-Laboratories-1994-10-K-18">[18]</a></sup> National Health Laboratories already held long-term debt of US$351 million.<sup id="cite_ref-stockguide_12-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-stockguide-12">[12]</a></sup> Together with the Roche debt, the combined companies owed US$590 million prior to the merger. Another US$288 million was added to help finance the payout to shareholders.<sup id="cite_ref-nyt941215_17-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nyt941215-17">[17]</a></sup> By year-end 1995, the new company's total debt reached US$959 million.<sup id="cite_ref-stockguide_12-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-stockguide-12">[12]</a></sup></p><p>Roche Biomedical Laboratories had been created by and was a wholly owned subsidiary of <a href="/wiki/Hoffmann-La_Roche" title="Hoffmann-La Roche">Hoffmann-La Roche</a>, Inc., the American arm of the Swiss medical conglomerate, <a href="/wiki/Roche_Holding,_Limited" class="mw-redirect" title="Roche Holding, Limited">Roche Holding, Limited</a>. Before 1982, the core of Roche Biomedical Laboratories had been <a href="/w/index.php?title=Biomedical_Reference_Laboratories&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Biomedical Reference Laboratories (page does not exist)">Biomedical Reference Laboratories</a>, which dated from the late 1960s, and was  located in Burlington, North Carolina. That core company had become publicly traded in 1979. Hoffman-La Roche acquired it for US$163.5 million in 1982 and then merged it with all of its laboratories, and incorporated the merged company that year as Roche Biomedical Laboratories, Inc. in Burlington. By the early 1990s, Roche Biomedical had become one of the largest clinical laboratory networks in the United States, with US$600 million in sales.<sup id="cite_ref-fundingroche_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fundingroche-22">[22]</a></sup></p><p>By 1993 Roche Biomedical Laboratories had revenues of US$712 million, with 17 major laboratories. Dr. <a href="/w/index.php?title=James_Powell_(biologist)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="James Powell (biologist) (page does not exist)">James Powell</a> was President of Roche Biomedical, and after the merger with National Health Laboratories he became President and CEO of the new company, Laboratory Corporation of America Holdings, which then relocated from La Jolla, California to the Roche Biomedical headquarters in Burlington, North Carolina. Hoffmann-La Roche also contributed US$186.7 million in cash to the deal, and retained 49.9 interest in the new merged company.<sup id="cite_ref-National-Health-Laboratories-1994-10-K_18-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-National-Health-Laboratories-1994-10-K-18">[18]</a></sup></p><p>By year-end 1995, the new Laboratory Corporation of America Holdings suffered a marginal loss of a few million dollars. The stock price dropped by almost half again through the year, to within 10% of its all-time low since going public a half dozen years earlier. By early the next year, it broke marginally below that level, and set a new all-time low.<sup id="cite_ref-stockguide_12-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-stockguide-12">[12]</a></sup></p><p>In July 1998, LabCorp acquired the Michigan-based laboratory division of <i>Universal Standard Healthcare</i> (UHCI). LabCorp also acquired an equity position in Universal Standard Healthcare and has become UHCI's clinical laboratory long-term testing provider<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> but terminated this agreement one year later.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p><p>In June 2000, LabCorp completed the acquisition of <a href="/wiki/San_Diego" title="San Diego">San Diego</a>-based <i>Pathology Medical Laboratories</i> for an undisclosed amount.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p><p>In 2000, LabCorp generated revenues of US$1.9 billion with over 18,000 employees.<sup id="cite_ref-sec011204_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sec011204-4">[4]</a></sup></p><p>In May 2001, LabCorp completed its acquisition of <i>Path Lab Holdings Inc.</i>, the largest regional laboratory in <a href="/wiki/New_England" title="New England">New England</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> and just a few weeks later the acquisition of <a href="/wiki/Minneapolis" title="Minneapolis">Minneapolis</a>-based <i>ViroMed Inc.</i>, specialized on clinical diagnostic testing in <a href="/wiki/Virology" title="Virology">virology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Molecular_biology" title="Molecular biology">molecular biology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Serology" title="Serology">serology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Microbiology" title="Microbiology">microbiology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mycology" title="Mycology">mycology</a> and mycobacteriology, as well as in tissue/eye bank testing, for an undisclosed amount.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p><p>In December 2001, LabCorp became the exclusive marketer for genomics and <a href="/wiki/Proteomics" title="Proteomics">proteomics</a> predictive cancer test products made by <a href="/wiki/Myriad_Genetics" title="Myriad Genetics">Myriad Genetics</a>, Inc.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p><p>In May 2002, LabCorp announced the acquisition of all outstanding Shares of Canadian medical laboratory services company <a href="/wiki/Dynacare" title="Dynacare">Dynacare Inc.</a> for $480 million in cash and own shares.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p><p>In January 2003, LabCorp acquired all of the outstanding shares of <i>Dianon</i>, a provider of <a href="/wiki/Anatomical_pathology" title="Anatomical pathology">anatomical pathology</a> and <a href="/wiki/Oncology" title="Oncology">oncology</a> testing services,<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> for $598 million in cash.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p><p>In 2005, LabCorp's revenues totaled $3.3 billion; in 2006, revenues were $3.6 billion; and in 2007, revenues reached $4.1 billion.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p><p>In March 2005, LabCorp announced the acquisition of all outstanding Shares of <i>Esoterix, Inc.</i>, a leading provider of specialty reference testing, for approximately $150 million in cash from private equity firm <i>Behrman Capital</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p><p>In November 2006, LabCorp acquired <i>Litholink Corporation</i>, a <a href="/wiki/Kidney_stone_disease" title="Kidney stone disease">kidney stone</a> analysis laboratory.<sup id="cite_ref-reuters_3-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-reuters-3">[3]</a></sup></p><p>In December 2007, LabCorp acquired <i>Tandem Labs</i>, a <a href="/wiki/Contract_research_organization" title="Contract research organization">Contract research organization</a> specializing in advanced <a href="/wiki/Mass_spectrometry" title="Mass spectrometry">mass spectrometry</a>, immunoanalytical support, <a href="/wiki/Pharmacokinetics" title="Pharmacokinetics">pharmacokinetics</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Pharmacodynamics" title="Pharmacodynamics">pharmacodynamics</a>, headquartered in <a href="/wiki/Salt_Lake_City,_UT" class="mw-redirect" title="Salt Lake City, UT">Salt Lake City, UT</a> for an undisclosed amount.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></p><p>In June 2009, LabCorp acquired <a href="/wiki/Monogram_Biosciences" title="Monogram Biosciences">Monogram Biosciences</a>, a diagnostic lab specializing in HIV resistance testing, headquartered in <a href="/wiki/South_San_Francisco,_California" title="South San Francisco, California">South San Francisco, CA</a>, in a cash tender of $4.55 per share for approximately $155 million including debt.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></p><p>In May 2010, LabCorp acquired the assets of its <a href="/wiki/Santa_Ana,_California" title="Santa Ana, California">Santa Ana, California</a>-based rival <i>Westcliff Medical Laboratory</i> which had just filed a <a href="/wiki/Chapter_11,_Title_11,_United_States_Code" title="Chapter 11, Title 11, United States Code">Chapter 11</a> bankruptcy action in federal court.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> The FTC challenged this acquisition<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> but lost in court.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></p><p>In September 2010, LabCorp announced the acquisition of <i>Genzyme Genetics</i>, formerly a division of <a href="/wiki/Genzyme_Corporation" class="mw-redirect" title="Genzyme Corporation">Genzyme</a>, with 9 testing laboratories and approximately 1900 employees, for $925 million in cash.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup></p><p>In June 2011, LabCorp acquired their Canadian central labs partner <i>Clearstone</i> from investment firm <i>Czura Thornton</i> for an undisclosed amount, thus adding Clearstone's global network of central laboratories, including sites in China, France, Singapore and Canada, and the central laboratory protocol management system APOLLO CLPM to their portfolio.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup></p><p>In November 2011, LabCorp completed the acquisition of more than 90% of the shares of DNA testing company <i>Orchid Cellmark</i> for $85 million but had to sell parts of Orchid's <a href="/wiki/DNA_paternity_testing" title="DNA paternity testing">paternity business</a> to <i>DNA Diagnostics Center</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup></p><p>In May 2012, LabCorp Clinical Trials sold its European biological sampling kit building operation located in <a href="/wiki/Hamburg" title="Hamburg">Hamburg</a> to clinical <a href="/wiki/Supply_chain_management" title="Supply chain management">supply chain</a> solutions provider <i>Marken</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup></p><p>In August 2012, LabCorp completed the acquisition of rival testing lab <i>Medtox Scientific</i> for $241 million.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup></p><p>In September 2013, LabCorp acquired <i>MuirLab</i>, the clinical laboratory <a href="/wiki/Outreach" title="Outreach">outreach</a> business from <a href="/wiki/John_Muir_Health" title="John Muir Health">John Muir Health</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup></p><p>In October 2014, LabCorp announced it had entered into an agreement to acquire <a href="/wiki/Covance_Inc." class="mw-redirect" title="Covance Inc.">Covance Inc.</a> for approximately $6.1 billion.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> Just a few weeks later, LabCorp completed its $85.3 million acquisition of <i>LipoScience</i>, a developer of diagnostic tests based on <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_magnetic_resonance_spectroscopy" title="Nuclear magnetic resonance spectroscopy">nuclear magnetic resonance technology</a> measuring heart disease risk.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup></p><p>In December 2014, LabCorp announced the completion of its acquisition of <i>Bode Technology Group, Inc.</i>, a provider of <a href="/wiki/Forensic_science" title="Forensic science">forensic</a> DNA analysis, <a href="/wiki/DNA_collection" class="mw-redirect" title="DNA collection">DNA collection</a> products, and relationship testing, <i>from SolutionPoint International, Inc.</i> for an undisclosed amount.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup>  Bode was described in 2019 as the largest DNA forensic testing company in the USA.<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup></p><p>In October 2015, LabCorp announced the acquisition of <i>Safe Foods International Holdings, LLC</i> and its two operating companies, <i>International Food Network</i> and <i>The National Food Laboratory</i> for an undisclosed amount, thus expanding their capabilities in food and beverage product-development and product-integrity.<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup></p><p>In March 2016, LabCorp completed the acquisition of <a href="/wiki/Torrance,_California" title="Torrance, California">Torrance, CA</a>-based laboratory firm <i>Pathology Inc.</i>, a provider of expertise in reproductive FDA donor testing as well as anatomic, molecular and digital pathology services, for an undisclosed amount.<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup></p><p>In August 2016, LabCorp announced it would acquire <a href="/wiki/Sequenom" title="Sequenom">Sequenom</a> for $371 million including debt, thus expanding its reach overseas, especially in Europe and Asia.<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup></p><p>In October 2016, LabCorp acquired <i>ClearPath Diagnostics</i>, a provider of laboratory diagnostic services in the Northeastern United States, from private equity firm <i>Shore Capital Partners</i> for an undisclosed amount.<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup></p><p>In May 2017, LabCorp acquired the Pathology Associates Medical Laboratories in Spokane, WA from Providence Health &amp; Services and Catholic Health Initiatives for an undisclosed sum.<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup></p><p>In July 2017, LabCorp acquired the contract research organisation (CRO) <i>Chiltern</i> for $1.2 Billion.<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup></p><p>In March 2020, LabCorp received <a href="/wiki/Emergency_use_authorization" class="mw-redirect" title="Emergency use authorization">emergency use authorization</a> from the FDA for a test for <a href="/wiki/2019-nCov" class="mw-redirect" title="2019-nCov">2019-nCov</a> to help address the <a href="/wiki/2019-20_coronavirus_pandemic" class="mw-redirect" title="2019-20 coronavirus pandemic">2019-20 coronavirus pandemic</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup></p><p><br><a class="mw-selflink selflink">LabCorp</a>, is one of the six large commercial lab companies that are conducting most of the United States' coronavirus testing, along with <a href="/wiki/ARUP" class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" title="ARUP">ARUP</a>, <a href="/w/index.php?title=BioReference_Laboratories&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="BioReference Laboratories (page does not exist)">BioReference Laboratories</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mayo_Clinic" title="Mayo Clinic">Mayo Clinic</a>, <a href="/wiki/Quest_Diagnostics" title="Quest Diagnostics">Quest Diagnostics</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Sonic_Healthcare" title="Sonic Healthcare">Sonic Healthcare</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-usatoday_Alltucker_20200402_56-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-usatoday_Alltucker_20200402-56">[56]</a></sup> According to the <a href="/w/index.php?title=American_Clinical_Laboratory_Association&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="American Clinical Laboratory Association (page does not exist)">American Clinical Laboratory Association</a>, by April 1, together these laboratories had completed 807,000 COVID-19 tests.<sup id="cite_ref-usatoday_Alltucker_20200402_56-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-usatoday_Alltucker_20200402-56">[56]</a></sup></p>

<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Incidents">Incidents</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=LabCorp&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Incidents" data-section="7" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>LabCorp has been criticized for its practice of paying the salaries of genetic counselors in hospitals and doctors' offices, which is perceived to be a possible conflict of interest.<sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup></p><p>In July 2018, LabCorp was hit with a <a href="/wiki/Ransomware" title="Ransomware">ransomware</a> attack.<sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-59" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup></p>
