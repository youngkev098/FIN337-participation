</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(1)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Corporate_structure">Corporate structure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eversource_Energy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Corporate structure" data-section="1" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-1 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-1">
<p>Before its rebranding the company operated six main subsidiaries: CL&amp;P, PSNH, WMECO, Yankee Gas Services Company (Yankee Gas), NSTAR Electric and NSTAR Gas.<sup id="cite_ref-nu.com_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nu.com-7">[7]</a></sup>  All now currently operate under the Eversource name.
</p><p>Eversource remains Connecticut's largest electric utility, serving more than 1.2 million residential, municipal, commercial and industrial customers in approximately 149 cities and towns. It also serves approximately 229,000 natural gas customers in 73 cities and towns.
</p><p>Eversource is also New Hampshire's largest electric utility, serving more than 500,000 homes and businesses in 211 cities and towns throughout the state.
</p><p>It is also a main distributor of energy to 1.7 million customers throughout Massachusetts, including more than 1.4 million electric customers in 140 communities and more than 300,000 natural gas customers in 51 communities.
</p>

<p>Eversource predecessor Northeast Utilities (NU) was formed on July 1, 1966, with the merger of <a href="/wiki/Connecticut_Light_and_Power_Company" class="mw-redirect" title="Connecticut Light and Power Company">Connecticut Light and Power Company</a> (CL&amp;P, formed in 1917), <a href="/wiki/Western_Massachusetts_Electric_Company" class="mw-redirect" title="Western Massachusetts Electric Company">Western Massachusetts Electric Company</a> (WMECO, formed in 1886), and the <a href="/wiki/Hartford_Electric_Light_Company" title="Hartford Electric Light Company">Hartford Electric Light Company</a> (HELCO, formed in 1878) under a single parent company, creating the first new multi-state public utility holding company since the enactment of the <a href="/wiki/Public_Utility_Holding_Company_Act_of_1935" title="Public Utility Holding Company Act of 1935">Public Utility Holding Company Act of 1935</a>. In 1967, <a href="/wiki/Holyoke_Water_Power_Company" class="mw-redirect" title="Holyoke Water Power Company">Holyoke Water Power Company</a> (HWP) (formed in 1859) joined the NU System, and in 1992 the <b>Public Service Company of New Hampshire</b> (<a href="/wiki/PSNH" title="PSNH">PSNH</a>, formed in 1926) followed.
</p><p>After some up-and-down growth under CEO <a href="/wiki/Lelan_Sillin" class="mw-redirect" title="Lelan Sillin">Lelan Sillin</a> (1970–1983) and his successor, William B. Ellis, PSNH declared bankruptcy in January 1988 due to problems getting a license for the completed <a href="/wiki/Seabrook_Nuclear_Power_Plant" class="mw-redirect" title="Seabrook Nuclear Power Plant">Seabrook Nuclear Power Plant</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p><p>In 1999 Con Edison and Northeast Utilities entered negotiations that would have created one of the largest utilities in the United States. However, Con Edison backed out of the merger in 2001 after Connecticut's <a href="/wiki/Attorney_General" class="mw-redirect" title="Attorney General">Attorney General</a> <a href="/wiki/Richard_Blumenthal" title="Richard Blumenthal">Richard Blumenthal</a> threatened lawsuits to block it.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p><p>Legislation passed in the late 1990s deregulated the electricity market in New England and required regulated utilities to divest generating stations to competitive suppliers. In 1999 the company divested all of the generating assets of WMECO and CL&amp;P per requirements of the Massachusetts and Connecticut legislation. The company retained some of these assets by transferring them to a new subsidiary called Northeast Generation which functioned as a competitive supplier and sold the other assets entirely: WMECO's <a href="/wiki/West_Springfield_Generating_Station" title="West Springfield Generating Station">West Springfield Generating Station</a> and several related hydroelectric and fossil fuel generating units were sold to Con Edison, while other assets (most notably the <a href="/wiki/Northfield_Mountain_hydroelectric_facility" class="mw-redirect" title="Northfield Mountain hydroelectric facility">Northfield Mountain hydroelectric facility</a>) were transferred to Northeast Generation.
</p><p>In 2001, NU sold the assets and operations of its subsidiary, the Holyoke Water Power Company, to the <a href="/wiki/Holyoke,_Massachusetts" title="Holyoke, Massachusetts">City of Holyoke</a> including the HWP electrical distribution system and customer base and all generation with the exception of the <a href="/wiki/Mount_Tom_Station" title="Mount Tom Station">Mt. Tom coal-fired power plant</a> which NU retained. The city's municipal gas and electric department assumed responsibility for the generators and absorbed the HWP distribution customer base.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Between 2000 and 2002 due to state laws, NU divested WMECO, CL&amp;P, and PSNH's nuclear generating assets which consisted of their stakes in the <a href="/wiki/Seabrook_Station_Nuclear_Power_Plant" title="Seabrook Station Nuclear Power Plant">Seabrook</a>, <a href="/wiki/Millstone_Nuclear_Power_Plant" title="Millstone Nuclear Power Plant">Millstone</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Vermont_Yankee" class="mw-redirect" title="Vermont Yankee">Vermont Yankee</a> stations.
</p><p>In November 2005, the company announced it would sell its unregulated competitive businesses, including generation and energy services.  In November 2006 the company had essentially completed the divestiture of its competitive businesses.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p><p>In 2006, NU decided to sell the generating units it had earlier retained in the 1999 divestiture as competitive suppliers and shut down its competitive generation business units. The Northeast Generation assets, including <a href="/wiki/Mount_Tom_Station" title="Mount Tom Station">Mount Tom Station</a> and Northfield Mountain, were all sold to Energy Capital Partners. PSNH continued to operate regulated hydroelectric and fossil fuel generation assets to serve its default/basic service customers who did not choose an alternative competitive supplier.
</p><p>In October 2010, Northeast Utilities announced that it would merge with <a href="/wiki/NSTAR_(company)" title="NSTAR (company)">NSTAR</a>, the major electric and gas provider in <a href="/wiki/Greater_Boston" title="Greater Boston">Greater Boston</a>, with the resulting company retaining the Northeast Utilities name for the next several years.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup>  After government approvals, the deal closed in April 2012.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p><p>In 2015 the company (now known as Eversource) agreed to sell all of its New Hampshire generation assets in the same manner it sold its assets in Massachusetts and Connecticut between 2000 and 2006. The sale of the New Hampshire generation fleet was approved by the state's Public Utilities Commission on November 29, 2017,<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> and completed on January 10, 2018.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p><p>In June 2017 Eversource announced its merger with Aquarion Water Company for $1.675 billion. <sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup>  Aquarion would become a fully owned subsidiary and retain its own name, adding 300 employees and 230,000 customers in Connecticut, Massachusetts, and New Hampshire. In December 2017, the merger was completed after government approval. <sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(3)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Major_projects">Major projects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eversource_Energy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Major projects" data-section="3" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-3 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-3">
<p>Eversource Energy has participated in a number of projects to improve the reliability of the <a href="/wiki/Power_grid" class="mw-redirect" title="Power grid">power grid</a> in southwest Connecticut.  The first project was construction of the $350 million 345 kilovolt <a href="/w/index.php?title=Bethel-Norwalk_transmission_line&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Bethel-Norwalk transmission line (page does not exist)">Bethel-Norwalk transmission line</a> through the western part of the state, and was constructed entirely by the company when it was still known as Northeast Utilities.
</p><p>With <a href="/wiki/United_Illuminating" class="mw-redirect" title="United Illuminating">United Illuminating</a>, an upgrade to the 69-mile (112 km), 345 kilovolt <a href="/wiki/Middletown-Norwalk_transmission_line" title="Middletown-Norwalk transmission line">Middletown-Norwalk transmission line</a> was energized in 2009 at a cost of $900 million.
</p><p>In 2013, the Greater Springfield Reliability Project, a component of the ongoing New England East-West Solution, was energized at a cost of $795 million. The project addressed numerous reliability issues with the Springfield, MA area's 115 kV transmission system by constructing two new 345 kV lines to the Agawam substation; one line north to Ludlow and the other south to North Bloomfield, Connecticut.
</p><p>The new 345 kV corridor added a new strong interface between Massachusetts and Connecticut. The project also involved rebuilding all of the 115 kV lines along the transmission corridor between South Agawam and Ludlow to increase their capacities, building a new 115 kV transmission substation in East Springfield (Cadwell), replacing the Fairmont 115 kV transmission substation in Chicopee with a new substation across the street, and configuring a new 115 kV line from South Agawam to Southwick using a combination of both new and old line segments of the former 115 kV path between Agawam and North Bloomfield. The new Cadwell and Fairmont switching stations allowed a number of three-terminal 115 kV lines to be broken up into two-terminal lines. Finally, the project allowed a problematic underground 115 kV transmission path through the city of Springfield that was vulnerable to thermal overloads to be removed from service by breaking it in half at the middle. The underground lines now function solely to supply the distribution load served out of the Breckwood substation in Springfield. A previously proposed costly project that would have replaced the underground cables is no longer necessary. On November 20, 2013, cutover of 115 kV lines to the new Fairmont Switching Station was complete, marking substantial completion of the GSRP.
</p><p>In May 2016, Eversource began an electrical infrastructure project in QueensBrook. The project involves replacing underground wire and transformers throughout the QB.
</p><p>Eversource has taken action to support the use of <a href="/wiki/Electric_vehicle" title="Electric vehicle">electric vehicles</a>. Starting in 2018, the company began spending $45 million over five years to install over 400 electric vehicle charging stations in Massachusetts. The project is part of the company's Grid Modernization plan. The company has switched much of its power source from coal to <a href="/wiki/Natural_gas" title="Natural gas">natural gas</a>, wind, <a href="/wiki/Hydroelectricity" title="Hydroelectricity">hydroelectricity</a> and solar power.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(4)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span id="HVDC_transmission_.28.22Northern_Pass.22_Project.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id='HVDC_transmission_("Northern_Pass"_Project)'>HVDC transmission ("Northern Pass" Project)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eversource_Energy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title='Edit section: HVDC transmission ("Northern Pass" Project)' data-section="4" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-4 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-4">
<p>As Northeast Utilities the company signed on a joint venture with <a href="/wiki/Hydro-Qu%C3%A9bec" title="Hydro-Québec">Hydro-Québec</a> and <a href="/wiki/NSTAR_(company)" title="NSTAR (company)">NSTAR</a> to build a new <a href="/wiki/High-voltage_direct_current" title="High-voltage direct current">High-voltage direct current</a> (HVDC) line from <a href="/wiki/Windsor,_Quebec" title="Windsor, Quebec">Windsor, Quebec</a> (connecting with the <a href="/wiki/Hydro-Qu%C3%A9bec%27s_electricity_transmission_system" title="Hydro-Québec's electricity transmission system">Quebec grid</a>) to a location in <a href="/wiki/Franklin,_New_Hampshire" title="Franklin, New Hampshire">Franklin, New Hampshire</a>.  It is projected that the line will either run in an existing right-of-way adjacent to the HVDC line that runs through New Hampshire, or it will connect to a right-of-way in northern New Hampshire that will run through the White Mountains.  This 180- to 190-mile line, projected to carry 1,200 megawatts, will carry electricity to approximately one million homes.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> The issue of buying hydropower from Hydro-Québec had been an issue during the <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts_gubernatorial_election,_2010" class="mw-redirect" title="Massachusetts gubernatorial election, 2010">Massachusetts gubernatorial election of 2010</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> In November 2015, the <a href="/wiki/Sierra_Club" title="Sierra Club">Sierra Club</a> of New Hampshire also expressed opposition for the new line, saying that it would not only benefit Connecticut and Massachusetts residents more than those in New Hampshire, but also the concern of the flooding of <a href="/wiki/Boreal_forest_of_Canada" title="Boreal forest of Canada">Boreal forests</a> during the construction of Hydro-Québec's dams in northern Quebec, disputes with the <a href="/wiki/Innu" title="Innu">Innu</a> First Nations, and the effects of tourism and the environment within the <a href="/wiki/White_Mountain_National_Forest" title="White Mountain National Forest">White Mountain National Forest</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p><p>On January 25, 2018, Massachusetts Governor Baker selected this "Northern Pass Transmission" (NPT) project<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> as the winner for a clean energy procurement RFP.
</p><p>However, days later on February 1, 2018, the New Hampshire Site Evaluation Committee voted unanimously to deny<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> Eversource's controversial Northern Pass project a permit, leaving the future of the project, and $1.6B of Eversource's Transmission Rate Base Growth Projections<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> in doubt.
</p><p>On July 26, 2019, Eversource Energy announced that it was giving up Northern Pass after the New Hampshire Supreme Court rejected its appeal and sided with the SEC.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(5)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span id="Transmission_controversy_.28FERC_Complaint.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Transmission_controversy_(FERC_Complaint)">Transmission controversy (FERC Complaint)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eversource_Energy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Transmission controversy (FERC Complaint)" data-section="5" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-5 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-5">
<p>While Eversource reported that its electric transmission earnings were up 80 percent in Q2 2015,<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> the Federal Energy Regulatory Commission (FERC) is now<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Chronological_items" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers"><span title="The date of the event predicted near this tag has passed. (November 2019)">needs update</span></a></i>]</sup> investigating the utility for having transmission rates that appear to be “unjust, unreasonable and unduly discriminatory or preferential”.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> Meanwhile, the potential for rooftop solar to prevent the need for new transmission lines is growing and Eversource wants to cap rooftop solar growth in the state.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(6)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Political_connections">Political connections</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eversource_Energy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Political connections" data-section="6" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-6 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-6">
<p>In 2015, Eversource fought the rooftop solar industry and supported anti-solar policies. In Massachusetts, they staffed the State House with lobbyists in order to end legislation promoting growth of the solar industry.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> During the 2015 legislative session in New Hampshire, Eversource opposed an increase to the state's solar net metering cap.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> New Hampshire's cap is lower than all neighboring states.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p><p>Eversource disclosed on its website politically related organization expenditures of $110,000 to the Democratic Governors Association in 2016, and payments made to trade associations that were used for lobbying or other political activities in excess of $135,000.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(7)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Rate_hikes_and_solar_charges">Rate hikes and solar charges</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eversource_Energy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Rate hikes and solar charges" data-section="7" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-7 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-7">
<p>In Massachusetts, Attorney General <a href="/wiki/Maura_Healey" title="Maura Healey">Maura Healey</a> testified in March, 2017 before the DPU urging it to deny Eversource's proposed $300 million rate increase. In her testimony,<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> she challenged the need for Eversource's rate increase, noting NSTAR's and WMECo's high returns over the last few years. Referencing NSTAR's 2015 return of more than 13 percent, Attorney General Healey told the DPU that “[l]ast year, no state public utility commission in the country allowed a return that high.” Between 2010 and 2015, Eversource's shareholders of common stock received a cumulative total return (including quarterly dividends and the change in the market price per share) of 89 percent.
</p><p>On November 30, 2017, the Massachusetts Department of Public Utilities on Thursday approved a much-reduced rate hike<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> for Eversource Energy that will allow it to charge its Massachusetts electric customers tens of millions of dollars more a year. On December 20, 2017, Attorney General Maura Healey appealed the DPU ruling in the Eversource rate case, specifically the DPU's approval of a costly 10 percent shareholder return, one of the highest rates allowed by an electric distribution company regulator in the last five years.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></p><p>On January 30, 2018, Massachusetts Rep. Thomas Golden and Sen. Michael Barrett held an Oversight Hearing on the DPU's decision to approve Eversource's proposal to include a demand charge as part of a monthly minimum reliability contribution on net metering customers. Rep. Golden accused the utility of purposefully making the new charges "as confusing as possible." He said, "Let me tell you something gentlemen, I'm not happy how this was rolled out. I'm not happy with the lack of information my office has received." Golden, co-chairman of the Committee on Telecommunications, Utilities and Energy, helped write the 2016 law that permits utilities to levy a new minimum monthly charge, and he told Eversource executives they were making it "extremely, extremely difficult" for him to continue to support them in the policy.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(8)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Price_manipulation_controversy">Price manipulation controversy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eversource_Energy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Price manipulation controversy" data-section="8" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-8 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-8">
<p>In 2017, an environmental group accused Eversource and Avangrid of driving up electric, gas rates over several winters<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> by buying up shipment capacity on a major pipeline that they ultimately did not use. The Environmental Defense Fund said both utilities routinely reserved big deliveries of natural gas on the Algonquin pipeline system for frigid days, but then sharply reduced those orders too late in the day for others to use that capacity. Those orders had the effect of driving up wholesale prices for natural gas during peak winter heating periods and in turn increasing the costs of electricity generated by gas-fired power plants. The two utilities “engaged in behavior that would tend to have the largest impact on prices,” said N. Jonathan Peress, a senior director at the New York-based environmental group. “That implies they knew their efforts would have some sort of pricing impact that would provide them with some commercial benefit.” Representatives for both utilities denied they did anything improper.
</p><p>Massachusetts Attorney General Maura Healey is reviewing the findings,<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Chronological_items" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers"><span title="The date of the event predicted near this tag has passed. (November 2019)">needs update</span></a></i>]</sup> U.S. Senator <a href="/wiki/Richard_Blumenthal" title="Richard Blumenthal">Richard Blumenthal</a> asked the Federal Energy Regulatory Commission to open an investigation on the matter, and both the Connecticut Public Utilities Regulatory Authority and the Massachusetts Public Utilities Department are launching inquiries of their own.
</p><p>On Feb. 27, 2018, FERC announced their investigation “revealed no evidence of anticompetitive withholding of natural gas pipeline capacity on Algonquin Gas Transmission by New England shippers.”  It said that following an extensive review Commission staff “determined that EDF’s study was flawed and led to incorrect conclusions about the alleged withholding.”<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup></p><p>A class-action lawsuit<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> filed on November 14, 2017 against Avangrid, Inc. and Eversource Energy claims the two companies caused electricity consumers to incur overcharges of $3.6 billion in a years-long scheme that impacted six states and affected 14.7 million people.  The lawsuit states that 7.1 million retail electricity customers and an overall population of 14.7 million people have been affected by Eversource and Avangrid's “unique monopoly” spanning at least from 2013 to 2016. On June 10, 2019, the case was thrown out of court as the judge stated natural gas prices are federally regulated and could not be interfered with by the court.. <sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup></p><p>On December 11, 2017, the utility sent a cease-and-desist letter to the Environmental Defense Fund, demanding the group remove a report from its website that accused Eversource of driving up electricity prices with its contracting practices for natural gas.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup></p>
