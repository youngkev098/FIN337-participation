</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(1)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=VF_Corporation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History" data-section="1" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-1 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-1">
<p>The company was established first as Reading Glove and Mitten Manufacturing Company in <a href="/wiki/Pennsylvania" title="Pennsylvania">Pennsylvania</a> in October 1899 by John Barbey and a group of investors. The company was started with $11,000 and a 320-square-foot (30 m<sup>2</sup>) factory that was leased for $60/month.  It was incorporated in Pennsylvania on December 4 of that same year. The manufacture of undergarments began in 1919, accompanied by a name change to Vanity Fair Mills. Shares were initially sold to the public in 1951.
</p><p>The H.D. Lee Company (now <a href="/wiki/Lee_(Jeans)" class="mw-redirect" title="Lee (Jeans)">Lee</a>) was acquired by the company in 1969 and the corporate name was changed to VF Corporation to reflect the more diverse product line. Blue Bell Inc., the owner of such brands as <a href="/wiki/Wrangler_Jeans" class="mw-redirect" title="Wrangler Jeans">Wrangler</a> and <a href="/wiki/JanSport" title="JanSport">JanSport</a>, was acquired in 1986, effectively doubling the size of VF and making it the largest publicly held clothing company.
</p><p>In 1998, VF moved its headquarters from <a href="/wiki/Wyomissing,_Pennsylvania" title="Wyomissing, Pennsylvania">Wyomissing, Pennsylvania</a> to <a href="/wiki/Greensboro,_North_Carolina" title="Greensboro, North Carolina">Greensboro, North Carolina</a> to be closer to more of its operations.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p><p>The company sold its "Vanity Fair" lingerie business to <a href="/wiki/Fruit_of_the_Loom" title="Fruit of the Loom">Fruit of the Loom</a> for $350 million in cash on January 23, 2007.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p><p>On February 28, 2007, VF Corporation acquired <a href="/wiki/Majestic_Athletic" title="Majestic Athletic">Majestic Athletic</a>. On July 26, 2007, VF Corporation announced the purchase of <a href="/wiki/7_for_all_Mankind" class="mw-redirect" title="7 for all Mankind">7 for all Mankind</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lucy_Activewear" title="Lucy Activewear">Lucy Activewear</a> for a combined $885 million.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Eric_C._Wiseman" title="Eric C. Wiseman">Eric C. Wiseman</a> became President, CEO, and Chairman in 2008.<sup id="cite_ref-bloomberg_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bloomberg-8">[8]</a></sup> Effective January 1, 2017 Steve Rendle took over CEO and President responsibilities.
</p><p>In 2011, VF Corporation announced its intention to purchase <a href="/wiki/The_Timberland_Company" title="The Timberland Company">Timberland</a> for $2.2 billion.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> The deal closed in September 2011.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> On December 21, 2012, VF Imagewear was awarded a multimillion-dollar contract to provide uniforms and insignia for Customs and Border Protection officers.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> In February 2013, Imagewear was awarded a $50-million contract to manufacture uniforms for <a href="/wiki/Transportation_Security_Administration" title="Transportation Security Administration">Transportation Security Administration</a> officers.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Spinoff">Spinoff</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=VF_Corporation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Spinoff" data-section="2" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>In August 2018, it was announced that VF would be splitting into two separate companies. The jeans and outlet stores would be <a href="/wiki/Corporate_spin-off" title="Corporate spin-off">spun off</a> as <a href="/wiki/Kontoor_Brands" title="Kontoor Brands">Kontoor Brands</a>. VF maintained the sports apparel and footwear businesses and moved their corporate headquarters (and around 800 employees) to Denver, Colorado. An 11-story office building at 1551 Wewatta Street near <a href="/wiki/Denver_Union_Station" title="Denver Union Station">Denver Union Station</a> will become the new headquarters.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p><p>Kontoor Brands Inc. became a separate company with the stock symbol KTB on May 23, 2019. Kontoor includes <a href="/wiki/Lee_(jeans)" title="Lee (jeans)">Lee</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wrangler_(jeans)" title="Wrangler (jeans)">Wrangler</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rock_%26_Republic" title="Rock &amp; Republic">Rock &amp; Republic</a> and VF Outlets. The company had 17,000 employees,<sup id="cite_ref-Kontoor_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kontoor-14">[14]</a></sup> 600 at the headquarters,<sup id="cite_ref-Wilson_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wilson-15">[15]</a></sup> at 400 North Elm Street in Greensboro, and merchandising, design, product development and innovation at Revolution Mill.<sup id="cite_ref-Craver_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Craver-16">[16]</a></sup> A support center is located on South Elm-Eugene Street. Lee moved to Elm Street from <a href="/wiki/Merriam,_Kansas" title="Merriam, Kansas">Merriam, Kansas</a>. CEO Scott Baxter said the name Kontoor referred to the "contour" of the body, but the original spelling could not be used.<sup id="cite_ref-Wilson_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wilson-15">[15]</a></sup></p>

<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:VF_Outlet_Store.jpg" class="image"><noscript><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5f/VF_Outlet_Store.jpg/220px-VF_Outlet_Store.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="2560" data-file-height="1712"></noscript><span class="lazy-image-placeholder" style="width: 220px;height: 147px;" data-src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5f/VF_Outlet_Store.jpg/220px-VF_Outlet_Store.jpg" data-alt="" data-width="220" data-height="147" data-class="thumbimage"> </span></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:VF_Outlet_Store.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Vanity Fair Outlet, <br><a href="/wiki/Michigan_City,_Indiana" title="Michigan City, Indiana">Michigan City</a>, <a href="/wiki/Indiana" title="Indiana">Indiana</a>.</div></div></div>
<p>In 1970, it was the suggestion of M.O. Lee, then President of VF Corporation, that established the VF Outlet business. Surplus products from VF sources including Berkshire International and Vanity Fair were sold to the public from a 5,000 square foot factory store, with only a drop cloth separating it from the company's manufacturing facility.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> In doing so, VF Corporation created a brand-new retail industry, the <a href="/wiki/Outlet_mall" class="mw-redirect" title="Outlet mall">outlet mall</a>. The first VF Outlet factory store opened in <a href="/wiki/Reading,_Pennsylvania" title="Reading, Pennsylvania">Reading, Pennsylvania</a>. The outlet mall, located in Vanity Fair's old manufacturing mills, was dubbed the official "Outlet Capital of the World."<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> The corporate name was changed from VF Outlet Village to VF Outlet Center in 2008. Today, the VF Outlet Center is owned and operated by VF Outlet, Inc. with over 1,000,000 square feet of retail space and more than 20 stores.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p><p>VF Outlet stores offer everyday apparel including brand name <a href="/wiki/Jeans" title="Jeans">jeans</a>, <a href="/wiki/Intimate_apparel" class="mw-redirect" title="Intimate apparel">intimate apparel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Activewear" class="mw-redirect" title="Activewear">activewear</a>, <a href="/wiki/Swimwear" class="mw-redirect" title="Swimwear">swimwear</a>, and more for women, men and children. The company currently operates 79 stores in 31 states nationwide.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p><p>In October 2013, VF Outlet launched their <a href="/wiki/ECommerce" class="mw-redirect" title="ECommerce">E-commerce</a> store.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p><p>The VF Outlet business moved to Kontoor Brands Inc., established as a separate company May 23, 2019.<sup id="cite_ref-Kontoor_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kontoor-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
