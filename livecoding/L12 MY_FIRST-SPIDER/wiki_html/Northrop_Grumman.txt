href="/wiki/SpaceShipOne" title="SpaceShipOne">SpaceShipOne</a>, the first privately financed, built, and flown space vehicle.<sup id="cite_ref-northropgrumman.com1_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-northropgrumman.com1-9">[9]</a></sup></p><p>Northrop Grumman currently leads the development of the <a href="/wiki/B-21_Raider" class="mw-redirect" title="B-21 Raider">B-21 Raider</a>, a long-range, <a href="/wiki/Stealth_aircraft" title="Stealth aircraft">stealthy</a> <a href="/wiki/Strategic_bomber" title="Strategic bomber">strategic bomber</a> that can drop conventional and <a href="/wiki/Thermonuclear_weapon" title="Thermonuclear weapon">nuclear weapons</a>; it will replace Northrop's own <a href="/wiki/B-2_Spirit" class="mw-redirect" title="B-2 Spirit">B-2 Spirit</a>, the world's only known stealth bomber. Among its other current projects are development and production of the <a href="/wiki/James_Webb_Space_Telescope" title="James Webb Space Telescope">James Webb Space Telescope</a>, an <a href="/wiki/Space_telescope" title="Space telescope">orbiting observatory</a> slated for launch in 2021; and production of the solid rocket boosters for NASA's <a href="/wiki/Space_Launch_System" title="Space Launch System">Space Launch System</a> program. It is also the sole bidder on the Air Force's <a href="/wiki/Ground_Based_Strategic_Deterrent" title="Ground Based Strategic Deterrent">Ground Based Strategic Deterrent program</a>, which aims to develop and build a new <a href="/wiki/Intercontinental_ballistic_missile" title="Intercontinental ballistic missile">intercontinental ballistic missile</a>.

</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(1)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Business_sectors">Business sectors</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Northrop_Grumman&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Business sectors" data-section="1" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-1 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-1">
<p>Northrop Grumman is made up of four main business sectors: Aeronautics Systems, Defense Systems, Mission Systems and Space Systems.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Prior to Northrop Grumman's reorganization of its divisions on January 1, 2020, the divisions were: Aerospace Systems, Mission Systems, Technology Services, and Innovation Systems.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Aerospace_Systems">Aerospace Systems</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Northrop_Grumman&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Aerospace Systems" data-section="2" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:B-2_Spirit_-_060530-F-5040D-320.jpg" class="image"><noscript><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/34/B-2_Spirit_-_060530-F-5040D-320.jpg/220px-B-2_Spirit_-_060530-F-5040D-320.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="138" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="2000" data-file-height="1252"></noscript><span class="lazy-image-placeholder" style="width: 220px;height: 138px;" data-src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/34/B-2_Spirit_-_060530-F-5040D-320.jpg/220px-B-2_Spirit_-_060530-F-5040D-320.jpg" data-alt="" data-width="220" data-height="138" data-class="thumbimage"> </span></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:B-2_Spirit_-_060530-F-5040D-320.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Northrop Grumman developed and built the <a href="/wiki/Northrop_Grumman_B-2_Spirit" title="Northrop Grumman B-2 Spirit">B-2 Spirit</a> strategic bomber.</div></div></div>
<p>Aerospace Systems, headquartered in <a href="/wiki/Redondo_Beach,_California" title="Redondo Beach, California">Redondo Beach, California</a>, produces aircraft, spacecraft, high-energy laser systems and microelectronics for the U.S. and other nations. This includes surveillance and reconnaissance, protected communications, intelligence, battle management, strike operations, electronic warfare, and missile defense to Earth observation, space science and space exploration.<sup id="cite_ref-aero_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-aero-12">[12]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Northrop_Grumman_B-2_Spirit" title="Northrop Grumman B-2 Spirit">B-2 Spirit</a> strategic bomber, the <a href="/wiki/E-8_Joint_STARS" class="mw-redirect" title="E-8 Joint STARS">E-8C Joint STARS</a> <a href="/wiki/Surveillance_aircraft" title="Surveillance aircraft">surveillance aircraft</a>, the <a href="/wiki/RQ-4_Global_Hawk" class="mw-redirect" title="RQ-4 Global Hawk">RQ-4 Global Hawk</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/T-38_Talon" class="mw-redirect" title="T-38 Talon">T-38 Talon</a> supersonic trainer are used by the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Air_Force" title="United States Air Force">US Air Force</a>. The US Army uses Northrop Grumman's <a href="/wiki/RQ-5_Hunter" class="mw-redirect" title="RQ-5 Hunter">RQ-5 Hunter</a> unmanned air vehicle, which has been in operational use since 1995.  The U.S. Navy uses Northrop Grumman-built aerial vehicles such as the <a href="/wiki/BQM-74_Chukar" class="mw-redirect" title="BQM-74 Chukar">BQM-74 Chukar</a>, RQ-4 Global Hawk-based <a href="/wiki/MQ-4C_Triton" class="mw-redirect" title="MQ-4C Triton">MQ-4C Triton</a>, <a href="/wiki/Northrop_Grumman_MQ-8_Fire_Scout" title="Northrop Grumman MQ-8 Fire Scout">MQ-8 Fire Scout</a>, <a href="/wiki/Grumman_C-2_Greyhound" title="Grumman C-2 Greyhound">Grumman C-2 Greyhound</a>, <a href="/wiki/Grumman_E-2_Hawkeye" class="mw-redirect" title="Grumman E-2 Hawkeye">Grumman E-2 Hawkeye</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Northrop_Grumman_EA-6B_Prowler" title="Northrop Grumman EA-6B Prowler">EA-6B Prowler</a>.  Northrop Grumman provides major components and assemblies for different aircraft such as <a href="/wiki/F/A-18_Hornet" class="mw-redirect" title="F/A-18 Hornet">F/A-18 Hornet</a>, <a href="/wiki/Boeing_F/A-18E/F_Super_Hornet" title="Boeing F/A-18E/F Super Hornet">F/A-18E/F Super Hornet</a>, <a href="/wiki/Boeing_EA-18G_Growler" title="Boeing EA-18G Growler">EA-18G Growler</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> and the <a href="/wiki/Lockheed_Martin_F-35_Lightning_II" title="Lockheed Martin F-35 Lightning II">Lockheed Martin F-35 Lightning II</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-northropgrumman.com2_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-northropgrumman.com2-14">[14]</a></sup> Aerospace systems also serves as the contractor for numerous space payloads and is the prime contractor for the <a href="/wiki/James_Webb_Space_Telescope" title="James Webb Space Telescope">James Webb Space Telescope</a><sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Global_Hawk_1.jpg" class="image"><noscript><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9d/Global_Hawk_1.jpg/220px-Global_Hawk_1.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="144" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="3000" data-file-height="1965"></noscript><span class="lazy-image-placeholder" style="width: 220px;height: 144px;" data-src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9d/Global_Hawk_1.jpg/220px-Global_Hawk_1.jpg" data-alt="" data-width="220" data-height="144" data-class="thumbimage"> </span></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Global_Hawk_1.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Northrop_Grumman_RQ-4_Global_Hawk" title="Northrop Grumman RQ-4 Global Hawk">RQ-4 Global Hawk</a>, a high-altitude, long-endurance surveillance platform</div></div></div>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Mission_Systems">Mission Systems</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Northrop_Grumman&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Mission Systems" data-section="3" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>Northrop Grumman Mission Systems, headquartered in <a href="/wiki/Linthicum,_Maryland" title="Linthicum, Maryland">Linthicum, Maryland</a> creates military radar, sensors and related products, including <a href="/wiki/C4I" class="mw-redirect" title="C4I">C4I</a> <a href="/wiki/Radar" title="Radar">radar</a> systems for <a href="/wiki/Air_defense" class="mw-redirect" title="Air defense">air defense</a>, Airspace Management radar systems such as <a href="/wiki/Airport_Movement_Area_Safety_System" title="Airport Movement Area Safety System">AMASS</a>, and battlefield surveillance systems like the Airborne Reconnaissance Low (ARL). Tactical aircraft sensors include the <a href="/wiki/AN/APG-68" title="AN/APG-68">AN/APG-68</a> radar, the <a href="/wiki/AN/APG-80" title="AN/APG-80">AN/APG-80</a> <a href="/wiki/Active_electronically_scanned_array" title="Active electronically scanned array">AESA</a> radar, and the <a href="/wiki/AN/APG-83" title="AN/APG-83">AN/APG-83</a> AESA radar upgrade for the <a href="/wiki/General_Dynamics_F-16_Fighting_Falcon" title="General Dynamics F-16 Fighting Falcon">F-16 Fighting Falcon</a>, the <a href="/wiki/AN/APG-77" title="AN/APG-77">AN/APG-77</a> AESA radar for the <a href="/wiki/F-22_Raptor" class="mw-redirect" title="F-22 Raptor">F-22 Raptor</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/AN/APG-81" title="AN/APG-81">AN/APG-81</a> AESA radar for the <a href="/wiki/Lockheed_Martin_F-35_Lightning_II" title="Lockheed Martin F-35 Lightning II">F-35 Lightning II</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/AN/AAQ-37" title="AN/AAQ-37">AN/AAQ-37 electro-optical Distributed Aperture System (DAS)</a> for the F-35, and the APQ-164 Passive Electronically Scanned Array (PESA) radar for the <a href="/wiki/B-1_Lancer" class="mw-redirect" title="B-1 Lancer">B-1 Lancer</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Mission Systems produces and maintains the <a href="/wiki/E-3_Sentry" class="mw-redirect" title="E-3 Sentry">AWACS</a> aerial surveillance systems for the U.S., the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>, NATO, Japan, and others. Northrop Grumman is the prime contractor for the development and integration of the Air Force's $2-billion <a href="/wiki/Multi-Platform_Radar_Technology_Insertion_Program" title="Multi-Platform Radar Technology Insertion Program">Multi-Platform Radar Technology Insertion Program</a>.  Northrop Grumman also supports the U.S. ballistic missile program, integrates various command, control and intelligence systems and provides technical and management services to governmental and military customers, all with an emphasis on cyber security. Many other smaller products are made by Northrop Grumman, such as <a href="/wiki/Night_vision_goggles" class="mw-redirect" title="Night vision goggles">night vision goggles</a> and secure communications equipment.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Technology_Services">Technology Services</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Northrop_Grumman&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Technology Services" data-section="4" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>The Technology Services sector headquartered in <a href="/wiki/McNair,_Virginia" title="McNair, Virginia">McNair, Virginia</a> (with a <a href="/wiki/Herndon,_Virginia" title="Herndon, Virginia">Herndon</a> mailing address<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup>), works on "the entire life cycle of civil and defense platforms and capabilities through a range of services".<sup id="cite_ref-tech_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-tech-18">[18]</a></sup><a href="/wiki/Vinnell" title="Vinnell">Vinnell</a>, a Northrop Grumman subsidiary, provides training and communications for the military. In 2003, it landed a $48 million contract to train the <a href="/wiki/New_Iraqi_Army" class="mw-redirect" title="New Iraqi Army">Iraqi Army</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-cpi_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cpi-19">[19]</a></sup> In 2005 the company won a $2 billion contract with <a href="/wiki/Virginia" title="Virginia">Virginia</a> to overhaul most of the state's IT operations.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> Later that year, The <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a> paid $1.2 billion in a contract with the company to provide maintenance of the country's defensive radar.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p><p>Northrop Grumman performs various functions in the <a href="/wiki/War_on_Drugs" class="mw-redirect" title="War on Drugs">War on Drugs</a>. The company sends planes to spray <a href="/wiki/Herbicide" title="Herbicide">herbicides</a> on suspected <a href="/wiki/Cocaine" title="Cocaine">cocaine</a> fields in <a href="/wiki/Colombia" title="Colombia">Colombia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Opium_poppy" class="mw-redirect" title="Opium poppy">opium poppy</a> fields in <a href="/wiki/Afghanistan" title="Afghanistan">Afghanistan</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-maxjourdan_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-maxjourdan-22">[22]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>

<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Innovation_Systems">Innovation Systems</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Northrop_Grumman&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Innovation Systems" data-section="5" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>On June 7, 2018, the acquisition of <a href="/wiki/Orbital_ATK" class="mw-redirect" title="Orbital ATK">Orbital ATK</a> was completed and the former company was absorbed in Northrop Grumman as a new business sector called <a href="/wiki/Northrop_Grumman_Innovation_Systems" title="Northrop Grumman Innovation Systems">Northrop Grumman Innovation Systems</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:0_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-11">[11]</a></sup> With this acquisition, Northrop Grumman got more involved in the space industry, which now includes the construction and launch of the <a href="/wiki/Cygnus_(spacecraft)" title="Cygnus (spacecraft)">Cygnus</a> spacecraft. The firm is in the process of developing the <a href="/wiki/Omega_(rocket)" class="mw-redirect" title="Omega (rocket)">Omega</a> space launch vehicle, which will bring U.S. government's <a href="/wiki/National_security" title="National security">national security</a> satellites into space.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Affiliated_companies_and_partners">Affiliated companies and partners</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Northrop_Grumman&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Affiliated companies and partners" data-section="6" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>Remotec, a subsidiary, is a manufacturer of <a href="/wiki/Remote_control_vehicle" title="Remote control vehicle">remote control vehicles</a> for explosive ordnance disposal and hazardous material handling. A UK-based subsidiary, Park Air Systems, provides VHF and UHF ground-to-air communications systems for the civil and defense markets. Northrop Grumman has also worked closely with Antenna Associates, Inc., a manufacturer of <a href="/wiki/Identification_friend_or_foe" title="Identification friend or foe">Identification friend or foe</a> (IFF)/<a href="/wiki/Secondary_Surveillance_Radar" class="mw-redirect" title="Secondary Surveillance Radar">Secondary Surveillance Radar</a> (SSR) antennas located in Massachusetts.<sup id="cite_ref-Antenna_Associates_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Antenna_Associates-26">[26]</a></sup></p><p>In August 2007, Northrop Grumman acquired <a href="/wiki/Scaled_Composites" title="Scaled Composites">Scaled Composites</a> in which it had previously owned a 40% stake.<sup id="cite_ref-Scaled_Composites_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Scaled_Composites-27">[27]</a></sup></p><p>In 2008, Northrop Grumman began working with DHS Systems LLC, manufacturer of the Deployable Rapid Assembly Shelter (DRASH) in New York, as part of the U.S. Army's Standard Integrated Command Post System program.<sup id="cite_ref-SICPS_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SICPS-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(2)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Northrop_Grumman&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: History" data-section="7" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-2 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-2">
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Northrop_Grumman.svg" class="image"><noscript><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/59/Northrop_Grumman.svg/220px-Northrop_Grumman.svg.png" decoding="async" width="220" height="61" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="512" data-file-height="143"></noscript><span class="lazy-image-placeholder" style="width: 220px;height: 61px;" data-src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/59/Northrop_Grumman.svg/220px-Northrop_Grumman.svg.png" data-alt="" data-width="220" data-height="61" data-srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/59/Northrop_Grumman.svg/330px-Northrop_Grumman.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/59/Northrop_Grumman.svg/440px-Northrop_Grumman.svg.png 2x" data-class="thumbimage"> </span></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Northrop_Grumman.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Company logo used from 1994 to 2020</div></div></div>
<p>Northrop Grumman can trace its lineage back to the beginning of the 20th century and has created a myriad of products such as <a href="/wiki/Ballistic_missile" title="Ballistic missile">ballistic missiles</a>, all-weather radars, <a href="/wiki/Apollo_Lunar_Module" title="Apollo Lunar Module">Apollo Lunar Module</a>, land and sea-based <a href="/wiki/Fighter_aircraft" title="Fighter aircraft">fighter aircraft</a> and <a href="/wiki/Stealth_bomber" class="mw-redirect" title="Stealth bomber">Stealth bombers</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-northropgrumman.com3_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-northropgrumman.com3-29">[29]</a></sup> Originally formed in California in 1939 by <a href="/wiki/Jack_Northrop" title="Jack Northrop">Jack Northrop</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Northrop_Corporation" title="Northrop Corporation">Northrop Corporation</a> was reincorporated in Delaware in 1985.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> After the end of the <a href="/wiki/Cold_War" title="Cold War">Cold War</a>, Northrop went on a series of acquisitions where they bought noteworthy companies such as <a href="/wiki/Grumman" title="Grumman">Grumman Aerospace</a>, Westinghouse and <a href="/wiki/TRW_Inc." title="TRW Inc.">TRW Inc.</a> in addition to a number of other enterprises.<sup id="cite_ref-northropgrumman.com3_29-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-northropgrumman.com3-29">[29]</a></sup> In 2018, Northrop Grumman completed the purchase of Orbital ATK. which has since been renamed to <a href="/wiki/Northrop_Grumman_Innovation_Systems" title="Northrop Grumman Innovation Systems">Northrop Grumman Innovation Systems</a>. Today, Northrop Grumman is one of the largest defense contractors in the world.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="1990s">1990s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Northrop_Grumman&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: 1990s" data-section="8" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>In 1994, Northrop Aircraft bought <a href="/wiki/Grumman_Aerospace" class="mw-redirect" title="Grumman Aerospace">Grumman Aerospace</a>, which built the <a href="/wiki/Apollo_Lunar_Module" title="Apollo Lunar Module">Apollo Lunar Module</a><sup id="cite_ref-Smithsonian_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Smithsonian-33">[33]</a></sup> to create Northrop Grumman (NG).<sup id="cite_ref-Sims_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sims-34">[34]</a></sup> In 1996, the new company acquired <a href="/wiki/Westinghouse_Electronic_Systems" class="mw-redirect" title="Westinghouse Electronic Systems">Westinghouse Electronic Systems</a>, a major manufacturer of radar systems,<sup id="cite_ref-Gilpin-Westinghouse_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gilpin-Westinghouse-35">[35]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Xetron_Corporation" class="mw-redirect" title="Xetron Corporation">Xetron Corporation</a>. In 1997, the defense computer contractor Logicon was added, which had acquired Geodynamics Corporation in March 1996 and Syscon Corporation in February 1995.<sup id="cite_ref-Gilpin-Logicon_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gilpin-Logicon-36">[36]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FundingUniverse_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FundingUniverse-37">[37]</a></sup> Northrop was the prime contractor on the radical <a href="/wiki/YF-23" class="mw-redirect" title="YF-23">YF-23</a> which was one of the two airplanes down selected for the <a href="/wiki/Advanced_Tactical_Fighter" title="Advanced Tactical Fighter">Advanced Tactical Fighter</a> but would eventually lose out to the <a href="/wiki/F-22" class="mw-redirect" title="F-22">F-22</a> raptor.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> Northrop would later partner with Lockheed on the <a href="/wiki/F-35" class="mw-redirect" title="F-35">F-35</a> and now serves as a principal member of the Lockheed Martin industry led team.<sup id="cite_ref-northropgrumman.com2_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-northropgrumman.com2-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Northop.JPG" class="image"><noscript><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bf/Northop.JPG/220px-Northop.JPG" decoding="async" width="220" height="121" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="443" data-file-height="243"></noscript><span class="lazy-image-placeholder" style="width: 220px;height: 121px;" data-src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bf/Northop.JPG/220px-Northop.JPG" data-alt="" data-width="220" data-height="121" data-class="thumbimage"> </span></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Northop.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The <a href="/wiki/Assembly_line" title="Assembly line">assembly line</a> for <a href="/wiki/Northrop_P-61_Black_Widow" title="Northrop P-61 Black Widow">Northrop P-61 Black Widows</a> at the Northrop plant in <a href="/wiki/Hawthorne,_California" title="Hawthorne, California">Hawthorne, California</a> in World War II.  Center wings and fuselages take shape on the left, with more nearly finished airplanes on the right.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup></div></div></div>
<p>In 1998, a merger between Northrop Grumman and competitor <a href="/wiki/Lockheed_Martin" title="Lockheed Martin">Lockheed Martin</a> was considered but abandoned after resistance from the Department of Defense and Department of Justice.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> That same year, it acquired Inter-National Research Institute Inc. In 1999, the company acquired <a href="/wiki/Teledyne-Ryan" class="mw-redirect" title="Teledyne-Ryan">Teledyne Ryan</a>, developer of surveillance systems and unmanned aircraft, California Microwave, Inc., and the Data Procurement Corporation.<sup id="cite_ref-Gara_41-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gara-41">[41]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Schneider_42-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Schneider-42">[42]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Johnston_43-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Johnston-43">[43]</a></sup> On March 19, 1999, Northrop Grumman announced to restate its fourth-quarter results downward to a net loss because of problems related to its dealings with start-up satellite launch company <a href="/wiki/Rocketplane_Kistler" title="Rocketplane Kistler">Kistler Aerospace</a> Corp.<sup id="cite_ref-sec_44-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sec-44">[44]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Vagueness" title="Wikipedia:Vagueness"><span title="This information is too vague. (December 2014)">vague</span></a></i>]</sup> In 1999, Northrop Grumman and <a href="/wiki/Science_Applications_International_Corporation" title="Science Applications International Corporation">SAIC</a> created <a href="/w/index.php?title=AMSEC_LLC&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="AMSEC LLC (page does not exist)">AMSEC LLC</a> as a joint venture, which grew "from $100 million in revenue in 2000 to approximately $500 million in fiscal year 2007."<sup id="cite_ref-Hubler_45-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hubler-45">[45]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="2000s">2000s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Northrop_Grumman&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: 2000s" data-section="9" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>In 2000, NG acquired Federal Data Corporation, Navia Aviation As, Comptek Research, Inc., and Sterling Software, Inc.<sup id="cite_ref-Times100_46-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Times100-46">[46]</a></sup></p><p>In 2001, the company acquired <a href="/wiki/Litton_Industries" title="Litton Industries">Litton Industries</a>, a shipbuilder and defense electronics systems provider for the <a href="/wiki/U.S._Navy" class="mw-redirect" title="U.S. Navy">U.S. Navy</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Schneider-Litton_47-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Schneider-Litton-47">[47]</a></sup> During the acquisition process, a new Delaware holding company, <a href="/w/index.php?title=NNG,_Inc.&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="NNG, Inc. (page does not exist)">NNG, Inc.</a>, was formed, which merged with Northrop Grumman through a one-for-one common shares exchange in April 2001. Both Northrop Grumman and Litton became subsidiaries of the new holding company. The original Northrop Grumman Corporation then changed its name to "Northrop Grumman Systems Corporation"; the holding company, NNG, Inc., changed its name to "Northrop Grumman Corporation". Later that year, <a href="/wiki/Northrop_Grumman_Newport_News" class="mw-redirect" title="Northrop Grumman Newport News">Newport News Shipbuilding</a> was added.<sup id="cite_ref-Merle_48-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Merle-48">[48]</a></sup></p><p>On November 1, 2001, Northrop Grumman restated its third-quarter profit after stopping work on two ships for American Classic Voyages, which filed for bankruptcy protection.<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup></p><p>In 2002, Northrop Grumman acquired <a href="/wiki/TRW_Inc." title="TRW Inc.">TRW Inc.</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-Wayne_50-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wayne-50">[50]</a></sup> which had acquired <a href="/wiki/Braddock_Dunn_%26_McDonald" title="Braddock Dunn &amp; McDonald">Braddock Dunn &amp; McDonald</a> (BDM) in 1997, and became the Space Technology sector based in Redondo Beach, California, and the Mission Systems sector based in Reston, Virginia, with sole interest in their space systems and laser systems manufacturing.<sup id="cite_ref-Wait-TRW_51-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wait-TRW-51">[51]</a></sup> The Aeronautical division was sold to <a href="/wiki/Goodrich_Corporation" title="Goodrich Corporation">Goodrich</a>, and the automotive divisions were spun off and retained the TRW name.<sup id="cite_ref-AW-TRW_52-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AW-TRW-52">[52]</a></sup></p><p>There were 15 acquisitions from 1994 to 2003.<sup id="cite_ref-GS1_53-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GS1-53">[53]</a></sup></p><p>Northrop Grumman partnered with <a href="/wiki/EADS" class="mw-redirect" title="EADS">EADS</a> from the mid-2000s to offer the KC-30 Multi Role Tanker Transport aircraft<sup id="cite_ref-DID-Tanker_54-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DID-Tanker-54">[54]</a></sup> in the U.S. Air Force's <a href="/wiki/KC-X" title="KC-X">KC-X</a> tanker competition.<sup id="cite_ref-usaf1_55-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-usaf1-55">[55]</a></sup> In February 2008 the U.S. Air Force chose KC-30,<sup id="cite_ref-AP-AF_56-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AP-AF-56">[56]</a></sup> but in September 2008 the Defense Department stopped the tanker program and in March 2010, Northrop Grumman announced it was withdrawing from the competition.<sup id="cite_ref-FI_NG_declines_57-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FI_NG_declines-57">[57]</a></sup></p><p>On January 1, 2006, Northrop Grumman opened its business sector called 'Technical Services'.<sup id="cite_ref-LAT-AerospaceBriefs_58-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LAT-AerospaceBriefs-58">[58]</a></sup> Northrop Grumman and <a href="/wiki/Boeing" title="Boeing">Boeing</a> collaborated on a design concept for <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA</a>'s upcoming <a href="/wiki/Orion_spacecraft" class="mw-redirect" title="Orion spacecraft">Orion spacecraft</a> (previously the Crew Exploration Vehicle), but the contract went to rival <a href="/wiki/Lockheed_Martin" title="Lockheed Martin">Lockheed Martin</a> on August 31, 2006.<sup id="cite_ref-Borenstein_59-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Borenstein-59">[59]</a></sup></p><p>In 2006, Northrop Grumman had intended to bid for the U.S. Air Force's <a href="/wiki/Next-Generation_Bomber" title="Next-Generation Bomber">Next-Generation Bomber</a>. Though it has not built a large manned aircraft since wrapping up B-2 Spirit production in the 1990s, the company has "been working hard to turn that perception around, with the skills and capabilities that back it up."<sup id="cite_ref-wsj_20060710_nextgen_bomber_60-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wsj_20060710_nextgen_bomber-60">[60]</a></sup> However, by 2009, the teams working on this were told to close, as USAF focus turned to long-range strike instead. Northrop Grumman was one of two teams competing for the <a href="/wiki/Long_Range_Strike_Bomber" class="mw-redirect" title="Long Range Strike Bomber">Long Range Strike Bomber</a>, and in October 2015 won the contract for the Long Range Strike Bomber.<sup id="cite_ref-lrs-b-dodbuzz-news_61-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-lrs-b-dodbuzz-news-61">[61]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-62" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup></p><p>On July 20, 2007, Northrop Grumman became the sole owner of <a href="/wiki/Burt_Rutan" title="Burt Rutan">Burt Rutan</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Scaled_Composites" title="Scaled Composites">Scaled Composites</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-63" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup></p><p>In 2007, Northrop Grumman created 'National Workforce Centers' as an alternative to <a href="/wiki/Offshoring" title="Offshoring">offshoring</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Northrop_Grumman's_National_Work_Force_Center_Initiative_64-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Northrop_Grumman's_National_Work_Force_Center_Initiative-64">[64]</a></sup> Locations are Auburn, Alabama; Corsicana, Texas; Fairmont, West Virginia; Helena, Montana; Johnstown, Pennsylvania; and <a href="/wiki/Lebanon,_Virginia" title="Lebanon, Virginia">Lebanon, Virginia</a>. The Rapid City, South Dakota location closed in January 2012.<sup id="cite_ref-65" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup></p><p>In July 2008, three of four Northrop Grumman employees (<a href="/wiki/Thomas_Howes_(hostage)" title="Thomas Howes (hostage)">Thomas Howes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Marc_Gonsalves" title="Marc Gonsalves">Marc Gonsalves</a> and <a href="/wiki/Keith_Stansell" title="Keith Stansell">Keith Stansell</a>) were freed during <a href="/wiki/Operation_Jaque" title="Operation Jaque">Operation Jaque</a> after five years of captivity following their aircraft crash in the Colombian jungle. The fourth employee, Tom Janis, had been killed by the <a href="/wiki/FARC" class="mw-redirect" title="FARC">FARC</a> shortly after the crash in 2003.<sup id="cite_ref-66" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:James_Webb_Space_Telescope_2009_top.jpg" class="image"><noscript><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/47/James_Webb_Space_Telescope_2009_top.jpg/220px-James_Webb_Space_Telescope_2009_top.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="176" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="10240" data-file-height="8192"></noscript><span class="lazy-image-placeholder" style="width: 220px;height: 176px;" data-src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/47/James_Webb_Space_Telescope_2009_top.jpg/220px-James_Webb_Space_Telescope_2009_top.jpg" data-alt="" data-width="220" data-height="176" data-class="thumbimage"> </span></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:James_Webb_Space_Telescope_2009_top.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Rendering of the $8.7B James Webb Space Telescope</div></div></div>
<p>In January 2008, Northrop Grumman combined its Newport News and Ship Systems sectors into a new business unit named Northrop Grumman Shipbuilding.<sup id="cite_ref-DP_67-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DP-67">[67]</a></sup> On March 31, 2011, this was spun off as <a href="/wiki/Huntington_Ingalls_Industries" title="Huntington Ingalls Industries">Huntington Ingalls Industries</a> Inc (<a href="/wiki/New_York_Stock_Exchange" title="New York Stock Exchange">NYSE</a>: <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://www.nyse.com/quote/XNYS:HII">HII</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-68" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="2010_to_present">2010 to present</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Northrop_Grumman&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: 2010 to present" data-section="10" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>In November 2010, NASA selected Northrop Grumman for consideration of potential contract awards for <a href="/wiki/Heavy_lift_launch_vehicle" class="mw-redirect" title="Heavy lift launch vehicle">heavy lift launch vehicle</a> system concepts, and propulsion technologies.<sup id="cite_ref-Bates_69-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bates-69">[69]</a></sup></p><p>From 2013, Northrop Grumman participates in the DARPA <a href="/wiki/Tactically_Exploited_Reconnaissance_Node" class="mw-redirect" title="Tactically Exploited Reconnaissance Node">Tactically Exploited Reconnaissance Node</a> (TERN) program, and received $2.9 million for Phase 1<sup id="cite_ref-70" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup> and $19 million for Phase 2.<sup id="cite_ref-71" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-72" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-72">[72]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-73" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-73">[73]</a></sup> The TERN program attempts to launch and recover a <a href="/wiki/UAV" class="mw-redirect" title="UAV">UAV</a> from mid-size ships to provide long distance intelligence gathering.<sup id="cite_ref-74" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-74">[74]</a></sup></p><p>In July 2013, Northrop Grumman won a training-simulation contract potentially worth $490 million to support the <a href="/wiki/U.S._Air_Force" class="mw-redirect" title="U.S. Air Force">U.S. Air Force</a>'s next-generation <a href="/wiki/Aerial_warfare" title="Aerial warfare">aerial warfare</a> virtual-training network.<sup id="cite_ref-75" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-75">[75]</a></sup></p><p>As of 2018, Northrop Grumman is the primary contractor for the <a href="/wiki/James_Webb_Space_Telescope" title="James Webb Space Telescope">James Webb Space Telescope</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-76" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-76">[76]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-77" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-77">[77]</a></sup></p><p>In October 2015, the US Military announced it had awarded Northrop Grumman the contract for the successor to the <a href="/wiki/Rockwell_B-1_Lancer" title="Rockwell B-1 Lancer">B-1</a> and <a href="/wiki/B-52" class="mw-redirect" title="B-52">B-52</a>, subsequently identified as the <a href="/wiki/Northrop_Grumman_B-21" class="mw-redirect" title="Northrop Grumman B-21">B-21</a>. The initial value is $21.4 billion, and could eventually be worth up to $80 billion.<sup id="cite_ref-78" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-78">[78]</a></sup></p><p>In September 2017, Northrop announced its intention to acquire missile and rocket manufacturer <a href="/wiki/Orbital_ATK" class="mw-redirect" title="Orbital ATK">Orbital ATK</a> Inc for $9.2 billion: $7.8 billion in cash plus $1.4 billion in net debt.<sup id="cite_ref-79" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-79">[79]</a></sup> On November 29, 2017, the acquisition was approved by Orbital ATK stockholders<sup id="cite_ref-80" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-80">[80]</a></sup> and on June 6, 2018 the merger closed after final FTC approval. The acquired company assets and naming were absorbed and become a division named Northrop Grumman Innovation Systems.<sup id="cite_ref-Insinna_81-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Insinna-81">[81]</a></sup></p>

<p>From 1990 to 2003, before the merger with Grumman in 1994, <a href="/wiki/Kent_Kresa" title="Kent Kresa">Kent Kresa</a> was the <a href="/wiki/CEO" class="mw-redirect" title="CEO">CEO</a> of the company, who led the serial-acquisition strategy with a total of 15 additional acquisitions from 1994 to 2003, including <a href="/wiki/Litton_Industries" title="Litton Industries">Litton</a>, <a href="/wiki/Logicon" class="mw-redirect" title="Logicon">Logicon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Westinghouse_Electronic_Systems" class="mw-redirect" title="Westinghouse Electronic Systems">Westinghouse</a>'s defense electronics business, Ryan Aeronautical and <a href="/wiki/Newport_News_Shipbuilding" title="Newport News Shipbuilding">Newport News Shipbuilding</a>, and TRW. He then retired in 2003 at age 65.
</p><p>In 2003 <a href="/wiki/Ronald_Sugar" title="Ronald Sugar">Ronald Sugar</a>, the former <a href="/wiki/Chief_operating_officer" title="Chief operating officer">chief operating officer</a>, took over as CEO.<sup id="cite_ref-bb20030106_84-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bb20030106-84">[84]</a></sup> Effective October 1, 2003, Sugar also served as the company <a href="/wiki/Chairman" class="mw-redirect" title="Chairman">chairman</a> of the board.<sup id="cite_ref-85" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-85">[85]</a></sup></p><p>In January 2010, <a href="/wiki/Wes_Bush" class="mw-redirect" title="Wes Bush">Wes Bush</a> succeeded as CEO and became company president.<sup id="cite_ref-fm20090916_86-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fm20090916-86">[86]</a></sup></p><p>In November 2015, Gloria Flach was named COO. She is the former president of the company's electric services sector.<sup id="cite_ref-87" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-87">[87]</a></sup></p><p>On July 12, 2018, Wes Bush announced that he would step down as CEO effective January 1, 2019, and would remain chairman of the board until July 2019. His successor is <a href="/wiki/Kathy_J._Warden" title="Kathy J. Warden">Kathy J. Warden</a>, who has served in numerous roles at the company, most recently president and COO.
</p>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(5)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Corporate_headquarters">Corporate headquarters</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Northrop_Grumman&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Corporate headquarters" data-section="13" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-5 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-5">
<p>Since 2011, Northrop Grumman's headquarters are in <a href="/wiki/West_Falls_Church,_Virginia" title="West Falls Church, Virginia">West Falls Church, Virginia</a> (previously Jefferson), <a href="/wiki/Unincorporated_area" title="Unincorporated area">unincorporated</a> <a href="/wiki/Fairfax_County,_Virginia" title="Fairfax County, Virginia">Fairfax County, Virginia</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-NewHQ_88-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NewHQ-88">[88]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-89" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-89">[89]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-90" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-90">[90]</a></sup></p><p>Until 2010 headquartered in <a href="/wiki/Century_City,_Los_Angeles" class="mw-redirect" title="Century City, Los Angeles">Century City, Los Angeles</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-91" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-91">[91]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-92" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-92">[92]</a></sup> it announced plans on January 4, 2010 to move to the <a href="/wiki/Washington_Metropolitan_Area" class="mw-redirect" title="Washington Metropolitan Area">Washington Metropolitan Area</a> by 2011,<sup id="cite_ref-93" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-93">[93]</a></sup> to be closer to government customers.<sup id="cite_ref-CroweMove_94-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CroweMove-94">[94]</a></sup> CEO Wesley Bush stated that the company needed to be located close to <a href="/wiki/Capitol_Hill" title="Capitol Hill">Capitol Hill</a> lawmakers and officials from intelligence and military communities.<sup id="cite_ref-ProctorFlew_95-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ProctorFlew-95">[95]</a></sup> Northrop Grumman considered sites in <a href="/wiki/Washington,_D.C." title="Washington, D.C.">Washington, D.C.</a> and in suburbs in <a href="/wiki/Maryland" title="Maryland">Maryland</a> and <a href="/wiki/Virginia" title="Virginia">Virginia</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-CroweMove_94-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CroweMove-94">[94]</a></sup>  The <i><a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles_Business_Journal" title="Los Angeles Business Journal">Los Angeles Business Journal</a></i> wrote "In a way, the announcement was not a surprise" due to the trend of aerospace companies moving to the DC area, the fact that the new CEO is from West Virginia and that CEOs often move corporate headquarters to places that they want the headquarters located. A Los Angeles area economic development consultant described the move announcement as a "structural failure at all levels for Los Angeles County."<sup id="cite_ref-ProctorFlew_95-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ProctorFlew-95">[95]</a></sup></p><p>District of Columbia economic development officials were "pitching the city's urban hipness and proximity to Capitol Hill power brokers" to Northrop Grumman. Maryland promoted its highly educated workforce and its large number of federal facilities, while Virginia marketed itself as a state with relatively low taxes.<sup id="cite_ref-96" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-96">[96]</a></sup></p><p>In July 2010, the company announced its purchase of an existing building in Fairfax County and its move in summer 2011. It planned to consolidate its Century City headquarters and its existing <a href="/wiki/Arlington_County,_Virginia" title="Arlington County, Virginia">Arlington County, Virginia</a>, offices into the new headquarters. It employed about 40,000 in the Washington DC metropolitan area, including DC and surrounding Maryland and Virginia.<sup id="cite_ref-NewHQ_88-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NewHQ-88">[88]</a></sup></p>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(6)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Accolades_and_criticism">Accolades and criticism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Northrop_Grumman&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Accolades and criticism" data-section="14" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-6 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-6">
<p>Northrop Grumman was named <a href="/wiki/Forbes" title="Forbes">Forbes</a>'s Company of the Year in 2002.<sup id="cite_ref-bb20030106_84-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bb20030106-84">[84]</a></sup> Forbes's announcement credited the company with "master[ing] the art of innovation."<sup id="cite_ref-97" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-97">[97]</a></sup> As of 2019, the company is included on <a href="/wiki/Forbes" title="Forbes">Forbes</a>' list of "America's Best Large Employers".<sup id="cite_ref-98" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-98">[98]</a></sup></p><p>Since 2005 Northrop Grumman credits itself with sponsoring educational programs<sup id="cite_ref-99" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-99">[99]</a></sup> and donating thousands of dollars to various charities.<sup id="cite_ref-100" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-100">[100]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-101" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-101">[101]</a></sup></p><p>Many members of the U.S. government have attended company events and spoken highly of the company and its contributions, for example <a href="/wiki/John_McCain" title="John McCain">John McCain</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-102" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-102">[102]</a></sup> In December 2007, Northrop Grumman Corporation was awarded the <a href="/wiki/Ron_Brown_Award" title="Ron Brown Award">Ron Brown Award</a> for Corporate Leadership, the only presidential award recognizing companies for outstanding achievement in employee and community relations.<sup id="cite_ref-103" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-103">[103]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Environmental_record">Environmental record</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Northrop_Grumman&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Environmental record" data-section="15" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>In 2000, Northrop Grumman was designated a Primary Responsible Party under federal <a href="/wiki/Superfund" title="Superfund">Superfund</a> laws at 13 hazardous waste sites and under state Superfund laws at eight sites.<sup id="cite_ref-sec_44-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sec-44">[44]</a></sup> The corporation has also been linked to 52 superfund <a href="/wiki/Toxic_waste" title="Toxic waste">toxic waste</a> sites.<sup id="cite_ref-104" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-104">[104]</a></sup> Based on 2008 data, Northrop Grumman was the 62nd-largest corporate producer of <a href="/wiki/Air_pollution_in_the_United_States" title="Air pollution in the United States">air pollution in the United States</a>, per the <a href="/wiki/Political_Economy_Research_Institute" title="Political Economy Research Institute">Political Economy Research Institute</a> of the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Massachusetts_Amherst" title="University of Massachusetts Amherst">University of Massachusetts Amherst</a>. Northrop Grumman facilities released more than 23,798 pounds of toxic chemicals into the air in that year.<sup id="cite_ref-105" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-105">[105]</a></sup></p><p>In 2002, the Bethpage Community Park in <a href="/wiki/Bethpage,_New_York" title="Bethpage, New York">Bethpage, New York</a>, owned by the company until the 1960s, was closed due to soil contamination with <a href="/wiki/Polychlorinated_biphenyls" class="mw-redirect" title="Polychlorinated biphenyls">polychlorinated biphenyls</a> (PCBs).<sup id="cite_ref-Dooley_106-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dooley-106">[106]</a></sup> The company dumped cadmium, arsenic, chromium-tainted sludge, solvents, paints and PCBs at the site between 1949 and 1962.<sup id="cite_ref-Dooley_106-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dooley-106">[106]</a></sup>  Additionally, two toxic chemical plumes centered under Bethpage Community Park and other surrounding land formerly owned by Grumman or Northrop Grumman have spread to under neighboring houses.<sup id="cite_ref-Dooley_106-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dooley-106">[106]</a></sup> In November 2013, the Bethpage Water District filed a multimillion-dollar lawsuit against Northrop Grumman in Federal Court for the Eastern District of New York for contaminating the groundwater in Bethpage.<sup id="cite_ref-107" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-107">[107]</a></sup></p><p>In 2003, the company was among 84 parties with which the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Environmental_Protection_Agency" title="United States Environmental Protection Agency">United States Environmental Protection Agency</a>, the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Justice" title="United States Department of Justice">U.S. Department of Justice</a>, and the state of <a href="/wiki/New_York_(state)" title="New York (state)">New York</a> reached an estimated US$15 million settlement for the rehabilitation of the Mattiace Petrochemical Company Superfund site in <a href="/wiki/Glen_Cove,_New_York" title="Glen Cove, New York">Glen Cove</a>, <a href="/wiki/Long_Island" title="Long Island">Long Island</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-108" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-108">[108]</a></sup> In the same year, Northrop Grumman agreed to pay $33,214 after EPA inspectors found <a href="/wiki/Hazardous_waste" title="Hazardous waste">hazardous waste</a> violations at the Capistrano test site.<sup id="cite_ref-109" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-109">[109]</a></sup></p><p>As a response to many of the previous claims, the company has stood up as an organization for social responsibility.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> In 2008, Northrop Grumman launched its Environmental Sustainability program and an EHS Leadership Council, to advance its commitment to environmental performance both internally and externally . The Greenhouse Gas Inventory Project was launched to accurately quantify company-wide greenhouse gas emissions and to reduce the carbon footprint of Northrop Grumman operations, in anticipation of upcoming regulations.<sup id="cite_ref-110" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-110">[110]</a></sup></p><p>In October 2010 the company was named one of Computerworld's Top 12 Green-IT Organizations for its large-scale data center migration effort.<sup id="cite_ref-111" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-111">[111]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Political_contributions_and_governmental_ties">Political contributions and governmental ties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Northrop_Grumman&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Political contributions and governmental ties" data-section="16" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>From 1990 to 2002, Northrop Grumman contributed $8.5 million to federal campaigns.<sup id="cite_ref-112" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-112">[112]</a></sup> According to <a href="/wiki/Political_action_committee" title="Political action committee">PAC</a> summary data compiled by <a href="/wiki/Source_Watch" class="mw-redirect" title="Source Watch">Source Watch</a>, the company gave US$1,011,260 to federal candidates in the 2005–2006 election cycle, compared to $10,612,837 given by all defense contractors in the same cycle.<sup id="cite_ref-113" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-113">[113]</a></sup>  This donation amount was only behind that of General Dynamics and Lockheed Martin in the defense industry. The majority of the contributions, 63%, went to Republicans.<sup id="cite_ref-114" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-114">[114]</a></sup> Former Northrop Grumman Electronics Systems chief <a href="/wiki/James_G._Roche" title="James G. Roche">James G. Roche</a> served as Secretary of the Air Force for two years under George W. Bush. Roche would eventually be nominated to head the Army, but  withdrew his nomination among accusations of mismanaging a contract with <a href="/wiki/Boeing" title="Boeing">Boeing</a> and of failing to properly handle the Air Force sexual assault scandals of 2003.<sup id="cite_ref-115" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-115">[115]</a></sup> According to <a href="/wiki/CorpWatch" title="CorpWatch">CorpWatch</a>, "at least seven former officials, consultants, or shareholders of Northrop Grumman" have held posts "in the Bush administration...including Deputy Secretary of Defense <a href="/wiki/Paul_Wolfowitz" title="Paul Wolfowitz">Paul Wolfowitz</a>, Vice-Presidential Chief of Staff I. <a href="/wiki/Lewis_Libby" class="mw-redirect" title="Lewis Libby">Lewis Libby</a>, Pentagon Comptroller <a href="/wiki/Dov_S._Zakheim" title="Dov S. Zakheim">Dov S. Zakheim</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Sean_O%27Keefe" title="Sean O'Keefe">Sean O'Keefe</a>, director of NASA." Wolfowitz and Libby have both since left the government amid scandals.<sup id="cite_ref-corps_116-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-corps-116">[116]</a></sup></p><p>The company engages third-party lobbying firms in jurisdictions where it has interests. For example, in South Australia it works with lobbying firm CMAX Communications.<sup id="cite_ref-117" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-117">[117]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Controversies">Controversies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Northrop_Grumman&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Controversies" data-section="17" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>In the late 1980s and early 1990s, Northrop was the target of several high-profile criminal and civil cases.<sup id="cite_ref-Corpwatch_118-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Corpwatch-118">[118]</a></sup></p><p>In 1995, Robert Ferro, an employee for TRW Inc., a company Northrop Grumman acquired in 2002, discovered that satellite components manufactured for the U.S. Air Force (USAF) were faulty and likely to fail in operation. TRW suppressed Ferro's report of the problem and hid the information from the USAF, even after a satellite in space equipped with the faulty components experienced serious anomalies. Ferro later sued Northrop Grumman in federal court under the federal <a href="/wiki/Whistle-blower" class="mw-redirect" title="Whistle-blower">whistle-blower</a> law.<sup id="cite_ref-Ferro_lawsuit_119-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ferro_lawsuit-119">[119]</a></sup></p><p>In 1999, the company was sued for knowingly giving the Navy defective aircraft. This suit sought $210 million in damages.<sup id="cite_ref-rightweb_120-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rightweb-120">[120]</a></sup> Ten years later, on April 2, 2009, Northrop Grumman agreed to pay $325 million to settle the suit.<sup id="cite_ref-121" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-121">[121]</a></sup> Ferro was awarded $48.8 million of the settlement. Northrop Grumman stated, "it believed that TRW had 'acted properly under its contracts' and that the company had substantive defenses against the claims."<sup id="cite_ref-122" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-122">[122]</a></sup></p><p>In 2001, federal investigators probed NG for fraud of more than $100 million, systematic overcharging for radar jammers and other high-tech devices used in the B-1 bomber, the F-15 fighter and the B-2 Stealth bomber.<sup id="cite_ref-chic_123-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-chic-123">[123]</a></sup>
In 2003, the company was sued for overcharging the U.S. government for space projects in the 1990s.<sup id="cite_ref-cw_124-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cw-124">[124]</a></sup> Northrop Grumman paid $111.2 million to settle out of court.<sup id="cite_ref-TRW_Press_Release_125-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TRW_Press_Release-125">[125]</a></sup></p><p>From August 25 to September 2, 2010, <a href="/wiki/Virginia" title="Virginia">Virginia</a>'s computer system operated by NG, under a $2.4 billion contract, experienced an outage which resulted in around 45,000 citizens not being able to renew their drivers licenses prior to their expiration. Computer systems for 26 of the state's 89 agencies were affected and Governor <a href="/wiki/Bob_McDonnell" title="Bob McDonnell">Bob McDonnell</a> announced that some data may have been permanently lost.<sup id="cite_ref-126" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-126">[126]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-127" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-127">[127]</a></sup> In 2010 Northrop Grumman apologized for the outage, and agreed to fund an investigation.<sup id="cite_ref-128" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-128">[128]</a></sup></p><p>In 2012, controversy began over Northrop Grumman's <a href="/wiki/Integrated_Air_and_Missile_Battle_Command_System_(IBCS)" class="mw-redirect" title="Integrated Air and Missile Battle Command System (IBCS)">Integrated Air and Missile Battle Command System (IBCS)</a>, when it missed its first deadline. Since then, the system has had a number of controversial developments.<sup id="cite_ref-history_129-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-history-129">[129]</a></sup> In 2016, a <a href="/wiki/Director,_Operational_Test_and_Evaluation" title="Director, Operational Test and Evaluation">Director, Operational Test and Evaluation</a> found a number of major faults with the system, concluding it was "neither mature nor stable".<sup id="cite_ref-defensenews_130-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-defensenews-130">[130]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span id="International_Traffic_in_Arms_Regulations_.28ITAR.29_Violations"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="International_Traffic_in_Arms_Regulations_(ITAR)_Violations">International Traffic in Arms Regulations (ITAR) Violations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Northrop_Grumman&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: International Traffic in Arms Regulations (ITAR) Violations" data-section="18" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>U.S. State Department investigators found that <a href="/wiki/Litton_Industries" title="Litton Industries">Litton Industries</a>, a subsidiary acquired by Northrop Grumman in 2000, had provided portions of <a href="/wiki/Source_code" title="Source code">source code</a> used by guidance and navigation system interfaces aboard <a href="/wiki/Boeing_VC-25" title="Boeing VC-25">Air Force One</a> to a company in Russia in 1998. Northrop Grumman agreed to pay a $15 million fine for 110 violations, occurring between September 1998 and November 1998, of the <a href="/wiki/Arms_Export_Control_Act" title="Arms Export Control Act">Arms Export Control Act</a> and the <a href="/wiki/International_Traffic_in_Arms_Regulations" title="International Traffic in Arms Regulations">International Traffic in Arms Regulations</a> (ITAR).<sup id="cite_ref-131" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-131">[131]</a></sup></p><p>Additionally, documents filed by the State Department state that between 1994 and 2003, Northrop Grumman failed to notify the U.S. State Department about the computer guidance systems also being transferred to <a href="/wiki/Angola" title="Angola">Angola</a>, <a href="/wiki/Indonesia" title="Indonesia">Indonesia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Israel" title="Israel">Israel</a>, <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ukraine" title="Ukraine">Ukraine</a> and <a href="/wiki/Yemen" title="Yemen">Yemen</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-132" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-132">[132]</a></sup></p>
