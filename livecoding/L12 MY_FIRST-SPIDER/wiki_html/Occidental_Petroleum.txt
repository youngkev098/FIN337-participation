<p><b>Occidental Petroleum Corporation</b> (often abbreviated <b>Oxy</b> in reference to its <a href="/wiki/Ticker_symbol" title="Ticker symbol">ticker symbol</a> and logo) is an American company engaged in <a href="/wiki/Hydrocarbon_exploration" title="Hydrocarbon exploration">hydrocarbon exploration</a> in the United States, the <a href="/wiki/Middle_East" title="Middle East">Middle East</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Colombia" title="Colombia">Colombia</a> as well as <a href="/wiki/Petrochemical" title="Petrochemical">petrochemical</a> manufacturing in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Chile" title="Chile">Chile</a>. It is organized in <a href="/wiki/Delaware" title="Delaware">Delaware</a> and headquartered in <a href="/wiki/Houston" title="Houston">Houston</a>. </section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(1)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Occidental_Petroleum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History" data-section="1" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-1 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-1">
<ul><li><b>1920:</b> Occidental Petroleum was founded in California.</li>
<li><b>1957:</b> <a href="/wiki/Armand_Hammer" title="Armand Hammer">Armand Hammer</a> was elected president and chief executive officer of the company after he acquired a controlling stake in the firm for tax reasons.</li>
<li><b>1961:</b> The company discovered the Lathrop Gas Field in <a href="/wiki/Lathrop,_California" title="Lathrop, California">Lathrop, California</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-moscow_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-moscow-5">[5]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>1960s:</b> The company expanded internationally with operations in <a href="/wiki/Peru" title="Peru">Peru</a>, <a href="/wiki/Venezuela" title="Venezuela">Venezuela</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bolivia" title="Bolivia">Bolivia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Trinidad" title="Trinidad">Trinidad</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>.</li>
<li><b>1965:</b> Occidental won exploration rights in Libya.<sup id="cite_ref-moscow_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-moscow-5">[5]</a></sup> and operated there until all activities were suspended in 1986 after the United States imposed economic sanctions on Libya.<sup id="cite_ref-libya_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-libya-6">[6]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>1968:</b> The company entered the chemical business with the acquisition of <a href="/wiki/Hooker_Chemical_Company" title="Hooker Chemical Company">Hooker Chemical Company</a>, 26 years after the contamination at <a href="/wiki/Love_Canal" title="Love Canal">Love Canal</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-love_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-love-7">[7]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>1971:</b> Occidental received permission to develop an <a href="/wiki/Oil_refinery" title="Oil refinery">oil refinery</a> in <a href="/wiki/Canvey_Island" title="Canvey Island">Canvey Island</a> in <a href="/wiki/Essex,_England" class="mw-redirect" title="Essex, England">Essex, England</a>. The company began construction but stopped in 1975 as a result of the <a href="/wiki/1970s_energy_crisis" title="1970s energy crisis">1970s energy crisis</a>. The site remained derelict; the tanks and the chimney were subsequently demolished. Only some concrete foundations and the river jetty remain extant.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>1972:</b> The company was one of the first companies to research developing <a href="/wiki/Oil_shale" title="Oil shale">oil shale</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>1981:</b> The company acquired <a href="/wiki/IBP,_Inc." title="IBP, Inc.">IBP, Inc.</a>, one of largest producers of beef and pork products in the United States.<sup id="cite_ref-ibp_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ibp-10">[10]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>1983:</b> The company and <a href="/wiki/Ecopetrol" title="Ecopetrol">Ecopetrol</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Colombia" title="Colombia">Colombian</a> state-owned oil company, discovered the giant <a href="/wiki/Ca%C3%B1o_Lim%C3%B3n_oilfield" title="Caño Limón oilfield">Caño Limón oilfield</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>1986:</b> The company suspends operation in Libya due to economic sanctions imposed on that country by the United States.</li>
<li><b>April 1988:</b> Occidental acquired <a href="/wiki/Cain_Chemical" class="mw-redirect" title="Cain Chemical">Cain Chemical</a> for .<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>July 6, 1988:</b> An explosion and subsequent inferno on the company's <a href="/wiki/Piper_Alpha" title="Piper Alpha">Piper Alpha</a> platform in the Scottish North Sea, resulted in 167 fatalities in what remains the world's most deadly offshore disaster.<sup id="cite_ref-piper_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-piper-15">[15]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>September 1988:</b> The company formed a joint venture with <a href="/wiki/Church_%26_Dwight" title="Church &amp; Dwight">Church &amp; Dwight</a>, which makes <a href="/wiki/Arm_%26_Hammer" title="Arm &amp; Hammer">Arm &amp; Hammer</a> products, for a potassium carbonate plant at <a href="/wiki/Muscle_Shoals,_Alabama" title="Muscle Shoals, Alabama">Muscle Shoals, Alabama</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>1990:</b> Armand Hammer died and <a href="/wiki/Ray_R._Irani" title="Ray R. Irani">Ray R. Irani</a> became chairman and chief executive officer of the company.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>1991:</b> The company sold its stake in <a href="/wiki/IBP,_Inc." title="IBP, Inc.">IBP, Inc.</a><sup id="cite_ref-ibp_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ibp-10">[10]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>1993:</b> Occidental sold its remaining coal operations.<sup id="cite_ref-coal_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-coal-19">[19]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>February 1996:</b> The company announced layoffs of 450 people in its chemical division.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>July 1996:</b> The company sold its interest in 3 oilfields in the Congo to the Congolese government for $215 million.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>1997:</b> Occidental paid $3.65 billion to acquire the <a href="/wiki/Elk_Hills_Oil_Field" title="Elk Hills Oil Field">Elk Hills Oil Field</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>2005:</b> The company and its partner, Liwa, won 8 out of 15 exploration spots on the EPSA-4 auction, making both companies among the first to enter the Libyan market since the United States lifted its embargo on Libya.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>October 2005:</b> The company acquired Vintage Petroleum for $3.8 billion.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>2006:</b> The government of <a href="/wiki/Ecuador" title="Ecuador">Ecuador</a> seized the company's interest in block 15 of the <a href="/wiki/Amazon_Rainforest" class="mw-redirect" title="Amazon Rainforest">Amazon Rainforest</a>, forcing the company to take a $306 million after-tax charge.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> In 2016, Ecuador agreed to pay $980 million in restitution to the company, down from the original award of $1.77 billion. The agreement was based on a 2012 arbitration award from the <a href="/wiki/International_Center_for_Settlement_of_Investment_Disputes" class="mw-redirect" title="International Center for Settlement of Investment Disputes">International Center for Settlement of Investment Disputes</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>2007:</b> Occidental's compensation policies came under scrutiny after it was announced that Irani received $460 million in compensation in 2006.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>2008:</b> The company acquired a 10% stake in <a href="/wiki/Plains_All_American_Pipeline" title="Plains All American Pipeline">Plains All American Pipeline</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> The company also acquired assets from <a href="/wiki/Plains_Exploration_%26_Production" title="Plains Exploration &amp; Production">Plains Exploration &amp; Production</a> for $1.3 billion.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>February 2009:</b> The company closed its Louisville OxyVinyl <a href="/wiki/Polyvinyl_chloride" title="Polyvinyl chloride">polyvinyl chloride</a> production plant.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>October 2009:</b> The company acquired <a href="/wiki/Citigroup" title="Citigroup">Citigroup</a>'s controversial <a href="/wiki/Phibro" title="Phibro">Phibro</a> energy-trading business, for its net asset value of approximately $250 million.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> The unit was managed by <a href="/wiki/Andrew_Hall_(hedge_fund_manager)" title="Andrew Hall (hedge fund manager)">Andrew J. Hall</a>, who received compensation of approximately $100 million per year in 2007 and 2008. After the acquisition, the division reported its first losses since the 1990s.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> In 2016, Phibro was wound down and sold.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>December 2010:</b> Occidental acquired shale oil properties in the <a href="/wiki/Williston_Basin" title="Williston Basin">Williston Basin</a> in <a href="/wiki/North_Dakota" title="North Dakota">North Dakota</a> for $1.4 billion.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> These assets, as well as other assets acquired by Oxy in the Williston Basin, were sold in 2015 for $600 million.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>December 2010:</b> The company sold its proven and probable reserves of 393 million <a href="/wiki/Barrel_of_oil_equivalent" title="Barrel of oil equivalent">barrels of oil equivalent</a> (2.40<span style="margin:0 .15em 0 .25em">×</span>10<sup>9</sup> GJ) in Argentina to <a href="/wiki/Sinopec" title="Sinopec">Sinopec</a>, a subsidiary of <a href="/wiki/China_Petrochemical_Corporation" title="China Petrochemical Corporation">China Petrochemical Corporation</a>. It also acquired properties in South Texas and North Dakota for $3.2 billion.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>January 2011:</b> Occidental partnered with Abu Dhabi's state oil company in developing the Shah Field, one of the largest natural gas fields in the Middle East, through a joint venture known as Al Hosn Gas.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> Al Hosn Gas became operational in 2015.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>May 2011:</b> Ray R. Irani retires as CEO after <a href="/wiki/CalSTRS" title="CalSTRS">CalSTRS</a> and Relational Investors, two major shareholders, objected to the company's compensation policies for top executives.<sup id="cite_ref-iraniret_43-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-iraniret-43">[43]</a></sup> President Stephen I. Chazen was named CEO to replace Irani and in 2013, shareholders ousted Irani as chairman.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> Despite his outlandish compensation, during Irani's tenure, the company grew from a collection of unrelated businesses to one that focuses on oil and gas and the <a href="/wiki/Market_capitalization" title="Market capitalization">market capitalization</a> of the company went from $5.5 billion to $80 billion.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>2013:</b> Oxychem sold its investment in <a href="/wiki/Unipar_Carbocloro" title="Unipar Carbocloro">Unipar Carbocloro</a> for R$550 million.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>September 2014:</b> Occidental moved its headquarters to Houston, Texas.<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>November 2014:</b> The company sold its 50% interest in BridgeTex Pipeline Company, owner of a 300,000 barrel-per-day crude oil pipeline system that extends from <a href="/wiki/Colorado_City,_Texas" title="Colorado City, Texas">Colorado City, Texas</a> to <a href="/wiki/Texas_City,_Texas" title="Texas City, Texas">Texas City, Texas</a>, for $1.075 billion.<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>December 2014:</b> The company distributed 80.5% of its shares in California Resources Corporation, the largest producer of oil and natural gas on a gross-operated barrels of oil equivalent basis in California, to Occidental shareholders<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup> and distributed its remaining stake to shareholders in March 2016.<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>October 2015:</b> Occidental completed the first phase of a $500 million carbon dioxide flooding project in <a href="/wiki/Hobbs,_New_Mexico" title="Hobbs, New Mexico">Hobbs, New Mexico</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>January 2016:</b> The company sold an office tower in <a href="/wiki/Dallas" title="Dallas">Dallas</a>, Texas for $95 million.<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>May 2016:</b> <a href="/wiki/Vicki_Hollub" title="Vicki Hollub">Vicki Hollub</a>, who had worked at Occidental since 1981 and joined the board in 2015, became the chief executive officer of the company, the first female to serve as chief executive officer of a major U.S. oil and gas company.<sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>March 2017:</b> the company and its 50/50 joint venture partner <a href="/wiki/Mexichem" class="mw-redirect" title="Mexichem">Mexichem</a> began operations of a 1.2-billion-pound per year capacity <a href="/wiki/Ethylene" title="Ethylene">ethylene</a> cracker at the OxyChem plant in <a href="/wiki/Ingleside,_Texas" title="Ingleside, Texas">Ingleside, Texas</a>, along with pipelines and storage at <a href="/wiki/Markham,_Texas" title="Markham, Texas">Markham, Texas</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>June 2017:</b> the company sold land in the Permian Basin for $600 million and used the proceeds to acquire other assets in the area.<sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>August 2019:</b> the company acquired <a href="/wiki/Anadarko_Petroleum" title="Anadarko Petroleum">Anadarko Petroleum</a> for $57 billion, making the deal the world's fourth biggest oil and gas acquisition to date.<sup id="cite_ref-59" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup></li></ul></section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(2)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Current_operations">Current operations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Occidental_Petroleum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Current operations" data-section="2" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-2 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-2">
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Oil_and_gas">Oil and gas</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Occidental_Petroleum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Oil and gas" data-section="3" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>The company's oil and gas operations are concentrated in three geographic areas: the United States, the Middle East, and Colombia. As of December 31, 2019, Occidental had 3.827 <a href="/wiki/1000000000_(number)" class="mw-redirect" title="1000000000 (number)">billion</a> <a href="/wiki/Barrel_of_oil_equivalent" title="Barrel of oil equivalent">barrels of oil equivalent</a> (2.341<span style="margin:0 .15em 0 .25em">×</span>10<sup>10</sup> GJ) of oil equivalent net proved reserves, of which 51% was petroleum, 19% was natural gas liquids, and 30% was natural gas. In 2019, the company had production of 1,029 thousand <a href="/wiki/Barrel_of_oil_equivalent" title="Barrel of oil equivalent">barrels of oil equivalent</a> (6,300,000 GJ) per day.<sup id="cite_ref-10K_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10K-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h4 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="United_States">United States</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Occidental_Petroleum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: United States" data-section="4" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h4>
<p>In 2019, the company's United States operations produced 714 thousand <a href="/wiki/Barrel_of_oil_equivalent" title="Barrel of oil equivalent">barrels of oil equivalent</a> (4,370,000 GJ) per day, representing 69% of the company's worldwide production, including 509 thousand <a href="/wiki/Barrel_of_oil_equivalent" title="Barrel of oil equivalent">barrels of oil equivalent</a> (3,110,000 GJ) per day in <a href="/wiki/Permian_Basin_(North_America)" title="Permian Basin (North America)">Permian Basin</a>, where Occidental is the largest operator and oil producer. The company produced 355 thousand <a href="/wiki/Barrel_of_oil_equivalent" title="Barrel of oil equivalent">barrels of oil equivalent</a> (2,170,000 GJ) per day from <a href="/wiki/Unconventional_oil" title="Unconventional oil">unconventional oil</a> <a href="/wiki/Directional_drilling" title="Directional drilling">directional drilling</a> via Permian Resources and 154 thousand <a href="/wiki/Barrel_of_oil_equivalent" title="Barrel of oil equivalent">barrels of oil equivalent</a> (940,000 GJ) per day using a technique called <a href="/wiki/Enhanced_oil_recovery" title="Enhanced oil recovery">enhanced oil recovery</a>, whereby <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> and water are injected into underground formations to extract the oil and gas. The company also produced 120 thousand <a href="/wiki/Barrel_of_oil_equivalent" title="Barrel of oil equivalent">barrels of oil equivalent</a> (730,000 GJ) per day in the <a href="/wiki/Denver_Basin" title="Denver Basin">Denver Basin</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-10K_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10K-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h4 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Middle_East">Middle East</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Occidental_Petroleum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Middle East" data-section="5" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h4>
<p>The company's oil and gas operations in the Middle East are in <a href="/wiki/Oman" title="Oman">Oman</a>, <a href="/wiki/Qatar" title="Qatar">Qatar</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/United_Arab_Emirates" title="United Arab Emirates">United Arab Emirates</a> and are via <a href="/wiki/Production_sharing_agreement" title="Production sharing agreement">production sharing agreements</a>. The region produced 248 thousand <a href="/wiki/Barrel_of_oil_equivalent" title="Barrel of oil equivalent">barrels of oil equivalent</a> (1,520,000 GJ) per day, representing approximately 24% of 2019 total production. The region also held 28% of the company's proved reserves in 2019.<sup id="cite_ref-10K_1-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10K-1">[1]</a></sup></p><p>The company is the largest independent oil producer in Oman.<sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup> In Qatar, the company is the second-largest oil producer offshore and is a partial owner in the <a href="/wiki/Dolphin_Gas_Project" title="Dolphin Gas Project">Dolphin Gas Project</a>, which delivers gas to Oman and the United Arab Emirates.<sup id="cite_ref-61" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup></p>
<h4 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Colombia">Colombia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Occidental_Petroleum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Colombia" data-section="6" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h4>
<p>In Colombia, which accounted for 34 thousand <a href="/wiki/Barrel_of_oil_equivalent" title="Barrel of oil equivalent">barrels of oil equivalent</a> (210,000 GJ) per day of production, or 3% of total production in 2019, the company operates the <a href="/wiki/Ca%C3%B1o_Lim%C3%B3n_oilfield" title="Caño Limón oilfield">Caño Limón oilfield</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-10K_1-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10K-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Chemical">Chemical</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Occidental_Petroleum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Chemical" data-section="7" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>OxyChem manufactures <a href="/wiki/Polyvinyl_chloride" title="Polyvinyl chloride">polyvinyl chloride</a> (PVC) resins, <a href="/wiki/Chlorine" title="Chlorine">chlorine</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sodium_hydroxide" title="Sodium hydroxide">sodium hydroxide</a> (caustic soda) used in plastics, pharmaceuticals and water treatment chemicals. Other products manufactured by the company include caustic <a href="/wiki/Potash" title="Potash">potash</a>, <a href="/wiki/Organochloride" title="Organochloride">chlorinated organics</a>, sodium silicates, chlorinated <a href="/wiki/Cyanuric_acid" title="Cyanuric acid">cyanuric acid</a> (isocyanurate), and <a href="/wiki/Calcium_chloride" title="Calcium chloride">calcium chloride</a>. OxyChem has manufacturing facilities in the United States, Canada and Chile. In a joint venture with <a href="/wiki/Church_%26_Dwight" title="Church &amp; Dwight">Church &amp; Dwight</a>, OxyChem owns Armand Products Company, which sells <a href="/wiki/Potassium_carbonate" title="Potassium carbonate">potassium carbonate</a> and <a href="/wiki/Potassium_bicarbonate" title="Potassium bicarbonate">potassium bicarbonate</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-10K_1-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10K-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Lobbying_to_do_business_in_Libya">Lobbying to do business in Libya</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Occidental_Petroleum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Lobbying to do business in Libya" data-section="9" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>The company began operations in <a href="/wiki/Libya" title="Libya">Libya</a> in 1965 and operated there until <a href="/wiki/Economic_sanctions" title="Economic sanctions">economic sanctions</a> were imposed in 1986 by the United States.<sup id="cite_ref-libya_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-libya-6">[6]</a></sup> The company was one of the first American companies to resume negotiations in Libya after the sanctions were lifted in 2004.<sup id="cite_ref-62" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup> In 2008, the company, along with 5 other oil companies, was criticized for hiring <a href="/wiki/Hogan_Lovells" title="Hogan Lovells">Hogan Lovells</a> to lobby to exempt Libya from a law written by U.S. Senator <a href="/wiki/Frank_Lautenberg" title="Frank Lautenberg">Frank Lautenberg</a> (D-NJ) to assist American terror victims in seizing assets of countries found culpable in terror attacks, such as the Libyan bombing of <a href="/wiki/Pan_Am_Flight_103" title="Pan Am Flight 103">Pan Am Flight 103</a> over <a href="/wiki/Lockerbie" title="Lockerbie">Lockerbie</a> in 1988.<sup id="cite_ref-63" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup> and to remove a provision in the <a href="/wiki/Dodd%E2%80%93Frank_Wall_Street_Reform_and_Consumer_Protection_Act" title="Dodd–Frank Wall Street Reform and Consumer Protection Act">Dodd–Frank Wall Street Reform and Consumer Protection Act</a> that requires disclosure of payments to foreign governments.<sup id="cite_ref-64" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup> In early 2011, the company ceased exploration activities and production operations in Libya due to the growing civil unrest in the country and U.S. sanctions. In June 2011, the <a href="/wiki/U.S._Securities_and_Exchange_Commission" title="U.S. Securities and Exchange Commission">U.S. Securities and Exchange Commission</a> and United Kingdom prosecutors requested information from the company, <a href="/wiki/ExxonMobil" title="ExxonMobil">ExxonMobil</a> and <a href="/wiki/ConocoPhillips" title="ConocoPhillips">ConocoPhillips</a> related to the <a href="/wiki/Libyan_Investment_Authority" title="Libyan Investment Authority">Libyan Investment Authority</a> (LIA), an investment firm controlled by Libyan leader <a href="/wiki/Muammar_Gaddafi" title="Muammar Gaddafi">Muammar Gaddafi</a>, to determine if there were any violations of international bribery laws.<sup id="cite_ref-Ruffel_65-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ruffel-65">[65]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-66" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup> The Libyan Investment Authority's investments were frozen by the U.S. government in early 2011 following the Gaddafi regime's attacks on Libyan civilians.<sup id="cite_ref-Ruffel_65-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ruffel-65">[65]</a></sup> In 2016, the company ceased operations in Libya.
</p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Environmental_record">Environmental record</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Occidental_Petroleum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Environmental record" data-section="10" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>In 2017, the company was ranked 55th on the Carbon Majors Report, a list of the Top 100 producers and their cumulative greenhouse gas emissions from 1988-2015.<sup id="cite_ref-67" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup></p><p>The company has stated that its use of <a href="/wiki/Enhanced_oil_recovery" title="Enhanced oil recovery">enhanced oil recovery</a> for a portion of its production is one way in which it reduces emissions.<sup id="cite_ref-10K_1-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10K-1">[1]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-68" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup></p>
<h4 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Cleanup_of_the_Copper_Basin">Cleanup of the Copper Basin</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Occidental_Petroleum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Cleanup of the Copper Basin" data-section="11" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h4>
<p>In 1982, the company acquired land in the Copper Basin in <a href="/wiki/Tennessee" title="Tennessee">Tennessee</a>, formerly the site of the <a href="/wiki/Burra_Burra_Mine_(Tennessee)" title="Burra Burra Mine (Tennessee)">Burra Burra Mine</a>, where copper and sulfur had been mined in the 1800s. In 2016, Occidental agreed to spend $50 million to clean up the Copper Basin and restore the water quality of its creeks.<sup id="cite_ref-69" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-70" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup></p>
<h4 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Love_Canal">Love Canal</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Occidental_Petroleum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Love Canal" data-section="12" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h4>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Love_Canal" title="Love Canal">Love Canal</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Abandoned_parking_lot_in_Love_Canal.jpg" class="image"><noscript><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c4/Abandoned_parking_lot_in_Love_Canal.jpg/220px-Abandoned_parking_lot_in_Love_Canal.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="159" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="3584" data-file-height="2592"></noscript><span class="lazy-image-placeholder" style="width: 220px;height: 159px;" data-src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c4/Abandoned_parking_lot_in_Love_Canal.jpg/220px-Abandoned_parking_lot_in_Love_Canal.jpg" data-alt="" data-width="220" data-height="159" data-class="thumbimage"> </span></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Abandoned_parking_lot_in_Love_Canal.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>An abandoned parking lot near <a href="/wiki/Love_Canal" title="Love Canal">Love Canal</a></div></div></div>
<p>Since the 1920s, several companies and the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Armed_Forces" title="United States Armed Forces">United States Armed Forces</a> used the <a href="/wiki/Love_Canal" title="Love Canal">Love Canal</a> as a chemical disposal site. In 1942, <a href="/wiki/Hooker_Chemical_Company" title="Hooker Chemical Company">Hooker Chemical Company</a> began disposing chemical waste at the site and, in 1947, it became the sole owner and user of the land. In 1952, the site was filled to capacity and closed off. The company leased the land to the school board in 1953. Later in the 1950s, the local school board requested that the company sell the land, and threatened to use <a href="/wiki/Eminent_domain" title="Eminent domain">eminent domain</a>. The school board intended to build a school on an unused area of the dump.<sup id="cite_ref-71" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup></p><p>A school was built on the site, and later a middle-class residential district was built on land adjacent to the site. The construction broke through the 4-foot (1.2 m) clay seal containing the waste. In 1968, Hooker Chemical was purchased by Occidental. In 1978, residents became concerned about unusual health issues in the region, including high rates of cancer and <a href="/wiki/Birth_defect" title="Birth defect">birth defects</a>. This subsequently became a national news story, and in 1980, president <a href="/wiki/Jimmy_Carter" title="Jimmy Carter">Jimmy Carter</a> declared a federal emergency in the area. Residents were eventually relocated, and the company paid $129 million in restitution.<sup id="cite_ref-72" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-72">[72]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-love_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-love-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h4 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Oleum_spill">Oleum spill</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Occidental_Petroleum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Oleum spill" data-section="13" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h4>
<p>On Saturday, October 11, 2008, an accidental spill of <a href="/wiki/Oleum" title="Oleum">oleum</a>, a chemical mixture of <a href="/wiki/Sulfuric_acid" title="Sulfuric acid">sulfuric acid</a>, occurred at a facility in <a href="/wiki/Petrolia,_Pennsylvania" title="Petrolia, Pennsylvania">Petrolia, Pennsylvania</a> belonging to Indspec, an affiliate of Occidental Chemical Corporation. The accident caused contamination of the ventilation system and a cloud of toxic gas in the sky and forced the evacuation of over 2,000 residents of the area for the day.<sup id="cite_ref-73" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-73">[73]</a></sup> The spill was determined to be caused by the use of an auxiliary pump <a href="/wiki/Power_supply" title="Power supply">power supply</a> that lacked safety interlocks to prevent tank overfilling.<sup id="cite_ref-74" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-74">[74]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-love_7-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-love-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h4 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Colombia_2">Colombia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Occidental_Petroleum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Colombia" data-section="14" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h4>
<p>From 1992 to 2001, the company tried to drill for oil in the territory of the <a href="/wiki/U%27wa_people" title="U'wa people">U'wa people</a>, in northeast <a href="/wiki/Colombia" title="Colombia">Colombia</a>. The locals resisted, concerned about <a href="/wiki/Environmental_degradation" title="Environmental degradation">environmental degradation</a> and fears that development would bring strangers and be a target for <a href="/wiki/Guerrilla_warfare" title="Guerrilla warfare">guerrilla warfare</a>. There also were tribal beliefs that oil is the "blood of the earth" and should not be removed.<sup id="cite_ref-75" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-75">[75]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-76" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-76">[76]</a></sup> In 2002, after years of <a href="/wiki/Shareholder_resolution" title="Shareholder resolution">shareholder resolutions</a>, legal battles, protests, and a failed test well, the company abandoned the project.<sup id="cite_ref-77" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-77">[77]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-78" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-78">[78]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Repsol" title="Repsol">Repsol</a> took over the project.
</p>
<h4 class="in-block"><span id="Ca.C3.B1o_Lim.C3.B3n"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Caño_Limón">Caño Limón</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Occidental_Petroleum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Caño Limón" data-section="15" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h4>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Ca%C3%B1o_Lim%C3%B3n_%E2%80%93_Cove%C3%B1as_pipeline" class="mw-redirect" title="Caño Limón – Coveñas pipeline">Caño Limón – Coveñas pipeline</a></div>
<p>On December 13, 1998, 17 civilians, including 7 children, were killed when the <a href="/wiki/Colombian_Air_Force" title="Colombian Air Force">Colombian Air Force</a> (CAF) dropped a cluster bomb in the hamlet of Santo Domingo, Colombia, after <a href="/wiki/AirScan" title="AirScan">AirScan</a>, Occidental's security contractor, misidentified it as a hostile guerrilla target. Groups such as <a href="/wiki/FARC" class="mw-redirect" title="FARC">FARC</a> and the <a href="/wiki/National_Liberation_Army_(Colombia)" title="National Liberation Army (Colombia)">National Liberation Army</a> were active in the area. Three employees of AirScan were flying the Skymaster plane from which they provided the Colombian military with the coordinates to drop the bombs. The operation had been planned by the CAF and AirScan at Occidental's complex in Caño Limón. In April 2003, Luis Alberto Galvis Mujica, a witness and survivor of the accident, sued Occidental.<sup id="cite_ref-79" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-79">[79]</a></sup> The courts ruled that Occidental was not liable for the incident.<sup id="cite_ref-80" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-80">[80]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-81" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-81">[81]</a></sup></p>
<h4 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Maynas_Carijano_v._Occidental_Petroleum">Maynas Carijano v. Occidental Petroleum</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Occidental_Petroleum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Maynas Carijano v. Occidental Petroleum" data-section="16" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h4>
<p>On May 10, 2007, a group of 25 <a href="/wiki/Achuar" title="Achuar">Achuar</a> <a href="/wiki/Peruvians" title="Peruvians">Peruvians</a>, a group of <a href="/wiki/Indigenous_peoples" title="Indigenous peoples">Indigenous peoples</a>, filed suit against the company, demanding <a href="/wiki/Environmental_remediation" title="Environmental remediation">environmental remediation</a> and <a href="/wiki/Reparation_(legal)" title="Reparation (legal)">reparations</a> for <a href="/wiki/Environmental_degradation" title="Environmental degradation">environmental degradation</a> allegedly caused by the company between 1971 and 2000, when it drilled in Block 1-AB in Peru. The plaintiffs claimed that the company violated <a href="/wiki/Technical_standard" title="Technical standard">technical standards</a> and <a href="/wiki/Environmental_law" title="Environmental law">environmental law</a> when it dumped a total of 9 billion barrels (1.4<span style="margin-left:0.2em">×<span style="margin-left:0.1em">10</span></span><s style="display:none">^</s><sup>9</sup> m<sup>3</sup>) of toxic oil <a href="/wiki/By-product" title="By-product">by-products</a>, such as cadmium, lead, and <a href="/wiki/Arsenic" title="Arsenic">arsenic</a>, in <a href="/wiki/Drainage_basin" title="Drainage basin">drainage basins</a> used by the Achuar people to fish, drink, and bathe. This environmental damage was alleged to have caused premature deaths and <a href="/wiki/Birth_defect" title="Birth defect">birth defects</a>. A 2006 study by the <a href="/wiki/Ministry_of_Health_(Peru)" title="Ministry of Health (Peru)">Ministry of Health of Peru</a>, found that all but 2 of the 199 people tested had levels of <a href="/wiki/Cadmium" title="Cadmium">cadmium</a> in their blood above safe levels.<sup id="cite_ref-82" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-82">[82]</a></sup></p><p>The Achuar were represented by EarthRights International and the law firm Schonbrun DeSimone Seplow Harris &amp; Hoffman LLP.<sup id="cite_ref-payout_83-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-payout-83">[83]</a></sup></p><p>On March 3, 2010, EarthRights International argued to the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Court_of_Appeals_for_the_Ninth_Circuit" title="United States Court of Appeals for the Ninth Circuit">United States Court of Appeals for the Ninth Circuit</a> that the case should be litigated in Los Angeles, where the company was headquartered.<sup id="cite_ref-84" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-84">[84]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-85" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-85">[85]</a></sup> The court agreed with a trial in the United States, overturning the decision of the lower courts, and, in 2013, the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Supreme_Court" class="mw-redirect" title="United States Supreme Court">United States Supreme Court</a> refused to hear the company's appeal. In March 2015, the company made a <a href="/wiki/Settlement_(litigation)" title="Settlement (litigation)">settlement</a> for an undisclosed amount, with the funds to be used for health, education, and nutrition projects in 5 Achuar communities in the <a href="/wiki/Corrientes_River" title="Corrientes River">Corrientes River</a> basin.<sup id="cite_ref-payout_83-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-payout-83">[83]</a></sup></p>
<h4 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Anadarko_Petroleum"><a href="/wiki/Anadarko_Petroleum" title="Anadarko Petroleum">Anadarko Petroleum</a></span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Occidental_Petroleum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Anadarko Petroleum" data-section="17" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h4>
<p>In 2019, Occidental Petroleum acquired <a href="/wiki/Anadarko_Petroleum" title="Anadarko Petroleum">Anadarko Petroleum</a> inheriting a significant legacy of environmental infractions including the largest environmental contamination settlement in American history,<sup id="cite_ref-tucker_86-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-tucker-86">[86]</a></sup> involvement with the <a href="/wiki/Deepwater_Horizon_explosion" title="Deepwater Horizon explosion"><i>Deepwater Horizon</i> BP disaster</a><sup id="cite_ref-87" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-87">[87]</a></sup> and fines under the Clean Water Act.<sup id="cite_ref-88" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-88">[88]</a></sup></p><p>The deal was clinched as legendary investor and <a href="/wiki/Berkshire_Hathaway" title="Berkshire Hathaway">Berkshire Hathaway</a> CEO <a href="/wiki/Warren_Buffett" title="Warren Buffett">Warren Buffett</a> pledged $10 billion to finance the deal in exchange for 100,000 shares of cumulative perpetual preferred stock with a value of $100,000 per share. Buffett and Berkshire also received a warrant to purchase up to 80 million more shares at an exercise price of $62.50 a share<sup id="cite_ref-89" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-89">[89]</a></sup>.
</p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Political_record">Political record</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Occidental_Petroleum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Political record" data-section="18" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<h4 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Contributions">Contributions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Occidental_Petroleum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Contributions" data-section="19" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h4>
<p>Occidental has disclosed its contributions to <a href="/wiki/Political_action_committee" title="Political action committee">political action committees</a>, lobbyists, and <a href="/wiki/Trade_association" title="Trade association">trade associations</a> on its website.<sup id="cite_ref-90" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-90">[90]</a></sup></p><p>In 2005, the company was among 53 entities which contributed the maximum of $250,000 to the <a href="/wiki/Second_inauguration_of_George_W._Bush" title="Second inauguration of George W. Bush</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-91" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-91">[91]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-92" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-92">[92]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-93" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-93">[93]</a></sup></p><p>The company also donated between $10,000 and $25,000 to the <a href="/wiki/Clinton_Foundation" title="Clinton Foundation">Clinton Foundation</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-94" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-94">[94]</a></sup></p>
<h4 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Gore_family">Gore family</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Occidental_Petroleum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Gore family" data-section="20" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h4>
<p>Former CEO Armand Hammer was a long time friend of former <a href="/wiki/U.S._Senator" class="mw-redirect" title="U.S. Senator">U.S. Senator</a> <a href="/wiki/Albert_Gore,_Sr." class="mw-redirect" title="Albert Gore, Sr.">Albert Gore, Sr.</a> and Gore was a member of the board of directors of the company. In 1972, after he lost an election for the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Senate" title="United States Senate">United States Senate</a>, Gore became the head of Island Creek Coal Company, an Occidental subsidiary. Much of the company's coal and phosphate production was in <a href="/wiki/Tennessee" title="Tennessee">Tennessee</a>, the state Gore represented in the Senate, and Gore owned shares in the company.<sup id="cite_ref-95" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-95">[95]</a></sup> The company liquidated its coal assets in 1993 after Hammer died.<sup id="cite_ref-coal_19-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-coal-19">[19]</a></sup></p><p>Former Vice President of the United States <a href="/wiki/Al_Gore" title="Al Gore">Al Gore</a> was criticized by environmentalists when he inherited shares in the company after the death of his father in 1998; however, the shares were immediately sold.<sup id="cite_ref-goreties_96-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-goreties-96">[96]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-97" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-97">[97]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-98" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-98">[98]</a></sup></p><p>In 1998, the U.S. government sold the <a href="/wiki/Elk_Hills_Oil_Field" title="Elk Hills Oil Field">Elk Hills Oil Field</a> to Occidental for $3.65 billion after an auction process that involved selling the field in segments and offering it to multiple bidders.<sup id="cite_ref-99" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-99">[99]</a></sup> However, critics cited the Gore family's involvement with the company as evidence of <a href="/wiki/Graft_(politics)" title="Graft (politics)">graft</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-goreties_96-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-goreties-96">[96]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Safety_record">Safety record</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Occidental_Petroleum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Safety record" data-section="21" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>In 1999, OxyChem achieved Star Status under OSHA's Voluntary Protection Programs as being among the safest work sites in the U.S.<sup id="cite_ref-100" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-100">[100]</a></sup></p>
<h4 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Piper_Alpha">Piper Alpha</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Occidental_Petroleum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Piper Alpha" data-section="22" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h4>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Piper_Alpha" title="Piper Alpha">Piper Alpha</a></div>
<p>On July 6, 1988, the company's <a href="/wiki/Piper_Alpha" title="Piper Alpha">Piper Alpha</a> offshore production platform in the <a href="/wiki/North_Sea" title="North Sea">North Sea</a> was destroyed when an out of service gas <a href="/wiki/Condensate_pump" title="Condensate pump">condensate pump</a> was started with its pressure <a href="/wiki/Safety_valve" title="Safety valve">safety valve</a> removed. The subsequent <a href="/wiki/Gas_leak" title="Gas leak">gas leak</a>, explosion and fire resulted in the deaths of 167 workers in what remains the world's deadliest offshore disaster.<sup id="cite_ref-piper_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-piper-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Greenmail">Greenmail</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Occidental_Petroleum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Greenmail" data-section="23" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>In 1984, billionaire <a href="/wiki/David_Murdock" class="mw-redirect" title="David Murdock">David Murdock</a> owned about 5% of the company and was a member of its board of directors, after the company acquired <a href="/wiki/IBP,_Inc." title="IBP, Inc.">IBP, Inc.</a>, of which Murdock owned 19%. After disagreements between Murdock and then CEO Armand Hammer, the company paid <a href="/wiki/Greenmail" title="Greenmail">greenmail</a> to buy Murdock's shares at $40.09 each, while the market price was $28.75.<sup id="cite_ref-101" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-101">[101]</a></sup></p>
