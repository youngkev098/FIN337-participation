			<div id="bodyContent" class="content"><div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><script>function mfTempOpenSection(id){var block=document.getElementById("mf-section-"+id);block.className+=" open-block";block.previousSibling.className+=" open-block";}</script><div class="mw-parser-output"><section class="mf-section-0" id="mf-section-0"><p><b>Parker-Hannifin Corporation</b>, originally Parker Appliance Company, usually referred to as just <b>Parker</b>, is an American corporation specializing in motion and control technologies. Its corporate headquarters are in <a href="/wiki/Mayfield_Heights,_Ohio" title="Mayfield Heights, Ohio">Mayfield Heights, Ohio</a>, in <a href="/wiki/Greater_Cleveland" title="Greater Cleveland">Greater Cleveland</a> (with a <a href="/wiki/Cleveland,_Ohio" class="mw-redirect" title="Cleveland, Ohio">Cleveland</a> mailing address).<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> The company was founded in 1917 and has been publicly traded on the <a href="/wiki/NYSE" class="mw-redirect" title="NYSE">NYSE</a> since December 9, 1964. The firm is one of the largest companies in the world in motion control technologies, including aerospace, climate control, electromechanical, filtration, fluid and gas handling, hydraulics, pneumatics, <a href="/wiki/Process_control" title="Process control">process control</a>, and sealing and shielding. Parker employs about 58,000 people globally.

</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(1)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Parker_Hannifin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History" data-section="1" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-1 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-1">
<h3 class="in-block"><span id="1917.E2.80.931950"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="1917–1950">1917–1950</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Parker_Hannifin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: 1917–1950" data-section="2" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Arthur_L._Parker" title="Arthur L. Parker">Arthur L. Parker</a> founded the firm as the Parker Appliance Company in Ohio around 1917 or 1918.<sup id="cite_ref-Zerega_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Zerega-6">[6]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Alexander_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Alexander-7">[7]</a></sup> In its early years, it built <a href="/wiki/Pneumatic" class="mw-redirect" title="Pneumatic">pneumatic</a> brake systems for buses, trucks and trains.<sup id="cite_ref-Zerega_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Zerega-6">[6]</a></sup> In 1919, Parker's truck slid over a cliff, causing the company to lose its entire inventory and forcing the founder to return to his previous job. Nonetheless, he restarted Parker Appliance Company in 1924.<sup id="cite_ref-Vault_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Vault-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Copy_of_early_20th_century_photograph,_an_aerial_view,_showing_the_plant_from_the_south_looking_north._Photo_owned_by_the_Parker-_Hannifin_Corporation._-_Cleveland-Chandler_HAER_OHIO,18-CLEV,25G-11.tif" class="image"><noscript><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/75/Copy_of_early_20th_century_photograph%2C_an_aerial_view%2C_showing_the_plant_from_the_south_looking_north._Photo_owned_by_the_Parker-_Hannifin_Corporation._-_Cleveland-Chandler_HAER_OHIO%2C18-CLEV%2C25G-11.tif/lossy-page1-300px-thumbnail.tif.jpg" decoding="async" width="300" height="239" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="5000" data-file-height="3976"></noscript><span class="lazy-image-placeholder" style="width: 300px;height: 239px;" data-src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/75/Copy_of_early_20th_century_photograph%2C_an_aerial_view%2C_showing_the_plant_from_the_south_looking_north._Photo_owned_by_the_Parker-_Hannifin_Corporation._-_Cleveland-Chandler_HAER_OHIO%2C18-CLEV%2C25G-11.tif/lossy-page1-300px-thumbnail.tif.jpg" data-alt="" data-width="300" data-height="239" data-class="thumbimage"> </span></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Copy_of_early_20th_century_photograph,_an_aerial_view,_showing_the_plant_from_the_south_looking_north._Photo_owned_by_the_Parker-_Hannifin_Corporation._-_Cleveland-Chandler_HAER_OHIO,18-CLEV,25G-11.tif" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>In 1935, <a href="/wiki/Arthur_L._Parker" title="Arthur L. Parker">Art Parker</a> relocated his headquarters into an expanded <a href="/wiki/Euclid_Avenue_(Cleveland)" title="Euclid Avenue (Cleveland)">Euclid Ave</a> location on the east side of <a href="/wiki/Cleveland" title="Cleveland">Cleveland</a>.</div></div></div>
<p>By 1927, the firm had expanded into airplanes. For his flight across the Atlantic Ocean, <a href="/wiki/Charles_Lindbergh" title="Charles Lindbergh">Charles Lindbergh</a> requested Parker parts be used in the construction of his aircraft the <i><a href="/wiki/Spirit_of_St._Louis" title="Spirit of St. Louis">Spirit of St. Louis</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Alexander_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Alexander-7">[7]</a></sup> The firm contributed the system that linked the aircraft's 16 fuel tanks.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p><p>During <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, Parker experienced a boom in business as the <a href="/wiki/U.S._Air_Force" class="mw-redirect" title="U.S. Air Force">U.S. Air Force's</a> primary supplier of valves and fluid connectors.<sup id="cite_ref-Alexander_7-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Alexander-7">[7]</a></sup> By 1943, the firm employed 5,000 <a href="/wiki/Cleveland,_Ohio" class="mw-redirect" title="Cleveland, Ohio">Cleveland, Ohio</a>, residents. After Arthur Parker's death in 1945<sup id="cite_ref-Krupa_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Krupa-10">[10]</a></sup> and the end of the war, the company neared bankruptcy due to the sudden drop in demand. Arthur Parker's wife, Helen Parker, assumed control of the company and prevented its liquidation.<sup id="cite_ref-Salpukas_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Salpukas-11">[11]</a></sup> She hired new management staff and directed the company's focus back to civilian manufacturing.<sup id="cite_ref-Krupa_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Krupa-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span id="1950s.E2.80.931960s"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="1950s–1960s">1950s–1960s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Parker_Hannifin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: 1950s–1960s" data-section="3" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>In the early 1950s, the firm's executives set a goal to make Parker, as <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times" title="The New York Times">The New York Times</a></i> put it, "the <a href="/wiki/General_Electric" title="General Electric">General Electric</a> of <a href="/wiki/Hydraulics" title="Hydraulics">fluid power</a>", a goal it generally achieved in the coming decades.<sup id="cite_ref-Salpukas_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Salpukas-11">[11]</a></sup> In 1957, the company purchased Hannifin, a producer of valve and cylinder products, and changed its name to Parker Hannifin.<sup id="cite_ref-Krupa_10-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Krupa-10">[10]</a></sup> Many more acquisitions followed, with the company reaching 40 acquisitions by the year 1979.<sup id="cite_ref-Salpukas_11-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Salpukas-11">[11]</a></sup></p><p>In 1953, Arthur Parker's son <a href="/wiki/Patrick_S._Parker" title="Patrick S. Parker">Patrick S. Parker</a> began working full-time at the company.<sup id="cite_ref-Sloane_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sloane-12">[12]</a></sup> He rose to become its president in 1968, and  served as CEO from 1971 to 1983 and as chairman from 1977 to 1999. During and after his tenure, the firm grew dramatically, with revenues rising from $197 million in 1968 to over $7 billion in 2005.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p><p>The company debuted on the <a href="/wiki/New_York_Stock_Exchange" title="New York Stock Exchange">New York Stock Exchange</a> in 1964, under the ticker symbol PH.<sup id="cite_ref-CWRU_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CWRU-14">[14]</a></sup> In 1966, the company joined the Fortune 500.<sup id="cite_ref-Vault_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Vault-8">[8]</a></sup> The company designed parts for the craft used in <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA's</a> first manned <a href="/wiki/Apollo_11" title="Apollo 11">moon landing in 1969</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Alexander_7-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Alexander-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span id="1970s.E2.80.931990s"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="1970s–1990s">1970s–1990s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Parker_Hannifin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: 1970s–1990s" data-section="4" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>An economic downturn in 1970 forced the company to expand beyond its focus on hydraulic systems. In the following years it began to expand into the automotive aftermarket, considered a more stable industry. The company also directed itself toward growth in aerospace, acquiring companies that created flight controls and wheel brake equipment for airplanes. By 1979, Parker Hannifin employed 20,000 people in 100 plants, selling 90,000 items for machinery, airplanes, cars and construction equipment to 60,000 customers.<sup id="cite_ref-Salpukas_11-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Salpukas-11">[11]</a></sup> The company made some of the equipment inside the mechanical shark in the 1975 movie <i><a href="/wiki/Jaws_(film)" title="Jaws (film)">Jaws</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Alexander_7-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Alexander-7">[7]</a></sup></p><p>In 1982, Paul G. Schloemer replaced Patrick Parker as the company's president (although Patrick Parker remained chairman and CEO).<sup id="cite_ref-Sloane_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sloane-12">[12]</a></sup> That same year, the firm entered the Mexican market. By 2008, Parker Hannifin Mexico would come to operate 11 plants in the country, seven of which made parts exclusively for the U.S. market. In 1988, the company reached $2 billion in sales.<sup id="cite_ref-Krupa_10-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Krupa-10">[10]</a></sup></p><p>The firm opened its first retail "ParkerStore" in Cleveland in 1993. Within 10 years, the network of stores expanded to 200 locations in the U.S. and more than 400 worldwide. ParkerStores offer a variety of Parker products, including hydraulics, automation, and hose and fitting components, at locations close to industrial product buyers.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Parker Hannifin systems helped control the massive replica of the <i><a href="/wiki/Titanic_(ship)" class="mw-redirect" title="Titanic (ship)">Titanic</a></i> in the <a href="/wiki/Titanic_(1997_film)" title="Titanic (1997 film)">1997 film of the same name</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Vault_8-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Vault-8">[8]</a></sup> In 1997, the firm moved its headquarters from Cleveland to a new building in <a href="/wiki/Mayfield_Heights" class="mw-redirect" title="Mayfield Heights">Mayfield Heights</a>, a suburb of Cleveland.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> In 1999, the company's sales reached approximately $5 billion.<sup id="cite_ref-Davis_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Davis-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span id="2000s.E2.80.93present"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="2000s–present">2000s–present</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Parker_Hannifin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: 2000s–present" data-section="5" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>Parker Hannifin acquired Commercial Intertech Corporation, a maker of hydraulic systems, in 2000.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> With a cost of $366 million, this was at the time Parker Hannifin's biggest acquisition.<sup id="cite_ref-Davis_18-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Davis-18">[18]</a></sup></p><p>In 2001, CEO Don Washkewicz introduced <a href="/wiki/Lean_startup" title="Lean startup">lean startup</a> methods to company operations and has said that over the decade this reduced the time to obtain price quotes by 60% and cut product development lead times by 25%.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p><p>In 2002 the company appointed Craig Maxwell as head of engineering; Maxwell brought a focus on innovation as well as rigor; he argued for and was given a $20M annual budget to fund blue sky inventions made by engineers and has given engineers time to pursue them; at the same time his team developed software that allows tracking each of the company's 1700 ongoing R&amp;D projects graded by risk and potential reward, and closely managing their progress. In 2011 he hired Ryan Farris out of Vanderbilt University and licensed patents covering a <a href="/wiki/Powered_exoskeleton" title="Powered exoskeleton">powered exoskeleton</a> that Farris had worked on at Vanderbilt.  In 2015 the company opened an internal <a href="/wiki/Business_incubator" title="Business incubator">business incubator</a> that Maxwell had proposed when he was first hired.<sup id="cite_ref-Alexander_7-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Alexander-7">[7]</a></sup></p><p>The company won $2 billion in contracts to build fuel and hydraulic systems for <a href="/wiki/Airbus_A350" class="mw-redirect" title="Airbus A350">Airbus A350</a> airliners in 2008<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> Two years later, its products were used in repairing the <i><a href="/wiki/Deepwater_Horizon" title="Deepwater Horizon">Deepwater Horizon</a></i> oil rig.<sup id="cite_ref-Zerega_6-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Zerega-6">[6]</a></sup></p><p>Thomas Williams took over the CEO role from Washkewicz in 2015.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> In 2016, the completed its largest acquisition to date, buying <a href="/wiki/Clarcor" title="Clarcor">Clarcor</a>, a filtration systems manufacturer, for $4.3 billion.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(2)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Aerospace">Aerospace</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Parker_Hannifin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Aerospace" data-section="6" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-2 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-2">
<p>Parker Hannifin's aerospace division, <b>Parker Aerospace</b>, designs and manufactures aerospace hydraulic equipment. It is a provider of aircraft parts to aircraft manufacturers, including fuel system components and high-temperature bleed air valves.<sup id="cite_ref-NDN_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NDN-26">[26]</a></sup> Based in <a href="/wiki/Irvine,_California" title="Irvine, California">Irvine, California</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-LAT_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LAT-27">[27]</a></sup> Parker Aerospace also operates facilities in Arizona and Mexico.<sup id="cite_ref-NDN_26-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NDN-26">[26]</a></sup> The company has had contracts to contribute parts and maintenance for machinery produced by <a href="/wiki/Airbus" title="Airbus">Airbus</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Rolls-Royce_Holdings" title="Rolls-Royce Holdings">Rolls-Royce</a>, <a href="/wiki/Commercial_Aircraft_Corporation_of_China" class="mw-redirect" title="Commercial Aircraft Corporation of China">Commercial Aircraft Corporation of China</a> as well as other manufacturers.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p><p>In 1993, the <a href="/wiki/Federal_Aviation_Administration" title="Federal Aviation Administration">Federal Aviation Administration</a> contracted Parker Aerospace to develop a new monitoring device, the Multi-Sensor Enroute Flight Inspection System, for flight inspection aircraft.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p><p>Notable acquisitions by the division include the <a href="/wiki/Kalamazoo,_Michigan" title="Kalamazoo, Michigan">Kalamazoo, Michigan</a>-based Abex/NWL division of Pneumo Abex in 1996,<sup id="cite_ref-LAT_27-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LAT-27">[27]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Naples,_Florida" title="Naples, Florida">Naples, Florida</a>-based Shaw Aero Devices, in 2007.<sup id="cite_ref-NDN_26-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NDN-26">[26]</a></sup> In 2012, the company partnered with <a href="/wiki/General_Electric" title="General Electric">General Electric</a> to form a 50-50 joint venture, Advanced Atomization Technologies, for producing fuel nozzles for commercial aircraft engines.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Boeing_737_accidents_and_incidents">Boeing 737 accidents and incidents</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Parker_Hannifin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Boeing 737 accidents and incidents" data-section="7" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Boeing_737_rudder_issues" title="Boeing 737 rudder issues">Boeing 737 rudder issues</a></div>
<p>In 1995, it was discovered that failures in a servo unit supplied by Parker Hannifin to Boeing for use in their 737 aircraft may have contributed to several accidents and incidents, including the crashes of <a href="/wiki/United_Airlines_Flight_585" title="United Airlines Flight 585">United Airlines Flight 585</a> and <a href="/wiki/USAir_Flight_427" title="USAir Flight 427">USAir Flight 427</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></p><p>In 2004, a Los Angeles jury ordered Parker Hannifin to pay US$43 million to the plaintiff families of the 1997 <a href="/wiki/SilkAir_Flight_185" title="SilkAir Flight 185">SilkAir Flight 185</a> crash in <a href="/wiki/Indonesia" title="Indonesia">Indonesia</a>. Parker Hannifin subsequently appealed the verdict, which resulted in an out-of-court settlement for an undisclosed amount. The Indonesian <a href="/wiki/National_Transportation_Safety_Committee" title="National Transportation Safety Committee">National Transportation Safety Committee</a> (NTSC) could not determine the cause of the crash due to the near total lack of physical evidence because of the complete destruction;<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> The US <a href="/wiki/National_Transportation_Safety_Board" title="National Transportation Safety Board">National Transportation Safety Board</a> (NTSB), however disagreed,  and concluded that the crash was caused, possibly intentionally, by the pilot.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></p><p>The <a href="/wiki/FAA" class="mw-redirect" title="FAA">FAA</a> ordered an upgrade of all Boeing 737 rudder control systems by November 12, 2002.  The firm argued that the components they supplied were not at fault, citing that the product has one of the safest records in its class, but the FAA directive went through regardless.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="F-35_fueldraulic_line_failure">F-35 fueldraulic line failure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Parker_Hannifin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: F-35 fueldraulic line failure" data-section="8" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>On 18 January 2013, the F-35B variant of the <a href="/wiki/Lockheed_Martin_F-35_Lightning_II" title="Lockheed Martin F-35 Lightning II">Lockheed Martin F-35 Lightning II</a> was grounded after the failure of a <a href="/wiki/Fueldraulic" class="mw-redirect" title="Fueldraulic">fueldraulic</a> line in the aircraft's propulsion system that controls the exhaust vectoring system.  This followed an incident two days earlier on 16 January, in which the propulsion system experienced a fueldraulic failure prior to a conventional takeoff.  The precautionary flight suspension was intended to preserve safety while providing time to understand the origin of the failure of the propulsion fueldraulic line.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> The failure was found to be a manufacturing defect by Parker Hannifin's Stratoflex division.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup></p>
