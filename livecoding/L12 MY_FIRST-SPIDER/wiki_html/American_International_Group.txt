</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(1)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=American_International_Group&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History" data-section="1" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-1 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-1">

<h3 class="in-block"><span id="1919.E2.80.931945:_Early_years"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="1919–1945:_Early_years">1919–1945: Early years</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=American_International_Group&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: 1919–1945: Early years" data-section="2" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>AIG was founded December 19, 1919<sup id="cite_ref-loc_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-loc-1">[1]</a></sup> when American <a href="/wiki/Cornelius_Vander_Starr" title="Cornelius Vander Starr">Cornelius Vander Starr</a> (1892-1968) established a general insurance agency, American Asiatic Underwriters (AAU), in Shanghai, China.<sup id="cite_ref-Laing_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Laing-18">[18]</a></sup>  Business grew rapidly, and two years later, Starr formed a life insurance operation.<sup id="cite_ref-BloomberBusinsssWeek_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BloomberBusinsssWeek-19">[19]</a></sup>  By the late 1920s, AAU had branches throughout <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a> and Southeast Asia, including the <a href="/wiki/Philippines" title="Philippines">Philippines</a>, <a href="/wiki/Indonesia" title="Indonesia">Indonesia</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Malaysia" title="Malaysia">Malaysia</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-AIG_Europe_–_History_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AIG_Europe_%E2%80%93_History-20">[20]</a></sup>  In 1926, Mr. Starr opened his first office in the United States, American International Underwriters Corporation (AIU).<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup>  He also focused on opportunities in <a href="/wiki/Latin_America" title="Latin America">Latin America</a> and, in the late 1930s, AIU entered <a href="/wiki/Havana" title="Havana">Havana</a>, Cuba.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> The steady growth of the Latin American agencies proved significant as it would offset the decline in business from Asia due to the impending World War II.<sup id="cite_ref-Laing_18-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Laing-18">[18]</a></sup> In 1939, Mr. Starr moved his headquarters from Shanghai, China, to New York City.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span id="1945.E2.80.931959:_International_and_domestic_expansion"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="1945–1959:_International_and_domestic_expansion">1945–1959: International and domestic expansion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=American_International_Group&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: 1945–1959: International and domestic expansion" data-section="3" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>After World War II, American International Underwriters (AIU) entered Japan<sup id="cite_ref-Laing_18-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Laing-18">[18]</a></sup> and Germany,<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> to provide insurance for American military personnel. Throughout the late 1940s and early 1950s, AIU continued to expand in Europe, with offices opening in France, Italy,<sup id="cite_ref-AIG_Europe_–_History_20-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AIG_Europe_%E2%80%93_History-20">[20]</a></sup> and the United Kingdom.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> In 1952, Mr. Starr began to focus on the American market by acquiring Globe &amp; Rutgers Fire Insurance Company and its subsidiary, American Home Fire Assurance Company.<sup id="cite_ref-Fallen_Giant_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fallen_Giant-27">[27]</a></sup>  By the end of the decade, C.V. Starr's general and life insurance organization included an extensive network of agents and offices in over 75 countries.<sup id="cite_ref-Fallen_Giant_27-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fallen_Giant-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span id="1959.E2.80.931979:_Reorganization_and_specialization"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="1959–1979:_Reorganization_and_specialization">1959–1979: Reorganization and specialization</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=American_International_Group&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: 1959–1979: Reorganization and specialization" data-section="4" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>In 1960, C.V. Starr hired <a href="/wiki/Maurice_R._Greenberg" title="Maurice R. Greenberg">Maurice R. Greenberg</a> to develop an international accident and health business.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> Two years later, Mr. Greenberg reorganized one of C.V. Starr's U.S. holdings into a successful multiple line carrier.<sup id="cite_ref-Fallen_Giant_27-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fallen_Giant-27">[27]</a></sup> Greenberg focused on selling insurance through independent brokers rather than agents to eliminate agent salaries. Using brokers, AIU could price insurance according to its potential return even if it suffered decreased sales of certain products for great lengths of time with very little extra expense. In 1967, American International Group, Inc. (AIG) was incorporated as a unifying umbrella organization for most of C.V. Starr's general and life insurance businesses.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup>  In 1968, Starr named Greenberg his successor. The company went public in 1969.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:AIG_Headquarters.jpg" class="image"><noscript><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7f/AIG_Headquarters.jpg/220px-AIG_Headquarters.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="330" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="3708" data-file-height="5562"></noscript><span class="lazy-image-placeholder" style="width: 220px;height: 330px;" data-src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7f/AIG_Headquarters.jpg/220px-AIG_Headquarters.jpg" data-alt="" data-width="220" data-height="330" data-class="thumbimage"> </span></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:AIG_Headquarters.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>AIG Headquarters in New York</div></div></div>
<p>The 1970s presented many challenges for AIG as operations in the Middle East and Southeast Asia were curtailed or ceased altogether due to the changing political landscape. However, AIG continued to expand its markets by introducing specialized energy, transportation, and shipping products to serve the needs of niche industries.<sup id="cite_ref-The_AIG_Story_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-The_AIG_Story-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span id="1979.E2.80.932000:_New_opportunities_and_directions"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="1979–2000:_New_opportunities_and_directions">1979–2000: New opportunities and directions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=American_International_Group&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: 1979–2000: New opportunities and directions" data-section="5" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>During the 1980s, AIG continued expanding its market distribution and worldwide network by offering a wide range of specialized products, including pollution liability<sup id="cite_ref-The_AIG_Story_31-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-The_AIG_Story-31">[31]</a></sup> and political risk.<sup id="cite_ref-The_AIG_Story_31-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-The_AIG_Story-31">[31]</a></sup>  In 1984, AIG listed its shares on the New York Stock Exchange (NYSE).<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup>  Throughout the 1990s, AIG developed new sources of income through diverse investments, including the acquisition of International Lease Finance Corporation (ILFC), a provider of leased aircraft to the airline industry.<sup id="cite_ref-Laing_18-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Laing-18">[18]</a></sup>  In 1992, AIG received the first foreign insurance license granted in over 40 years by the Chinese government. Within the U.S., AIG acquired SunAmerica Inc. a retirement savings company managed by <a href="/wiki/Eli_Broad" title="Eli Broad">Eli Broad</a>, in 1999.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span id="2000.E2.80.932012:_Further_expansion_and_decline"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="2000–2012:_Further_expansion_and_decline">2000–2012: Further expansion and decline</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=American_International_Group&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: 2000–2012: Further expansion and decline" data-section="6" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Los_Angeles_Valley,_Warner_Center,_AIG_Towers.jpg" class="image"><noscript><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ea/Los_Angeles_Valley%2C_Warner_Center%2C_AIG_Towers.jpg/220px-Los_Angeles_Valley%2C_Warner_Center%2C_AIG_Towers.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="100" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="2222" data-file-height="1010"></noscript><span class="lazy-image-placeholder" style="width: 220px;height: 100px;" data-src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ea/Los_Angeles_Valley%2C_Warner_Center%2C_AIG_Towers.jpg/220px-Los_Angeles_Valley%2C_Warner_Center%2C_AIG_Towers.jpg" data-alt="" data-width="220" data-height="100" data-class="thumbimage"> </span></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Los_Angeles_Valley,_Warner_Center,_AIG_Towers.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The AIG Towers of <a href="/wiki/Woodland_Hills,_Los_Angeles" title="Woodland Hills, Los Angeles">Woodland Hills, Los Angeles</a>.</div></div></div>
<h4 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Growth">Growth</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=American_International_Group&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Growth" data-section="7" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h4>
<p>The early 2000s saw a marked period of growth as AIG acquired American General Corporation, a leading domestic life insurance and annuities provider,<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> and AIG entered new markets including India.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> In February 2000, AIG created a strategic advisory venture team with the <a href="/wiki/Blackstone_Group" class="mw-redirect" title="Blackstone Group">Blackstone Group</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kissinger_Associates" title="Kissinger Associates">Kissinger Associates</a> "to provide financial advisory services to corporations seeking high level independent strategic advice".<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> AIG was an investor in Blackstone from 1998 to March 2012, when it sold all of its shares in the company. Blackstone acted as an adviser for AIG during the 2007-2008 financial crisis.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></p><p>In March 2003 American General merged with Old Line Life Insurance Company.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></p><p>In the early 2000s, AIG made significant investments in Russia as the country recovered from its <a href="/wiki/1998_Russian_financial_crisis" title="1998 Russian financial crisis">financial crisis</a>. In July 2003, Maurice Greenberg met with Putin to discuss AIG's investments and improving U.S.-Russia economic ties, in anticipation of Putin's meeting with U.S. President George W. Bush later that year."<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup></p><p>In November 2004, AIG reached a US$126 million settlement with the <a href="/wiki/U.S._Securities_and_Exchange_Commission" title="U.S. Securities and Exchange Commission">U.S. Securities and Exchange Commission</a> and the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Justice" title="United States Department of Justice">Justice Department</a> partly resolving a number of regulatory matters, but the company must still cooperate with investigators continuing to probe the sale of a non-traditional insurance product.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup></p>
<h4 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Accounting_scandal">Accounting scandal</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=American_International_Group&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Accounting scandal" data-section="8" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h4>
<p>In 2005, AIG became embroiled in a series of fraud investigations conducted by the <a href="/wiki/U.S._Securities_and_Exchange_Commission" title="U.S. Securities and Exchange Commission">Securities and Exchange Commission</a>, <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Justice" title="United States Department of Justice">U.S. Justice Department</a>, and <a href="/wiki/New_York_State_Attorney_General" class="mw-redirect" title="New York State Attorney General">New York State Attorney General</a>'s Office. Greenberg was ousted amid an accounting scandal in February 2005.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> The New York Attorney General's investigation led to a $1.6 billion fine for AIG and criminal charges for some of its executives.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup></p><p>On May 1, 2005, investigations conducted by outside counsel at the request of AIG's Audit Committee and the consultation with AIG's independent auditors, PricewaterhouseCoopers LLP resulted in AIG's decision to restate its financial statements for the years ended December 31, 2003, 2002, 2001 and 2000, the quarters ended March 31, June 30 and September 30, 2004 and 2003 and the quarter ended December 31, 2003.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup>
On November 9, 2005, the company was said to have delayed its third-quarter earnings report because it had to restate earlier financial results, to correct accounting errors.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup></p>
<h4 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Expansion_to_the_credit_default_insurance_market">Expansion to the credit default insurance market</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=American_International_Group&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Expansion to the credit default insurance market" data-section="9" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h4>
<p><a href="/wiki/Martin_J._Sullivan" title="Martin J. Sullivan">Martin J. Sullivan</a> became CEO of the company in 2005. He began his career at AIG as a clerk in its London office in 1970.<sup id="cite_ref-NYT-Andrews-2008-09-16_47-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NYT-Andrews-2008-09-16-47">[47]</a></sup> AIG then took on tens of billions of dollars of risk associated with mortgages. It insured tens of billions of dollars of derivatives against default, but did not purchase reinsurance to hedge that risk. Secondly, it used collateral on deposit to buy mortgage-backed securities. When losses hit the mortgage market in 2007-2008, AIG had to pay out insurance claims and also replace the losses in its collateral accounts.<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup></p><p>AIG purchased the remaining 39% that it did not own of online auto insurance specialist <a href="/wiki/21st_Century_Insurance" title="21st Century Insurance">21st Century Insurance</a> in 2007 for $749 million.<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> With the failure of the parent company and the continuing recession in late 2008, AIG rebranded its insurance unit to 21st Century Insurance.<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup></p><p>On June 11, 2008, three stockholders, collectively owning 4% of the outstanding stock of AIG, delivered a letter to the Board of Directors of AIG seeking to oust CEO Martin Sullivan and make certain other management and Board of Directors changes. This letter was the latest volley in what <i><a href="/wiki/The_Wall_Street_Journal" title="The Wall Street Journal">The Wall Street Journal</a></i> called a "public spat" between the company's board and management, on the one hand, and its key stockholders, and former CEO Maurice Greenberg on the other hand.<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup></p><p>On June 15, 2008, after disclosure of financial losses and subsequent to a falling stock price, Sullivan resigned and was replaced by <a href="/wiki/Robert_B._Willumstad" title="Robert B. Willumstad">Robert B. Willumstad</a>, Chairman of the AIG Board of Directors since 2006. Willumstad was forced by the US government to step down and was replaced by <a href="/wiki/Ed_Liddy" title="Ed Liddy">Ed Liddy</a> on September 17, 2008.<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup> AIG's board of directors named <a href="/wiki/Bob_Benmosche" title="Bob Benmosche">Bob Benmosche</a> CEO on August 3, 2009, to replace Liddy, who earlier in the year announced his retirement.<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup></p>
<h4 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="2008_Liquidity_crisis_and_government_bailout">2008 Liquidity crisis and government bailout</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=American_International_Group&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: 2008 Liquidity crisis and government bailout" data-section="10" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h4>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Subprime_mortgage_crisis" title="Subprime mortgage crisis">Subprime mortgage crisis</a> and <a href="/wiki/Financial_crisis_of_2007%E2%80%9308" title="Financial crisis of 2007–08">Financial crisis of 2007–08</a></div>
<p>In late 2008, the federal government bailed out AIG for $180 billion, and technically assumed control, because many believed its failure would endanger the financial integrity of other major firms that were its trading partners--<a href="/wiki/Goldman_Sachs" title="Goldman Sachs">Goldman Sachs</a>, <a href="/wiki/Morgan_Stanley" title="Morgan Stanley">Morgan Stanley</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bank_of_America" title="Bank of America">Bank of America</a> and <a href="/wiki/Merrill_Lynch" class="mw-redirect" title="Merrill Lynch">Merrill Lynch</a>, as well as dozens of European banks.<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup> In January 2011, the Financial Crisis Inquiry Commission issued one of many critical governmental reports, deciding that AIG failed and was rescued by the government primarily because its enormous sales of credit default swaps were made without putting up the initial collateral, setting aside capital reserves, or hedging its exposure, which one analyst considered a profound failure in corporate governance, particularly its risk management practices.<sup id="cite_ref-Phil_Angelides_2011_352_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Phil_Angelides_2011_352-15">[15]</a></sup> Other analysts believed AIG's failure was possible because of the sweeping deregulation of over-the-counter (OTC) derivatives, including credit default swaps, which effectively eliminated federal and state regulation of these products, including capital and margin requirements that would have lessened the likelihood of AIG's failure.<sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup></p><p>Illustrating the danger of an AIG bankruptcy, a September 17, 2008, <i>CNN Money</i> article stated, "But in AIG's case, the situation is even more serious. The company is much larger and complex than Lehman Brothers and its assets hitting the market all at once would likely cause worldwide chaos and send values plummeting. Experts question whether there are even enough qualified buyers out there to digest the company and its subsidiaries."<sup id="cite_ref-Why_the_Fed_pulled_the_trigger_on_AIG,_CNN_Money,_September_17,_2008_59-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Why_the_Fed_pulled_the_trigger_on_AIG,_CNN_Money,_September_17,_2008-59">[59]</a></sup></p><p>AIG had sold credit protection through its London unit in the form of <a href="/wiki/Credit_default_swap" title="Credit default swap">credit default swaps</a> (CDSs) on <a href="/wiki/Collateralized_debt_obligation" title="Collateralized debt obligation">collateralized debt obligations</a> (CDOs) but by 2008, they had declined in value.<sup id="cite_ref-New_York_Times_60-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-New_York_Times-60">[60]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-61" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup>  AIG's Financial Products division, headed by <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Cassano" title="Joseph Cassano">Joseph Cassano</a> in London, had entered into credit default swaps to insure $441 billion worth of securities originally rated AAA. Of those securities, $57.8 billion were structured debt securities backed by subprime loans.<sup id="cite_ref-New_York_Times_60-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-New_York_Times-60">[60]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-62" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup> As a result, AIG's credit rating was downgraded and it was required to post additional collateral with its trading counter-parties, leading to a liquidity crisis that began on September 16, 2008, and essentially bankrupted all of AIG. The New York United States Federal Reserve Bank (led by <a href="/wiki/Timothy_Geithner" title="Timothy Geithner">Timothy Geithner</a> who would later become Treasury secretary) stepped in, announcing creation of a secured credit facility, initially of up to US$85 billion to prevent the company's collapse, enabling AIG to deliver additional collateral to its credit default swap trading partners. The credit facility was secured by the stock in AIG-owned subsidiaries in the form of warrants for a 79.9% equity stake in the company and the right to suspend dividends to previously issued common and preferred stock.<sup id="cite_ref-63" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-64" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-65" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup> The AIG board accepted the terms of the Federal Reserve rescue package that same day, making it the largest government bailout of a private company in U.S. history.<sup id="cite_ref-66" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-67" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup></p><p>On March 17, 2009, AIG compounded public cynicism concerning the "too big to fail" firm's bailout by announcing that it would pay its executives over $165 million in executive bonuses.<sup id="cite_ref-68" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template noprint noexcerpt Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:NOTRS" class="mw-redirect" title="Wikipedia:NOTRS"><span title="This claim needs references to better sources. (September 2019)">better source needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Total bonuses for the financial unit could reach $450 million, and bonuses for the entire company could reach $1.2 billion. Newly installed President Barack Obama, who had voted for <a href="/wiki/Troubled_Asset_Relief_Program" title="Troubled Asset Relief Program">TARP</a> as a Senator<sup id="cite_ref-69" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup> responded to the planned payments by saying "[I]t's hard to understand how derivative traders at AIG warranted any bonuses, much less $165 million in extra pay. How do they justify this outrage to the taxpayers who are keeping the company afloat?"<sup id="cite_ref-70" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup> Both Democratic and Republican politicians reacted with similar outrage to the planned bonuses, as did political commentators and journalists in the <a href="/wiki/AIG_bonus_payments_controversy" title="AIG bonus payments controversy">AIG bonus payments controversy</a>.
</p><p>AIG began selling some assets to pay off its government loans in September 2008 despite a global decline in the valuation of insurance businesses, and the weakening financial condition of potential bidders.<sup id="cite_ref-71" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup> In December 2009, AIG formed international life insurance subsidiaries, <a href="/wiki/American_International_Assurance_Company,_Limited" class="mw-redirect" title="American International Assurance Company, Limited">American International Assurance Company, Limited</a> (AIA) and <a href="/wiki/American_Life_Insurance_Company" class="mw-redirect" title="American Life Insurance Company">American Life Insurance Company</a> (ALICO) which were transferred to the <a href="/wiki/Federal_Reserve_Bank_of_New_York" title="Federal Reserve Bank of New York">Federal Reserve Bank of New York</a> to reduce its debt by US$25 billion.<sup id="cite_ref-72" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-72">[72]</a></sup> AIG sold its <a href="/wiki/Hartford_Steam_Boiler_Inspection_and_Insurance_Company" title="Hartford Steam Boiler Inspection and Insurance Company">Hartford Steam Boiler</a> unit on March 31, 2009, to <a href="/wiki/Munich_Re" title="Munich Re">Munich Re</a> for $742 million.<sup id="cite_ref-73" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-73">[73]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-74" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-74">[74]</a></sup> On April 16, 2009, AIG announced plans to sell 21st Century Insurance subsidiary to <a href="/wiki/Farmers_Insurance_Group" title="Farmers Insurance Group">Farmers Insurance Group</a> for $1.9 billion.<sup id="cite_ref-75" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-75">[75]</a></sup> June 10, 2009. AIG sold down its majority ownership of reinsurer Transatlantic Re.<sup id="cite_ref-76" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-76">[76]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/The_Wall_Street_Journal" title="The Wall Street Journal">The Wall Street Journal</a></i> reported on September 7, 2009, that <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Century_Regional_Developments_Limited" class="mw-redirect" title="Pacific Century Regional Developments Limited">Pacific Century Group</a> had agreed to pay $500 million for a part of AIG's asset management business, and that they also expected to pay an additional $200 million to AIG in carried interest and other payments linked to future performance of the business.<sup id="cite_ref-77" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-77">[77]</a></sup></p><p>AIG then sold its American Life Insurance Co. (ALICO) to MetLife Inc. for $15.5 billion in cash and MetLife stock in March 2010.<sup id="cite_ref-78" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-78">[78]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Bloomberg_L.P." title="Bloomberg L.P.">Bloomberg L.P.</a> reported on March 29, 2010, that after almost three months of delays, AIG had completed the $500 million sale of a portion of its asset management business, branded PineBridge Investments, to the Asia-based <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Century_Regional_Developments_Limited" class="mw-redirect" title="Pacific Century Regional Developments Limited">Pacific Century Group</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-79" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-79">[79]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Fortress_Investment_Group" title="Fortress Investment Group">Fortress Investment Group</a> purchased 80% of the interest in financing company <a href="/wiki/Springleaf" class="mw-redirect" title="Springleaf">American General Finance</a> in August 2010.<sup id="cite_ref-80" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-80">[80]</a></sup> In September AIG sold AIG Starr and AIG Edison, two of its Japan-based companies, to <a href="/wiki/Prudential_Financial" title="Prudential Financial">Prudential Financial</a> for $4.2 billion in cash and $600 million in assumption of third party AIG debt by Prudential.<sup id="cite_ref-MarketWatch_81-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MarketWatch-81">[81]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-MarketWatch_81-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MarketWatch-81">[81]</a></sup> On November 1, 2010, AIG raised $36.71 billion from both the sale of ALICO and its IPO of AIA. Proceeds went to pay off FRB of New York loan.<sup id="cite_ref-82" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-82">[82]</a></sup></p><p>In October 2010, <i>The Wall Street Journal</i> reported that the Tomlinson family was allowed to join a civil suit involving AIG for its alleged complicity in a '<a href="/wiki/Stranger-originated_life_insurance" title="Stranger-originated life insurance">stranger-originated life insurance</a>' scheme.<sup id="cite_ref-Scismm_83-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Scismm-83">[83]</a></sup> The case involved <a href="/wiki/JB_Carlson" title="JB Carlson">JB Carlson</a> and Germaine Tomlinson, and alleged that AIG managers allowed Carlson to take out life insurance policies against Tomlinson without an <a href="/wiki/Insurable_interest" title="Insurable interest">insurable interest</a>, which some labeled a "death bet".<sup id="cite_ref-Scismm_83-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Scismm-83">[83]</a></sup> AIG initially filed a suit against Carlson in 2008 to invalidate the claim altogether, arguing that it was filed fraudulently.<sup id="cite_ref-McLaughlin_84-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-McLaughlin-84">[84]</a></sup> The case was settled and dismissed with prejudice by U.S. District Court Judge Sarah Evans Barker on May 25, 2011<sup id="cite_ref-McLaughlin_84-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-McLaughlin-84">[84]</a></sup></p><p>AIG sold its Taiwanese life insurance company Nan Shan Life to a consortium of buyers for $2.16 billion in January 2011.<sup id="cite_ref-85" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-85">[85]</a></sup> Due to the Q3 2011 net loss widening, on November 3, 2011, AIG shares plunged 49 percent year to date. The insurer's board approved a share buyback of as much as $1 billion.<sup id="cite_ref-86" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-86">[86]</a></sup></p><p>Nine years after the initial bailout, in 2017, the U.S. Financial Stability Oversight Council removed AIG from its list of too-big-to-fail institutions.<sup id="cite_ref-87" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-87">[87]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span id="2012.E2.80.93present:_Modern_era"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="2012–present:_Modern_era">2012–present: Modern era</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=American_International_Group&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: 2012–present: Modern era" data-section="11" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_the_Treasury" title="United States Department of the Treasury">United States Department of the Treasury</a> announced an offering of 188.5 million shares of AIG for a total of $5.8 billion on May 7, 2012. The sale reduced Treasury's stake in AIG to 61 percent, from 70 percent before the transaction.<sup id="cite_ref-88" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-88">[88]</a></sup> Four months later, on September 6, 2012, AIG sold $2 billion of its investment in AIA to repay government loans. The board also approved a $5 billion stock repurchase of government-owned shares in AIA.<sup id="cite_ref-89" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-89">[89]</a></sup> The next week, on September 14, 2012, the Department of Treasury completed its fifth sale of AIG common stock, with proceeds of approximately $20.7 billion, reducing the Treasury's ownership stake in AIG to approximately 15.9 percent from 53 percent. Government commitments were fully recovered, and Treasury and the FRBNY to date had received a combined positive return of approximately $15.1 billion.<sup id="cite_ref-90" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-90">[90]</a></sup></p><p>On October 12, 2012, AIG announced a five and a half year agreement to sponsor six New Zealand-based rugby teams, including world champion All Blacks. The AIG logo and the Adidas logo, the league's primary sponsor, were displayed on the league's team jerseys.<sup id="cite_ref-91" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-91">[91]</a></sup></p><p>The U.S. Department of the Treasury in December 2012 published an itemized list of the loans, stock purchases, special purpose vehicles (SPVs) and other investments engaged in with AIG, the amount AIG paid back and the positive return on the loans and investments to the government.<sup id="cite_ref-92" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-92">[92]</a></sup> The Treasury said that it and the Federal Reserve Bank of New York provided a total $182.3 billion to AIG, which paid back a total $205 billion, for a total positive return, or profit, to the government of $22.7 billion. In addition, AIG sold off a number of its own assets to raise money to pay back the government. On December 14, 2012, the Treasury Department sold the last of its AIG stock in its sixth stock sale for a total of approximately $7.6 billion. In total, the Treasury Department realized a gain of more than $22 billion from the sale of AIG common stock and $0.9 billion from the sale of AIG preferred stock.<sup id="cite_ref-finalsales_17-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-finalsales-17">[17]</a></sup> The same month, Robert Benmosche announced that he would be stepping down from his position as President and CEO due to his advancing lung cancer.<sup id="cite_ref-93" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-93">[93]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-94" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-94">[94]</a></sup></p><p>AIG began an advertising campaign on January 1, 2013, called "Thank You America," in which several company employees, including AIG President and CEO Robert Benmosche, talked directly to the camera and offered their thanks for the government assistance.<sup id="cite_ref-95" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-95">[95]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Peter_Hancock_(businessman)" title="Peter Hancock (businessman)">Peter Hancock</a> succeeded Benmosche as President and CEO of AIG in September 2014.<sup id="cite_ref-Russolillo_96-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Russolillo-96">[96]</a></sup> While Benmosche stayed on in an advisory role,<sup id="cite_ref-Russolillo_96-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Russolillo-96">[96]</a></sup> he died in February the following year.<sup id="cite_ref-Kandell_97-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kandell-97">[97]</a></sup></p><p>In June 2015, <a href="/wiki/Taiwan" title="Taiwan">Taiwan</a>'s Nan Shan Life Insurance acquired a stake in AIG's subsidiary in Taiwan for a fee of $158 million.<sup id="cite_ref-98" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-98">[98]</a></sup> Later that year, activist investor <a href="/wiki/Carl_Icahn" title="Carl Icahn">Carl Icahn</a> called for a breakup of AIG, describing the company as "too big to succeed".<sup id="cite_ref-Nasr_99-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Nasr-99">[99]</a></sup> AIG announced plans for an <a href="/wiki/Initial_public_offering" title="Initial public offering">initial public offering</a> of 19.9 percent of United Guaranty Corp., a <a href="/wiki/Greensboro,_North_Carolina" title="Greensboro, North Carolina">Greensboro, North Carolina</a>-based provider of <a href="/wiki/Lenders_mortgage_insurance" title="Lenders mortgage insurance">mortgage insurance for lenders</a> in January 2016.<sup id="cite_ref-100" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-100">[100]</a></sup> Later that year, Icahn won a seat on the board of directors and continued to pressure the company to split up its major divisions.<sup id="cite_ref-Basak_101-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Basak-101">[101]</a></sup> AIG also began a <a href="/wiki/Joint_venture" title="Joint venture">joint venture</a> with <a href="/wiki/Brian_Duperreault#Hamilton_Insurance_Group" title="Brian Duperreault">Hamilton Insurance Group</a> and Two Sigma Investments to serve the insurance needs of small- to medium-sized enterprises. Industry veteran <a href="/wiki/Brian_Duperreault" title="Brian Duperreault">Brian Duperreault</a> became the chairman of the new entity, and Richard Friesenhahn, the executive vice president of U.S. casualty lines at AIG, became CEO.<sup id="cite_ref-102" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-102">[102]</a></sup> In August 2016, AIG sold off United Guaranty, its mortgage-guarantee unit, to <a href="/wiki/Arch_Capital_Group" title="Arch Capital Group">Arch Capital Group</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Bermuda" title="Bermuda">Bermuda</a>-based insurer, for $3.4 billion.<sup id="cite_ref-103" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-103">[103]</a></sup></p><p>Brian Duperreault was appointed CEO of AIG on May 15, 2017.<sup id="cite_ref-Scism_9-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Scism-9">[9]</a></sup> That September, the company reorganized into three segments, comprising a general insurance unit, a life and retirement unit, and a stand-alone technology-focused unit called Blackboard Insurance, led by Seraina Macia.<sup id="cite_ref-Barlyn_8-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Barlyn-8">[8]</a></sup> In January 2020, AIG announced it was ending its decade-long sponsorship of the All Blacks in 2021.<sup id="cite_ref-media_104-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-media-104">[104]</a></sup></p>

<p>In Australia and China, AIG is identified as a large financial institution and provider of financial services including credit security mechanisms. 
</p><p>In the United States, AIG is the largest <a href="/wiki/Underwriting" title="Underwriting">underwriter</a> of commercial and industrial insurance.<sup id="cite_ref-105" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-105">[105]</a></sup></p><p>AIG offers property casualty insurance, life insurance, retirement products, mortgage insurance and other financial services.<sup id="cite_ref-AIG_2012_Annual_Report_106-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AIG_2012_Annual_Report-106">[106]</a></sup> In the third quarter of 2012, the global property-and-casualty insurance business, Chartis, was renamed AIG Property Casualty.  SunAmerica, life-insurance and retirement-services division, was renamed AIG Life and Retirement, other existing brands continue to be used in certain geographies and market segments.<sup id="cite_ref-AIG_2012_Annual_Report_106-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AIG_2012_Annual_Report-106">[106]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Wall_Street_Journal_107-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wall_Street_Journal-107">[107]</a></sup></p>

<ul><li><i><b>Starr International Co v U.S. Federal Circuit Court of Appeals</b></i></li></ul><p>Hank Greenberg, with lead lawyer David Boies, independently sued the U.S. Government for money damages in the United States Court of Federal Claims in 2011.<sup id="cite_ref-110" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-110">[110]</a></sup> The AIG board had announced on January 9 that the company would not join the lawsuit.<sup id="cite_ref-111" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-111">[111]</a></sup> After hearing thirty-seven days of testimony from Ben Bernanke, Timothy Geithner, Hank Paulson and others,<sup id="cite_ref-112" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-112">[112]</a></sup> Judge Thomas C. Wheeler ruled that the Federal Reserve payment to AIG had been an illegal exaction, as the <a href="/wiki/Federal_Reserve_Act" title="Federal Reserve Act">Federal Reserve Act</a> did not authorize the New York Fed to nationalize a corporation by owning its stock.<sup id="cite_ref-Source_Name_1_16-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Source_Name_1-16">[16]</a></sup> Judge Wheeler did not award compensation to the plaintiffs, ruling that they did not suffer economic damage because "if the government had done nothing, the shareholders would have been left with 100 percent of nothing."<sup id="cite_ref-113" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-113">[113]</a></sup></p><p>Greenberg and the U.S. Government appealed to the Court of Appeals for the Federal Circuit, which ruled that Greenberg had no legal right to challenge the bailout because that right belonged to AIG, which in this case, chose not to sue.<sup id="cite_ref-114" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-114">[114]</a></sup></p>
<ul><li><i><b>AIG Inc. et al. v. Maiden Lane II LLC</b></i></li></ul><p>AIG filed suit against the New York Federal Reserve in January 2013 in order to maintain the former's right to sue Bank of America and other issuers of bad mortgage debt.<sup id="cite_ref-Stempel_BI_115-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Stempel_BI-115">[115]</a></sup> The specific issue was whether the New York Federal Reserve transferred $18 billion in litigation claims on troubled mortgage debt through Maiden Lane Transactions, entities created by the Fed in 2008. This transaction, AIG argued, prevented them from recouping losses from insured banks. Owing to events which allowed AIG to proceed in another related case (see below), AIG withdrew the Maiden Lane case "without prejudice" on May 28, 2013.<sup id="cite_ref-Stempel_116-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Stempel-116">[116]</a></sup></p>
<ul><li><i><b>AIG Inc. v. Bank of America Corporation LLC</b></i></li></ul><p>On May 7, 2013, Los Angeles U.S. District Judge, Mariana Pfaelzer, ruled in a case between AIG and Bank of America concerning possible misrepresentations by Merrill Lynch and Countrywide as to the quality of the mortgage portfolio, that $7.3 billion of the disputed claims had not been assigned.<sup id="cite_ref-Stempel_116-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Stempel-116">[116]</a></sup> The two parties settled in July 2014, with Bank of America paying out $650 million to AIG, who in turn dismissed their litigation.<sup id="cite_ref-Freifeld_117-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Freifeld-117">[117]</a></sup></p>
