</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(1)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Unum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History" data-section="1" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-1 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-1">
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Founding_as_Unum_corporation">Founding as Unum corporation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Unum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Founding as Unum corporation" data-section="2" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>Union Mutual Life Insurance was incorporated in 1848 in <a href="/wiki/Maine" title="Maine">Maine</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-journal_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-journal-8">[8]</a></sup> The company issued its first policy, which covered the life of founder and company president Elisha B. Pratt for $5000, on October 1, 1849.<sup id="cite_ref-journal_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-journal-8">[8]</a></sup> Union Mutual's principal office at the time was located in <a href="/wiki/Boston" title="Boston">Boston, Massachusetts</a>, where Pratt resided.<sup id="cite_ref-journal_8-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-journal-8">[8]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-unum_group_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-unum_group-9">[9]</a></sup> Union Mutual remained headquartered in Boston until 1881, when the state of Maine passed a law that required the principal office and headquarters of all insurance companies incorporated by the state to be located within the state.<sup id="cite_ref-journal_8-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-journal-8">[8]</a></sup> The company relocated to <a href="/wiki/Portland,_Maine" title="Portland, Maine">Portland, Maine</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-journal_8-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-journal-8">[8]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-unum_group_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-unum_group-9">[9]</a></sup></p><p>In 1940, the company acquired most of Massachusetts Accident Company's health and accident insurance business.<sup id="cite_ref-unum_group_9-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-unum_group-9">[9]</a></sup> The acquisition was Union Mutual's first expansion beyond individual life and <a href="/wiki/Endowment_policy" title="Endowment policy">endowment insurance</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-unum_group_9-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-unum_group-9">[9]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-pp1998_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pp1998-10">[10]</a></sup> In 1969, Union Mutual established the insurance industry's first downstream <a href="/wiki/Holding_company" title="Holding company">holding company</a>, which facilitated the company's continued expansion and diversification.<sup id="cite_ref-brenner_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-brenner-11">[11]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Union Mutual also established its group <a href="/wiki/Disability_insurance" title="Disability insurance">disability</a> business in the 1960s.<sup id="cite_ref-unum_group_9-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-unum_group-9">[9]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-pp1998_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pp1998-10">[10]</a></sup> Group disability would become the company's flagship product.<sup id="cite_ref-pp98_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pp98-13">[13]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p><p>Union Mutual became the first major <a href="/wiki/Mutual_insurance" title="Mutual insurance">mutual insurance</a> company to demutualize in 1986.<sup id="cite_ref-unum_group_9-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-unum_group-9">[9]</a></sup> Company CEO Colin Hampton had been pushing for demutualization since 1970 and the company formally began the process of converting to a publicly held company in January 1985.<sup id="cite_ref-unum_group_9-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-unum_group-9">[9]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Union Mutual, now renamed Unum, began trading on the <a href="/wiki/New_York_Stock_Exchange" title="New York Stock Exchange">New York Stock Exchange</a> in November 1986.<sup id="cite_ref-brenner_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-brenner-11">[11]</a></sup></p><p>The company also began selling off underperforming businesses during the 1980s.<sup id="cite_ref-unum_group_9-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-unum_group-9">[9]</a></sup> From 1982 to 1990, Unum abandoned many insurance products, including medical insurance, individual life insurance, general investment contracts, and individual annuities and pensions.<sup id="cite_ref-unum_group_9-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-unum_group-9">[9]</a></sup> Under the leadership of then-CEO James Orr, the company turned its focus to long-term group disability insurance.<sup id="cite_ref-unum_group_9-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-unum_group-9">[9]</a></sup></p><p>In March 1990, Unum acquired National Employers Life Assurance Holdings, which at the time was the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom's</a> largest disability insurer.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> The company later acquired Duncanson &amp; Holt, a <a href="/wiki/Reinsurer" class="mw-redirect" title="Reinsurer">reinsurer</a> and insurance <a href="/wiki/Underwriter" class="mw-redirect" title="Underwriter">underwriter</a> for the accident and health insurance sectors.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> In 1993, Unum acquired the Colonial Companies, parent company of <a href="/wiki/Colonial_Life_%26_Accident_Insurance_Company" title="Colonial Life &amp; Accident Insurance Company">Colonial Life &amp; Accident Insurance Company</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-nyt1992_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nyt1992-18">[18]</a></sup> Colonial focused on individual insurance products, including accident, cancer and life insurance policies.<sup id="cite_ref-nyt1992_18-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nyt1992-18">[18]</a></sup> Unum began offering individual disability policies under the Unum brand once again in 1995.<sup id="cite_ref-unum_group_9-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-unum_group-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Growth_as_the_Provident_companies">Growth as the Provident companies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Unum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Growth as the Provident companies" data-section="3" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>The Provident companies was founded in <a href="/wiki/Chattanooga,_Tennessee" title="Chattanooga, Tennessee">Chattanooga, Tennessee</a> in May 1887 as Mutual Medical Aid and Accident Insurance Company. The company originally specialized in providing both medical-aid and accident insurance to employees in high-risk industries, including miners and coal workers.<sup id="cite_ref-tennessee_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-tennessee-19">[19]</a></sup> Within a few months, the company’s founders became concerned that a single disease epidemic would bankrupt the company. Mutual Medical Aid and Accident purchased back all outstanding medical-aid policies and reincorporated as Provident Accident Insurance Company. Provident only issued accident policies.
</p><p>In 1892, Thomas Maclellan and John McMaster purchased a 50% stake in the company for $1000. The two men purchased the remaining 50% equity in 1895 and Maclellan was the company's sole owner and president by 1900.<sup id="cite_ref-tennessee_19-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-tennessee-19">[19]</a></sup> Maclellan reorganized the company from being mutually held to a stock company in 1910.
</p><p>Thomas Maclellan served as president of Provident until he was struck by an automobile and unexpectedly died 1916.<sup id="cite_ref-chattanoogan_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-chattanoogan-20">[20]</a></sup> Robert Maclellan, the son of Thomas, assumed the presidency of Provident following his father's death.<sup id="cite_ref-chattanoogan_20-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-chattanoogan-20">[20]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-hooverprovident_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hooverprovident-21">[21]</a></sup> Provident began selling life insurance the following year, in 1917, and the first life insurance policy issued by the company covered Robert Maclellan's life.<sup id="cite_ref-hooverprovident_21-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hooverprovident-21">[21]</a></sup> The company also began offering railroad insurance.<sup id="cite_ref-hooverprovident_21-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hooverprovident-21">[21]</a></sup></p><p>Provident wrote its first group policy in 1924. The company expanded its operations with its 1926 acquisition of <a href="/wiki/Detroit" title="Detroit">Detroit, Michigan</a>-based Standard Accident Company. Provident operated in 34 states by 1928. It also acquired Southern Surety Company in 1931 and Meridian Insurance of <a href="/wiki/West_Virginia" title="West Virginia">West Virginia</a> in 1938.<sup id="cite_ref-hooverprovident_21-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hooverprovident-21">[21]</a></sup> The company expanded into Canada in 1948.<sup id="cite_ref-hooverprovident_21-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hooverprovident-21">[21]</a></sup></p><p>In November 1993, J. Harold Chandler became president and chief executive officer of Provident.<sup id="cite_ref-wsj1993_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wsj1993-22">[22]</a></sup> Chandler had previously served as a senior executive at <a href="/wiki/NationsBank" title="NationsBank">NationsBank</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-wsj1993_22-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wsj1993-22">[22]</a></sup> Chandler initiated a company-wide restructuring at Provident, which included the 1995 sale of its health-related business to Healthsource for $231 million.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> Provident also increased its focus on individual disability and life policies with the 1997 acquisition of the Paul Revere Corporation, a <a href="/wiki/Worcester,_Massachusetts" title="Worcester, Massachusetts">Worcester, Massachusetts</a>-based provider of individual disability insurance, from <a href="/wiki/Textron" title="Textron">Textron</a> for $1.2 billion.<sup id="cite_ref-mbj1997_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mbj1997-24">[24]</a></sup> The purchase of Paul Revere made Provident the nation's largest provider of individual disability policies.<sup id="cite_ref-mbj1997_24-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mbj1997-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span id="Unum_Group_.281999.E2.80.93present.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Unum_Group_(1999–present)">Unum Group (1999–present)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Unum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Unum Group (1999–present)" data-section="4" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>Unum and Provident announced their intention to merge in November 1998.<sup id="cite_ref-pp98_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pp98-13">[13]</a></sup> When the merger was completed in 1999, the new company, named UnumProvident, was the United States' largest disability insurance provider.<sup id="cite_ref-ctimes1999_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ctimes1999-25">[25]</a></sup> Then-Unum chairman and chief executive James Orr was retained in as chairman and CEO of UnumProvident, which was headquartered in <a href="/wiki/Portland,_Maine" title="Portland, Maine">Portland, Maine</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-ctimes1999_25-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ctimes1999-25">[25]</a></sup></p><p>Company Chief Operating Officer J. Harold Chandler, who had served as Provident's chief executive prior to the merger, succeeded Orr as UnumProvident CEO in November 1999.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> The company sold Provident National Assurance, a holding company for its life insurance and variable annuity business, to <a href="/wiki/Allstate" title="Allstate">Allstate</a> in 2001.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> In 2002, UnumProvident relocated its headquarters from Portland, Maine to <a href="/wiki/Chattanooga,_Tennessee" title="Chattanooga, Tennessee">Chattanooga, Tennessee</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-knoxville_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-knoxville-28">[28]</a></sup> The former Provident Companies was headquartered in Chattanooga.<sup id="cite_ref-knoxville_28-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-knoxville-28">[28]</a></sup></p><p>Unum received negative attention in 2002, when California regulators fined Unum, and alleged that the company inappropriately denied long-term disability insurance claims.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> Unum stated that "only 2% of the policyholders who filed a claim with the company last year [2001] were found not to be disabled, an amount consistent with prior experience."<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p><p>UnumProvident acquired Sun Life Financial's <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>-based group insurance business in 2003.<sup id="cite_ref-brief_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-brief-31">[31]</a></sup> <a href="/w/index.php?title=Thomas_Watjen&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Thomas Watjen (page does not exist)">Thomas Watjen</a> replaced Orr as UnumProvident’s chief executive later that year.<sup id="cite_ref-pp2003_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pp2003-7">[7]</a></sup> The company also sold its Japanese and Argentine businesses.<sup id="cite_ref-brief_31-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-brief-31">[31]</a></sup></p><p>In 2004, Unum entered into a regulatory settlement agreement (RSA) with insurance regulators in over 40 states.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> The settlement related to Unum's handling of disability claims and required the company "to make significant changes in corporate governance, implement revisions to claim procedures and provide for a full re-examination of both reassessed claims and disability insurance claim decisions after the January 2005 effective date of the RSA."<sup id="cite_ref-fednews_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fednews-33">[33]</a></sup>  The review was completed in 2008, and resulted in a 41.7% reversal, "in whole or part", and an additional $676.2 million of benefits paid nationwide by Unum.<sup id="cite_ref-Maine2008_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Maine2008-34">[34]</a></sup> The state of Maine press release announcing the result, praised regulators for their work, and the company for coming into compliance.<sup id="cite_ref-Maine2008_34-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Maine2008-34">[34]</a></sup></p><p>In 2007, UnumProvident was renamed Unum.<sup id="cite_ref-poovey_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-poovey-35">[35]</a></sup> The Unum moniker was last used by the Unum Corporation prior to the 1999 merger.<sup id="cite_ref-poovey_35-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-poovey-35">[35]</a></sup> In 2012 and 2013, Unum partnered with the <a href="/wiki/Consumer_Federation_of_America" title="Consumer Federation of America">Consumer Federation of America</a> to release reports which examined the use of disability insurance by American workers.<sup id="cite_ref-bucks_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bucks-36">[36]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-freepress_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-freepress-37">[37]</a></sup> According to numbers released by the <a href="/wiki/Bureau_of_Labor_Statistics" title="Bureau of Labor Statistics">Bureau of Labor Statistics</a> that were cited by the report, two-thirds of American workers do not carry disability insurance policies.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> In addition, the report found that, among other things, workers know little about disability insurance, despite expecting financial hardship if they were to become unable to work.<sup id="cite_ref-bucks_36-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bucks-36">[36]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-freepress_37-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-freepress-37">[37]</a></sup></p><p>The company was involved with the UK's controversial <a href="/wiki/Welfare_Reform" class="mw-redirect" title="Welfare Reform">Welfare Reform</a> Bill in 2007 and was investigated by the BBC in England at that time.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup></p><p>In 2008 Unum opened their IT centre of excellence in Carlow, Republic of Ireland. 
</p>

<p>Unum is headquartered in Chattanooga, Tennessee.<sup id="cite_ref-sec_1-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sec-1">[1]</a></sup> The company has additional offices in Portland, Maine; <a href="/wiki/Columbia,_South_Carolina" title="Columbia, South Carolina">Columbia, South Carolina</a>; Worcester, Massachusetts; <a href="/wiki/Glendale,_California" title="Glendale, California">Glendale, California</a>;  <a href="/wiki/Milton_Court" title="Milton Court">Milton Court</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dorking" title="Dorking">Dorking</a>, <a href="/wiki/Surrey" title="Surrey">Surrey</a>, England; and at <a href="/wiki/Carlow" title="Carlow">Carlow</a>, <a href="/wiki/County_Carlow" title="County Carlow">Co. Carlow</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-sec_1-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sec-1">[1]</a></sup> Thomas Watjen served as Unum president and chief executive from 2003 until his retirement in 2015. Watjen became Chairman of the Unum Board of Directors on May 21, 2015. He was succeeded as President and CEO by Richard McKenny.
</p><p>Unum is divided into three business segments: Unum US, Unum UK and Colonial Life.<sup id="cite_ref-sec_1-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sec-1">[1]</a></sup> It provides disability insurance, as well as group benefits, life insurance and other services.<sup id="cite_ref-forbes2013_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-forbes2013-5">[5]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup></p>
