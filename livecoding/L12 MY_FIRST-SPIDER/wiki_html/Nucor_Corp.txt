</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(1)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Current_operations">Current operations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nucor&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Current operations" data-section="1" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-1 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-1">
<p>In 2016, the company produced and sold approximately 22 million tons of steel. Nucor operates 23 scrap-based steel production mills.<sup id="cite_ref-10K_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10K-1">[1]</a></sup></p><p>Nucor produces steel bars (carbon and alloy steel), <a href="/wiki/Beams" title="Beams">beams</a>, sheet / flat rolled steel, plate, steel joists, joist girders, steel deck, fabricated concrete reinforcing steel, cold finished steel, steel fasteners, metal building systems, light gauge steel framing, steel grating, expanded metal, and wire mesh. In addition, through its David J. Joseph Company subsidiary, Nucor also brokers <a href="/wiki/Ferrous" title="Ferrous">ferrous</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nonferrous" class="mw-redirect" title="Nonferrous">nonferrous</a> metals, pig iron and HRI/<a href="/wiki/Direct_reduced_iron" title="Direct reduced iron">DRI</a>; supplies ferro-alloys; and processes ferrous and nonferrous scrap.
</p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span id="Nucor.27s_divisions"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Nucor's_divisions">Nucor's divisions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nucor&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Nucor's divisions" data-section="2" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>Nucor operates many subsidiaries that operate independently, including:
</p>
<ul><li><b>Nucor Steel</b> operations are located in Alabama, Arkansas, Illinois, Indiana, Kentucky, Mississippi, Nebraska, North Carolina, Ohio, South Carolina, Tennessee, Texas, Utah and Washington. Nucor's steelmaking operations are based on the mini-mill concept, using <a href="/wiki/Electric_arc_furnace" title="Electric arc furnace">electric arc furnaces</a> and <a href="/wiki/Continuous_casting" title="Continuous casting">continuous casting</a> technologies to recycle steel. The Nucor Steel plant in Louisiana and Nu-Iron in Trinidad, produce direct reduced iron, which is used in electric arc furnaces to supplement iron units in Nucor’s steel recycling operations. Duferdofin is a joint venture between Nucor and Duferco in Italy producing merchant bar.</li>
<li><b>Nucor Cold Finish</b> facilities use <a href="/wiki/Hot_rolled" class="mw-redirect" title="Hot rolled">hot rolled</a> steel bar to produce cold drawn bar using a process known as <a href="/wiki/Drawing_(manufacturing)" title="Drawing (manufacturing)">drawing (manufacturing)</a>. These divisions are located in <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a> (Laurel), <a href="/wiki/Georgia_(U.S._state)" title="Georgia (U.S. state)">Georgia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Missouri" title="Missouri">Missouri</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nebraska" title="Nebraska">Nebraska</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ohio" title="Ohio">Ohio</a>, <a href="/wiki/South_Carolina" title="South Carolina">South Carolina</a>, <a href="/wiki/Utah" title="Utah">Utah</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wisconsin" title="Wisconsin">Wisconsin</a>.</li>
<li><b>Steel joist</b> and <b>engineered building</b> divisions, include Vulcraft (AL, IN, NE, NY, SC, TX, UT, Ontario, Alberta), Nucor Building Systems (IN, SC, TX, UT), American Buildings Company (AL, IL, CA, VA), CBC Steel Buildings (CA), and Kirby Building Systems (TN).</li>
<li><b>Nucor Fastener</b>, based in Indiana, produces carbon and alloy steel standard and special screws, bolts and nuts.</li>
<li><b>Harris Rebar</b> is a leading fabricator, installer and distributor of concrete <a href="/wiki/Reinforcing_steel" class="mw-redirect" title="Reinforcing steel">reinforcing steel</a> and related products. <b>Harris Supply Solutions</b> is a  distributor of steel and concrete products including <a href="/wiki/Rebar" title="Rebar">rebar</a>, Black Rebar, Epoxy Coated Rebar, Fabricated Rebar and Steel Remesh.</li>
<li><b>Skyline Steel</b> is a steel foundation supplier serving the U.S., Canada, Mexico, the Caribbean, Central America, and Colombia markets.</li>
<li>The <b>David J. Joseph Company</b> is a scrap metal brokerage firm, <a href="/wiki/Ferrous_metals" class="mw-redirect" title="Ferrous metals">ferrous</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nonferrous_metal" class="mw-redirect" title="Nonferrous metal">nonferrous metal</a> recycling and transportation provider. DJJ created the <b>U-Pull-&amp;-Pay</b> chain of self-service autoparts stores</li>
<li><b>Nucor Sales</b> has offices in the United States, Colombia, Mexico, Switzerland, and the United Arab Emirates.</li>
<li><b>Nucor Grating</b> is a manufacturer of bar grating products.</li></ul></section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(2)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nucor&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: History" data-section="3" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-2 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-2">
<p>Nucor's history consists of distinct eras: the <a href="/wiki/Reo_Motor_Car" class="mw-redirect" title="Reo Motor Car">Reo Motor Car</a> era, the Nuclear Corporation of America era, the Iverson years (1967–98), the DiMicco era (2000-2012), and the Ferriola era (2013–present).<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Reo_Motor_Car_era">The <a href="/wiki/Reo_Motor_Car" class="mw-redirect" title="Reo Motor Car">Reo Motor Car</a> era</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nucor&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: The Reo Motor Car era" data-section="4" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>Nucor's origins are with auto manufacturer <a href="/wiki/Ransom_E._Olds" title="Ransom E. Olds">Ransom E. Olds</a>, who founded Olds Motor Vehicle Company in 1897 (later, as <a href="/wiki/Oldsmobile" title="Oldsmobile">Oldsmobile</a>, to become a part of <a href="/wiki/General_Motors_Corporation" class="mw-redirect" title="General Motors Corporation">General Motors Corporation</a>). In 1905, Olds left Oldsmobile and established a new company, <a href="/wiki/REO_Motor_Car_Company" title="REO Motor Car Company">REO Motor Car Company</a>, in <a href="/wiki/Lansing,_Michigan" title="Lansing, Michigan">Lansing, Michigan</a>. Though Olds' products, including the luxurious REO Flying Cloud car and <a href="/wiki/REO_Speed_Wagon" title="REO Speed Wagon">REO Speed Wagon</a> truck, were popular, they were not profitable, and the company filed for <a href="/wiki/Bankruptcy_protection" class="mw-redirect" title="Bankruptcy protection">bankruptcy protection</a> in 1938.<sup id="cite_ref-REO_Motor_Company_Records_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-REO_Motor_Company_Records-5">[5]</a></sup></p><p>After the bankruptcy reorganization, REO exited the car business to concentrate on trucks but continued to suffer losses. In December 1954, REO sold its entire <a href="/wiki/Manufacturing_operations" title="Manufacturing operations">manufacturing operations</a> to <a href="/wiki/Bohn_Aluminum_and_Brass_Corporation" title="Bohn Aluminum and Brass Corporation">Bohn Aluminum and Brass Corporation</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-REO_Motor_Company_Records_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-REO_Motor_Company_Records-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Nuclear_Corporation_era">The Nuclear Corporation era</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nucor&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: The Nuclear Corporation era" data-section="5" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>The company initiated liquidation proceedings after the sale of its operations, but a group of dissident <a href="/wiki/Activist_shareholders" class="mw-redirect" title="Activist shareholders">activist shareholders</a>, noticing the existence of a usable tax loss, successfully challenged the liquidation in a <a href="/wiki/Proxy_fight" title="Proxy fight">proxy fight</a> in September 1955, and forced REO to take over a tiny nuclear services company called Nuclear Consultants, Inc. in a <a href="/wiki/Reverse_takeover" title="Reverse takeover">reverse takeover</a>. The company was renamed "Nuclear Corporation of America Inc.", and relocated to offices in the <a href="/wiki/Empire_State_Building" title="Empire State Building">Empire State Building</a> in <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a>. Nuclear's attempt to recast itself as a nuclear industry services company was unsuccessful, and it followed the example of other companies in the 1950s and 60s and attempted to become a <a href="/wiki/Conglomerate_(company)" title="Conglomerate (company)">conglomerate</a>, moving its headquarters to <a href="/wiki/Phoenix,_Arizona" title="Phoenix, Arizona">Phoenix, Arizona</a>. It made several acquisitions, including Vulcraft Corporation, a steel joist manufacturer located in <a href="/wiki/Florence,_South_Carolina" title="Florence, South Carolina">Florence, South Carolina</a>. Vulcraft had been founded by Sanborn Chase (no relation to <a href="/wiki/Chase_%26_Sanborn_Coffee" class="mw-redirect" title="Chase &amp; Sanborn Coffee">Chase &amp; Sanborn Coffee</a>), who died at an early age, leaving the company to his widow. Nuclear purchased Vulcraft from Chase's widow in 1962 and hired <a href="/wiki/F._Kenneth_Iverson" title="F. Kenneth Iverson">F. Kenneth Iverson</a> as general manager. In March 1965, the company filed for bankruptcy. The Board of Directors fired Nuclear's President (and had to return his <a href="/wiki/Business_jet" title="Business jet">business jet</a> to him), but could not find a replacement. Eventually Samuel Siegel, an accountant with Nuclear and friend of Iverson, who had been looking to leave the company, informed the Board that he would remain with the company under two conditions: Iverson would become President and he (Siegel) would become <a href="/wiki/Chief_Financial_Officer" class="mw-redirect" title="Chief Financial Officer">CFO</a>, conditions the Board accepted.<sup id="cite_ref-companyhistory_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-companyhistory-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span id="The_Nucor_.28Iverson.29_era"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Nucor_(Iverson)_era">The Nucor (Iverson) era</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nucor&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: The Nucor (Iverson) era" data-section="6" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>Iverson and Siegel reorganized Nucor around its only profitable business, the steel fabricator Vulcraft. All other businesses were either sold or liquidated. In 1966, the company moved its headquarters to <a href="/wiki/Charlotte,_North_Carolina" title="Charlotte, North Carolina">Charlotte, North Carolina</a> to be closer to its main Vulcraft plant.<sup id="cite_ref-companyhistory_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-companyhistory-6">[6]</a></sup></p><p>In 1968, unable to get favorable steel prices from American manufacturers and unhappy with the imported steel available at the time, Iverson, a metallurgist by training, decided to extend Nucor vertically into <a href="/wiki/Steelmaking" title="Steelmaking">steelmaking</a> by building its first steel bar mill in <a href="/wiki/Darlington,_South_Carolina" title="Darlington, South Carolina">Darlington, South Carolina</a>. The company chose to purchase an <a href="/wiki/Electric_arc_furnace" title="Electric arc furnace">electric arc furnace</a>, which was far cheaper than the traditional steel blast furnace, with a $6 million <a href="/wiki/Loan" title="Loan">loan</a> secured by all of the company's assets. Production delays and staffing problems caused the stock to drop to pennies but earnings soared in 1971 and 1972.<sup id="cite_ref-companyhistory_6-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-companyhistory-6">[6]</a></sup></p><p>In 1972, the company, recognizing that it was misnamed, adopted its current name. Since that time, Nucor has expanded into other steel products, gained some control of its raw material supply, became a leader in recycling technology, and expanded its market presence. Decrease in cost of production by EAFs and market demand for low price raw material especially rebars, attracted Nucor not only to produce steel for itself but also selling it to companies in vicinity that otherwise were importing steel.
</p><p>In 1988, Nucor became the first minimill to manufacture wide flange beams with a depth of 40". The company opened its building products division that same year.
</p><p>In 1989, Nucor opened a facility in <a href="/wiki/Crawfordsville,_Indiana" title="Crawfordsville, Indiana">Crawfordsville, Indiana</a>, which was the first mini mill to produce flat rolled steel using thin-slab technology.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p><p>In March 2000, a joint venture, owned 47.5% by Nucor, 47.5% by <a href="/wiki/BlueScope" title="BlueScope">BlueScope</a>, and 5% by <a href="/wiki/IHI_Corporation" title="IHI Corporation">IHI Corporation</a> was formed to license Castrip technology. This technology allows for <a href="/wiki/Continuous_casting" title="Continuous casting">continuous casting</a> of sheet steel directly from molten steel without the need for heavy, expensive, and energy-consuming rollers. As a result, mills built with this technology are significantly cheaper and take up significantly less space than other mills.
</p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="DiMicco_era">DiMicco era</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nucor&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: DiMicco era" data-section="7" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>In September 2000, <a href="/wiki/Dan_DiMicco" title="Dan DiMicco">Dan DiMicco</a>, formerly the general manager of the company's highly profitable Nucor-Yamato Steel joint venture, was appointed CEO.<sup id="cite_ref-retire_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-retire-9">[9]</a></sup></p><p>In the years that followed, the company made several acquisitions.
</p><p>In 2001, the company acquired Auburn Steel, its first acquisition in 36 years.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> In 2002, Nucor bought <a href="/wiki/Birmingham_Steel" class="mw-redirect" title="Birmingham Steel">Birmingham Steel</a>, including the <a href="/wiki/Mississippi_Steel" title="Mississippi Steel">Mississippi Steel</a> plant and <a href="/wiki/Birmingham,_Alabama" title="Birmingham, Alabama">Birmingham, Alabama</a>, operations.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p><p>In 2004, Nucor acquired Corus Tuscaloosa.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> and in 2005, Nucor purchased Fort Howard Steel<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> and Marion Steel.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p><p>In 2006, Nucor acquired Connecticut Steel,<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Verco Decking,<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> and Harris Steel.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> The $1.07 billion acquisition of Harris Steel expanded Nucor into production of fabricated <a href="/wiki/Rebar" title="Rebar">rebar</a>, a strong market due to increased spending on infrastructure.
</p><p>In 2007, the company's Nucor Building Systems division acquired Magnatrax, which bolstered its share of the pre-engineered metal building systems market.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p><p>In March 2008, in order to take more control of its raw material supply and prices, Nucor purchased the David J. Joseph Company, one of the largest scrap brokers and recycling companies in the United States.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p><p>In 2012, Nucor purchased Skyline Steel.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Ferriola_era">Ferriola era</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nucor&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Ferriola era" data-section="8" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>Effective January 1, 2013, <a href="/wiki/John_J._Ferriola" title="John J. Ferriola">John J. Ferriola</a> was named as Chief Executive Officer of Nucor and Daniel R. DiMicco was named Executive Chairman.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> DiMicco retired effective January 1, 2014.<sup id="cite_ref-retire_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-retire-9">[9]</a></sup></p><p>In 2014, the company purchased Gallatin Steel Company.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p><p>In 2015, the company acquired additional cold finish bar facilities in Ohio and Georgia.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p><p>In 2016, Nucor announced the acquisition of a steel plate mill in <a href="/wiki/Longview,_Texas" title="Longview, Texas">Longview, Texas</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> and structural tube mills in <a href="/wiki/Mississippi" title="Mississippi">Mississippi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alabama" title="Alabama">Alabama</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Illinois" title="Illinois">Illinois</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> The acquisition of these facilities expanded Nucor's capabilities in the plate market and added structural tube to the company's product mix.
</p><p>In April 2017, the company announced an $85 million upgrade of the rolling mill at its <a href="/wiki/Marion,_Ohio" title="Marion, Ohio">Marion, Ohio</a> rebar and signpost operation.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p><p>In August 2017, the company acquired St. Louis Cold Drawn, Inc.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p><p>On September 6, 2019 Nucor announced that John Ferriola would retire at the end of the year to be replaced by <a href="/w/index.php?title=Leon_J._Topalian&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Leon J. Topalian (page does not exist)">Leon J. Topalian</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>

<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Environmental_record">Environmental record</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nucor&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Environmental record" data-section="10" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>In 2000, Nucor paid $98 million to settle with the <a href="/wiki/U.S._Justice_Department" class="mw-redirect" title="U.S. Justice Department">U.S. Justice Department</a> and the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Environmental_Protection_Agency" title="United States Environmental Protection Agency">United States Environmental Protection Agency</a> to resolve allegations that it had not adequately controlled the emission of toxic chemicals into the air, water, and soil of Alabama, Arkansas, Indiana, Nebraska, South Carolina, Texas, and Utah. The settlement was "the largest and most comprehensive environmental settlement ever with a steel manufacturer."<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p><p>In 2012, as part of a settlement with the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Environmental_Protection_Agency" title="United States Environmental Protection Agency">United States Environmental Protection Agency</a>, the company agreed to reduce air pollution at its plant in Marion, Ohio.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p><p>In 2016, the company unsuccessfully filed a lawsuit to block the Environmental Protection Agency from adopting a plan to control visible pollution in Arkansas.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
