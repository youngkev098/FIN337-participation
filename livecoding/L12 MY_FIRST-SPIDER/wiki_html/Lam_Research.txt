</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(1)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lam_Research&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History" data-section="1" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-1 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-1">
<p>Lam Research was founded in 1980 by David K. Lam, a Chinese-born engineer who had previously worked at <a href="/wiki/Xerox" title="Xerox">Xerox</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hewlett-Packard" title="Hewlett-Packard">Hewlett-Packard</a> and <a href="/wiki/Texas_Instruments" title="Texas Instruments">Texas Instruments</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-semi_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-semi-5">[5]</a></sup>  It was while he was at Hewlett Packard that he saw the need for better plasma etching equipment, to keep up with the rapid miniaturization of semiconductor wafers.<sup id="cite_ref-semi_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-semi-5">[5]</a></sup>  He credited <a href="/wiki/Bob_Noyce" class="mw-redirect" title="Bob Noyce">Bob Noyce</a>, founder of <a href="/wiki/Intel" title="Intel">Intel</a>, for assisting him in getting funding by ensuring his business plan made sense.
</p><p>In 1981, the company introduced its first product, the AutoEtch 480, an automated polysilicon plasma etcher.  The name AutoEtch was chosen to convey that the etcher was automated, while the 80 in 480 came from 1980, the year the company was founded.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>  The first system was sold in January 1982.<sup id="cite_ref-10k-1996_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10k-1996-7">[7]</a></sup> In 1982, Roger Emerick was appointed CEO.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p><p>In May 1984, the company issued an IPO and was listed on NASDAQ, with the symbol LRCX.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> In 1985, David Lam left the company to join Link Technologies, which eventually was bought by Wyse<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> and is now <a href="/wiki/Dell_Wyse" title="Dell Wyse">Dell Wyse</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-funding_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-funding-11">[11]</a></sup> In the mid-1980s, Lam Research continued its global expansion, concentrating on Taiwan and also opening customer support centers throughout Europe, the United States and Japan.<sup id="cite_ref-funding_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-funding-11">[11]</a></sup></p><p>By the early 1990s, the company had a presence in China, Korea, Singapore, and Taiwan.<sup id="cite_ref-funding_11-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-funding-11">[11]</a></sup> In March 1997, the company purchased OnTrak Systems Inc., a chip equipment manufacturer that specialized in <a href="/wiki/Chemical-mechanical_planarization" class="mw-redirect" title="Chemical-mechanical planarization">chemical-mechanical planarization</a> (CMP) cleaning, for $225 Million.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> CMP cleaning is a hybrid process to smooth surfaces using both etching and mechanical polishing. In August 1997, the company named OnTrak's CEO Jim Bagley as its CEO.<sup id="cite_ref-funding_11-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-funding-11">[11]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-bloomberg_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bloomberg-13">[13]</a></sup> In 1998, Bagley was named chairman of the board.<sup id="cite_ref-bloomberg_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bloomberg-13">[13]</a></sup></p><p>In 2005, Steve Newberry was appointed as CEO.<sup id="cite_ref-eetimes1_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-eetimes1-14">[14]</a></sup> In 2006, Lam Research acquired Bullen Semiconductor, now Silfex, Inc.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> In 2008, Lam Research acquired SEZ AG,<sup id="cite_ref-eetimes2_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-eetimes2-16">[16]</a></sup> now Lam Research AG.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> In 2011, Lam Research agreed to buy <a href="/wiki/San_Jose,_California" title="San Jose, California">San Jose, California</a> chip equipment manufacturer <a href="/wiki/Novellus_Systems" title="Novellus Systems">Novellus Systems</a>, for $3.3B.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup>  The deal was completed in June 2012.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> In 2012, Martin Anstice was appointed as CEO.<sup id="cite_ref-eetimes1_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-eetimes1-14">[14]</a></sup> In October 2015, Lam Research announced plans to buy <a href="/wiki/Milpitas,_California" title="Milpitas, California">Milpitas, California</a>-based <a href="/wiki/Wafer_(electronics)" title="Wafer (electronics)">wafer</a> inspection equipment vendor <a href="/wiki/KLA-Tencor" class="mw-redirect" title="KLA-Tencor">KLA-Tencor</a> for $10.6B, in what was viewed as a semiconductor industry consolidation move.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> In June 2016, it was announced that Lam Research had joined the <a href="/wiki/Fortune_500" title="Fortune 500">Fortune 500</a> for the first time.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> In October 2016, the company announced it had terminated its offer for KLA-Tencor amidst concerns that the deal would not meet regulatory approval from the U.S. Department of Justice<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> over antitrust concerns.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p><p>In September 2017, the company announced the acquisition of <a href="/wiki/Cary,_North_Carolina" title="Cary, North Carolina">Cary, North Carolina</a>-based chip simulation firm Coventor.  The company's software reportedly would allow Lam to reduce the time to market for its new chips.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> In November, the company launched Lam Research Capital (Lam Capital), a venture capital group chartered to invest in companies.<sup id="cite_ref-semieng_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-semieng-25">[25]</a></sup></p><p>In January 2018, the company announced that COO Tim Archer was being promoted to president, and CEO Martin Anstice was remaining in the CEO role.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p><p>In December 2018, Martin Anstice resigned as CEO due to allegations of personal misconduct and the Board of Directors of Lam Research named Tim Archer as president and chief executive officer.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> Archer was also appointed to the Board of Directors of Lam Research. Prior to this appointment, Archer was the company's president and chief operating officer.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(2)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Products">Products</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lam_Research&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Products" data-section="2" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-2 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-2">
<p>Lam Research designs and builds products for semiconductor manufacturing, including equipment for <a href="/wiki/Thin_film_deposition" class="mw-redirect" title="Thin film deposition">thin film deposition</a>, <a href="/wiki/Plasma_etch" class="mw-redirect" title="Plasma etch">plasma etch</a>, <a href="/wiki/Photoresist#Applications" title="Photoresist">photoresist strip</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Wafer_(electronics)#Cleaning.2C_texturing_and_etching" title="Wafer (electronics)">wafer cleaning</a> processes.  Repeated throughout semiconductor manufacturing, these technologies help create transistors, interconnects, advanced memory, and packaging structures.  They are also used for applications in related markets like <a href="/wiki/Microelectromechanical_systems" title="Microelectromechanical systems">microelectromechanical systems</a> (MEMS) and <a href="/wiki/Light-emitting_diode" title="Light-emitting diode">light-emitting diodes</a> (LEDs).<sup id="cite_ref-2019-report_1-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2019-report-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Thin_film_deposition">Thin film deposition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lam_Research&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Thin film deposition" data-section="3" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>Lam's thin film deposition systems lay down the sub-microscopic layers of conducting (metal) or insulating (dielectric) materials that make up an integrated circuit. The processes require uniformity at the <a href="/wiki/Nanoscale" class="mw-redirect" title="Nanoscale">nanoscale</a> level.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p><p>The company employs <a href="/wiki/Electroplating" title="Electroplating">electrochemical deposition</a> (ECD) and <a href="/wiki/Chemical_vapor_deposition" title="Chemical vapor deposition">chemical vapor deposition</a> (CVD) technologies to form copper and other metal films for conducting structures.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Atomic_layer_deposition" title="Atomic layer deposition">Atomic layer deposition</a> (ALD) is also used for tungsten metal films in features like <a href="/wiki/Ohmic_contact" title="Ohmic contact">contacts</a> and plugs, which are vertical connections between metal lines in multilevel interconnect chip designs.
</p><p>Plasma-enhanced (PE) CVD and ALD technologies create <a href="/wiki/Dielectric" title="Dielectric">dielectric</a> films for a wide range of insulating parts. For gapfill processes, which require depositing dielectric material into narrow spaces, Lam uses high-density plasma (HDP) CVD technology. PECVD and ALD are also used to form <a href="/wiki/Hardmask" title="Hardmask">hardmasks</a>, layers which can be removed to improve circuit patterning processes.
</p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Plasma_etch">Plasma etch</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lam_Research&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Plasma etch" data-section="4" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>Lam Research uses proprietary technology in its equipment for plasma etch,<sup id="cite_ref-semimd_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-semimd-32">[32]</a></sup> the process of selectively removing materials from the surface of a wafer in order to create the semiconductor device's features and patterns. The equipment helps chip manufacturers carve small features such as those needed for the latest multiple patterning sequences, transistors, and advanced memory structures, which involve increasingly complex <a href="/wiki/Thermodynamics_of_nanostructures#Thin_films" title="Thermodynamics of nanostructures">film stacks</a> and ever <a href="/wiki/Dry_etching#High_aspect_ratio_structures" title="Dry etching">higher aspect ratio structures</a>.
</p><p>The company uses <a href="/wiki/Reactive-ion_etching" title="Reactive-ion etching">reactive ion etch</a> (RIE) and <a href="/wiki/Atomic_layer_etching" title="Atomic layer etching">atomic layer etching</a> (ALE) to shape a variety of conductive and dielectric features.<sup id="cite_ref-semimd_32-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-semimd-32">[32]</a></sup> The company’s <a href="/wiki/Deep_reactive-ion_etching" title="Deep reactive-ion etching">deep RIE</a> technologies help create structures for applications like MEMS and <a href="/wiki/Through-silicon_via" title="Through-silicon via">through-silicon vias</a> (TSVs).
</p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Photoresist_strip">Photoresist strip</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lam_Research&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Photoresist strip" data-section="5" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>Lam’s dry strip systems use plasma technology to selectively remove the photoresist mask following a range of front-end wafer processing and advanced packaging applications.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Wafer_cleaning">Wafer cleaning</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lam_Research&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Wafer cleaning" data-section="6" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>Lam Research's wet spin clean and plasma-based bevel clean products remove particles, residues, and films from the wafer surface before or after adjacent processes.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></p><p>The company’s spin wet <a href="/wiki/Clean_technology" title="Clean technology">clean technology</a> is used between chip-processing steps to remove yield-limiting residues and defects. Lam’s <a href="/wiki/Bevel#Semiconductor_wafers" title="Bevel">bevel</a> clean technology directs a plasma at the very edge of the wafer to clean unwanted particles, residues, and films. If not removed, these materials can impact yield if they flake off and re-deposit on the device area during subsequent manufacturing steps.
</p>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(3)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Lam_Capital">Lam Capital</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lam_Research&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Lam Capital" data-section="7" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-3 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-3">
<p>The company's Lam Capital investment group invests in semiconductor-related businesses, including those in the fields of life sciences and industrial automation.<sup id="cite_ref-semieng_25-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-semieng-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(4)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Customers">Customers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lam_Research&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Customers" data-section="8" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-4 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-4">
<p>The company markets its products and services primarily to companies involved in the production of semiconductors in the United States, Europe and Asia.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(5)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Facilities">Facilities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lam_Research&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Facilities" data-section="9" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-5 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-5">
<p>Lam has manufacturing facilities in the United States, Austria, and Korea and sales and services offices throughout the United States, Europe, and Asia.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
