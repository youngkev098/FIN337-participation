</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(1)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Entergy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History" data-section="1" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-1 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-1">
<p>Entergy traces its history to November 13, 1913, with the formation of Arkansas Power Company.  Founder <a href="/wiki/Harvey_C._Couch" title="Harvey C. Couch">Harvey C. Couch</a> used sawdust from a lumber company to bring electricity to rural Arkansas.  In the 1920s, Couch set his sights on buying electric companies in other states. In 1923, he merged four independent companies in Mississippi into Mississippi Power and Light. Two years later, he formed Louisiana Power and Light to provide power to his Mississippi customers from northern Louisiana's natural gas fields.<sup id="cite_ref-Entergy_Turns_100_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Entergy_Turns_100-1">[1]</a></sup></p><p>Meanwhile, in 1922, Sidney Mitchel of Electric Bond and Share Company (EBASCO), a subsidiary of <a href="/wiki/General_Electric_Company" title="General Electric Company">General Electric</a>, had merged several competing electric utilities in New Orleans into New Orleans Public Service, an EBASCO subsidiary.  Mitchel began turning his attention to other territories, and eventually began competing with Couch.  The two men ultimately decided to merge their resources.  In 1925, Electric Power and Light Corporation, an EBASCO subsidiary headquartered in New Orleans, was formed with Couch as its president.  It was the parent company for Mississippi Power and Light, Louisiana Power and Light, New Orleans Public Service, and Arkansas Power and Light.<sup id="cite_ref-Entergy_Turns_100_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Entergy_Turns_100-1">[1]</a></sup></p><p>EBASCO was ordered dissolved in 1949 under the provisions of the <a href="/wiki/Public_Utility_Holding_Company_Act" class="mw-redirect" title="Public Utility Holding Company Act">Public Utility Holding Company Act</a>. Mississippi Power and Light, Louisiana Power and Light, New Orleans Public Service and Arkansas Power and Light were deemed to be an integrated system, and were reorganized under the control of a new holding company, Middle South Utilities.<sup id="cite_ref-Entergy_Turns_100_1-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Entergy_Turns_100-1">[1]</a></sup>  It changed its name to Entergy in 1989, and merged/bought <a href="/wiki/Gulf_States_Utilities" title="Gulf States Utilities">Gulf States Utilities</a>, based in <a href="/wiki/Beaumont,_Texas" title="Beaumont, Texas">Beaumont, Texas</a>, as of 12:00 midnight, January 1, 1994.
</p><p>Since its inception, Entergy has been headquartered in New Orleans. That city had also been home to Entergy's various corporate predecessors since 1925. After <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Katrina" title="Hurricane Katrina">Hurricane Katrina</a> hit the city of New Orleans in August 2005, Entergy temporarily relocated the 1,500 employees and contractors who worked at the headquarters to other cities, including <a href="/wiki/Clinton,_Mississippi" title="Clinton, Mississippi">Clinton, Mississippi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Little_Rock,_Arkansas" title="Little Rock, Arkansas">Little Rock, Arkansas</a>, and <a href="/wiki/The_Woodlands,_Texas" title="The Woodlands, Texas">The Woodlands, Texas</a>. In April 2006, the company began moving back into its New Orleans headquarters.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p><p>Paul Hinnenkamp was named Chief Operating Officer and senior vice president in November 2015. He replaced Mark Savoff, who announced his retirement a few months earlier.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p><p>In 2011, Entergy and <a href="/wiki/ChargePoint" title="ChargePoint">Coulomb Technologies</a>, an <a href="/wiki/Electric_vehicle" title="Electric vehicle">electric vehicle</a> charging station maker, began to donate free electric vehicle charging stations at 16 sites at college campuses in the southern U.S. Its first installation was at <a href="/wiki/Louisiana_State_University" title="Louisiana State University">Louisiana State University</a> in <a href="/wiki/Baton_Rouge,_Louisiana" title="Baton Rouge, Louisiana">Baton Rouge</a>, and is free to use for faculty and students.
</p>

</p><p>Louisiana Power and light Company, for example (today's Entergy Louisiana) used this logo (shown at right) extensively from about 1967 to 1989, on buildings equipment, and advertising.
</p>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(4)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Operations">Operations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Entergy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Operations" data-section="4" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-4 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-4">
<p>Entergy's service territory includes the southeast corner of Louisiana and the cities of Lafayette and Baton Rouge, the eastern three-fourths of <a href="/wiki/Arkansas" title="Arkansas">Arkansas</a> and the western half of <a href="/wiki/Mississippi" title="Mississippi">Mississippi</a>. It also includes part of southeastern <a href="/wiki/Texas" title="Texas">Texas</a>, including the Beaumont-Port Arthur-Orange and Conroe-Woodlands-Kingwood areas.
</p><p>A member of the <a href="/wiki/Fortune_500" title="Fortune 500">Fortune 500</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Entergy owns and operates power plants with approximately 30,000 megawatts of electric generating capacity, and it is the second-largest <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_power" title="Nuclear power">nuclear</a> generator in the United States after <a href="/wiki/Exelon" title="Exelon">Exelon Corporation</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability"><span title="The material near this tag needs to be fact-checked with the cited source(s). (May 2017)">verification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> It had annual revenues of more than $11 billion in 2010 and approximately 15,000 employees.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability"><span title="The material near this tag needs to be fact-checked with the cited source(s). (May 2017)">verification needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p><p>Entergy's main operating segments consist of the U.S. utility segment and the non-utility nuclear segment. The U.S. utility segment provides retail electricity services to approximately 2.9 million customers in <a href="/wiki/Arkansas" title="Arkansas">Arkansas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Louisiana" title="Louisiana">Louisiana</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mississippi" title="Mississippi">Mississippi</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Texas" title="Texas">Texas</a>. The non-utility nuclear segment operates a total of nine <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_power_plants" class="mw-redirect" title="Nuclear power plants">nuclear power plants</a>:
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/New_York_(state)" title="New York (state)">New York</a> – <a href="/wiki/Indian_Point_Energy_Center" title="Indian Point Energy Center">Indian Point Energy Center</a> (scheduled for closing April 2020 and April 2021)<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Massachusetts" title="Massachusetts">Massachusetts</a> – <a href="/wiki/Pilgrim_Nuclear_Generating_Station" class="mw-redirect" title="Pilgrim Nuclear Generating Station">Pilgrim</a> (scheduled for closing in 2019)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Vermont" title="Vermont">Vermont</a> – <a href="/wiki/Vermont_Yankee_Nuclear_Power_Plant" title="Vermont Yankee Nuclear Power Plant">Vermont Yankee</a> (scheduled for decommission beginning in 2015)<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Michigan" title="Michigan">Michigan</a> – <a href="/wiki/Palisades_Nuclear_Generating_Station" title="Palisades Nuclear Generating Station">Palisades Nuclear Station</a> (scheduled for closing in Spring of 2022)<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Arkansas" title="Arkansas">Arkansas</a> – <a href="/wiki/Arkansas_Nuclear_One" title="Arkansas Nuclear One">Arkansas Nuclear One</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Louisiana" title="Louisiana">Louisiana</a> – <a href="/wiki/River_Bend_Nuclear_Generating_Station" title="River Bend Nuclear Generating Station">River Bend</a> and <a href="/wiki/Waterford_Nuclear_Generating_Station" title="Waterford Nuclear Generating Station">Waterford</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Mississippi" title="Mississippi">Mississippi</a> – <a href="/wiki/Grand_Gulf_Nuclear_Generating_Station" class="mw-redirect" title="Grand Gulf Nuclear Generating Station">Grand Gulf</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Nebraska" title="Nebraska">Nebraska</a> – support services for the <a href="/wiki/Cooper_Nuclear_Station" title="Cooper Nuclear Station">Cooper Nuclear Station</a> through 2014, extended until January 2029</li></ul><p>The company's nuclear division is headquartered in <a href="/wiki/Jackson,_Mississippi" title="Jackson, Mississippi">Jackson</a>, Mississippi.
</p><p>Entergy operates more than 40 plants using <a href="/wiki/Natural_gas" title="Natural gas">natural gas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_power" title="Nuclear power">nuclear</a>, <a href="/wiki/Coal_power" class="mw-redirect" title="Coal power">coal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Petroleum" title="Petroleum">oil</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hydroelectric_power" class="mw-redirect" title="Hydroelectric power">hydroelectric power</a> with approximately 30,000 megawatts of electric generating capacity to serve its 2.9 million customers in the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_South" class="mw-redirect" title="Gulf South">Gulf South</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Its extensive transmission system carries approximately 30,000 megawatts of power across more than 15,700 miles (25,300 km) of interconnected lines within a 114,000-square-mile (300,000 km<sup>2</sup>) area.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p><p>Entergy is the only U.S. utility to make the Dow Jones Sustainability Index (DJSI) nine years in a row.  The DJSI is a listing of the companies whose overall environmental, social and economic sustainability performance scores were in the top 10 percent for their sector.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>  Entergy was named in 2008 to <i><a href="/wiki/Forbes" title="Forbes">Forbes</a></i> list of <i>America's Most Trustworthy Companies</i>, a ranking based on corporate governance practices and accounting transparency.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p><p>On February 24, 2010, the <a href="/wiki/Vermont_Senate" title="Vermont Senate">Vermont Senate</a> voted to prevent the Vermont Public Service Board from issuing the necessary certificate that would allow for the Vermont Yankee plant <sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> to have its license renewed for another 20 years.  The vote will not affect current operation of the plant, and the issue could be revisited by the legislature in either a special session later in 2010 or in its next regular session in 2011.
</p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Entergy_Texas">Entergy Texas</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Entergy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Entergy Texas" data-section="5" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>Entergy Texas operates as a wholly owned subsidiary. This was done to prepare the Texas side for de-regulation under Texas law but later Entergy notified the <a href="/wiki/Public_Utility_Commission_of_Texas" title="Public Utility Commission of Texas">Texas PUC</a> it would not be splitting the Texas side off as a de-regulated operation (to do so would cost billions). So the Texas side has remained connected to the rest of the Entergy network, though it has its own CEO and until 2012, was based where the former <a href="/wiki/Gulf_States_Utilities" title="Gulf States Utilities">Gulf States Utilities</a> was, in Beaumont, Texas.  Entergy Texas has moved to the rapidly developing area of <a href="/wiki/The_Woodlands,_Texas" title="The Woodlands, Texas">The Woodlands</a>, which is now larger population wise than the Beaumont area.
</p>
