</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(1)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Southwest_Airlines&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History" data-section="1" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-1 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-1">
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/History_of_Southwest_Airlines" title="History of Southwest Airlines">History of Southwest Airlines</a></div>
<p>Southwest Airlines was founded in 1966 by <a href="/wiki/Herbert_Kelleher" class="mw-redirect" title="Herbert Kelleher">Herbert Kelleher</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rollin_King" title="Rollin King">Rollin King</a>, and in 1967 it was incorporated as Air Southwest Co. Three other airlines took legal action to try to prevent the company from its planned strategy of undercutting their prices by flying only within Texas and thus being exempt from various regulations. The lawsuits were resolved in 1970, and in 1971 the airline began operating regularly scheduled flights between <a href="/wiki/Dallas_Love_Field" title="Dallas Love Field">Dallas Love Field</a> and <a href="/wiki/George_Bush_Intercontinental_Airport" title="George Bush Intercontinental Airport">Houston</a> and between Love Field and <a href="/wiki/San_Antonio_International_Airport" title="San Antonio International Airport">San Antonio</a>, and adopted the name Southwest Airlines Co. In 1975, Southwest began operating flights to various additional cities within Texas, and in 1979 it began flying to adjacent states. Service to the East and the Southeast started in the 1990s.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>

<p>To date, Southwest schedules flights to 102 destinations in 40 states (also the <a href="/wiki/District_of_Columbia" class="mw-redirect" title="District of Columbia">District of Columbia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rico" title="Puerto Rico">Puerto Rico</a>), <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">Mexico</a>, <a href="/wiki/Central_America" title="Central America">Central America</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Caribbean" title="Caribbean">Caribbean</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Southwest does not use the <a href="/wiki/Spoke-hub_distribution_paradigm" class="mw-redirect" title="Spoke-hub distribution paradigm">hub and spoke</a> system of other major airlines, preferring the <a href="/wiki/Point-to-point_transit" title="Point-to-point transit">point-to-point</a> system, combined with a <a href="/wiki/Depeaking" class="mw-redirect" title="Depeaking">rolling hub</a> model in its base cities.

<p>While most U.S. airlines now charge passengers for checked luggage, Southwest continues to permit 2 free checked bags per passenger. Regarding last-minute itinerary changes, Southwest also does not charge any change fees; passengers are permitted to change their flight as late as 10 minutes prior to their flights, and only pay the difference if their new flight is more expensive than the original flight (or receive a credit if the new flight is less expensive than the original flight). In the event of a cancellation, passengers are refunded a travel credit in the amount spent on their ticket, and the credit may be used toward any other Southwest Airlines or Southwest Vacations purchase within a year of the original ticket purchase.
</p><p>Southwest offers free in-flight non-alcoholic beverages and offers alcoholic beverages for sale for $6 to $7/beverage, with Rapid Rewards members eligible to receive drinks vouchers with their tickets. Free alcoholic drinks are offered on popular holidays such as <a href="/wiki/Valentine%27s_Day" title="Valentine's Day">Valentine's Day</a> and Halloween, provided the passenger is at least 21. Southwest has complimentary pretzels on all flights, and most flights have free <a href="/wiki/Nabisco" title="Nabisco">Nabisco</a> snacks. Southwest is known for colorful boarding announcements and crews that burst out in song, which is quite popular among passengers.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p><p>According to the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Transportation" title="United States Department of Transportation">Department of Transportation</a> (DOT) Southwest ranks number one (lowest number of complaints) of all U.S. airlines for customer complaints. Southwest Airlines has consistently received the fewest ratio of complaints per passengers boarded of all major U.S. carriers that have been reporting statistics to the DOT since 1987, which is when the DOT began tracking customer satisfaction statistics and publishing its Air Travel Consumer Report.
</p><p>Prior to 2007, Southwest boarded passengers by grouping the passengers into three groups, labeled A, B and C. Passengers would line up at their specified letter and board.<sup id="cite_ref-abcnews_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-abcnews-25">[25]</a></sup></p><p>In 2007, Southwest modified their boarding procedure by introducing a number. Each passenger receives a letter (A, B or C) and a number 1 through 60. Passengers line up in numerical order within each letter group and choose any open seat on the aircraft.<sup id="cite_ref-abcnews_25-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-abcnews-25">[25]</a></sup> According to a 2012 study by <a href="/wiki/Mythbusters" class="mw-redirect" title="Mythbusters">Mythbusters</a>, this is the fastest method currently in use for non-first class passengers to board a plane; on average, it is 10 minutes faster than the standard method used by most airlines of boarding from the back frontward.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="In-flight_entertainment">In-flight entertainment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Southwest_Airlines&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: In-flight entertainment" data-section="8" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a href="/wiki/File:SWA_Evolve_B737-800.jpg" class="image"><noscript><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0e/SWA_Evolve_B737-800.jpg/200px-SWA_Evolve_B737-800.jpg" decoding="async" width="200" height="267" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="2448" data-file-height="3264"></noscript><span class="lazy-image-placeholder" style="width: 200px;height: 267px;" data-src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0e/SWA_Evolve_B737-800.jpg/200px-SWA_Evolve_B737-800.jpg" data-alt="" data-width="200" data-height="267" data-class="thumbimage"> </span></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:SWA_Evolve_B737-800.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A Southwest 737-800 with the evolve interior and old branding, succeeded by the Heart interior</div></div></div>
<p>All Southwest Airlines aircraft are equipped with <a href="/wiki/Wi-Fi" title="Wi-Fi">Wi-Fi</a>, free streaming live television, free movies, free streaming music from iHeartRadio, and free app messaging. After completing a testing phase that began in February 2009, Southwest announced on August 21, 2009 that it would begin rolling out in-flight Wi-Fi Internet connectivity via Global Eagle Entertainment's satellite-broadband based product. Southwest began adding Wi-Fi to its aircraft in the first quarter of 2010. The airline began testing streaming live television in the summer of 2012 and video on demand in January 2013.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> As of November 1, 2018, live in-flight TV, movies, messaging (iMessage and WhatsApp) and real-time flight tracking information via Wi-Fi are available for free to all passengers, with full Internet access available at a fee for regular passengers and free to A-List Preferred Rapid Rewards members.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Evolve_interior">Evolve interior</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Southwest_Airlines&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Evolve interior" data-section="9" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>On January 17, 2012, Southwest introduced a plan to retrofit its fleet with a new interior. Improvements include a modern cabin design, lighter and more comfortable seats made of eco-friendly products, increased under-seat space, new netted seatback pockets to provide more knee room, a new fixed-wing headrest and improved ergonomics. All Boeing 737-700s and 115 737-800s  have the Evolve Interior.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> Though not originally planned, because of space saved, Southwest was able to fit an extra row of seats on its planes. All Boeing 737-800s have the Boeing Sky Interior, which features sculpted sidewalls and redesigned window housings, along with increased headroom and LED <a href="/wiki/Mood_lighting" title="Mood lighting">mood lighting</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Heart_interior">Heart interior</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Southwest_Airlines&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Heart interior" data-section="10" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>On June 20, 2016, Southwest introduced its newest interior, called the Heart Interior. It includes the widest seat to fit a Boeing 737 that provides additional space for passengers and also includes a new galley.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> The seat is being delivered on all new 737-800s and 737 MAX aircraft.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> All current evolve equipped 737s will be retrofitted with new bulkheads and bold blue seat cushions to match the look of the heart interior.
</p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Rapid_Rewards">Rapid Rewards</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Southwest_Airlines&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Rapid Rewards" data-section="11" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>Southwest first began to offer a <a href="/wiki/Frequent-flyer_program" title="Frequent-flyer program">frequent-flyer program</a> on June 18, 1987, calling it <b>The Company Club</b>. Unlike many competitors' programs that were based on miles flown, The Company Club credited for trips flown regardless of distance.<sup id="cite_ref-company_club_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-company_club-34">[34]</a></sup> Southwest Airlines renamed its frequent flyer program <b>Rapid Rewards</b> on April 25, 1996.<sup id="cite_ref-rr_name_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rr_name-35">[35]</a></sup></p><p>The original Rapid Rewards program offered one credit per one-way flight from an origin to a destination including any stops or connections on Southwest Airlines. When 16 credits were accumulated in a 24-month period, Southwest awarded one free round-trip ticket that was valid for 12 months.<sup id="cite_ref-rr_old_rules_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rr_old_rules-36">[36]</a></sup></p><p>On March 1, 2011, Rapid Rewards changed to a points system based on ticket cost. Members earn and redeem points based on a three-tier fare scale multiplier and the cost of the ticket. Changes also included no <a href="/wiki/Blackout_dates" class="mw-redirect" title="Blackout dates">blackout dates</a>, seat restrictions or expiring credits. Since 18 October 2019, Rapid Rewards points don't expire, as long as the member is alive.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> It also adds more options to use points.<sup id="cite_ref-rr_pr_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rr_pr-38">[38]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-rr_new_39-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rr_new-39">[39]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup></p>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(5)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Corporate_affairs">Corporate affairs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Southwest_Airlines&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Corporate affairs" data-section="12" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-5 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-5">
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Headquarters">Headquarters</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Southwest_Airlines&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Headquarters" data-section="13" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Southwest_airlines_hq_from_east_2009-06-22.jpg" class="image"><noscript><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f0/Southwest_airlines_hq_from_east_2009-06-22.jpg/220px-Southwest_airlines_hq_from_east_2009-06-22.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="161" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="1554" data-file-height="1140"></noscript><span class="lazy-image-placeholder" style="width: 220px;height: 161px;" data-src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f0/Southwest_airlines_hq_from_east_2009-06-22.jpg/220px-Southwest_airlines_hq_from_east_2009-06-22.jpg" data-alt="" data-width="220" data-height="161" data-class="thumbimage"> </span></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Southwest_airlines_hq_from_east_2009-06-22.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Southwest Airlines headquarters in <a href="/wiki/Dallas" title="Dallas">Dallas</a></div></div></div>
<p>The Southwest Airlines headquarters is located on the grounds of Dallas Love Field in the <a href="/wiki/Love_Field,_Dallas,_Texas_(neighborhood)" class="mw-redirect" title="Love Field, Dallas, Texas (neighborhood)">Love Field</a> neighborhood of Dallas, Texas.<sup id="cite_ref-factsheet_6-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-factsheet-6">[6]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> Chris Sloan of <i><a href="/wiki/Airways_Magazine" class="mw-redirect" title="Airways Magazine">Airways Magazine</a></i> stated it is "as much a living, breathing museum and showcase for the “culture that LUV built” as they are corporate offices."<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup></p><p>On September 17, 2012, Southwest broke ground on a new Training and Operational Support (TOPS) building.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> The TOPS Building is across the street from its current headquarters building. The property includes a two-story, 100,000 square foot Network Operations Control (NOC) building that can withstand an EF3 tornado. It also includes a four-story, 392,000 square foot office and training facility with two levels devoted to each function. The new facilities house 24-hour coordination and maintenance operations, customer support and services, and training. The project was completed in late 2013, with occupancy beginning in 2014.
</p><p>On June 2, 2016, Southwest broke ground on its new office and training facility known as Wings. The newest addition to the corporate campus is composed of a 420,000 square foot six story office building, and 380,000 square foot adjoining structure called the LEAD (Leadership Education and Aircrew Development) Center which serves as the new pilot training facility.  The LEAD Center has capacity to house and support 18 flight simulators. It is designed to be expanded to accommodate up to 26 simulator bays.   The building opened on April 3, 2018.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup></p><p>On August 16, 2019, Southwest announced an expansion of the LEAD Center to accommodate eight additional simulators for future operational and training demands.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup>  On January 2, 2020, it was announced that Southwest would be purchasing an additional 3 acres of land adjacent to its Wings and LEAD facilities.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> No additional details were disclosed.
</p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Employment">Employment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Southwest_Airlines&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Employment" data-section="14" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>As of September 30, 2019, Southwest Airlines has 60,590 active full-time equivalent employees.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_5-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-5">[5]</a></sup></p><p><a href="/wiki/Gary_C._Kelly" title="Gary C. Kelly">Gary C. Kelly</a> is Chairman and CEO of Southwest Airlines. Kelly replaced former CEO Jim Parker on July 15, 2004 and assumed the title of "President" on July 15, 2008, replacing former President <a href="/wiki/Colleen_Barrett" title="Colleen Barrett">Colleen Barrett</a>. In July 2008, Herb Kelleher resigned his position as Chairman. Colleen Barrett left her post on the Board of Directors and as Corporate Secretary in May 2008 and as President in July 2008. Kelleher was President and CEO of Southwest from September 1981 – June 2001.<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup></p><p>On January 10, 2017, Southwest announced changes to the Company's executive Leadership ranks with Thomas M. Nealon named as President and Michael G. Van de Ven named as the airline's Chief Operating Officer.<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup></p><p>Approximately 83% of Southwest employees are <a href="/wiki/Labor_union" class="mw-redirect" title="Labor union">members of a union</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> The Southwest Airline Pilots' Association, a union not affiliated with the <a href="/wiki/Air_Line_Pilots_Association" class="mw-redirect" title="Air Line Pilots Association">Air Line Pilots Association</a>, represents the airline's pilots.<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup> The Aircraft Maintenance Technicians are represented by the Aircraft Mechanics Fraternal Association (<a href="/wiki/AMFA" class="mw-redirect" title="AMFA">AMFA</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup> Customer Service Agents and Reservation Agents are represented by the International Association of Machinists and Aerospace Workers Union (IAM). Flight Dispatchers, Flight Attendants, Ramp agents and Operations agents are represented by the Transport Workers Union (TWU).
</p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Impact_on_carriers">Impact on carriers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Southwest_Airlines&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Impact on carriers" data-section="15" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>Southwest has been a major inspiration to other low-cost carriers, and its business model has been repeated many times around the world. The competitive strategy combines high level of employee and aircraft productivity with low unit costs by reducing aircraft turn around time particularly at the gate.<sup id="cite_ref-upinair_53-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-upinair-53">[53]</a></sup> Europe's <a href="/wiki/EasyJet" title="EasyJet">EasyJet</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ryanair" title="Ryanair">Ryanair</a> are two of the best known airlines to follow Southwest's business strategy in that continent. Other airlines with a business model based on Southwest's system include Canada's <a href="/wiki/WestJet" title="WestJet">WestJet</a>, Malaysia's <a href="/wiki/AirAsia" title="AirAsia">AirAsia</a> (the first and biggest LCC in Asia), India's <a href="/wiki/IndiGo" title="IndiGo">IndiGo</a>, Australia's <a href="/wiki/Jetstar_Airways" title="Jetstar Airways">Jetstar</a>, a subsidiary of <a href="/wiki/Qantas" title="Qantas">Qantas</a> (although Jetstar now operates three aircraft types), <a href="/wiki/Philippines" title="Philippines">Philippines</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Cebu_Pacific" title="Cebu Pacific">Cebu Pacific</a>, Thailand's <a href="/wiki/Nok_Air" title="Nok Air">Nok Air</a>, Mexico's <a href="/wiki/Volaris" title="Volaris">Volaris</a>, Indonesia's <a href="/wiki/Lion_Air" title="Lion Air">Lion Air</a> and <a href="/wiki/Turkey" title="Turkey">Turkey</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Pegasus_Airlines" title="Pegasus Airlines">Pegasus Airlines</a>. Although Southwest has been a major inspiration to many other airlines, including Ryanair, AirAsia, Lion Air and Jetstar, the management strategies, for example, of Ryanair, AirAsia, Lion Air and Jetstar differ significantly from those of Southwest.<sup id="cite_ref-upinair_53-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-upinair-53">[53]</a></sup> All these different management strategies can be seen as means of differentiation from other competitors in order to gain competitive advantages.<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Advertising">Advertising</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Southwest_Airlines&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Advertising" data-section="16" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>The company has always employed humor in its advertising. Former slogans include "Love Is Still Our Field," "Just Plane Smart," "The Somebody Else Up There Who Loves You," "You're Now Free To Move About The Country," "THE Low Fare Airline," "Grab your bag, It's On!" and "Welcome Aboard." The airline's current slogan is "Low fares. Nothing to hide. That's TransFarency!"<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:N8675A.jpg" class="image"><noscript><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6a/N8675A.jpg/220px-N8675A.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="5184" data-file-height="3456"></noscript><span class="lazy-image-placeholder" style="width: 220px;height: 147px;" data-src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6a/N8675A.jpg/220px-N8675A.jpg" data-alt="" data-width="220" data-height="147" data-class="thumbimage"> </span></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:N8675A.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A Southwest 737-800 in the Heart livery at <a href="/wiki/Baltimore%E2%80%93Washington_International_Airport" title="Baltimore–Washington International Airport">BWI Airport</a></div></div></div>
<p>In March 1992, shortly after Southwest started using the "Just Plane Smart" motto, Stevens Aviation, which had been using "Plane Smart" for its motto, advised Southwest that it was infringing on its trademark.<sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup></p><p>Instead of a lawsuit, the CEOs for both companies staged an arm wrestling match. Held at the now-demolished <a href="/wiki/Dallas_Sportatorium" title="Dallas Sportatorium">Dallas Sportatorium</a> (the famed wrestling facility) and set for two out of three rounds, the loser of each round was to pay $5,000 to the charity of his choice, with the winner gaining the use of the trademarked phrase. A promotional video was created showing the CEOs "training" for the bout (with CEO Herb Kelleher being helped up during a sit up where a cigarette and glass of <a href="/wiki/Wild_Turkey_(bourbon)" title="Wild Turkey (bourbon)">Wild Turkey</a> 101 whiskey was waiting) and distributed among the employees and also as a video press release along with the video of the match itself. Herb Kelleher lost the match for Southwest, with Stevens Aviation winning the rights to the phrase. Kurt Herwald, CEO of Stevens Aviation, immediately granted the use of "Just Plane Smart" to Southwest Airlines. The net result was both companies having use of the trademark, $15,000 going to charity, and good publicity for both companies.<sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup></p>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(6)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Accidents_and_incidents">Accidents and incidents</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Southwest_Airlines&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Accidents and incidents" data-section="17" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-6 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-6">
<p>Southwest Airlines accidents and incidents include three deaths: one accidental passenger death inflight, one non-passenger death on the ground and one passenger death from injuries he sustained when subdued by other passengers while attempting to break into the cockpit of an aircraft. In March 2019 a Boeing 737 Max 8 crashed in Ethiopia (<a href="/wiki/Ethiopian_Airlines_Flight_302" title="Ethiopian Airlines Flight 302">Ethiopian Airlines Flight 302</a>), following the crash of another Max 8 in Indonesia (<a href="/wiki/Lion_Air_Flight_610" title="Lion Air Flight 610">Lion Air Flight 610</a>) which caused the <a href="/wiki/Boeing_737_MAX_groundings" title="Boeing 737 MAX groundings">worldwide grounding of the aircraft type.</a> <sup id="cite_ref-59" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup> Southwest made the decision to move some of its 737 Max aircraft for storage to the <a href="/wiki/Southern_California_Logistics_Airport" title="Southern California Logistics Airport">Southern California Logistics Airport</a>.
</p><p>Southwest has had 8 accidents, including two aircraft <a href="/wiki/Hull_loss" title="Hull loss">hull losses</a>. The airline was considered among the ten safest airlines in the world in 2012.<sup id="cite_ref-In_depth:_World's_10_Safest_Airlines_61-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-In_depth:_World's_10_Safest_Airlines-61">[61]</a></sup></p>

<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Access_Now_v._Southwest_Airlines" class="mw-redirect" title="Access Now v. Southwest Airlines">Access Now v. Southwest Airlines</a>, <a href="/wiki/Flying_while_Muslim#Southwest_Airlines" title="Flying while Muslim">Flying while Muslim § Southwest Airlines</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Boeing_737_MAX_groundings" title="Boeing 737 MAX groundings">Boeing 737 MAX groundings</a></div>
<p>On June 22, 2011, a March 25 recording of an in-flight transmission of Southwest pilot James Taylor apparently unintentionally broadcasting a conversation with his first officer was released to the press. The conversation was peppered with foul language directed at gay, overweight, and older flight attendants. According to Southwest, the pilot was reprimanded and temporarily <a href="/wiki/Suspended_without_pay" class="mw-redirect" title="Suspended without pay">suspended without pay</a> and received <a href="/wiki/Diversity_education" class="mw-redirect" title="Diversity education">diversity education</a> before being reinstated. Taylor also sent an e-mail apology to all of Southwest's employees, especially the crew members who were criticized.<sup id="cite_ref-75" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-75">[75]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-76" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-76">[76]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-77" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-77">[77]</a></sup></p><p>On September 26, 2017, a woman was removed from a Southwest flight after claiming to have a life-threatening allergy to dogs, two of which were present on the aircraft with one being a certified service animal, and having to be removed by law enforcement after failing to follow the instructions of airline staff. After learning about the woman's allergy, Southwest employees requested that she prove her condition with the correct documentation. When she failed to do so, staff asked her to exit the aircraft multiple times. She refused, which prompted law enforcement to step in and remove the passenger. The interactions between the woman and the officers were recorded and posted online to many social media platforms, and gained much attention.<sup id="cite_ref-78" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-78">[78]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-79" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-79">[79]</a></sup></p><p>On December 29, 2017, a family was removed from a flight from <a href="/wiki/Chicago_Midway_Airport" class="mw-redirect" title="Chicago Midway Airport">Chicago Midway Airport</a> to Santa Ana, California because of an unconfirmed lice accusation. The family did not have lice after all, and was re-accommodated on a flight two days later.  Southwest claims to have refunded the full fare.  The family claims that the airline never compensated them for the interruption to their trip.<sup id="cite_ref-80" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-80">[80]</a></sup></p><p>In October 2019 a Southwest flight attendant filed a lawsuit against the airline, claiming that two pilots had livestreamed footage from a camera hidden in the plane's toilet to an iPad, and that one of the pilots had admitted that such cameras were a "top-secret security measure" installed in all the airline's <a href="/wiki/Boeing_737_Next_Generation#737-800" title="Boeing 737 Next Generation">737-800</a> planes.<sup id="cite_ref-81" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-81">[81]</a></sup> Both Southwest and the pilot union state this never happened, and that it was a "poor attempt at humor" and the video viewed was previously recorded by the pilot which he shot of himself fully clothed.<sup id="cite_ref-82" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-82">[82]</a></sup></p><p>In February 2020, a report conducted by the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Transportation" title="United States Department of Transportation">Transportation Department</a>'s inspector general found that Southwest was flying airplanes with safety concerns and that the <a href="/wiki/Federal_Aviation_Administration" title="Federal Aviation Administration">Federal Aviation Administration</a> was failing to properly oversee the airline.<sup id="cite_ref-83" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-83">[83]</a></sup></p>
