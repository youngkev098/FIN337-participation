<p><b>Hasbro, Inc.</b> (<span class="rt-commentedText nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'h' in 'hi'">h</span><span title="/æ/: 'a' in 'bad'">æ</span><span title="'z' in 'zoom'">z</span><span title="'b' in 'buy'">b</span><span title="'r' in 'rye'">r</span><span title="/oʊ/: 'o' in 'code'">oʊ</span></span>/</a></span></span>; a <a href="/wiki/Syllabic_abbreviation" class="mw-redirect" title="Syllabic abbreviation">syllabic abbreviation</a> of its original name, <b>Hassenfeld Brothers</b>) is an American <a href="/wiki/Multinational" class="mw-disambig" title="Multinational">multinational</a> <a href="/wiki/Conglomerate_(company)" title="Conglomerate (company)">conglomerate</a> with <a href="/wiki/Toy" title="Toy">toy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Board_game" title="Board game">board game</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Media_conglomerate" title="Media conglomerate">media</a> assets. </section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(1)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span></h2><section class="mf-section-1 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-1">
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Hassenfeld_Brothers">Hassenfeld Brothers</span></h3>
<p>Three <a href="/wiki/History_of_the_Jews_in_Poland" title="History of the Jews in Poland">Polish-Jewish</a> brothers,<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Herman, Hillel, and Henry Hassenfeld<sup id="cite_ref-idch_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-idch-7">[7]</a></sup> founded Hassenfeld Brothers in <a href="/wiki/Providence,_Rhode_Island" title="Providence, Rhode Island">Providence, Rhode Island</a>, in 1923, a company selling textile remnants. Over the next two decades, the company expanded to produce pencil cases and school supplies. In 1926, Hassenfeld Brothers was incorporated; Hillel left for another textile business while Henry took charge of the corporation. They began making their own pencils when their pencil supplier began making pencil cases as well.<sup id="cite_ref-idch_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-idch-7">[7]</a></sup></p><p>Hassenfeld Brothers produced modeling clay and then doctor and nurse kits as their first toys, and they became primarily a toy company by 1942. Hillel died in 1943 and Henry Hassenfeld became CEO, while his son Merrill became president. The company entered the plastic fields during World War II to support its toy line.<sup id="cite_ref-idch_7-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-idch-7">[7]</a></sup> Hassenfeld Brothers' first toy hit was <a href="/wiki/Mr._Potato_Head" title="Mr. Potato Head">Mr. Potato Head</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-idch_7-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-idch-7">[7]</a></sup> which the company purchased from <a href="/wiki/George_Lerner" title="George Lerner">George Lerner</a> in 1952. In 1954, the company became a <a href="/wiki/Disney" class="mw-redirect" title="Disney">Disney</a> major licensee.<sup id="cite_ref-idch_7-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-idch-7">[7]</a></sup></p><p>In 1960, Henry died and Merrill took over the parent company, and his older brother Harold ran the pencil-making business of Empire Pencil. Hassenfeld Brothers expanded to Canada with Hassenfeld Brothers (Canada) Ltd. in 1961. The company was approached in 1963 to license a toy based on <i><a href="/wiki/The_Lieutenant" title="The Lieutenant">The Lieutenant</a></i>, which they turned down because they did not want to be tied to a possibly short-lived television series. Instead, Hassenfeld Brothers produced the <a href="/wiki/G.I._Joe" title="G.I. Joe">G.I. Joe</a> toy in 1964 which they termed an "<a href="/wiki/Action_figure" title="Action figure">action figure</a>" in order to market it to boys who wouldn't want to play with dolls. In 1964 and 1965, G.I. Joe accounted for two-thirds of Hassenfeld's sales.<sup id="cite_ref-idch_7-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-idch-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Hasbro_Industries">Hasbro Industries</span></h3>
<p>The company had previously sold toys under the Hasbro trade name, and it shortened its name to <b>Hasbro Industries</b> in 1968 and sold a minor stake in the corporation to the public. The unpopular <a href="/wiki/Vietnam_War" title="Vietnam War">Vietnam War</a> was at its height in 1969, so Hasbro redesigned GI Joe to be less militaristic and more adventure oriented.<sup id="cite_ref-idch_7-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-idch-7">[7]</a></sup> Its promotional efforts included the catchphrase "Boy Oh Boy! It's A Hasbro Toy!" in television commercials and print ads. Also in 1969, Hasbro bought <a href="/wiki/Claster_Television" title="Claster Television">Burt Claster Enterprises</a> which produced "<a href="/wiki/Romper_Room" title="Romper Room">Romper Room</a>" and had just begun a Romper Room toy line. A month-long Teamsters strike and Far Eastern supplier troubles caused the company to have a $1 million loss for the year.<sup id="cite_ref-idch_7-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-idch-7">[7]</a></sup></p><p>In 1970, Hasbro began a plan of diversification and opened the Romper Room Nursery School franchise chain to cash in on President <a href="/wiki/Richard_M._Nixon" class="mw-redirect" title="Richard M. Nixon">Richard M. Nixon</a>'s Family Assistance Plan which subsidized day care for working mothers. By 1975, the company had ended the nursery chain. Hasbro also entered the cookware field with the <a href="/wiki/Galloping_Gourmet" class="mw-redirect" title="Galloping Gourmet">Galloping Gourmet</a> line based on a television cooking show.
</p><p>Two new 1970s toys were public relations disasters. One of the toys was named <a href="/wiki/Lawn_darts" title="Lawn darts">Javelin Darts</a> which were similar to the ancient Roman <i><a href="/wiki/Plumbata" title="Plumbata">plumbata</a></i>. On December 19, 1988, the <a href="/wiki/U.S._Consumer_Product_Safety_Commission" title="U.S. Consumer Product Safety Commission">Consumer Product Safety Commission</a> banned lawn darts from sale in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> due to their hazards as a flying projectile with a sharp metal point causing multiple deaths.<sup id="cite_ref-CPSC_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CPSC-8">[8]</a></sup> The other toy was named The Hypo-Squirt, a hypodermic needle-shaped water gun tagged by the press as a "junior junkie" kit. Both were recalled. Romper Room and its toy line had continued success, although <a href="/wiki/Action_for_Children%27s_Television" title="Action for Children's Television">Action for Children's Television</a> citizens group considered the program to be an advertising channel for toys.<sup id="cite_ref-idch_7-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-idch-7">[7]</a></sup></p><p>Merrill Hassenfeld took over as CEO in 1974, and his son <a href="/wiki/Stephen_D._Hassenfeld" title="Stephen D. Hassenfeld">Stephen D. Hassenfeld</a> became president. The company became profitable once again but had mixed results due to cash flow problems from increasing the number of toys in the line to offset G.I. Joe's declining sales. Hasbro ended the G.I. Joe line in 1975 because of the rising price of plastic and crude oil's increasing prices. In 1977, Hasbro's losses were $2.5 million, and the company held a large debt load. That same year, Hasbro acquired licensing rights to <i><a href="/wiki/Peanuts" title="Peanuts">Peanuts</a></i> cartoon characters. With the financial situation poor, Hasbro's bankers made the company temporarily stop dividend payments in early 1979. The toy division's losses increased Harold Hassenfeld's resentment regarding the company's treatment of the Empire Pencil subsidiary as Empire received lower levels of capital spending to profits than did the toy division.
</p><p>With Merrill's death in 1979, Harold did not recognize Stephen's authority as the successor to the chairman and CEO position. As a solution, Hasbro spun off Empire Pencil in 1980, which was the nation's largest pencil maker, with Harold trading his Hasbro shares for those of Empire. Stephen then became both the CEO and chairman of the board. Between 1978 and 1981, Stephen reduced the Hasbro product line by one-third and its new products by one-half. Hasbro focused on simple, low cost, longer life-cycle toys like Mr. Potato Head. Hasbro thus stayed out of the electronic games field which went bust in the early 1980s.
</p><p>In 1982, Hasbro revived its G.I. Joe line with the help of <a href="/wiki/Marvel_Comics" title="Marvel Comics">Marvel Comics</a>, as an <a href="/wiki/Counter-terrorism" title="Counter-terrorism">anti-terrorist</a> commando based on current events. The company launched the successful <a href="/wiki/Transformers_(toy_line)" title="Transformers (toy line)">Transformers</a> toy line along with a <a href="/wiki/The_Transformers_(TV_series)" title="The Transformers (TV series)">children's animated TV series</a> two years later. With the toys and TV series being popular, Stephen Hassenfeld posed with the toys for a <i>People</i> magazine cover photo.
</p><p>In 1982, Hasbro produced the successful toy franchise <a href="/wiki/My_Little_Pony" title="My Little Pony">My Little Pony</a>. In 1983, they purchased GLENCO Infant Items, a manufacturer of infant products and the world's largest bib producer, and Knickerbocker Toy Company, a struggling <a href="/wiki/Warner_Communications" class="mw-redirect" title="Warner Communications">Warner Communications</a> subsidiary. Hasbro paid 37% of its stock to Warner, which was paid into Hasbro's executive-controlled voting trust, and also received a cash infusion. In 1984, <a href="/wiki/Alan_G._Hassenfeld" title="Alan G. Hassenfeld">Alan G. Hassenfeld</a> took over as president from his brother Stephen, who continued as CEO and chairman. That same year, the company was the nation's sixth best-selling toymaker, and then acquired the <a href="/wiki/Milton_Bradley_Company" title="Milton Bradley Company">Milton Bradley Company</a>, which was the nation's fifth best-selling toymaker. This brought <a href="/wiki/The_Game_of_Life" title="The Game of Life">The Game of Life</a>, <a href="/wiki/Twister_(game)" title="Twister (game)">Twister</a>, <a href="/wiki/Easy_Money_(board_game)" title="Easy Money (board game)">Easy Money</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Playskool" title="Playskool">Playskool</a> into the Hasbro fold and transformed Hasbro into <b>Hasbro Bradley</b>. Stephen Hassenfeld became the merged company's president and CEO, with Milton Bradley chief James Shea Jr. taking the chairman position. However, the executives clashed and Shea left after a few months, and Stephen and Alan returned to their previous positions.<sup id="cite_ref-idch_7-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-idch-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Hasbro">Hasbro</span></h3>
<h4 class="in-block"><span id="1985.E2.80.932007"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="1985–2007">1985–2007</span></h4>
<p>In 1985, the company changed its name again to just <b>Hasbro, Inc.</b><sup id="cite_ref-idch_7-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-idch-7">[7]</a></sup> The  Jumpstarters toys were the subject of a lawsuit in 1985 when Hasbro sued a toy manufacturer for selling toys based on their Transformers design. Hasbro won the suit.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p><p>In the mid-1980s, Hasbro moved past <a href="/wiki/Mattel" title="Mattel">Mattel</a> to become the world's largest toy company.  Hasbro then moved to outsell Mattel's <a href="/wiki/Barbie" title="Barbie">Barbie</a> in the fashion doll market with the 1986 introduction of <a href="/wiki/Jerrica_Benton" class="mw-redirect" title="Jerrica Benton">Jem</a>, a record producer/rock musician dual identity fashion doll. Jem initially posted strong sales but plummeted and was withdrawn from the market in 1987.  Hasbro followed up in 1988 with <a href="/wiki/Maxie%27s_World#Maxie_toy_line" title="Maxie's World">Maxie</a>, a Barbie-sized blonde doll, so that Barbie clothing and accessories would fit. Maxie lasted until 1990.<sup id="cite_ref-idch_7-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-idch-7">[7]</a></sup></p><p>Under Alan's initiative in the late 1980s, Hasbro moved to increase international sales by taking toys overseas that had failed in the US market and selling them for as much as four times the original price. This increased international sales from $268 million in 1985 to $433 million in 1988.<sup id="cite_ref-idch_7-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-idch-7">[7]</a></sup></p><p>In 1988, Hasbro purchased part of <a href="/wiki/Coleco" title="Coleco">Coleco</a> Industries' indoor and outdoor children's furniture and ride-on toy product lines for $21 million including two just closed manufacturing plants in Amsterdam, N.Y.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>  In July 1989, Hasbro acquired bankrupt <a href="/wiki/Coleco" title="Coleco">Coleco</a> for $85 million.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Stephen Hassenfeld died later that year with the company having gone from sales of $104 million in the year he took control to 1989 sales of over $1.4 billion.<sup id="cite_ref-idch_7-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-idch-7">[7]</a></sup></p><p>Alan succeeded Stephen as chairman and CEO. In 1991, Hasbro purchased Tonka Corp. for $486 million, along with its units <a href="/wiki/Parker_Brothers" title="Parker Brothers">Parker Brothers</a>, the maker of <a href="/wiki/Monopoly_(game)" title="Monopoly (game)">Monopoly</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Kenner_Products" title="Kenner Products">Kenner Products</a>. Milton Bradley and Parker Brothers were merged into one division. Alan moved to expand Hasbro overseas with new units in Greece, Hungary, and Mexico.<sup id="cite_ref-idch_7-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-idch-7">[7]</a></sup></p><p>Alan saw the Far East as an important market to expand. In 1992, Hasbro purchased Nomura Toys Ltd. in Japan, and majority ownership of Palmyra, a Southeast Asian toy distributor. These increased the proportion of international sales from 22% in 1985 to 45% ($1.28 billion) in 1995. In 1993, Hasbro lost its bid for <a href="/wiki/J._W._Spear_%26_Sons" title="J. W. Spear &amp; Sons">J. W. Spear &amp; Sons</a>, a U.K.-based game maker, to <a href="/wiki/Mattel" title="Mattel">Mattel</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-idch_7-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-idch-7">[7]</a></sup></p><p>In the US, Hasbro's growth since 1980 were from acquisitions and the leveraging of the new assets. New product development was not as successful except for movie and TV tie-in product lines with <a href="/wiki/Jurassic_Park_(film)" title="Jurassic Park (film)">Jurassic Park</a> and <a href="/wiki/Barney_%26_Friends" title="Barney &amp; Friends">Barney</a>.  Thus, US sales were stagnant in the early 1990s, falling from 1993 to 1995. To turn domestic performance around in 1994, Hasbro merged the Hasbro Toy, Playskool, Playskool Baby, Kenner, and Kid Dimension units into the Hasbro Toy Group.  Meanwhile, Mattel purchased <a href="/wiki/Fisher-Price" title="Fisher-Price">Fisher-Price</a> and retook the top spot in the toy industry.<sup id="cite_ref-idch_7-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-idch-7">[7]</a></sup></p><p><a href="/wiki/Hasbro_Interactive" title="Hasbro Interactive">Hasbro Interactive</a> was started in 1995 and released the Monopoly game on CD-ROM.  Mattel also proposed a merger that year, but was turned down by the Hasbro board in 1996 due to antitrust issues and Justice Department investigation into exclusionary policies between toy manufacturers and toy retailers, particularly <a href="/wiki/Toys_%22R%22_Us" title='Toys "R" Us'>Toys "R" Us</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-idch_7-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-idch-7">[7]</a></sup></p><p>In 1998, Hasbro bought <a href="/wiki/Avalon_Hill" title="Avalon Hill">Avalon Hill</a> for $6 million<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Galoob" title="Galoob">Galoob</a> for $220 million.<sup id="cite_ref-hasbrosale_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hasbrosale-14">[14]</a></sup> In 1999 <a href="/wiki/Wizards_of_the_Coast" title="Wizards of the Coast">Wizards of the Coast</a> was bought in a deal worth $325 million.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Wizards_of_the_Coast" title="Wizards of the Coast">Wizards of the Coast</a> is now a subsidiary of Hasbro and has Avalon Hill as its division. In 2001 money-losing <a href="/wiki/Hasbro_Interactive" title="Hasbro Interactive">Hasbro Interactive</a> was sold to <a href="/wiki/Infogrames" class="mw-redirect" title="Infogrames">Infogrames</a>, a French software concern, for $100 million.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Hasbro entered the building block toy with its <a href="/wiki/Built_to_Rule" title="Built to Rule">Built to Rule</a> line in 2003, which did not hold together well or were too hard for the targeted age group, thus ended in 2005.<sup id="cite_ref-ut_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ut-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<h4 class="in-block"><span id="2008.E2.80.93present"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="2008–present">2008–present</span></h4>
<p>In 2008, Hasbro acquired game maker <a href="/wiki/Cranium,_Inc." title="Cranium, Inc.">Cranium, Inc.</a> for $77.5 million. The deal was announced on January 4 and closed on January 25.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p><p>Hasbro and <a href="/wiki/Universal_Pictures" title="Universal Pictures">Universal Pictures</a> signed an agreement in February 2008 to derive four films from seven Hasbro properties for production.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> In May, Bennett Schneir was hired to lead its <a href="/wiki/Allspark_(company)#Allspark_Pictures" title="Allspark (company)">Hasbro Films</a> division,<sup id="cite_ref-ign_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ign-20">[20]</a></sup> while Hasbro also reacquired series based on their properties from <a href="/wiki/Sunbow_Entertainment" title="Sunbow Entertainment">Sunbow Productions</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> In 2009, <a href="/wiki/Hasbro_Studios" class="mw-redirect" title="Hasbro Studios">Hasbro Studios</a> was formed for TV development, production and distribution. In December 2012, Hasbro transferred all entertainment divisions to Hasbro Studios, including their Los Angeles-based film group, and Cake Mix Studio, the company's Rhode Island-based producer of commercials and short form content.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p><p>Hasbro collaborated with <a href="/wiki/Discovery_Communications" class="mw-redirect" title="Discovery Communications">Discovery</a> on <a href="/wiki/Hub_Network" class="mw-redirect" title="Hub Network">The Hub</a>, a cable network targeting young children and families, which launched on October 10, 2010. The venture found unexpected success with the television revival of the <i>My Little Pony</i> franchise, <i><a href="/wiki/My_Little_Pony:_Friendship_Is_Magic" title="My Little Pony: Friendship Is Magic">My Little Pony: Friendship Is Magic</a></i>, which became the network's highest-rated program and attracted a significant <a href="/wiki/Cult_following" title="Cult following">cult following</a><sup id="cite_ref-wired_commercial_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wired_commercial-23">[23]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/My_Little_Pony:_Friendship_Is_Magic_fandom" title="My Little Pony: Friendship Is Magic fandom">among teens and adults</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> The Hub Network was rebranded as <a href="/wiki/Discovery_Family" title="Discovery Family">Discovery Family</a> on October 13, 2014. 
</p><p>In 2013, Hasbro renewed its deal to produce <a href="/wiki/Marvel_Comics" title="Marvel Comics">Marvel Comics</a> and <a href="/wiki/Star_Wars" title="Star Wars">Star Wars</a> toys through at least 2020.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p><p>In 2011, <a href="/wiki/Greenpeace" title="Greenpeace">Greenpeace</a> accused Hasbro of purchasing paper for its packaging from ancient forests in Indonesia. Hasbro's new commitments are great news for Indonesian rainforests and the people and wildlife that depend on them."<sup id="cite_ref-Huffington_Post_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Huffington_Post-26">[26]</a></sup></p><p>By April 2011, Hasbro started 360 Manufacturing Services, a contract <a href="/wiki/OEM" class="mw-redirect" title="OEM">OEM</a> game manufacturing operating out of Hasbro's USA plant.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> After the Built to Rule line, Hasbro relaunched it into the building block area with the <a href="/wiki/Kre-O" title="Kre-O">Kre-O</a> line in late 2011 starting with some <i>Transformers</i>-based sets.<sup id="cite_ref-ut_17-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ut-17">[17]</a></sup></p><p>In 2012, Hasbro received a <a href="/wiki/United_States_dollar" title="United States dollar">US$</a>1.6 million tax credit from the state of <a href="/wiki/Rhode_Island" title="Rhode Island">Rhode Island</a> with a promise to create 245 new jobs in the state. Instead they laid off more than 125 workers. This was followed in 2013 with further layoffs of North American workers, amounting to 10% of its salaried employees. Meanwhile, CEO, Brian Goldner signed a new five-year contract.<sup id="cite_ref-independent.co.uk_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-independent.co.uk-28">[28]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> As of fiscal year 2012, CEO, Brian Goldner had a total calculated compensation of $9,684,285.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> In July 2013, Backflip Studios sold a 70% stake in the company to Hasbro for $112 million in cash.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p><p>Hasbro was named by <i><a href="/wiki/Fortune_(magazine)" title="Fortune (magazine)">Fortune</a></i> magazine as one of the top 100 companies to work for in 2013, citing that the "company enhanced its vacation policy by giving new employees three weeks off in their first year instead of having to wait five years."<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p><p>On November 12, 2014, it was reported that Hasbro was in talks to buy <a href="/wiki/DreamWorks_Animation" title="DreamWorks Animation">DreamWorks Animation</a>. Hasbro signed a five-year deal with Cartamundi to produce their board games at the East Longmeadow plant.<sup id="cite_ref-ml_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ml-36">[36]</a></sup></p><p>With <a href="/wiki/Mattel" title="Mattel">Mattel</a> adding two competing lines, and the expiration of their <a href="/wiki/Disney_Princess" title="Disney Princess">Disney Princess</a> license at the end of 2015, Disney gave Hasbro a chance to gain the license given their work on <i>Star Wars</i>, which led to a <i><a href="/wiki/Descendants_(2015_film)" title="Descendants (2015 film)">Descendants</a></i> license. DCP was also attempting to evolve the brand from one of them less as damsels and more as heroines. In September 2014, Disney announced that Hasbro would be the doll licensee for the Disney Princess line starting on January 1, 2016.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></p><p>In July 2016, Hasbro acquired Dublin-based <a href="/wiki/Boulder_Media_Limited" class="mw-redirect" title="Boulder Media Limited">Boulder Media Limited</a> and placed it under the control of its chief content officer.<sup id="cite_ref-vty_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-vty-38">[38]</a></sup> Hasbro announced that it would launch its own Construction will start in late December 2019 and will wrap up in late 2020.<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup></p><p>On August 22, 2019, Hasbro announced that they are buying <a href="/wiki/Entertainment_One" title="Entertainment One">Entertainment One</a> for about US$4 billion. <li><i><a href="/wiki/Bull%27s-Eye_Ball" title="Bull's-Eye Ball">Bull's-Eye Ball</a></i></li></ul><p>Hasbro also offers a memory game called <i>Simon</i> which involves memorising sequences of colors and lights.
</p><p>In 1995, Hasbro began a short-lived <a href="/wiki/Video_game_developer" title="Video game developer">video game development</a> and <a href="/wiki/Video_game_publisher" title="Video game publisher">publishing</a> venture called <a href="/wiki/Hasbro_Interactive" title="Hasbro Interactive">Hasbro Interactive</a>, but disbanded it in 2001 when it was bought by the now defunct <a href="/wiki/Infogrames_Entertainment,_SA" class="mw-redirect" title="Infogrames Entertainment, SA">Infogrames</a>. Now Hasbro develops video games based on its brands through third-party developers and licensing strategies, notably with major American companies such as <a href="/wiki/Activision" title="Activision">Activision</a>, <a href="/wiki/Electronic_Arts" title="Electronic Arts">Electronic Arts</a>, and <a href="/wiki/THQ" title="THQ">THQ</a>. Following the rise of <a href="/wiki/Smartphone" title="Smartphone">smartphones</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tablet_computer" title="Tablet computer">tablet PCs</a> in the 2010s, as well as major video gaming publishers cutting back on releasing games based on licensed IPs for various reasons, such as <a href="/wiki/Great_Recession" title="Great Recession">economic slumps</a>, several of Hasbro's brands were licensed towards mobile game developers such as <a href="/wiki/Gameloft" title="Gameloft">Gameloft</a>, releasing their games under the label Hasbro Gaming.
</p><p>On February 25, 2005, Hasbro announced that it would be introducing a musical <a href="/wiki/Toothbrush" title="Toothbrush">toothbrush</a> to the market. The <i><a href="/wiki/Tooth_Tunes" title="Tooth Tunes">Tooth Tunes</a></i>, released in early 2007, transmits music from the <a href="/wiki/Human_mandible" class="mw-redirect" title="Human mandible">jawbone</a> to the ear when the bristles touch the teeth.
</p>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(4)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Criticism_and_controversy">Criticism and controversy</span></h2><section class="mf-section-4 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-4">
<p>In 2007, a workers' rights group investigated several of Hasbro's Chinese suppliers and found that, in one instance, a toy factory in China's <a href="/wiki/Guangxi_Province" class="mw-redirect" title="Guangxi Province">Guangxi Province</a> had hired 1000 <a href="/wiki/Junior_high_school" class="mw-redirect" title="Junior high school">junior high school</a> students. The same group discovered other widespread <a href="/wiki/Labour_law" title="Labour law">labor violations</a>, including unsafe working conditions, mandatory overtime, <a href="/wiki/Verbal_abuse" title="Verbal abuse">verbal abuse</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sexual_harassment" title="Sexual harassment">sexual harassment</a> of employees. Hasbro issued a statement, saying that it would "act swiftly and decisively in making any necessary changes" and had "increased the intensity of [its] ongoing safety review efforts." Critics pointed out that Hasbro had no official regulatory control of these factories. Hasbro responded by hiring independent auditors. These auditors make unannounced visits to the <a href="/wiki/East_Asian" class="mw-redirect" title="East Asian">East Asian</a> subcontractors. Factory managers have been coached in how to fool the auditors.<sup id="cite_ref-73" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-73">[73]</a></sup></p><p>Hasbro has also been criticized for focusing some of its products on specific demographic groups. For example, a letter spread widely on <a href="/wiki/Social_media" title="Social media">social media</a> in November 2012 written by a six-year-old Irish girl complaining about the lack of female characters in the guessing game <i><a href="/wiki/Guess_Who%3F" title="Guess Who?">Guess Who?</a></i><sup id="cite_ref-independent.co.uk_28-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-independent.co.uk-28">[28]</a></sup> This garnered attention in the press after the girl's mother posted the exchange on her blog.<sup id="cite_ref-74" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-74">[74]</a></sup> <i>Guess Who?</i> had previously received complaints over gender and ethnic bias in its choice of 24 images.<sup id="cite_ref-independent.co.uk_28-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-independent.co.uk-28">[28]</a></sup></p><p>Hasbro primarily sells toys directed at either the girl or boy markets. As such, there have been criticisms that Hasbro's toys reinforce <a href="/wiki/Gender_stereotypes" class="mw-redirect" title="Gender stereotypes">gender stereotypes</a>. For example, in December 2012, 13-year-old McKenna Pope started a campaign on <a href="/wiki/Change.org" title="Change.org">Change.org</a>, calling on the company to create a "boy-friendly" version of the popular <a href="/wiki/Easy-Bake_Oven" title="Easy-Bake Oven">Easy-Bake Oven</a> and to feature boys on their packaging and materials.<sup id="cite_ref-auto_75-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-auto-75">[75]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-auto1_76-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-auto1-76">[76]</a></sup> Within a week, over 30,000 people signed her petition.<sup id="cite_ref-auto2_77-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-auto2-77">[77]</a></sup></p><p>Hasbro was criticized for "sexist" product design when its 2015 <a href="/wiki/Star_Wars" title="Star Wars">Star Wars</a> <a href="/wiki/Monopoly_board_game" class="mw-redirect" title="Monopoly board game">Monopoly board game</a> failed to feature <a href="/wiki/Rey_(Star_Wars)" title="Rey (Star Wars)">Rey</a>, the female protagonist in <i><a href="/wiki/Star_Wars:_The_Force_Awakens" title="Star Wars: The Force Awakens">Star Wars: The Force Awakens</a></i>, while including all of the supporting male characters. Hasbro explained that Rey was left out of the Monopoly game to avoid spoilers, because the game was released months before the movies.<sup id="cite_ref-78" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-78">[78]</a></sup> On January 5, 2016, Hasbro announced that Rey would be included in future versions.  Hasbro later stated that it struggled to distribute the updated Monopoly game that includes the Rey piece, because retailers (especially in the United States) showed "insufficient interest" after having already purchased stock of the first release.<sup id="cite_ref-79" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-79">[79]</a></sup></p><p>On October 2, 2015, Lorraine Markham sued Hasbro for breach of contract for failure to pay royalties to her. She was seeking a declaration from the U.S. District Court in Providence that her husband Bill Markham was the sole creator of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Game_of_Life" title="The Game of Life">The Game of Life</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-auto3_80-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-auto3-80">[80]</a></sup></p>
