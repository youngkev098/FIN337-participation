</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(1)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_Parcel_Service&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History" data-section="1" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-1 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-1">
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Foundation">Foundation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_Parcel_Service&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Foundation" data-section="2" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a href="/wiki/File:MPD1916UPS.jpg" class="image"><noscript><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ee/MPD1916UPS.jpg/300px-MPD1916UPS.jpg" decoding="async" width="300" height="173" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="1200" data-file-height="691"></noscript><span class="lazy-image-placeholder" style="width: 300px;height: 173px;" data-src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ee/MPD1916UPS.jpg/300px-MPD1916UPS.jpg" data-alt="" data-width="300" data-height="173" data-class="thumbimage"> </span></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:MPD1916UPS.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Merchants Parcel Delivery delivery vehicles in 1916</div></div></div>
<p>On August 28, 1907, <a href="/wiki/James_E._Casey" title="James E. Casey">James Casey</a> founded the <b>American Messenger Company</b> with Claude Ryan<sup id="cite_ref-cnn_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cnn-9">[9]</a></sup> in <a href="/wiki/Seattle,_Washington" class="mw-redirect" title="Seattle, Washington">Seattle, Washington</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> capitalized with $100 in debt.<sup id="cite_ref-HistoryLink_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HistoryLink-11">[11]</a></sup> Most deliveries at this time were made on foot and bicycles were used for longer trips.
</p><p>The American Messenger Company focused primarily on package delivery to retail stores with special delivery mail delivered for its largest client the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Postal_Service" title="United States Postal Service">United States Postal Service</a> (USPS). In 1913, the company acquired a <a href="/wiki/Model_T_Ford" class="mw-redirect" title="Model T Ford">Model T Ford</a> as its first delivery vehicle. Casey and Ryan merged with a competitor, Evert McCabe, and formed <b>Merchants Parcel Delivery</b>. Consolidated delivery was also introduced, combining packages addressed to a certain neighborhood onto one delivery vehicle.<sup id="cite_ref-HistoryLink_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HistoryLink-11">[11]</a></sup> In 1916, Charlie Soderstrom joined Merchants Parcel Delivery bringing in more vehicles for the growing delivery business. In 1919, the company expanded for the first time outside of Seattle to <a href="/wiki/Oakland,_California" title="Oakland, California">Oakland, California</a> and changed its name to United Parcel Service. The name change to United Parcel Service was to remind the company expansion that operations were still <i>United</i> under the same organization and <i>Parcel</i> identified the type of business offered as part of its <i>Service</i>. <a href="/wiki/Common_carrier" title="Common carrier">Common carrier</a> service was acquired in 1922 from a company in <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles,_California" class="mw-redirect" title="Los Angeles, California">Los Angeles, California</a>. UPS became one of the only companies in the United States to offer common carrier service. At first common carrier was only limited to a small area around Los Angeles but by 1927 expanded to areas up to 125 miles outside the city. In 1924, a conveyor belt system was debuted for the handling of packages for UPS operations.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p><p>In 1930, a consolidated service began in New York City, and soon after in other major cities in the <a href="/wiki/Eastern_United_States" title="Eastern United States">East</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Midwest" class="mw-redirect" title="Midwest">Midwest</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-UPS_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-UPS-2">[2]</a></sup> The use of common carrier for delivery between all customers placed UPS in direct competition with USPS and the <a href="/wiki/Interstate_Commerce_Commission" title="Interstate Commerce Commission">Interstate Commerce Commission</a>. The common carrier service was applied in cities where UPS could use the service without the authority of the ICC and state commerce commissions. The first city for UPS to use common carrier status outside California was <a href="/wiki/Chicago,_Illinois" class="mw-redirect" title="Chicago, Illinois">Chicago, Illinois</a> in 1953.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:332px;"><a href="/wiki/File:UPSModelT1921.jpg" class="image"><noscript><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e5/UPSModelT1921.jpg/330px-UPSModelT1921.jpg" decoding="async" width="330" height="214" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="1200" data-file-height="777"></noscript><span class="lazy-image-placeholder" style="width: 330px;height: 214px;" data-src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e5/UPSModelT1921.jpg/330px-UPSModelT1921.jpg" data-alt="" data-width="330" data-height="214" data-class="thumbimage"> </span></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:UPSModelT1921.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Ford_Model_T" title="Ford Model T">Ford Model T</a> UPS delivery vehicle in 1921</div></div></div>
<p>Air service through UPS was first used in 1929 through private airlines. However, <a href="/wiki/The_Great_Depression" class="mw-redirect" title="The Great Depression">The Great Depression</a> and a lack of volume ended the air service. In 1953, UPS resumed air service called UPS Blue Label Air with two-day service to major cities along the <a href="/wiki/East_Coast_of_the_United_States" title="East Coast of the United States">East Coast</a> and <a href="/wiki/West_Coast_of_the_United_States" title="West Coast of the United States">West Coast</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Expansion_and_diversification">Expansion and diversification</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_Parcel_Service&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Expansion and diversification" data-section="3" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>In 1975, UPS moved its headquarters to <a href="/wiki/Greenwich,_Connecticut" title="Greenwich, Connecticut">Greenwich, Connecticut</a> and began serving all of the 48 <a href="/wiki/Continental_United_States" class="mw-redirect" title="Continental United States">contiguous states</a> of the United States. The expanded operations to all 48 states made UPS the first package delivery company to serve every address in the Continental United States. UPS went international in 1975 establishing operations in <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a> and in 1976 operations were established in <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a>. On February 28, UPS Ltd. (later changed to UPS Canada Ltd.) began operations in Toronto, <a href="/wiki/Ontario" title="Ontario">Ontario</a>. UPS Canada's head office is located in <a href="/wiki/Burlington,_Ontario" title="Burlington, Ontario">Burlington, Ontario</a>. In 1976, UPS established a domestic operation in <a href="/wiki/West_Germany" title="West Germany">West Germany</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-UPS_Pressroom_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-UPS_Pressroom-15">[15]</a></sup></p><p>UPS Next Day Air Service was launched in 1985 for all 48 states plus <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rico" title="Puerto Rico">Puerto Rico</a>. In 1988, UPS Airlines was launched with authorization from the <a href="/wiki/Federal_Aviation_Administration" title="Federal Aviation Administration">Federal Aviation Administration</a>. UPS Airlines became the fastest-growing airline in FAA history and today is the 10th largest airline in the United States.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Domestic air service was added to <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a> in 1989.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> In 1991, UPS moved its headquarters to <a href="/wiki/Sandy_Springs,_Georgia" title="Sandy Springs, Georgia">Sandy Springs, Georgia</a>, a suburb of <a href="/wiki/Atlanta,_Georgia" class="mw-redirect" title="Atlanta, Georgia">Atlanta</a>. In 1992, UPS acquired both <a href="/w/index.php?title=Haulfast&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Haulfast (page does not exist)">Haulfast</a> and <a href="/w/index.php?title=Carryfast&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Carryfast (page does not exist)">Carryfast</a> and rebranded them UPS Supply Chain Solutions. Haulfast provided the pallet haulage and trucking network for the CarryFast group of companies. By 1993, UPS was delivering up to 11.5 million packages and documents per day.
</p><p>The large volume of UPS customers in the 1990s made UPS develop new technology for better efficiency. A handheld device called Delivery Information Acquisition Device (DIAD) was created to record and upload delivery information to the UPS network immediately upon pickup by every UPS driver. In 1992, UPS began tracking all ground shipments electronically. In 1994, UPS.com debuted, and provided an interface to make what was primarily internal operational information available for customer access. In 1995, UPS acquired <a href="/wiki/SonicAir" title="SonicAir">SonicAir</a> to offer service parts logistics and compete with <a href="/wiki/Choice_Logistics" title="Choice Logistics">Choice Logistics</a>. In the same year, UPS launched UPS Logistics Group to facilitate global supply chain management services and consulting for customer needs. In 1997, a <a href="/wiki/United_Parcel_Service_strike_of_1997" title="United Parcel Service strike of 1997">walkout</a> by the 185,000 members of the <a href="/wiki/Teamsters" class="mw-redirect" title="Teamsters">Teamsters</a> shut down UPS for 16 days.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> In 1998, UPS Capital was established to enable companies to grow their business through a comprehensive menu of integrated financial services through UPS.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> UPS acquired Challenge Air in 1999 to expand its operations in Latin America.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:The_UPS_Store_in_Tanasbourne_-_Hillsboro,_Oregon.JPG" class="image"><noscript><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cb/The_UPS_Store_in_Tanasbourne_-_Hillsboro%2C_Oregon.JPG/220px-The_UPS_Store_in_Tanasbourne_-_Hillsboro%2C_Oregon.JPG" decoding="async" width="220" height="216" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="1955" data-file-height="1921"></noscript><span class="lazy-image-placeholder" style="width: 220px;height: 216px;" data-src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cb/The_UPS_Store_in_Tanasbourne_-_Hillsboro%2C_Oregon.JPG/220px-The_UPS_Store_in_Tanasbourne_-_Hillsboro%2C_Oregon.JPG" data-alt="" data-width="220" data-height="216" data-class="thumbimage"> </span></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:The_UPS_Store_in_Tanasbourne_-_Hillsboro,_Oregon.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Mail_Boxes_Etc." title="Mail Boxes Etc.">Mail Boxes Etc., Inc.</a> was re-branded as The UPS Store in 2001.</div></div></div>
<p><br></p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:UPS_street_locker_11_Av_jeh.jpg" class="image"><noscript><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/17/UPS_street_locker_11_Av_jeh.jpg/220px-UPS_street_locker_11_Av_jeh.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="2915" data-file-height="1943"></noscript><span class="lazy-image-placeholder" style="width: 220px;height: 147px;" data-src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/17/UPS_street_locker_11_Av_jeh.jpg/220px-UPS_street_locker_11_Av_jeh.jpg" data-alt="" data-width="220" data-height="147" data-class="thumbimage"> </span></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:UPS_street_locker_11_Av_jeh.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>UPS Access Point in a New York City street</div></div></div>
<p>On November 10, 1999, UPS became a public company<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> in the largest <a href="/wiki/Initial_public_offering" title="Initial public offering">initial public offering</a> of the century.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="21st_century">21st century</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_Parcel_Service&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: 21st century" data-section="4" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>In 2001, UPS acquired <a href="/wiki/Mail_Boxes_Etc." title="Mail Boxes Etc.">Mail Boxes Etc., Inc.</a><sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> In 2003, approximately 3,000 Mail Boxes Etc., Inc. were rebranded as <a href="/wiki/The_UPS_Store" title="The UPS Store">The UPS Store</a>.
</p><p>In 2004, UPS entered the <a href="/wiki/Freight" class="mw-redirect" title="Freight">heavy freight</a> business with purchase of <a href="/wiki/Menlo_Worldwide" class="mw-redirect" title="Menlo Worldwide">Menlo Worldwide Forwarding</a>, a former subsidiary of <a href="/wiki/Menlo_Worldwide" class="mw-redirect" title="Menlo Worldwide">Menlo Worldwide</a>. UPS rebranded it as UPS Supply Chain Solutions. The purchase price was US$150 million and the assumption of US$110 million in long-term debt. On August 5, 2005, UPS announced that it has completed its acquisition of less-than-truckload (LTL) trucking company Overnite Transportation for US$1.25 billion.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> This was approved by the <a href="/wiki/Federal_Trade_Commission" title="Federal Trade Commission">FTC</a> and Overnite shareholders on August 4, 2005. In 2005, UPS offered non-stop delivery service between <a href="/wiki/Guangzhou" title="Guangzhou">Guangzhou</a> and the United States.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> On April 28, 2006, Overnite officially became <a href="/wiki/UPS_Freight" title="UPS Freight">UPS Freight</a>. On October 3, 2005, UPS completed the purchase of <a href="/wiki/Lynx_Express_(parcels)" title="Lynx Express (parcels)">Lynx Express</a>, one of the largest independent parcel carriers in the United Kingdom, for <a href="/wiki/Pound_sterling" title="Pound sterling">£</a>55.5 million (US$97.1 million) after receiving approval for the transaction from the <a href="/wiki/European_Commission" title="European Commission">European Commission</a>. The first joint package car center operation in <a href="/wiki/Dartford" title="Dartford">Dartford</a>, Kent, was opened in 2006.
</p><p>On August 28, 2007, United Parcel Service celebrated its 100th anniversary.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/All_Nippon_Airways" title="All Nippon Airways">All Nippon Airways</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Star_Alliance" title="Star Alliance">Star Alliance</a> member, and UPS formed a cargo alliance and code-share to transport member cargo in 2008, similarly to an <a href="/wiki/Airline_alliance" title="Airline alliance">airline alliance</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> On March 19, 2012, UPS announced that it intended to acquire <a href="/wiki/TNT_Express" title="TNT Express">TNT Express</a> for $6.8 billion, in a move to help expand its presence in European and Asian markets.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> However, the deal fell through in January 2013, after it was announced that UPS had failed to obtain permission from the European Commission and as such had been blocked on competition grounds.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> In February 2012, UPS acquired <a href="/wiki/Brussels" title="Brussels">Brussels</a>-based company Kiala that provides e-commerce retailers the option to have goods delivered to a conventional retail location.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p><p>In May 2019, UPS launched a partnership with autonomous trucking startup, TuSimple to carry cargo across Phoenix, AZ and Tucson, AZ. <sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p><p>In October 2019, UPS won the approval of the <a href="/wiki/Federal_Aviation_Administration" title="Federal Aviation Administration">Federal Aviation Administration</a> to fly drones.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> The certification will allow UPS to deliver health care supplies using a fleet of drones.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p><p>On January 29, 2020 UPS announced it was investing in UK start-up <a href="/wiki/Arrival_(company)" title="Arrival (company)">Arrival</a> and ordering 10,000 Generation 2 electric vehicles as a step towards a cleaner, more high-tech fleet. The deal runs from 2020 until 2024 and was reported to be worth more than $400 million.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></p><p>In March 2020, the company has appointed <a href="/wiki/Carol_Tom%C3%A9" title="Carol Tomé">Carol Tomé</a> to succeed David Abney as its chief executive officer. It was viewed as a move to steer the parcel delivery company through the turbulence of trade wars, technological disruption and the risk of a pandemic-induced recession.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(2)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Finances">Finances</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_Parcel_Service&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Finances" data-section="5" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-2 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-2">

</td></tr></tbody></table></section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(3)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Operations">Operations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_Parcel_Service&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Operations" data-section="6" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-3 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-3">
<p>UPS's primary business is the time-definite delivery of packages and documents worldwide. In recent years, UPS has extended its service portfolio to include <a href="/wiki/Less_than_truckload" class="mw-redirect" title="Less than truckload">less than truckload</a> (LTL) transportation (primarily in the U.S.) and <a href="/wiki/Supply_chain" title="Supply chain">supply chain</a> services.
UPS reports its operations in three segments: U.S. Domestic Package operations, International Package operations, and Supply Chain &amp; Freight operations.
</p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="U.S._Domestic_Package">U.S. Domestic Package</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_Parcel_Service&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: U.S. Domestic Package" data-section="7" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>U.S. Domestic Package operations include the time-definite delivery of letters, documents, and packages throughout the United States.
</p>

<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="International_Package">International Package</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_Parcel_Service&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: International Package" data-section="8" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>

<p>International Package operations include delivery to more than 220 countries and territories worldwide,<sup id="cite_ref-UPS_Fact_Sheet_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-UPS_Fact_Sheet-38">[38]</a></sup> including shipments wholly outside the United States, as well as shipments with either origin or distribution outside the United States.
</p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span id="Supply_Chain_.26_Freight"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Supply_Chain_&amp;_Freight">Supply Chain &amp; Freight</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_Parcel_Service&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Supply Chain &amp; Freight" data-section="9" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>Supply Chain &amp; Freight (UPS-SCS for UPS Supply Chain Solutions) includes UPS' <a href="/wiki/Freight_forwarder" title="Freight forwarder">forwarding</a> and <a href="/w/index.php?title=Contract_logistics&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Contract logistics (page does not exist)">contract logistics</a> operations,
<a href="/wiki/UPS_Freight" title="UPS Freight">UPS Freight</a>, and other related business units. UPS' forwarding and logistics business provides services in more than 175 countries and territories worldwide and includes worldwide supply chain design, execution and management, freight forwarding and distribution, customs brokerage, mail and consulting services. UPS Freight offers a variety of less than truckload ("LTL") and truckload ("TL") services to customers in North America.
</p><p>Other business units within this segment include <a href="/wiki/The_UPS_Store" title="The UPS Store">The UPS Store</a> and UPS Capital.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup></p>
<ul><li>Trailer / railroad <a href="/wiki/Reporting_marks" class="mw-redirect" title="Reporting marks">reporting marks</a>: UPGX, UPGZ, UPOZ, UPSZ, UPWZ, UPSC, UPSU, NONZ <sup id="cite_ref-CTG09_40-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CTG09-40">[40]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/National_Motor_Freight_Traffic_Association" title="National Motor Freight Traffic Association">NMFTA</a> <a href="/wiki/Standard_Carrier_Alpha_Codes" class="mw-redirect" title="Standard Carrier Alpha Codes">SCAC code</a>: UPSS, UPSN, UPSC</li></ul><h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Personnel_structure">Personnel structure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_Parcel_Service&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Personnel structure" data-section="10" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>UPS employs approximately 444,000 staff: 362,000 in the U.S. and 82,000 internationally.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> Approximately 240,000 UPS drivers, package handlers and clerks are represented by the <a href="/wiki/International_Brotherhood_of_Teamsters" title="International Brotherhood of Teamsters">International Brotherhood of Teamsters</a>. During the <a href="/wiki/United_Parcel_Service_strike_of_1997" title="United Parcel Service strike of 1997">United Parcel Service strike of 1997</a>, the company's only nationwide strike in its history, which lasted 16 days, Teamster President <a href="/wiki/Ron_Carey_(labor_leader)" title="Ron Carey (labor leader)">Ron Carey</a> negotiated a new contract for workers.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup></p>

<p>Major competitors in the United States include <a href="/wiki/United_States_Postal_Service" title="United States Postal Service">United States Postal Service</a> (USPS) and <a href="/wiki/FedEx" title="FedEx">FedEx</a>, along with regional carriers such as <a href="/wiki/OnTrac" title="OnTrac">OnTrac</a>, and <a href="/wiki/LSO_(company)" title="LSO (company)">LSO</a> (formerly Lonestar Overnight). In addition to these domestic carriers, UPS competes with a variety of international operators, including <a href="/wiki/Canada_Post" title="Canada Post">Canada Post</a> (and its subsidiary <a href="/wiki/Purolator_Courier" class="mw-redirect" title="Purolator Courier">Purolator</a>), <a href="/wiki/TransForce" class="mw-redirect" title="TransForce">TransForce</a>, <a href="/wiki/Deutsche_Post" title="Deutsche Post">Deutsche Post</a> (and its subsidiary <a href="/wiki/DHL_Express" class="mw-redirect" title="DHL Express">DHL</a>), <a href="/wiki/Royal_Mail" title="Royal Mail">Royal Mail</a>, <a href="/wiki/Japan_Post_Service" title="Japan Post Service">Japan Post Service</a>, <a href="/wiki/India_Post" title="India Post">India Post</a> and many other regional carriers, national postal services and air cargo handlers (see <a href="/wiki/Package_delivery" title="Package delivery">Package delivery</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mail" title="Mail">Mail</a> pages).
</p><p>Historically, the bulk of competition for UPS came from inexpensive ground-based delivery services, such as <a href="/wiki/Parcel_post" title="Parcel post">Parcel Post</a> (USPS) or <a href="/wiki/Choice_Logistics" title="Choice Logistics">Choice Logistics</a>. In 1998, FedEx expanded into ground parcel delivery through its acquisition of <a href="/wiki/Roadway_Package_System" class="mw-redirect" title="Roadway Package System">RPS</a> (formerly Roadway Package System), rebranding it as <a href="/wiki/FedEx_Ground" title="FedEx Ground">FedEx Ground</a> in 2000. In 2003, DHL acquired <a href="/wiki/Airborne_Express" title="Airborne Express">Airborne Express</a>, expanding its operations in the United States.  
</p><p>In response to the expansion of FedEx and DHL, UPS partnered with the US Postal Service to offer UPS Mail Innovations,<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> a program that allows UPS to pick up mail &amp; packages weighing under one pound separately from the main ground network and transfer them to a USPS center, or destination delivery unit (DDU),<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> for final distribution. This process is also known as zone skipping,<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> long used by Parcel Consolidators.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> UPS also has a separate product called "SurePost" which uses the UPS Ground network to deliver packages weighing under 10 pounds to the nearest UPS Package Center, which transfers them to the USPS DDU for "final mile" delivery.<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup></p><p>More recently, the continued growth of <a href="/wiki/Online_shopping" title="Online shopping">online shopping</a>, combined with increasing awareness of the role of transportation (including package delivery) on the <a href="/wiki/Natural_environment" title="Natural environment">environment</a>, has contributed to the rise of emerging competition from niche carriers or rebranded incumbents. For instance, the <a href="/wiki/US_Postal_Service" class="mw-redirect" title="US Postal Service">US Postal Service</a> claims "greener delivery" of parcels on the assumption that USPS letter carriers deliver to each US address, six days a week anyway, and therefore offer the industry's lowest fuel consumption per delivery. Other carriers, like ParcelPool.com,<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> which specializes in residential package delivery to <a href="/wiki/APO/FPO" class="mw-redirect" title="APO/FPO">APO/FPO</a> addresses, <a href="/wiki/Hawaii" title="Hawaii">Hawaii</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alaska" title="Alaska">Alaska</a>, <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rico" title="Puerto Rico">Puerto Rico</a>, and other US Territories, arose in response to increased demand from catalog retailers and online e-tailers for low-cost residential delivery services closely matching service standards normally associated with more expensive expedited parcel delivery.
</p>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(5)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Transportation">Transportation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_Parcel_Service&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Transportation" data-section="13" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-5 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-5">
<p>UPS operates over 119,000 delivery vehicles worldwide, ranging from bicycles to tractor-trailer trucks.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_50-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-50">[50]</a></sup> In a long-running company policy to avoid advertisement or endorsement of a vehicle manufacturer, all external manufacturer emblems and badging are removed when a vehicle enters service (whenever possible).    
</p><p>The typical lifespan of a UPS ground vehicle is 20–25 years (or more), lasting until the structural integrity is compromised. The company does not re-sell any of its ground vehicles, so retired vehicles are almost always stripped of reusable parts before being sent to be crushed/broken up. Prior to scrapping, UPS trucks and trailers have all company branding painted over and are assigned an ADA (Automotive Destruction Authorization) number and must be crushed under the supervision of UPS Automotive personnel, which records the vehicle's destruction. The only exception to this policy are vehicles taken off the road for internal use; package cars (repainted white) are used for various purposes (typically at large hubs); older semitractors sometimes see use as <a href="/wiki/Terminal_tractor" title="Terminal tractor">terminal tractors</a>.     
</p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Delivery_vans">Delivery vans</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_Parcel_Service&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Delivery vans" data-section="14" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>UPS refers to its <a href="/wiki/Multi-stop_truck" title="Multi-stop truck">delivery van</a> as a "package car". Several designs and sizes are used by the company, dependent on routes and package volume. The rounded-nose design of fiberglass hood of the UPS package car was patented by the company in 1965.<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup></p><p><a href="/wiki/Morgan_Olson" title="Morgan Olson">Morgan Olson</a> (Grumman Olson), <a href="/wiki/Utilimaster_Corporation" title="Utilimaster Corporation">Union City Body</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Utilimaster_Corporation" title="Utilimaster Corporation">Utilimaster</a> manufacture the bodies for UPS delivery vans; while older vehicles were based on Ford or General Motors P-chassis, vehicles manufactured since the 1990s are based on Navistar, Freightliner, or Workhorse chassis. Originally, UPS delivery vehicles were equipped with manual transmissions and steering, although many newer vehicles are updated with automatic transmissions.  
</p><p>For lower-volume delivery routes, UPS utilizes production-based vehicles, including minivans (including the <a href="/wiki/Ford_Transit_Connect" title="Ford Transit Connect">Ford Transit Connect</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dodge_Caravan" title="Dodge Caravan">Dodge Grand Caravan C/V</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Mercedes-Benz_Sprinter" title="Mercedes-Benz Sprinter">Mercedes-Benz (Dodge/Freightliner) Sprinter</a> box vans. UPS has ordered <a href="/wiki/Modec" title="Modec">Modec</a> <a href="/wiki/Electric_van" class="mw-redirect" title="Electric van">electric vans</a> for its UK and German fleets. Energy costs play a huge part in the potential profitability of package delivery companies.<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup></p>

<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Tractor-trailers">Tractor-trailers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_Parcel_Service&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Tractor-trailers" data-section="15" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>UPS commonly refers to its ground package tractor-trailers as "feeders"; these vehicles are distinct from the <a href="/wiki/UPS_Freight" title="UPS Freight">UPS Freight</a> fleet. In line with its delivery vehicles, nearly all UPS <a href="/wiki/Tractor_unit" title="Tractor unit">tractor units</a> are painted in a Pullman brown livery.  Prior to the 1990s, many tractors were of <a href="/wiki/Cab_over" title="Cab over">cabover</a> configuration; as trailer-length laws were revised in the United States, the company transitioned towards the use of conventional-style semitractor. As of 2018, nearly the entire UPS semitractor fleet consists of conventional-cab trucks.
</p><p>In North America, UPS utilizes a fleet of trucks from several manufacturers, including <a href="/wiki/Mack_Trucks" title="Mack Trucks">Mack Trucks</a> (Pinnacle and Vision) and <a href="/wiki/Navistar_International" title="Navistar International">Navistar</a> (International <a href="/wiki/International_ProStar" title="International ProStar">ProStar+</a> and <a href="/wiki/International_9000" title="International 9000">9900i</a>), along with PACCAR vehicles (<a href="/wiki/Kenworth" title="Kenworth">Kenworth</a> T800, T660 and T680). Older vehicles in the fleet include Ford/Sterling tractors (<a href="/wiki/Ford_L-Series" class="mw-redirect" title="Ford L-Series">Aeromax/A-Line</a>), Freightliner (<a href="/wiki/Freightliner_Argosy" title="Freightliner Argosy">Argosy</a>, the final cabover truck used by UPS); Mack CH and International 8100 tractors are in the process of being phased out.  
</p><p>Previous trucks used by UPS include the Ford L9000, Ford Aeromax, International 8100/8200, International 9670, GMC Astro, Peterbilt 362, Ford L9000, Ford Aeromax, WhiteGMC High Cabover/Road Commander II, Mack CH, Mack MH UltraLiner, Mack F, and Diamond T/Diamond Reo cabovers. At one time<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Chronological_items" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers"><span title="The time period mentioned near this tag is ambiguous. (May 2017)">when?</span></a></i>]</sup>, UPS used electric-powered trucks (made by <a href="/wiki/White_Motor_Company" title="White Motor Company">White Motors</a>) for deliveries in Manhattan, NYC. There were only a few hundred of them, but they were notable for their "spooky silence" when running<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>.
</p><p>Painted a light gray, company-owned <a href="/wiki/Semi-trailer" title="Semi-trailer">semitrailers</a> are distinguished by a large UPS emblem on the forward portion of the trailer sides; newer trailers are distinguished by the fitment of <a href="/wiki/Trailer_skirt" title="Trailer skirt">trailer skirts</a>. Designed in several configurations, UPS uses several lengths of trailers (28, 45, 48, and 53 feet). Along with standard flat-bottom van trailers, there are drop-frame trailers (largely phased out of the fleet) and trailer-on-flatcar trailers (TOFC); the latter are trailers designed for transport on railroad cars.
</p><p>Dependent on state and provincial regulations, many UPS short trailers are towed in tandem.

<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Cargo_airline">Cargo airline</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_Parcel_Service&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Cargo airline" data-section="18" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/UPS_Airlines" title="UPS Airlines">UPS Airlines</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:282px;"><a href="/wiki/File:N578UP_-_United_Parcel_Service_(UPS)_-_Boeing_747-45E(BCF)_-_HKG_(13219958434).jpg" class="image"><noscript><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/82/N578UP_-_United_Parcel_Service_%28UPS%29_-_Boeing_747-45E%28BCF%29_-_HKG_%2813219958434%29.jpg/280px-N578UP_-_United_Parcel_Service_%28UPS%29_-_Boeing_747-45E%28BCF%29_-_HKG_%2813219958434%29.jpg" decoding="async" width="280" height="187" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="4995" data-file-height="3330"></noscript><span class="lazy-image-placeholder" style="width: 280px;height: 187px;" data-src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/82/N578UP_-_United_Parcel_Service_%28UPS%29_-_Boeing_747-45E%28BCF%29_-_HKG_%2813219958434%29.jpg/280px-N578UP_-_United_Parcel_Service_%28UPS%29_-_Boeing_747-45E%28BCF%29_-_HKG_%2813219958434%29.jpg" data-alt="" data-width="280" data-height="187" data-class="thumbimage"> </span></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:N578UP_-_United_Parcel_Service_(UPS)_-_Boeing_747-45E(BCF)_-_HKG_(13219958434).jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>UPS Boeing 747-400BCF landing at <a href="/wiki/Hong_Kong_International_Airport" title="Hong Kong International Airport">Hong Kong International Airport</a> in 2014</div></div></div>
<p>The third-largest cargo airline worldwide, UPS Airlines flies to over 800 destinations worldwide (the most of any airline, cargo or passenger).  Formed in 1988, the airline is headquartered in Louisville, Kentucky (home to <a href="/wiki/WorldPort_(UPS_air_hub)" class="mw-redirect" title="WorldPort (UPS air hub)">Worldport</a>, its worldwide air hub) with additional hubs in the United States located in <a href="/wiki/Rockford,_Illinois" title="Rockford, Illinois">Rockford, Illinois</a> and <a href="/wiki/Philadelphia" title="Philadelphia">Philadelphia</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_50-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-50">[50]</a></sup>  Outside of North America, a hub in <a href="/wiki/Cologne" title="Cologne">Cologne, Germany</a> services Europe;<sup id="cite_ref-:1_60-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-60">[60]</a></sup> in Asia, UPS Airlines operates a facility in Hong Kong and two hubs in mainland China.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_50-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-50">[50]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:2_61-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:2-61">[61]</a></sup></p><p>Consisting of a fleet of over 260 aircraft,<sup id="cite_ref-:2_61-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:2-61">[61]</a></sup> the airline competes directly against <a href="/wiki/FedEx_Express" title="FedEx Express">FedEx Express</a>, facilitating air shipments by the company in over 200 countries and territories worldwide.<sup id="cite_ref-:1_60-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-60">[60]</a></sup></p>

The FAA's Part 135 Standard certification allows company to operate an unlimited number of drones of any size (even allowing the cargo to exceed 55 pounds) even at with an unlimited number of remote operators in command.<sup id="cite_ref-63" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup></p>

<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Brown">Brown</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_Parcel_Service&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Brown" data-section="21" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>The brown color that UPS uses on its vehicles and uniforms is called <i><a href="/wiki/Pullman_brown" class="mw-redirect" title="Pullman brown">Pullman brown</a></i>. Company founder James E. Casey originally wanted company vehicles to use a yellow paint scheme, but one of his partners, Charlie Soderstrom, stated that a yellow vehicle would be hard to keep clean and that <a href="/wiki/Pullman_(car_or_coach)" title="Pullman (car or coach)">Pullman railroad cars</a> were brown for just that reason.<sup id="cite_ref-cnn_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cnn-9">[9]</a></sup></p><p>During the 2000s, the company used the familiarity of its color scheme in an advertising slogan: <i>"What can Brown do for you?"</i><sup id="cite_ref-64" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Font">Font</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_Parcel_Service&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Font" data-section="22" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>UPS commissioned brand consultancy FutureBrand to develop its own font, <i>UPS Sans</i>, for use in marketing and communication material. <i>UPS Sans</i> was created by slightly altering certain parts of <a href="/wiki/FSI_FontShop_International" class="mw-redirect" title="FSI FontShop International">FSI FontShop International</a>'s font <i><a href="/wiki/FF_Dax" title="FF Dax">FF Dax</a></i> without permission. This has resulted in an agreement between FSI FontShop International and FutureBrand to avoid litigation.<sup id="cite_ref-65" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup></p>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(7)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Environmental_record">Environmental record</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_Parcel_Service&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Environmental record" data-section="23" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-7 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-7">
<p>As of 2013, UPS has over 104,900 vehicles in operation worldwide including nearly 7,000 alternative-fuel vehicles.<sup id="cite_ref-About_1-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-About-1">[1]</a></sup> In May 2008, UPS placed an order for 200 <a href="/wiki/Hybrid_electric_vehicle" title="Hybrid electric vehicle">hybrid electric vehicles</a> (adding to the 50 it had at that point) and 300 <a href="/wiki/Compressed_natural_gas" title="Compressed natural gas">compressed natural gas</a> (which are 20% more fuel efficient, and add to the 800 it already has) vehicles from <a href="/wiki/Daimler_Trucks_North_America" title="Daimler Trucks North America">Daimler Trucks North America</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-66" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-67" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-68" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup> The company added 200 hybrid electric vehicles to its fleet in 2016.<sup id="cite_ref-69" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup></p><p>UPS received a "striding" rating of 80 points out of 100 totals on the environmental scorecard by the Climate Counts Group for its efforts to lessen the company's impact on the environment.<sup id="cite_ref-70" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup> UPS has also been awarded the Clean Air Excellence Award by the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Environmental_Protection_Agency" title="United States Environmental Protection Agency">United States Environmental Protection Agency</a> because of the alternative fuel program it has developed.<sup id="cite_ref-71" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup></p><p>In October 2009, UPS became the first small-package carrier to offer customers the chance to buy <a href="/wiki/Carbon_offset" title="Carbon offset">carbon offsets</a> to neutralize the greenhouse gas emissions generated by the transport of their packages. Although initially only available on ups.com and to high-volume shippers, they are now widely available through UPS shipping systems and UPS Ready third-party shipping systems.<sup id="cite_ref-72" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-72">[72]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-73" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-73">[73]</a></sup></p>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(8)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Controversy">Controversy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_Parcel_Service&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Controversy" data-section="24" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-8 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-8">
<p>On May 26, 2017, a federal judge ordered United Parcel Service to pay nearly $247 million in damages and penalties for illegally shipping large amounts of untaxed cigarettes in <a href="/wiki/New_York_(state)" title="New York (state)">New York</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-74" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-74">[74]</a></sup></p>
