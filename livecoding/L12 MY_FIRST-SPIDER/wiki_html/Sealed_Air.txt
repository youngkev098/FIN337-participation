</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(1)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sealed_Air&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History" data-section="1" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-1 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-1">
<p>In 1957, American engineer Alfred W. Fielding and Swiss inventor Marc Chavannes attempted to invent plastic wallpaper with a paper backing.<sup id="cite_ref-stevens_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-stevens-6">[6]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-forbes1_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-forbes1-7">[7]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-investors_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-investors-8">[8]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-inc_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-inc-9">[9]</a></sup> While the wallpaper failed, Fielding and Chavannes later realized that what they had come up with could be used for packing material.<sup id="cite_ref-stevens_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-stevens-6">[6]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-investors_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-investors-8">[8]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-inc_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-inc-9">[9]</a></sup> Sealed Air was founded in 1960 based on this invention of <a href="/wiki/Bubble_Wrap" class="mw-redirect" title="Bubble Wrap">Bubble Wrap</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-stevens_6-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-stevens-6">[6]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-forbes1_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-forbes1-7">[7]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-investors_8-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-investors-8">[8]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-inc_9-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-inc-9">[9]</a></sup> The same year, Sealed Air raised $85,000 in its <a href="/wiki/Initial_public_offering" title="Initial public offering">initial public offering</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-encyclopedia_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-encyclopedia-10">[10]</a></sup> Fielding served as executive vice president and director of Sealed Air until his retirement in 1987, while Chavannes worked mostly as a consultant.<sup id="cite_ref-investors_8-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-investors-8">[8]</a></sup></p><p>T.J. Dermot Dunphy served as CEO from 1971 to 2000.<sup id="cite_ref-investors_8-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-investors-8">[8]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-encyclopedia_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-encyclopedia-10">[10]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-forbesdunphy_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-forbesdunphy-11">[11]</a></sup> An <a href="/wiki/Oxford_University" class="mw-redirect" title="Oxford University">Oxford University</a> graduate who received his <a href="/wiki/Master_of_Business_Administration" title="Master of Business Administration">MBA</a> from <a href="/wiki/Harvard_Business_School" title="Harvard Business School">Harvard Business School</a>, he became chairman of Kildare Enterprises, LLC in November 2000 after leaving Sealed Air.<sup id="cite_ref-encyclopedia_10-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-encyclopedia-10">[10]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-forbesdunphy_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-forbesdunphy-11">[11]</a></sup> During his tenure at Sealed Air, sales grew from $5 million to $3 billion.<sup id="cite_ref-smithsonian_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-smithsonian-12">[12]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-3dprinting_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3dprinting-13">[13]</a></sup></p><p>William Hickey served as CEO from the year 2000 to March 2013.<sup id="cite_ref-forbeshickey_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-forbeshickey-14">[14]</a></sup> He previously served in several capacities at Sealed Air, including COO, executive vice president, CFO, vice president, and general manager of the Food Packaging Division and the Cellu Products Division.<sup id="cite_ref-forbeshickey_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-forbeshickey-14">[14]</a></sup> Before working for Sealed Air, he was CPA at Arthur Young and CFO of <a href="/wiki/W._R._Grace_and_Company" title="W. R. Grace and Company">W. R. Grace and Company</a>'s Latin American operations.<sup id="cite_ref-forbeshickey_14-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-forbeshickey-14">[14]</a></sup></p><p>Sealed Air produced the foam that was ignited in the 2003 <a href="/wiki/The_Station_nightclub_fire" title="The Station nightclub fire">Station nightclub fire</a> and has <a href="/wiki/The_Station_nightclub_fire#Civil_settlements" title="The Station nightclub fire">paid $25 million to victims</a> in various <a href="/wiki/Settlement_(litigation)" title="Settlement (litigation)">civil settlements</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Barylick2012_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Barylick2012-15">[15]</a></sup></p><p>In March 2013, Jérôme Péribère took over as CEO and President of Sealed Air.<sup id="cite_ref-forbesperibere_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-forbesperibere-16">[16]</a></sup> He obtained his business economics and finance degree from <a href="/wiki/Sciences_Po" title="Sciences Po">Institut d'études politiques</a> in <a href="/wiki/Paris" title="Paris">Paris</a>, <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-forbesperibere_16-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-forbesperibere-16">[16]</a></sup> He previously served as president and COO of Sealed Air before taking over as CEO, and prior to joining Sealed Air, he worked in several managerial roles with the <a href="/wiki/Dow_Chemical_Company" title="Dow Chemical Company">Dow Chemical Company</a> from 1977 to 2012.<sup id="cite_ref-forbesperibere_16-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-forbesperibere-16">[16]</a></sup></p><p>On July 23, 2014, Sealed Air announced that it would be moving its global headquarters to <a href="/wiki/Charlotte,_North_Carolina" title="Charlotte, North Carolina">Charlotte</a>, <a href="/wiki/North_Carolina" title="North Carolina">North Carolina</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> In January 2018, Ted Doheny took over as CEO.<sup id="cite_ref-charlotteobserver_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-charlotteobserver-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(2)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Acquisitions">Acquisitions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sealed_Air&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Acquisitions" data-section="2" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-2 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-2">
<p>In 1970, Sealed Air acquired Smith Packaging Ltd., which was later renamed Sealed Air of Canada, Ltd., marking Sealed Air's first international move.<sup id="cite_ref-encyclopedia_10-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-encyclopedia-10">[10]</a></sup> In 1971, Sealed Air began marketing a new product; by laminating the AirCap cushioning to paper, the company now had Mail Lite padded shipping envelopes.<sup id="cite_ref-encyclopedia_10-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-encyclopedia-10">[10]</a></sup></p><p>In 1973, Sealed Air began marketing Ply-Mask, a pressure-sensitive polyethylene film used to protect delicate surfaces from scratches and the company's first product not based on its bubble technology.<sup id="cite_ref-encyclopedia_10-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-encyclopedia-10">[10]</a></sup> The same year, Sealed Air brought their market to Europe by acquiring 10 percent of Sibco Universal, S.A., a French manufacturing firm.<sup id="cite_ref-encyclopedia_10-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-encyclopedia-10">[10]</a></sup> Over the next few years, Sealed Air bought out the rest of Sibco and came up with the Sealed Air Solar Blanket.<sup id="cite_ref-encyclopedia_10-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-encyclopedia-10">[10]</a></sup></p><p>Acquired in 1977, Instapak foam is an expanding <a href="/wiki/Polyurethane" title="Polyurethane">polyurethane</a> foam that comes in a variety of densities. Used primarily for shipping, the foam-in-bag process molds to the shape of the object and expands to fill the void space of its shipping container.<sup id="cite_ref-encyclopedia_10-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-encyclopedia-10">[10]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-synergy_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-synergy-18">[18]</a></sup></p><p>Sealed Air acquired Cellu Products Co. and Dri-Loc in 1983, Jiffy in 1987, Sentinel in 1991, and the Shurtuff Division of <a href="/wiki/Hudson_Cotton_Manufacturing_Company" title="Hudson Cotton Manufacturing Company">Shuford Mills, Inc.</a> in 1993.<sup id="cite_ref-encyclopedia_10-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-encyclopedia-10">[10]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-ycharts_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ycharts-19">[19]</a></sup> In 1994, Sealed Air followed up with the further acquisitions of Hereford Paper and Allied Products Ltd., Sup-Air-Pack, Fill Air, and packaging companies based in <a href="/wiki/Norway" title="Norway">Norway</a>, France, and <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a>.
</p><p>In 1998, Sealed Air acquired Cryovac from W.R. Grace for $4.9 billion.<sup id="cite_ref-forbes1_7-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-forbes1-7">[7]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-nyt_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nyt-20">[20]</a></sup> In June 2000, Sealed Air purchased Dolphin Packaging for $119 million, to better serve its European customers.<sup id="cite_ref-nyt1_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nyt1-21">[21]</a></sup> In October 2011, Sealed Air acquired <a href="/wiki/Diversey,_Inc." title="Diversey, Inc.">Diversey Holdings, Inc</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-nyt_20-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nyt-20">[20]</a></sup> until its acquisition by <a href="/wiki/Bain_Capital" title="Bain Capital">Bain Capital</a> in September 2017.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-22">[22]</a></sup></p><p>In October 2017, Sealed Air acquired Fagerdala Singapore Pte Ltd., a manufacturer and fabricator of <a href="/wiki/Polyethylene" title="Polyethylene">polyethylene</a> foam.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(3)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Operations">Operations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sealed_Air&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Operations" data-section="3" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-3 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-3">
<p>Sealed Air's food care division makes packaging for the food and beverage industry, while its product care division produces protective and specialty packaging materials for a wide range of goods.<sup id="cite_ref-SEC2012_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SEC2012-4">[4]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-journaltimes_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-journaltimes-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Brands">Brands</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sealed_Air&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Brands" data-section="4" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<h4 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Bubble_Wrap">Bubble Wrap</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sealed_Air&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Bubble Wrap" data-section="5" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h4>
<p>Initially created as a failed wallpaper, Bubble was subsequently used as a greenhouse insulator.<sup id="cite_ref-encyclopedia_10-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-encyclopedia-10">[10]</a></sup> Finally, it took on its best-known use as a packaging material.<sup id="cite_ref-encyclopedia_10-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-encyclopedia-10">[10]</a></sup> In its earliest form, Bubble Wrap suffered from leaky bubbles, but by the mid 1960s a special coating was developed to prevent the bubbles from losing air.<sup id="cite_ref-encyclopedia_10-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-encyclopedia-10">[10]</a></sup> In 1969, Sealed Air reported $4 million in sales, mostly attributed to Bubble Wrap, as it was still a proprietary product at that time.<sup id="cite_ref-encyclopedia_10-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-encyclopedia-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h4 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Cryovac">Cryovac</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sealed_Air&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Cryovac" data-section="6" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h4>
<p>Cryovac is a thin plastic, used to shrink or wrap objects. Depending on the type of job required of it, the plastic comes in a variety of thickness and durability.<sup id="cite_ref-mdapackaging_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mdapackaging-24">[24]</a></sup></p><p>One of the uses of Cryovac is to wrap food. Once wrapped, most of the air in the package is removed to prevent oxidation and inhibit the growth of most pathogens.This process also gives food a longer shelf life in the refrigerator or freezer and makes freezer burn nearly impossible.<sup id="cite_ref-takepart_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-takepart-25">[25]</a></sup> Cryovac Inc., a <a href="/wiki/South_Carolina" title="South Carolina">South Carolina</a>-based company, created this product in the 1950 to extend the shipping distance of freshly slaughtered turkeys for <a href="/wiki/Thanksgiving" title="Thanksgiving">Thanksgiving</a> and <a href="/wiki/Christmas" title="Christmas">Christmas</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-takepart_25-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-takepart-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
