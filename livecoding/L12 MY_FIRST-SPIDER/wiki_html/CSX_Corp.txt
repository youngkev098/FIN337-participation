			<div id="bodyContent" class="content"><div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><script>function mfTempOpenSection(id){var block=document.getElementById("mf-section-"+id);block.className+=" open-block";block.previousSibling.className+=" open-block";}</script><div class="mw-parser-output"><section class="mf-section-0" id="mf-section-0">
<p><b>CSX Corporation</b> is an <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">American</a> <a href="/wiki/Holding_company" title="Holding company">holding company</a> focused on <a href="/wiki/Railroad" class="mw-redirect" title="Railroad">rail transportation</a> and <a href="/wiki/Real_estate" title="Real estate">real estate</a> in <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a>, among other industries. The company was established in 1980 as part of the <a href="/wiki/Chessie_System" title="Chessie System">Chessie System</a> and <a href="/wiki/Seaboard_Coast_Line_Industries" title="Seaboard Coast Line Industries">Seaboard Coast Line Industries</a> merger. The various railroads of the former Chessie System and Seaboard Coast Line Industries that are now owned by CSX Corporation were eventually merged into a single line in 1986 and it became known as <a href="/wiki/CSX_Transportation" title="CSX Transportation">CSX Transportation</a>. CSX Corporation currently has a number of subsidiaries beyond CSX Transportation. Based in <a href="/wiki/Richmond,_Virginia" title="Richmond, Virginia">Richmond, Virginia</a>, <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">USA</a> after the merger, in 2003 the CSX Corporation headquarters moved to <a href="/wiki/Jacksonville,_Florida" title="Jacksonville, Florida">Jacksonville, Florida</a>. CSX is a Fortune 500 company.

</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(1)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Subsidiaries_and_divisions">Subsidiaries and divisions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=CSX_Corporation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Subsidiaries and divisions" data-section="1" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-1 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-1">
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="CSX_Transportation">CSX Transportation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=CSX_Corporation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: CSX Transportation" data-section="2" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/CSX_Transportation" title="CSX Transportation">CSX Transportation</a> is a <a href="/wiki/Class_I_railroad" class="mw-redirect" title="Class I railroad">Class I railroad</a> operating in the eastern <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> and the Canadian provinces of <a href="/wiki/Ontario" title="Ontario">Ontario</a> and <a href="/wiki/Quebec" title="Quebec">Quebec</a>. The railroad operates approximately 21,000 route miles (34,000 km) of track.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p><p>As of December 30, 2016, CSX Transportation served population centers in 23 states east of the Mississippi River, the District of Columbia and the Canadian provinces of Ontario and Quebec. As of December 30, 2016, it had access to over 70 ocean, river and lake port terminals along the Atlantic and Gulf Coasts, the Mississippi River, the Great Lakes and the St. Lawrence Seaway.
</p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Conrail">Conrail</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=CSX_Corporation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Conrail" data-section="3" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Conrail" title="Conrail">Conrail</a> was the primary <a href="/wiki/Class_I_railroad" class="mw-redirect" title="Class I railroad">Class I railroad</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Northeastern_United_States" title="Northeastern United States">Northeastern United States</a> between 1976 and 1999. The trade name Conrail is a <a href="/wiki/Portmanteau" title="Portmanteau">portmanteau</a> based on the company's legal name(Consolidated Rail Corporation), and while it no longer operates trains it <a href="/wiki/Conrail_Shared_Assets_Operations" title="Conrail Shared Assets Operations">continues to do business</a> as an asset management and network services provider in three Shared Assets Areas that were excluded from the division of its operations during its acquisition by CSX Corporation and the <a href="/wiki/Norfolk_Southern_Railway" title="Norfolk Southern Railway">Norfolk Southern Railway</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Miracle_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Miracle-8">[8]</a></sup></p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Federal_government_of_the_United_States" title="Federal government of the United States">federal government</a> created Conrail to take over the lines of multiple <a href="/wiki/Bankrupt" class="mw-redirect" title="Bankrupt">bankrupt</a> carriers, including the <a href="/wiki/Penn_Central_Transportation_Company" title="Penn Central Transportation Company">Penn Central Transportation Company</a> and <a href="/wiki/Erie_Lackawanna_Railway" title="Erie Lackawanna Railway">Erie Lackawanna Railway</a>. After railroad regulations were lifted by the <a href="/wiki/Railroad_Revitalization_and_Regulatory_Reform_Act" title="Railroad Revitalization and Regulatory Reform Act">4R Act</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Staggers_Rail_Act" title="Staggers Rail Act">Staggers Act</a>, Conrail began to turn a profit in the 1980s and was privatized in 1987. The two remaining <a href="/wiki/Class_I_railroad" class="mw-redirect" title="Class I railroad">Class I railroads</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Eastern_United_States" title="Eastern United States">East</a>, CSX Transportation and the Norfolk Southern Railway (NS), agreed in 1997 to acquire the system and split it into two roughly-equal parts (alongside three residual shared-assets areas), returning rail freight competition to the Northeast by essentially undoing the 1968 merger of the <a href="/wiki/Pennsylvania_Railroad" title="Pennsylvania Railroad">Pennsylvania Railroad</a> and <a href="/wiki/New_York_Central_Railroad" title="New York Central Railroad">New York Central Railroad</a> that created <a href="/wiki/Penn_Central_Transportation_Company" title="Penn Central Transportation Company">Penn Central</a>. Following approval by the <a href="/wiki/Surface_Transportation_Board" title="Surface Transportation Board">Surface Transportation Board</a>, CSX and NS took control in August 1998, and on June 1, 1999 began operating their respective portions of Conrail.<sup id="cite_ref-Miracle_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Miracle-8">[8]</a></sup></p><p>The old company remains a jointly-owned subsidiary, with CSX and NS owning respectively 42 percent and 58 percent of its <a href="/wiki/Stock" title="Stock">stock</a>, corresponding to how much of Conrail's assets they acquired. Each parent, however, has an equal <a href="/wiki/Voting_interest" title="Voting interest">voting interest</a>. The primary asset retained by Conrail is ownership of the three <a href="/wiki/Shared_Assets_Areas" class="mw-redirect" title="Shared Assets Areas">Shared Assets Areas</a> in <a href="/wiki/New_Jersey" title="New Jersey">New Jersey</a>, <a href="/wiki/Philadelphia" title="Philadelphia">Philadelphia</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Detroit" title="Detroit">Detroit</a>. Both CSX and NS have the right to serve all shippers in these areas, paying Conrail for the cost of maintaining and improving <a href="/wiki/Rail_tracks" class="mw-redirect" title="Rail tracks">trackage</a>. They also make use of Conrail to perform <a href="/wiki/Switching_and_terminal_railroad" title="Switching and terminal railroad">switching and terminal</a> services within the areas, but not as a <a href="/wiki/Common_carrier" title="Common carrier">common carrier</a>, since contracts are signed between shippers and CSX or NS. Conrail also retains various support facilities including <a href="/wiki/Maintenance-of-way" class="mw-redirect" title="Maintenance-of-way">maintenance-of-way</a> and training, as well as a 51 percent share in the <a href="/wiki/Indiana_Harbor_Belt_Railroad" title="Indiana Harbor Belt Railroad">Indiana Harbor Belt Railroad</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Miracle_8-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Miracle-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Winston-Salem_Southbound_Railway">Winston-Salem Southbound Railway</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=CSX_Corporation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Winston-Salem Southbound Railway" data-section="4" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Winston-Salem_Southbound_Railway" title="Winston-Salem Southbound Railway">Winston-Salem Southbound Railway</a> is a 90-mile (140 km) <a href="/wiki/Short-line_railroad" class="mw-redirect" title="Short-line railroad">short-line railroad</a> jointly held by <a href="/wiki/CSX_Transportation" title="CSX Transportation">CSX Transportation</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Norfolk_Southern_Railway" title="Norfolk Southern Railway">Norfolk Southern Railway</a>, which provides it with equipment. It connects with Norfolk Southern at the north end in <a href="/wiki/Winston-Salem,_NC" class="mw-redirect" title="Winston-Salem, NC">Winston-Salem</a>, CSX at the south end in <a href="/wiki/Wadesboro,_NC" class="mw-redirect" title="Wadesboro, NC">Wadesboro</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-radioreference_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-radioreference-9">[9]</a></sup> and in between with NS at <a href="/wiki/Lexington,_NC" class="mw-redirect" title="Lexington, NC">Lexington</a> and <a href="/w/index.php?title=Whitney,_North_Carolina&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Whitney, North Carolina (page does not exist)">Whitney</a>, the subsidiary <a href="/wiki/High_Point,_Thomasville_and_Denton_Railroad" title="High Point, Thomasville and Denton Railroad">High Point, Thomasville and Denton Railroad</a> at <a href="/w/index.php?title=High_Rock,_North_Carolina&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="High Rock, North Carolina (page does not exist)">High Rock</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Aberdeen,_Carolina_and_Western_Railway" title="Aberdeen, Carolina and Western Railway">Aberdeen, Carolina and Western Railway</a> at <a href="/wiki/Norwood,_NC" class="mw-redirect" title="Norwood, NC">Norwood</a>. Originally owned jointly by the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Coast_Line_Railroad" title="Atlantic Coast Line Railroad">Atlantic Coast Line Railroad</a> and <a href="/wiki/Norfolk_and_Western_Railway" title="Norfolk and Western Railway">Norfolk and Western Railway</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> predecessors to CSX and NS, it was completed in November 1910.<sup id="cite_ref-shortline_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-shortline-11">[11]</a></sup></p><p>Commodities commonly carried by the railroad are grain, sand, gravel, stone, forest products, paper products, coal, coke, cement, clay fertilizer, aluminum, chemicals, iron, and steel. Its principal shippers are Corn Products Company of Winston-Salem, a manufacturer of corn syrup and related products, and Owens Brockway Glass Company of Eller, a manufacturer of glass products.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>

<p>CSX Corporation was formed on November 1, 1980, by combining the railroads of the former <a href="/wiki/Chessie_System" title="Chessie System">Chessie System</a> with <a href="/wiki/Seaboard_Coast_Line_Industries" title="Seaboard Coast Line Industries">Seaboard Coast Line Industries</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-CSXMergerFamilyTree_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CSXMergerFamilyTree-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Csx_corp_logo.svg" class="image"><noscript><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/15/Csx_corp_logo.svg/220px-Csx_corp_logo.svg.png" decoding="async" width="220" height="108" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="630" data-file-height="310"></noscript><span class="lazy-image-placeholder" style="width: 220px;height: 108px;" data-src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/15/Csx_corp_logo.svg/220px-Csx_corp_logo.svg.png" data-alt="" data-width="220" data-height="108" data-srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/15/Csx_corp_logo.svg/330px-Csx_corp_logo.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/15/Csx_corp_logo.svg/440px-Csx_corp_logo.svg.png 2x" data-class="thumbimage"> </span></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Csx_corp_logo.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Original logo for the <a class="mw-selflink selflink">CSX Corporation</a>, emphasizing the "multiplication symbol" X</div></div></div>
<p>The name came about during merger talks between Chessie System and SCL, commonly called "Chessie" and "Seaboard". The company chairmen said it was important for the new name to include neither of those names because it was a partnership. Employees were asked for suggestions, most of which consisted of combinations of the initials. At the same time, a temporary shorthand name was needed for discussions with the <a href="/wiki/Interstate_Commerce_Commission" title="Interstate Commerce Commission">Interstate Commerce Commission</a>. "CSC" was chosen but belonged to a trucking company in <a href="/wiki/Virginia" title="Virginia">Virginia</a>. "CSM" (for "Chessie-Seaboard Merger") was also taken. The lawyers decided to use "CSX", and the name stuck. In the public announcement, it was said that "CSX is singularly appropriate. C can stand for Chessie, S for Seaboard, and X, which actually has no meaning." However, an August 9, 2016, article on the <i>Railway Age</i> website stated that " ... the 'X' was for 'Consolidated' ".<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> The T had to be added to CSX when used as a <a href="/wiki/Reporting_mark" title="Reporting mark">reporting mark</a> because reporting marks that end in X means that the car is owned by a leasing company or private car owner. The company introduced its current slogan, "How Tomorrow Moves", in 2008.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p><p>The founding chairman of CSX Corporation was Prime F. Osborn III of Seaboard,<sup id="cite_ref-nieda_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nieda-20">[20]</a></sup> for whom Jacksonville's <a href="/wiki/Prime_F._Osborn_III_Convention_Center" title="Prime F. Osborn III Convention Center">Prime F. Osborn III Convention Center</a> is named. The first CEO and second chairman was Hays T. Watkins Jr. of Chessie. Watkins was succeeded by <a href="/wiki/John_W._Snow" title="John W. Snow">John W. Snow</a> as CEO in 1989 and as chairman in 1991. When Snow left the company in 2003 to become <a href="/wiki/United_States_Secretary_of_the_Treasury" title="United States Secretary of the Treasury">United States Secretary of the Treasury</a>, Michael J. Ward, who then headed CSX Transportation, was promoted to succeed him. Overall in 2003, Ward took on the positions of chairman, president, and  CEO.<sup id="cite_ref-lworkingonrailroad_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-lworkingonrailroad-21">[21]</a></sup> When president <a href="/wiki/Oscar_Munoz_(executive)" title="Oscar Munoz (executive)">Oscar Munoz</a> left CSX in September 2015 after obtaining the role earlier that year from Ward, the company underwent several management changes, with Clarence Gooden appointed president.<sup id="cite_ref-jelevatedotpresident_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-jelevatedotpresident-22">[22]</a></sup></p><p>The company went through major leadership changes in 2017 when activist investor Mantle Ridge, a <a href="/wiki/Hedge_fund" title="Hedge fund">hedge fund</a> that held 4.9% of CSX's stock, demanded a change in the board, that Michael Ward step down as CEO, that the company cut <a href="/wiki/Middle_management" title="Middle management">middle management</a>, and that the company hire <a href="/wiki/E._Hunter_Harrison" title="E. Hunter Harrison">Hunter Harrison</a>, known for leading the turnaround three other railroads, as CEO.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup>  Within months of Harrison's hiring in spring 2017, several members of CSX's executive management team stepped down. Harrison died on December 16, 2017 and shortly thereafter Chief Operating Officer James M. Foote was named president and chief executive officer.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FooteCEO_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FooteCEO-25">[25]</a></sup></p><p>In March 2018, Foote, said CSX would follow-through on Harrison's plans to transform the company and move it from a traditional railroad model to a scheduled railroad model in order to reduce costs and improve the quality of service. Part of this effort includes reducing the number of locomotives in service from 3,000 in late 2017 to between 2,370 and 2,420 in 2020. The company also plans to reduce the number of rail cars it owns from 136,000 in late 2017 to between 104,000 and 109,000 in 2020. In 2017, CSX cut its workforce by 3,300 employees. In 2018, roughly 2,200 jobs were cut. A further reduction of 4,000 positions is planned by 2020. CSX estimates that after these cuts it will have a workforce of about 21,000 people. 
</p><p>CSX is also trying to increase profits by monetizing some of its real estate. As of early 2018, the company planned to generate $800 million by 2020 by selling off some railroad lines and other real estate. As of the same date, CSX held real estate in 23 states, the District of Columbia, and two Canadian provinces.
</p>

Total revenue for 2018 was $12.25 billion, an increase from $11.408 billion the previous year. Operating income was $4.869 billion, up from $3.72 billion in 2017, while earnings before income taxes were $4.304 billion, compared to $3.142 billion the previous year.

<p>The CSX Transportation Building is a 251 feet (77 meters) high-rise office building in  <a href="/wiki/Jacksonville,_Florida" title="Jacksonville, Florida">Jacksonville, Florida</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-EMP_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EMP-29">[29]</a></sup> Completed in 1960, the building currently serves as headquarters for CSX Corporation.<sup id="cite_ref-UNF_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-UNF-30">[30]</a></sup> The building is located in the <a href="/wiki/Northbank_(Jacksonville)" class="mw-redirect" title="Northbank (Jacksonville)">Northbank</a> area of <a href="/wiki/Downtown_Jacksonville" title="Downtown Jacksonville">Downtown Jacksonville</a>, along the banks of the <a href="/wiki/St._Johns_River" title="St. Johns River">St. Johns River</a>. Its former names include the Atlantic Coast Line Building  and  the Seaboard Coastline Railroad Building.<sup id="cite_ref-FNDR_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FNDR-31">[31]</a></sup> Designed by <a href="/wiki/KBJ_Architects" title="KBJ Architects">KBJ Architects</a>, the CSX Transportation Building is a <a href="/wiki/LEED_certified" class="mw-redirect" title="LEED certified">LEED certified</a> building and is an example of <a href="/wiki/Mid-century_modern" title="Mid-century modern"> mid-century modern</a> and <a href="/wiki/International_style_(architecture)" class="mw-redirect" title="International style (architecture)">international style</a> architecture.<sup id="cite_ref-gb&amp;d_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gb&amp;d-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
