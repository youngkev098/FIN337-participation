Based in <a href="/wiki/Wilsonville,_Oregon" title="Wilsonville, Oregon">Wilsonville, Oregon</a>, United States and founded in 1978, the company makes thermal cameras and components for a wide variety of commercial and government applications.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> FLIR is a component of the <a href="/wiki/S%26P_500" class="mw-redirect" title="S&amp;P 500">S&amp;P 500</a> index with annual revenues in excess of $1.5 billion annually as of 2014. In the spring of 2013, Andrew C. Teich became FLIR's <a href="/wiki/Chief_executive_officer" title="Chief executive officer">chief executive officer</a> and president after the retirement of Earl Lewis. </section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(1)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=FLIR_Systems&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History" data-section="1" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-1 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-1">
<p>FLIR took its name from the <a href="/wiki/Acronym" title="Acronym">acronym</a> for <a href="/wiki/FLIR" class="mw-redirect" title="FLIR">forward-looking Infrared</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> The company began in 1978 with airborne <a href="/wiki/Infrared" title="Infrared">IR</a> systems, and developed from 1978 to 2004 through product development and acquisitions of related companies.<sup id="cite_ref-about_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-about-5">[5]</a></sup> Originally based in <a href="/wiki/Tigard,_Oregon" title="Tigard, Oregon">Tigard, Oregon</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-hughes_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hughes-6">[6]</a></sup> the company relocated to <a href="/wiki/Portland,_Oregon" title="Portland, Oregon">Portland</a> in the mid-1990s.<sup id="cite_ref-newprez_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-newprez-7">[7]</a></sup> FLIR teamed up with <a href="/wiki/Hughes_Aircraft_Company" title="Hughes Aircraft Company">Hughes Aircraft Company</a> in 1990, with Hughes taking part ownership of FLIR.<sup id="cite_ref-hughes_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hughes-6">[6]</a></sup></p><p>The company became publicly traded in a June 1993 <a href="/wiki/Initial_public_offering" title="Initial public offering">IPO</a><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> which raised $11.5 million for the company with shares offered at $12.50.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> In 1994, the company had grown to sales of $47 million annually.<sup id="cite_ref-newprez_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-newprez-7">[7]</a></sup> The next year, J. Kenneth Stringer III was named as president of the company.<sup id="cite_ref-newprez_7-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-newprez-7">[7]</a></sup> The company bought Sweden's Agema Infrared System in 1997,<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> which doubled the size of the company.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Acquisitions continued the following year when they purchased Inframetrics Inc. of <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts" title="Massachusetts">Massachusetts</a> for $48 million.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p><p>The company's president and chief executive officer Kenneth Stringer III was fired by the <a href="/wiki/Board_of_directors" title="Board of directors">board of directors</a> in May 2000 due to errors in the company's accounting practices.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> In September 2002, the <a href="/wiki/Securities_and_Exchange_Commission" class="mw-redirect" title="Securities and Exchange Commission">Securities and Exchange Commission</a> (SEC) sued FLIR over accounting irregularities.<sup id="cite_ref-sec_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sec-14">[14]</a></sup> The next year, three executives at the company were charged with fraudulent accounting related to the SEC case that included claims of inflated sales.<sup id="cite_ref-sec_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sec-14">[14]</a></sup> Sales at FLIR grew to $311 million in fiscal year 2003.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> In 2004, the company bought a building in Wilsonville from <a href="/wiki/Mentor_Graphics" title="Mentor Graphics">Mentor Graphics</a> for $10.3 million for use as a new headquarters.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p><p>In 2003, FLIR acquired Indigo Systems, a leading developer and supplier of a wide range of infrared imaging products, including cooled and uncooled infrared detectors, camera cores, and finished cameras.
</p><p>Beginning in 2005, the company began supplying <a href="/wiki/BMW" title="BMW">BMW</a> with imaging technology for use on the luxury automaker's vehicles.<sup id="cite_ref-growth_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-growth-17">[17]</a></sup> Also in 2005, FLIR was named the 55th fastest-growing company on <a href="/wiki/CNNMoney.com" class="mw-redirect" title="CNNMoney.com">CNNMoney</a>'s list of the 100 fastest-growing tech companies.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> In 2006, FLIR was listed as the 83rd best small business by <i><a href="/wiki/Forbes" title="Forbes">Forbes</a></i>. The previous year they were ranked 39th.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> That July, FLIR announced a seven-year contract worth up to $250 million with the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Army" title="United States Army">United States Army</a> for cameras to be installed on helicopters.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:FLIR_entrance_sign.JPG" class="image"><noscript><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/32/FLIR_entrance_sign.JPG/220px-FLIR_entrance_sign.JPG" decoding="async" width="220" height="200" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="2201" data-file-height="2000"></noscript><span class="lazy-image-placeholder" style="width: 220px;height: 200px;" data-src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/32/FLIR_entrance_sign.JPG/220px-FLIR_entrance_sign.JPG" data-alt="" data-width="220" data-height="200" data-class="thumbimage"> </span></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:FLIR_entrance_sign.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Entrance to company headquarters in Wilsonville, Oregon, which is adjacent to the <a href="/wiki/Mentor_Graphics" title="Mentor Graphics">Mentor Graphics</a> campus.</div></div></div>
<p>In March 2007, the company reported that it would restate its financial statements for the period from 1995 to 2005, primarily to record non-cash charges for compensation expense relating to past stock option grants.<sup id="cite_ref-grants_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-grants-21">[21]</a></sup> FLIR was also sued by investors over these same allegations.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p><p>The company purchased <a href="/wiki/Extech_Instruments" title="Extech Instruments">Extech Instruments</a> Corp. in October 2007 for $40 million.<sup id="cite_ref-split_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-split-23">[23]</a></sup> The next month FLIR executed its third <a href="/wiki/Stock_split" title="Stock split">stock split</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-split_23-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-split-23">[23]</a></sup> FLIR made another acquisition in April 2008 when it purchased Ifara Tecnologias of Spain for about $11 million.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> For the 2008 fiscal year, the company recorded $1.1 billion in sales, a record for the company.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> On January 1, 2009, FLIR was added to the <a href="/wiki/S%26P_500" class="mw-redirect" title="S&amp;P 500">S&amp;P 500</a> stock index, replacing <a href="/wiki/National_City_Corp." title="National City Corp.">National City Corporation</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> FLIR was named as the Northwest's top company by the <i><a href="/wiki/Seattle_Times" class="mw-redirect" title="Seattle Times">Seattle Times</a></i> in 2009, the third time the company was at the top (2002 and 2003).<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> It sold Extech Data Systems, a division of Extech which made portable printers, in December 2009,<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> and bought the security hardware maker Directed Perception that month for $20 million.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> FLIR continued to grow via acquisitions when it purchased <a href="/wiki/Bankrupt" class="mw-redirect" title="Bankrupt">bankrupt</a> <a href="/wiki/Raymarine" class="mw-redirect" title="Raymarine">Raymarine</a> in May 2010 for $180 million.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> In December 2012 FLIR acquired Lorex Technology Inc. for approximately $60 million.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup>  On November 30, 2015 FLIR announced that it had acquired DVTEL, Inc, a leader in software and hardware technologies for advanced video surveillance, for approximately $92 million in cash.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p><p>In November 2016, FLIR acquired Point Grey Research for $259 million.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> This acquisition made FLIR the owner of the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Brickstream&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Brickstream (page does not exist)">Brickstream</a> brand of camera products. <sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></p><p>In December 2016, FLIR acquired Prox Dynamics, the makers of the <a href="/wiki/Black_Hornet_Nano" title="Black Hornet Nano">Black Hornet</a>, a nano-drone used by the military and law enforcement for surveillance and reconnaissance, for $134 million.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></p><p>In January 2019, FLIR acquired <a href="/wiki/Aeryon_Labs" title="Aeryon Labs">Aeryon Labs</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></p><p>In February 2019, FLIR announced that it had entered into a definitive agreement to acquire Endeavor Robotics, a leading developer of battle-tested, tactical unmanned ground vehicles (UGVs) for the global military, public safety, and critical infrastructure markets.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(2)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Products">Products</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=FLIR_Systems&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Products" data-section="2" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-2 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-2">
<p>FLIR Systems sells consumer and commercial smartphone thermal cameras. The FLIR ONE is available in three generations, and is limited to 9 frames per second, due to United States regulatory concerns.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> The camera can be used to detect things like water and air leaks.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup></p>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(3)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Operations">Operations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=FLIR_Systems&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Operations" data-section="3" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-3 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-3">
<p>With headquarters in both Wilsonville, Oregon and Arlington, Virginia, the company also has manufacturing and R&amp;D facilities in Goleta (near Santa Barbara, California), North Billerica (a suburb of Boston, Massachusetts), Rosh Ha'ayin (Israel), Bozeman (Montana), Stillwater (Oklahoma), Freeport (Pennsylvania), Oak Ridge (Tennessee), West Lafayette (Indiana), Tallinn (Estonia), Täby (Sweden), Hønefoss (Norway),Fareham (UK) and West Malling (UK).<sup id="cite_ref-about_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-about-5">[5]</a></sup> Worldwide, FLIR employs in excess of 4,000 people, with more than 1,600 located in the United States.<sup id="cite_ref-aboutflir_40-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-aboutflir-40">[40]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-flir-thermal-imagers_41-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-flir-thermal-imagers-41">[41]</a></sup></p><p>The company is structured to focus on six specific business segments.<sup id="cite_ref-ZacksAnalysis_42-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ZacksAnalysis-42">[42]</a></sup> The Surveillance group addresses high-end military and other governmental thermal imaging markets.<sup id="cite_ref-ZacksAnalysis_42-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ZacksAnalysis-42">[42]</a></sup> The Instruments segment produces thermography cameras for building inspection, <a href="/wiki/Predictive_maintenance" title="Predictive maintenance">predictive maintenance</a>, and R&amp;D, as well as test and measurement tools.<sup id="cite_ref-ZacksAnalysis_42-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ZacksAnalysis-42">[42]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-2013InvPres_43-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2013InvPres-43">[43]</a></sup> Development of OEM camera cores and components, new technologies, and new applications for existing technologies are served by the OEM &amp; Emerging Markets group.<sup id="cite_ref-ZacksAnalysis_42-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ZacksAnalysis-42">[42]</a></sup> Marine electronics including thermal cameras under both the FLIR and Raymarine brands fall under the Maritime segment.<sup id="cite_ref-ZacksAnalysis_42-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ZacksAnalysis-42">[42]</a></sup> Thermal and visible light cameras for industrial, commercial, and residential security are serviced under the <a href="/wiki/Lorex_by_FLIR" class="mw-redirect" title="Lorex by FLIR">Lorex by FLIR</a> brands by the Security segment.<sup id="cite_ref-ZacksAnalysis_42-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ZacksAnalysis-42">[42]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-2013InvPres_43-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2013InvPres-43">[43]</a></sup> The Detection segment produces sensors for chemical, biological, radiation, and explosive detection.<sup id="cite_ref-ZacksAnalysis_42-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ZacksAnalysis-42">[42]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-2013InvPres_43-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2013InvPres-43">[43]</a></sup></p>
