<p><b>Visa Inc.</b> (<span class="rt-commentedText nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'v' in 'vie'">v</span><span title="/iː/: 'ee' in 'fleece'">iː</span><span title="'z' in 'zoom'">z</span><span title="/ə/: 'a' in 'about'">ə</span></span>/</a></span></span> or <span class="rt-commentedText nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'v' in 'vie'">v</span><span title="/iː/: 'ee' in 'fleece'">iː</span><span title="'s' in 'sigh'">s</span><span title="/ə/: 'a' in 'about'">ə</span></span>/</a></span></span>) (also known as <b>Visa</b>, stylized as <i><b>VISA</b></i>) is an American <a href="/wiki/Multinational_corporation" title="Multinational corporation">multinational</a> <a href="/wiki/Financial_services" title="Financial services">financial services</a> corporation headquartered in <a href="/wiki/Foster_City,_California" title="Foster City, California">Foster City, California</a>, United States.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> It facilitates <a href="/wiki/Electronic_funds_transfer" title="Electronic funds transfer">electronic funds transfers</a> throughout the world, most commonly through Visa-branded <a href="/wiki/Credit_card" title="Credit card">credit cards</a>, <a href="/wiki/Debit_card" title="Debit card">debit cards</a> and <a href="/wiki/Stored-value_card" title="Stored-value card">prepaid cards</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Visa does not issue cards, extend credit or set rates and fees for consumers; rather, Visa provides financial institutions with Visa-branded payment products that they then use to offer credit, debit, prepaid and cash-access programs to their customers. In 2015, the Nilson Report, a publication that tracks the credit card industry, found that Visa's global network (known as <b>VisaNet</b>) processed 100 billion transactions during 2014 with a total 0;line-height:1.2em;">Number of employees</div></th><td style="line-height: 1.35em;">19,500 (2019)<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a It was then renamed Visa in 1976.<sup id="cite_ref-Thomas_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Thomas-8">[8]</a></sup></p><p>Visa has operations across all six continents. Nearly all Visa transactions worldwide are processed through the company's directly operated VisaNet at one of four secure <a href="/wiki/Data_center" title="Data center">data centers</a>, located in <a href="/wiki/Ashburn,_Virginia" title="Ashburn, Virginia">Ashburn, Virginia</a>; <a href="/wiki/Highlands_Ranch,_Colorado" title="Highlands Ranch, Colorado">Highlands Ranch, Colorado</a>; <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">London, England</a>; and <a href="/wiki/Singapore" title="Singapore">Singapore</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> These facilities are heavily secured against natural disasters, crime, and terrorism; can operate independently of each other and from external utilities if necessary; and can handle up to 30,000 simultaneous transactions and up to 100 billion computations every second.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:1_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-5">[5]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Swartz_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Swartz-11">[11]</a></sup></p><p>Visa is the world's second-largest card payment organization (debit and credit cards combined), after being surpassed by <a href="/wiki/China_UnionPay" class="mw-redirect" title="China UnionPay">China UnionPay</a> in 2015, based on annual value of card payments transacted and number of issued cards.<sup id="cite_ref-Finextra_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Finextra-12">[12]</a></sup></p>

</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(1)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Visa_Inc.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History" data-section="1" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-1 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-1">
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Old_Bankamericard_sign.jpg" class="image"><noscript><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/77/Old_Bankamericard_sign.jpg/220px-Old_Bankamericard_sign.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="170" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="1900" data-file-height="1469"></noscript><span class="lazy-image-placeholder" style="width: 220px;height: 170px;" data-src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/77/Old_Bankamericard_sign.jpg/220px-Old_Bankamericard_sign.jpg" data-alt="" data-width="220" data-height="170" data-class="thumbimage"> </span></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Old_Bankamericard_sign.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Old "Your BankAmericard Welcome Here" sign</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:217px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Visad.jpg" class="image"><noscript><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/e/ec/Visad.jpg" decoding="async" width="215" height="300" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="215" data-file-height="300"></noscript><span class="lazy-image-placeholder" style="width: 215px;height: 300px;" data-src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/e/ec/Visad.jpg" data-alt="" data-width="215" data-height="300" data-class="thumbimage"> </span></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Visad.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A 1976 ad promoting the change of name to "Visa". <p>In mid-September 1958, <a href="/wiki/Bank_of_America" title="Bank of America">Bank of America</a> (BofA) launched its <b>BankAmericard</b> credit card program in <a href="/wiki/Fresno,_California" title="Fresno, California">Fresno, California</a>, with an initial mass mailing (or "drop", as they came to be called) of 60,000 unsolicited credit cards.<sup id="cite_ref-Visa_History_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Visa_History-6">[6]</a></sup> The original idea was the brainchild of BofA's in-house product development <a href="/wiki/Think_tank" title="Think tank">think tank</a>, the Customer Services Research Group, and its leader, <a href="/wiki/Joseph_P._Williams" title="Joseph P. Williams">Joseph P. Williams</a>. Williams convinced senior BofA executives in 1956 to let him pursue what became the world's first successful mass mailing of unsolicited credit cards (actual working cards, not mere applications) to a large population.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p><p>Williams' pioneering accomplishment was that he brought about the successful implementation of the all-purpose credit card (in the sense that his project was not cancelled outright), not in coming up with the idea.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> By the mid-1950s, the typical middle-class American already maintained <a href="/wiki/Revolving_credit" title="Revolving credit">revolving credit</a> accounts with several different merchants, which was clearly inefficient and inconvenient due to the need to carry so many cards and pay so many separate bills each month.<sup id="cite_ref-Nocera,_24_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Nocera,_24-15">[15]</a></sup> The need for a unified financial instrument was already evident to the American financial services industry, but no one could figure out how to do it. There were already <a href="/wiki/Charge_card" title="Charge card">charge cards</a> like <a href="/wiki/Diners_Club" class="mw-redirect" title="Diners Club">Diners Club</a> (which had to be paid in full at the end of each billing cycle), and "by the mid-1950s, there had been at least a dozen attempts to create an all-purpose credit card."<sup id="cite_ref-Nocera,_24_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Nocera,_24-15">[15]</a></sup> However, these prior attempts had been carried out by small banks which lacked the resources to make them work.<sup id="cite_ref-Nocera,_24_15-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Nocera,_24-15">[15]</a></sup> Williams and his team studied these failures carefully and believed they could avoid replicating those banks' mistakes; they also studied existing revolving credit operations at <a href="/wiki/Sears" title="Sears">Sears</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mobil_Oil" class="mw-redirect" title="Mobil Oil">Mobil Oil</a> to learn why they were successful.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Fresno was selected for its population of 250,000 (big enough to make a credit card work, small enough to control initial startup cost), BofA's market share of that population (45%), and relative isolation, to control public relations damage in case the project failed.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>

<p>The 1958 test at first went smoothly, but then BofA panicked when it confirmed rumors that another bank was about to initiate its own drop in San Francisco, BofA's home market.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> By March 1959, drops began in San Francisco and <a href="/wiki/Sacramento,_California" title="Sacramento, California">Sacramento</a>; by June, BofA was dropping cards in <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles" title="Los Angeles">Los Angeles</a>; by October, the entire state had been saturated with over 2 million credit cards, and BankAmericard was being accepted by 20,000 merchants.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> However, the program was riddled with problems, as Williams (who had never worked in a bank's loan department) had been too earnest and trusting in his belief in the basic goodness of the bank's customers, and he resigned in December 1959.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> 22% of accounts were delinquent, not the 4% expected, and police departments around the state were confronted by numerous incidents of the brand new crime of <a href="/wiki/Credit_card_fraud" title="Credit card fraud">credit card fraud</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> Both politicians and journalists joined the general uproar against Bank of America and its newfangled credit card, especially when it was pointed out that the cardholder agreement held customers liable for all charges, even those resulting from fraud.<sup id="cite_ref-Nocera,_31_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Nocera,_31-22">[22]</a></sup> BofA officially lost over $8.8 million on the launch of BankAmericard, but when the full cost of advertising and overhead was included, the bank's actual loss was probably around $20 million.<sup id="cite_ref-Nocera,_31_22-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Nocera,_31-22">[22]</a></sup></p><p>However, after Williams and some of his closest associates left, BofA management realized that BankAmericard was salvageable.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> They conducted a "massive effort" to clean up after Williams, imposed proper financial controls, published an open letter to 3 million households across the state apologizing for the credit card fraud and other issues their card raised, and eventually were able to make the new financial instrument work.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p><p>The original goal of BofA was to offer the BankAmericard product across <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a>, but in 1966, BofA began to sign licensing agreements with a group of banks outside of California, in response to a new competitor, Master Charge (now <a href="/wiki/MasterCard" class="mw-redirect" title="MasterCard">MasterCard</a>), which had been created by an alliance of several other California banks to compete against BankAmericard.  BofA itself (like all other U.S. banks at the time) could not expand directly into other states due to federal restrictions <a href="/wiki/Riegle-Neal_Interstate_Banking_and_Branching_Efficiency_Act_of_1994" title="Riegle-Neal Interstate Banking and Branching Efficiency Act of 1994">not repealed until 1994</a>. Over the following 11 years, various banks licensed the card system from Bank of America, thus forming a network of banks backing the BankAmericard system across the United States.<sup id="cite_ref-Visa_Latam_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Visa_Latam-7">[7]</a></sup> The "drops" of unsolicited credit cards continued unabated, thanks to BofA and its licensees and competitors, until they were outlawed in 1970,<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> but not before over 100 million credit cards had been distributed into the American population.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p><p>During the late 1960s, BofA also licensed the BankAmericard program to banks in several other countries, which began issuing cards with localized brand names. For example:<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<ul><li>In Canada, an alliance of banks (including <a href="/wiki/Toronto-Dominion_Bank" title="Toronto-Dominion Bank">Toronto-Dominion Bank</a>, <a href="/wiki/Canadian_Imperial_Bank_of_Commerce" title="Canadian Imperial Bank of Commerce">Canadian Imperial Bank of Commerce</a>, <a href="/wiki/Royal_Bank_of_Canada" title="Royal Bank of Canada">Royal Bank of Canada</a>, <a href="/wiki/Banque_Canadienne_Nationale" title="Banque Canadienne Nationale">Banque Canadienne Nationale</a> and <a href="/wiki/Scotiabank" title="Scotiabank">Bank of Nova Scotia</a>) issued credit cards under the <i>Chargex</i> name from 1968 to 1977.</li>
<li>In France, it was known as <i><a href="/wiki/Carte_Bleue" title="Carte Bleue">Carte Bleue</a></i> (Blue Card). The logo still appears on many French-issued Visa cards today.</li>
<li>In Japan, <a href="/wiki/The_Sumitomo_Bank" title="The Sumitomo Bank">The Sumitomo Bank</a> issued BankAmericards through the Sumitomo Credit Service.</li>
<li>In the UK, the only BankAmericard issuer for some years was <a href="/wiki/Barclaycard" title="Barclaycard">Barclaycard</a>. The branding still exists today, but is used not only on Visa cards issued by <a href="/wiki/Barclays" title="Barclays">Barclays</a>, but on its MasterCard and <a href="/wiki/American_Express" title="American Express">American Express</a> cards as well.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></li>
<li>In Spain until 1979 the only issuer was <a href="/wiki/Banco_de_Bilbao" class="mw-redirect" title="Banco de Bilbao">Banco de Bilbao</a>.</li></ul><p>In 1968, a manager at the National Bank of Commerce (later <a href="/wiki/Rainier_Bancorp" title="Rainier Bancorp">Rainier Bancorp</a>), <a href="/wiki/Dee_Hock" title="Dee Hock">Dee Hock</a>, was asked to supervise that bank's launch of its own licensed version of BankAmericard in the <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Northwest" title="Pacific Northwest">Pacific Northwest</a> market.  Although Bank of America had cultivated the public image that BankAmericard's troubled startup issues were now safely in the past, Hock realized that the BankAmericard licensee program itself was in terrible disarray because it had developed and grown very rapidly in an <i>ad hoc</i> fashion. For example, "interchange" transaction issues <i>between</i> banks were becoming a very serious problem, which had not been seen before when Bank of America was the sole issuer of BankAmericards.  Hock suggested to other licensees that they form a committee to investigate and analyze the various problems with the licensee program; they promptly made him the chair of that committee.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p><p>After lengthy negotiations, the committee led by Hock was able to persuade Bank of America that a bright future lay ahead for BankAmericard — outside Bank of America. In June 1970, Bank of America gave up control of the BankAmericard program. The various BankAmericard issuer banks took control of the program, creating National BankAmericard Inc. (NBI), an independent <a href="/wiki/Delaware_General_Corporation_Law" title="Delaware General Corporation Law">Delaware corporation</a> which would be in charge of managing, promoting and developing the BankAmericard system within the United States. In other words, BankAmericard was transformed from a franchising system into a jointly controlled consortium or alliance, like its competitor Master Charge. Hock became NBI's first president and CEO.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p><p>However, Bank of America retained the right to directly license BankAmericard to banks outside the United States, and continued to issue and support such licenses. By 1972, licenses had been granted in 15 countries. The international licensees soon encountered a variety of problems with their licensing programs, and they hired Hock as a consultant to help them restructure their relationship with BofA as he had done for the domestic licensees. As a result, in 1974, the International Bankcard Company (IBANCO), a multinational member corporation, was founded in order to manage the international BankAmericard program.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r923042769/mw-parser-output/.tmulti"><div class="thumb tmulti tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:308px;max-width:308px"><div class="trow"><div class="tsingle" style="width:152px;max-width:152px"><div class="thumbimage"><a href="/wiki/File:Barclaycard.png" class="image"><noscript><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6e/Barclaycard.png/150px-Barclaycard.png" decoding="async" width="150" height="95" data-file-width="200" data-file-height="126"></noscript><span class="lazy-image-placeholder" style="width: 150px;height: 95px;" data-src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6e/Barclaycard.png/150px-Barclaycard.png" data-alt="" data-width="150" data-height="95"> </span></a></div></div><div class="tsingle" style="width:152px;max-width:152px"><div class="thumbimage"><a href="/wiki/File:Chargex_logo.png" class="image"><noscript><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9d/Chargex_logo.png/150px-Chargex_logo.png" decoding="async" width="150" height="97" data-file-width="300" data-file-height="193"></noscript><span class="lazy-image-placeholder" style="width: 150px;height: 97px;" data-src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9d/Chargex_logo.png/150px-Chargex_logo.png" data-alt="" data-width="150" data-height="97"> </span></a></div></div></div><div class="trow"><div class="thumbcaption" style="background-color:transparent">Sample <a href="/wiki/Barclaycard" title="Barclaycard">Barclaycard</a> (left), as issued in the UK in the 1960s/70s. Co-branded cards were also issued by affiliates, such as <a href="/wiki/The_Co-operative_Bank" title="The Co-operative Bank">The Co-operative Bank</a> and <a href="/wiki/Yorkshire_Bank" title="Yorkshire Bank">Yorkshire Bank</a>. The Chargex logo (right) used in Canada.</div></div></div></div>
<p>In 1976, the directors of IBANCO determined that bringing the various international networks together into a single network with a single name internationally would be in the best interests of the corporation; however, in many countries, there was still great reluctance to issue a card associated with Bank of America, even though the association was entirely nominal in nature. For this reason, in 1976, BankAmericard, Barclaycard, Carte Bleue, Chargex, Sumitomo Card, and all other licensees united under the new name, "<b>Visa</b>", which retained the distinctive blue, white and gold flag. NBI became Visa USA and IBANCO became Visa International.<sup id="cite_ref-Thomas_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Thomas-8">[8]</a></sup></p><p>The term <i>Visa</i> was conceived by the company's founder, Dee Hock. He believed that the word was instantly recognizable in many languages in many countries, and that it also denoted universal acceptance.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup>
In October 2007, Bank of America announced it was resurrecting the BankAmericard brand name as the "BankAmericard Rewards Visa".<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Corporate_structure">Corporate structure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Visa_Inc.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Corporate structure" data-section="2" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>Prior to October 3, 2007, Visa comprised four non-stock, separately incorporated companies that employed 6,000 people worldwide: the worldwide parent entity Visa International Service Association (Visa), Visa USA Inc., Visa Canada Association, and Visa Europe Ltd. The latter three separately incorporated regions had the status of group members of Visa International Service Association.
</p><p>The unincorporated regions Visa Latin America (<a href="/wiki/Latin_America_and_the_Caribbean" title="Latin America and the Caribbean">LAC</a>), Visa Asia Pacific and Visa Central and Eastern Europe, Middle East and Africa (CEMEA) were divisions within Visa.
</p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Billing_and_finance_charge_methods">Billing and finance charge methods</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Visa_Inc.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Billing and finance charge methods" data-section="3" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>Initially, signed copies of sales drafts were included in each customer's monthly billing statement for verification purposes—an industry practice known as "country club billing"<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>. By the late 1970s, however, billing statements no longer contained these enclosures, but rather a summary statement showing posting date, purchase date, reference number, merchant name, and the dollar amount of each purchase.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> At the same time, many issuers, particularly Bank of America, were in the process of changing their methods of finance charge calculation. Initially, a "previous balance" method was used—calculation of finance charge on the unpaid balance shown on the prior month's statement. Later, it was decided to use "average daily balance" which resulted in increased revenue for the issuers by calculating the number of days each purchase was included on the prior month's statement. Several years later, "new average daily balance"—in which transactions from previous and current billing cycles were used in the calculation—was introduced. By the early 1980s, many issuers introduced the concept of the annual fee as yet another revenue enhancer.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="IPO_and_restructuring">IPO and restructuring</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Visa_Inc.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: IPO and restructuring" data-section="4" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>On October 11, 2006, Visa announced that some of its businesses would be merged and become a <a href="/wiki/Publicly_traded_company" class="mw-redirect" title="Publicly traded company">publicly traded company</a>, Visa Inc.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup>
Under the IPO restructuring, Visa Canada, Visa International, and Visa USA were merged into the new public company. Visa's Western Europe operation became a separate company, owned by its member banks who will also have a minority stake in Visa Inc.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> In total, more than 35 investment banks participated in the deal in several capacities, most notably as underwriters.
</p><p>On October 3, 2007, Visa completed its corporate restructuring with the formation of Visa Inc. The new company was the first step towards Visa's IPO.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> The second step came on November 9, 2007, when the new Visa Inc. submitted its $10 billion IPO filing with the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Securities_and_Exchange_Commission" class="mw-redirect" title="United States Securities and Exchange Commission">U.S. Securities and Exchange Commission</a> (SEC).<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> On February 25, 2008, Visa announced it would go ahead with an IPO of half its shares.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> The IPO took place on March 18, 2008. Visa sold 406 million shares at US$44 per share ($2 above the high end of the expected $37–42 pricing range), raising US$17.9 billion in the largest initial public offering in U.S. history.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> On March 20, 2008, the IPO underwriters (including JP Morgan, Goldman Sachs &amp; Co., Banc of America Securities LLC, Citi, HSBC, Merrill Lynch &amp; Co., UBS Investment Bank and Wachovia Securities) exercised their overallotment option, purchasing an additional 40.6 million shares, bringing Visa's total IPO share count to 446.6 million, and bringing the total proceeds to US$19.1 billion.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> Visa now trades under the <a href="/wiki/Ticker_symbol" title="Ticker symbol">ticker symbol</a> "V" on the <a href="/wiki/New_York_Stock_Exchange" title="New York Stock Exchange">New York Stock Exchange</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Visa_Europe">Visa Europe</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Visa_Inc.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Visa Europe" data-section="5" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>Visa Europe Ltd. was a membership association and cooperative of over 3,700 European banks and other <a href="/wiki/Payment_service_provider" title="Payment service provider">payment service providers</a><sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> that operated Visa branded products and services within Europe. Visa Europe was a company entirely separate from Visa Inc. having gained independence of Visa International Service Association in October 2007 when Visa Inc. became a <a href="/wiki/Publicly_traded_company" class="mw-redirect" title="Publicly traded company">publicly traded company</a> on the <a href="/wiki/New_York_Stock_Exchange" title="New York Stock Exchange">New York Stock Exchange</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_44-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-44">[44]</a></sup> Visa Inc. announced the plan to acquire Visa Europe on November 5, 2015, creating a single global company.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> On April 21, 2016 the agreement was amended in response to the feedback of <a href="/wiki/European_Commission" title="European Commission">European Commission</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> The acquisition of Visa Europe was completed on June 21, 2016.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup></p>

<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="WikiLeaks">WikiLeaks</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Visa_Inc.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: WikiLeaks" data-section="8" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>Visa Europe began suspending payments to <a href="/wiki/WikiLeaks" title="WikiLeaks">WikiLeaks</a> on December 7, 2010.<sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup> The company said it was awaiting an investigation into 'the nature of its business and whether it contravenes Visa operating rules' – though it did not go into details.<sup id="cite_ref-WikiAP_61-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WikiAP-61">[61]</a></sup> In return DataCell, the <a href="/wiki/Information_technology" title="Information technology">IT</a> company that enables WikiLeaks to accept credit and debit card donations, announced that it would take legal action against Visa Europe.<sup id="cite_ref-62" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup> On December 8, the group <a href="/wiki/Anonymous_(group)" title="Anonymous (group)">Anonymous</a> performed a <a href="/wiki/DDoS" class="mw-redirect" title="DDoS">DDoS</a> attack on visa.com,<sup id="cite_ref-63" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup> bringing the site down.<sup id="cite_ref-64" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup> Although the Norway-based financial services company Teller AS, which Visa ordered to look into WikiLeaks and its fundraising body, the Sunshine Press, found no proof of any wrongdoing, <i><a href="/wiki/Salon_(website)" title="Salon (website)">Salon</a></i> reported in January 2011 that Visa Europe "would continue blocking donations to the secret-spilling site until it completes its own investigation".<sup id="cite_ref-WikiAP_61-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WikiAP-61">[61]</a></sup></p><p>The <a href="/wiki/United_Nations" title="United Nations">United Nations</a> <a href="/wiki/High_Commissioner_for_Human_Rights" class="mw-redirect" title="High Commissioner for Human Rights">High Commissioner for Human Rights</a> <a href="/wiki/Navi_Pillay" title="Navi Pillay">Navi Pillay</a> stated that Visa may be "violating WikiLeaks' right to freedom of expression" by withdrawing their services.<sup id="cite_ref-65" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup></p><p>In July 2012, the Reykjavík District Court decided that <a href="/wiki/Valitor" title="Valitor">Valitor</a> (the Icelandic partner of Visa and MasterCard) was violating the law when it prevented donations to the site by credit card. It was ruled that the donations be allowed to return to the site within 14 days or they would be fined in the amount of US$6,000 per day.<sup id="cite_ref-66" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Litigation_and_regulatory_actions">Litigation and regulatory actions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Visa_Inc.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Litigation and regulatory actions" data-section="9" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<h4 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Anti-competitive_conduct_in_Australia">Anti-competitive conduct in Australia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Visa_Inc.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Anti-competitive conduct in Australia" data-section="10" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h4>
<p>In 2015, the Australian Federal Court ordered Visa to pay a pecuniary penalty of $20 million (including legal fees) for engaging in anti-competitive conduct against dynamic currency conversion operators, in proceedings brought by the Australian Competition and Consumer Commission.<sup id="cite_ref-67" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup></p>
<h4 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Antitrust_lawsuit_by_ATM_operators">Antitrust lawsuit by ATM operators</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Visa_Inc.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Antitrust lawsuit by ATM operators" data-section="11" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h4>
<p>MasterCard, along with Visa, have been sued in a class action by ATM operators that claims the credit card networks' rules effectively fix ATM access fees.<sup id="cite_ref-68" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup> The suit claims that this is a restraint on trade in violation of federal law. The lawsuit was filed by the National ATM Council and independent operators of automated teller machines. More specifically, it is alleged that MasterCard's and Visa's network rules prohibit ATM operators from offering lower prices for transactions over PIN-debit networks that are not affiliated with Visa or MasterCard. The suit says that this price fixing artificially raises the price that consumers pay using ATMs, limits the revenue that ATM-operators earn, and violates the <a href="/wiki/Sherman_Act" class="mw-redirect" title="Sherman Act">Sherman Act</a>'s prohibition against unreasonable restraints of trade. Johnathan Rubin, an attorney for the plaintiffs said, "Visa and MasterCard are the ringleaders, organizers, and enforcers of a conspiracy among U.S. banks to fix the price of ATM access fees in order to keep the competition at bay."<sup id="cite_ref-ATM_69-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ATM-69">[69]</a></sup></p>
<h4 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Debit_card_swipe_fees">Debit card swipe fees</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Visa_Inc.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Debit card swipe fees" data-section="12" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h4>
<p>Visa settled a 1996 <a href="/wiki/Antitrust" class="mw-redirect" title="Antitrust">antitrust</a> lawsuit brought by a class of U.S. merchants, including <a href="/wiki/Walmart" title="Walmart">Walmart</a>, for billions of dollars in 2003. Over 4 million class members were represented by the plaintiffs. According to a website associated with the suit,<sup id="cite_ref-70" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup> Visa and <a href="/wiki/MasterCard" class="mw-redirect" title="MasterCard">MasterCard</a> settled the plaintiffs' claims for a total of $3.05 billion. Visa's share of this settlement is reported to have been the larger.
</p>
<h4 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="U.S._Justice_Department_actions">U.S. Justice Department actions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Visa_Inc.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: U.S. Justice Department actions" data-section="13" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h4>
<p>In October 2010, Visa and MasterCard reached a settlement with the <a href="/wiki/U.S._Justice_Department" class="mw-redirect" title="U.S. Justice Department">U.S. Justice Department</a> in another antitrust case. The companies agreed to allow merchants displaying their logos to decline certain types of cards (because <a href="/wiki/Interchange_fee" title="Interchange fee">interchange fees</a> differ), or to offer consumers discounts for using cheaper cards.<sup id="cite_ref-71" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup></p><p>In 1998, the Department of Justice sued Visa over rules prohibiting its issuing banks from doing business with <a href="/wiki/American_Express" title="American Express">American Express</a> and <a href="/wiki/Discover_Card" title="Discover Card">Discover</a>. The Department of Justice won its case at trial in 2001 and the verdict was upheld on appeal. American Express and Discover filed suit as well.<sup id="cite_ref-Unfair_72-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Unfair-72">[72]</a></sup></p>
<h4 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Antitrust_issues_in_Europe">Antitrust issues in Europe</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Visa_Inc.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Antitrust issues in Europe" data-section="14" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h4>
<p>In 2002, the <a href="/wiki/European_Commission" title="European Commission">European Commission</a> exempted Visa's multilateral interchange fees from Article 81 of the EC Treaty that prohibits anti-competitive arrangements.<sup id="cite_ref-73" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-73">[73]</a></sup>  However, this exemption expired on December 31, 2007. In the United Kingdom, Mastercard has reduced its interchange fees while it is under investigation by the Office of Fair Trading.
</p><p>In January 2007, the European Commission issued the results of a two-year inquiry into the retail banking sector. The report focuses on payment cards and interchange fees. Upon publishing the report, Commissioner <a href="/wiki/Neelie_Kroes" title="Neelie Kroes">Neelie Kroes</a> said the "present level of interchange fees in many of the schemes we have examined does not seem justified." The report called for further study of the issue.<sup id="cite_ref-74" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-74">[74]</a></sup></p><p>On March 26, 2008, the European Commission opened an investigation into Visa's multilateral interchange fees for cross-border transactions within the EEA as well as into the "Honor All Cards" rule (under which merchants are required to accept all valid Visa-branded cards).<sup id="cite_ref-75" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-75">[75]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Chronological_items" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers"><span title="The date of the event predicted near this tag has passed. (February 2015)">needs update</span></a></i>]</sup></p><p>The antitrust authorities of EU member states (other than the United Kingdom) also investigated Mastercard's and Visa's interchange fees. For example, on January 4, 2007, the Polish Office of Competition and Consumer Protection fined twenty banks a total of PLN 164 million (about $56 million) for jointly setting Mastercard's and Visa's interchange fees.<sup id="cite_ref-76" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-76">[76]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-77" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-77">[77]</a></sup></p><p>In December 2010, Visa reached a settlement with the <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a> in yet another antitrust case, promising to reduce debit card payments to 0.2 percent of a purchase.<sup id="cite_ref-78" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-78">[78]</a></sup> A senior official from the <a href="/wiki/European_Central_Bank" title="European Central Bank">European Central Bank</a> called for a break-up of the Visa/Mastercard <a href="/wiki/Duopoly" title="Duopoly">duopoly</a> by creation of a new European debit card for use in the <a href="/wiki/Single_Euro_Payments_Area" title="Single Euro Payments Area">Single Euro Payments Area</a> (SEPA).<sup id="cite_ref-79" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-79">[79]</a></sup> After Visa's blocking of payments to <a href="/wiki/WikiLeaks" title="WikiLeaks">WikiLeaks</a>, members of the European Parliament expressed concern that payments from European citizens to a European corporation could apparently be blocked by the US, and called for a further reduction in the dominance of Visa and Mastercard in the European payment system.<sup id="cite_ref-80" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-80">[80]</a></sup></p>
<h4 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Payment_Card_Interchange_Fee_and_Merchant_Discount_Antitrust_Litigation">Payment Card Interchange Fee and Merchant Discount Antitrust Litigation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Visa_Inc.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Payment Card Interchange Fee and Merchant Discount Antitrust Litigation" data-section="15" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h4>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Payment_Card_Interchange_Fee_and_Merchant_Discount_Antitrust_Litigation" title="Payment Card Interchange Fee and Merchant Discount Antitrust Litigation">Payment Card Interchange Fee and Merchant Discount Antitrust Litigation</a></div>
<p>On November 27, 2012, a federal judge entered an order granting preliminary approval to a proposed settlement to a class-action lawsuit<sup id="cite_ref-81" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-81">[81]</a></sup> filed in 2005 by merchants and trade associations against Mastercard and Visa. The suit was filed due to alleged price fixing practices employed by Mastercard and Visa. About one quarter of the named class plaintiffs have decided to opt "out of the settlement". Opponents object to provisions that would bar future lawsuits and even prevent merchants from opting out of significant portions of the proposed settlement.<sup id="cite_ref-MasterCard_82-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MasterCard-82">[82]</a></sup></p><p>Plaintiffs allege that Visa and <a href="/wiki/Mastercard" title="Mastercard">Mastercard</a> fixed <a href="/wiki/Interchange_fees" class="mw-redirect" title="Interchange fees">interchange fees</a>, also known as swipe fees, that are charged to merchants for the privilege of accepting payment cards. In their complaint, the plaintiffs also alleged that the defendants unfairly interfere with merchants from encouraging customers to use less expensive forms of payment such as lower-cost cards, cash, and checks.<sup id="cite_ref-MasterCard_82-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MasterCard-82">[82]</a></sup></p><p>A settlement of <a href="/wiki/United_States_dollar" title="United States dollar">US$</a>6.24 billion has been reached and a court is scheduled to approve or deny the agreement on November 7, 2019.<sup id="cite_ref-83" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-83">[83]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="High_swipe_fees_in_Poland">High swipe fees in Poland</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Visa_Inc.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: High swipe fees in Poland" data-section="16" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>Very high interchange fee for Visa (1.5–1.6% from every transaction's final price, which also includes <a href="/wiki/VAT" class="mw-redirect" title="VAT">VAT</a>) in Poland started discussion about legality and need for government regulations of interchange fees to avoid high costs for business (which also block electronic payment market and acceptability of cards).<sup id="cite_ref-84" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-84">[84]</a></sup> This situation also led to the birth of new methods of payment in the year 2013, which avoid the need for go-between (middleman) companies like Visa or Mastercard, for example mobile application issued by major banks,<sup id="cite_ref-85" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-85">[85]</a></sup> and system by big chain of discount shops,<sup id="cite_ref-86" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-86">[86]</a></sup> or older public transport tickets buying systems.<sup id="cite_ref-87" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-87">[87]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Confrontation_with_Walmart_over_high_fees">Confrontation with Walmart over high fees</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Visa_Inc.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Confrontation with Walmart over high fees" data-section="17" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>In June 2016, the <i><a href="/wiki/Wall_Street_Journal" class="mw-redirect" title="Wall Street Journal">Wall Street Journal</a></i> reported that <a href="/wiki/Walmart_Canada" title="Walmart Canada">Walmart</a> threatened to stop accepting Visa cards in Canada. Visa objected saying that consumers should not be dragged into a dispute between the companies.<sup id="cite_ref-88" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-88">[88]</a></sup> In January 2017, Walmart Canada and Visa reached a deal to allow the continued acceptance of Visa.<sup id="cite_ref-89" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-89">[89]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Dispute_with_Kroger_over_high_credit_card_fees">Dispute with Kroger over high credit card fees</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Visa_Inc.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Dispute with Kroger over high credit card fees" data-section="18" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>In March 2019, U.S. retailer <a href="/wiki/Kroger" title="Kroger">Kroger</a> announced that its 250-strong <a href="/wiki/Smith%27s_Food_and_Drug" title="Smith's Food and Drug">Smith's</a> chain would stop accepting Visa credit cards as of April 3, 2019 due to the cards’ high ‘swipe’ fees. Kroger's California-based <a href="/wiki/Food_4_Less" title="Food 4 Less">Foods Co</a> stores stopped accepting Visa cards in August 2018. Mike Schlotman, Kroger's executive vice president/chief financial officer, said Visa had been “misusing its position and charging retailers excessive fees for a long time.” In response, Visa issued a statement saying it was “unfair and disappointing that Kroger is putting shoppers in the middle of a business dispute.”<sup id="cite_ref-90" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-90">[90]</a></sup> As of October 31, 2019, Kroger has settled their dispute with Visa and is now accepting the payment method.<sup id="cite_ref-91" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-91">[91]</a></sup></p>

<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Headquarters">Headquarters</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Visa_Inc.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Headquarters" data-section="20" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>As of October 1, 2012, Visa's headquarters are located in Foster City, California.<sup id="cite_ref-fosterHQ_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fosterHQ-1">[1]</a></sup> Visa had been headquartered in San Francisco until 1985, when it moved to <a href="/wiki/San_Mateo,_California" title="San Mateo, California">San Mateo</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Visapass_92-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Visapass-92">[92]</a></sup>  Around 1993, Visa began consolidating various scattered offices in San Mateo to a location in Foster City.<sup id="cite_ref-Visapass_92-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Visapass-92">[92]</a></sup> Visa became Foster City's largest employer.
</p><p>In 2009, Visa moved its corporate headquarters back to San Francisco when it leased the top three floors of the <a href="/wiki/595_Market_Street" title="595 Market Street">595 Market Street</a> office building, although most of its employees remained at its Foster City campus.<sup id="cite_ref-HQmove2009_93-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HQmove2009-93">[93]</a></sup>  In 2012, Visa decided to consolidate its headquarters in Foster City where 3,100 of its 7,700 global workers are employed.<sup id="cite_ref-fosterHQ_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fosterHQ-1">[1]</a></sup> Visa owns four buildings at the intersection of Metro Center Boulevard and Vintage Park Drive.
</p><p>In December 2012, Visa Inc. confirmed that it will build a global information technology center off of the <a href="/wiki/U.S._Route_183_in_Texas" title="U.S. Route 183 in Texas">US 183</a> Expressway in northwest <a href="/wiki/Austin,_Texas" title="Austin, Texas">Austin, Texas</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-94" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-94">[94]</a></sup> By 2019, Visa leased space in 4 buildings near Austin and employed nearly 2,000 people.<sup id="cite_ref-95" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-95">[95]</a></sup></p>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(5)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Operations">Operations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Visa_Inc.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Operations" data-section="21" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-5 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-5">
<p>Visa offers through its issuing members the following types of cards:
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Debit_card" title="Debit card">Debit cards</a> (pay from a checking / savings account)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Credit_card" title="Credit card">Credit cards</a> (pay monthly payments with or without interest depending on a customer paying on time.)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Stored-value_card" title="Stored-value card">Prepaid cards</a> (pay from a cash account that has no checkwriting privileges)</li></ul><p>Visa operates the <a href="/wiki/Plus_(interbank_network)" title="Plus (interbank network)">Plus</a> <a href="/wiki/Automated_teller_machine" title="Automated teller machine">automated teller machine</a> network and the <a href="/wiki/Interlink_(interbank_network)" title="Interlink (interbank network)">Interlink</a> <a href="/wiki/EFTPOS" title="EFTPOS">EFTPOS</a> <a href="/wiki/Point_of_sale" title="Point of sale">point-of-sale</a> network, which facilitate the "debit" protocol used with debit cards and prepaid cards. They also provide commercial payment solutions for small businesses, midsize and large corporations, and governments.<sup id="cite_ref-96" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-96">[96]</a></sup></p><p>Visa teamed with <a href="/wiki/Apple_Inc." title="Apple Inc.">Apple</a> in September 2014, to incorporate a new mobile wallet feature into Apple's new <a href="/wiki/IPhone" title="IPhone">iPhone</a> models, enabling users to more readily use their Visa, and other credit/debit cards.<sup id="cite_ref-iPhoneWallet_97-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-iPhoneWallet-97">[97]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Operating_regulations">Operating regulations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Visa_Inc.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Operating regulations" data-section="22" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>Visa has a set of rules that govern the participation of financial institutions in its payment system. <a href="/wiki/Acquiring_bank" title="Acquiring bank">Acquiring banks</a> are responsible for ensuring that their merchants comply with the rules.
</p><p>Rules address how a cardholder must be identified for <a href="/wiki/Security" title="Security">security</a>, how transactions may be denied by the bank and how banks may cooperate for <a href="/wiki/Fraud" title="Fraud">fraud</a> prevention, and how to keep that identification and fraud protection standard and non-discriminatory. Other rules govern what creates an enforceable proof of authorization by the cardholder.<sup id="cite_ref-Orion_98-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Orion-98">[98]</a></sup></p><p>The rules prohibit merchants from imposing a minimum or maximum purchase amount in order to accept a Visa card and from charging cardholders a fee for using a Visa card.<sup id="cite_ref-Orion_98-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Orion-98">[98]</a></sup> In ten U.S. states, surcharges for the use of a credit card are forbidden by law (<a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a>, <a href="/wiki/Colorado" title="Colorado">Colorado</a>, <a href="/wiki/Connecticut" title="Connecticut">Connecticut</a>, <a href="/wiki/Florida" title="Florida">Florida</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kansas" title="Kansas">Kansas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Maine" title="Maine">Maine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts" title="Massachusetts">Massachusetts</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_York_(state)" title="New York (state)">New York</a>, <a href="/wiki/Oklahoma" title="Oklahoma">Oklahoma</a> and <a href="/wiki/Texas" title="Texas">Texas</a>) but a discount for cash is permitted under specific rules.<sup id="cite_ref-99" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-99">[99]</a></sup> Some countries have banned the no-surcharge rule, most notably in Australia<sup id="cite_ref-100" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-100">[100]</a></sup> retailers may apply surcharges to any credit-card transaction, Visa or otherwise. In the UK the law was changed in January 2018 to prevent retailers from adding a surcharge to a transaction as per '<a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.legislation.gov.uk/uksi/2012/3110/contents/made">The Consumer Rights (Payment Surcharges) Regulations 2012</a>'.
</p><p>Visa permits merchants to ask for photo ID, although the merchant rule book states that this practice is discouraged. As long as the Visa card is signed, a merchant may not deny a transaction because a cardholder refuses to show a photo ID.<sup id="cite_ref-Orion_98-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Orion-98">[98]</a></sup></p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Dodd%E2%80%93Frank_Act" class="mw-redirect" title="Dodd–Frank Act">Dodd–Frank Act</a> allows U.S. merchants to set a minimum purchase amount on credit card transactions, not to exceed $10.<sup id="cite_ref-visa_101-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-visa-101">[101]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-paymentssource_102-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-paymentssource-102">[102]</a></sup></p><p>Recent complications include the addition of exceptions for non-signed purchases by telephone or on the Internet, and an additional security system called "<a href="/wiki/Verified_by_Visa" class="mw-redirect" title="Verified by Visa">Verified by Visa</a>" for purchases on the Internet.
</p><p>In September 2014, Visa Inc, launched a new service to replace account information on plastic cards with "token" –  a digital account number.<sup id="cite_ref-103" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-103">[103]</a></sup></p>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(6)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span id="Visa_Contactless_.28formerly_payWave.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Visa_Contactless_(formerly_payWave)">Visa Contactless (formerly payWave)<span id="payWave"></span><span id="Visa_Contactless"></span></span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Visa_Inc.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Visa Contactless (formerly payWave)" data-section="23" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-6 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-6">
<div class="floatright"><a href="/wiki/File:EMVCoContactlessIndicator.svg" class="image"><noscript><img alt="EMVCoContactlessIndicator.svg" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e0/EMVCoContactlessIndicator.svg/75px-EMVCoContactlessIndicator.svg.png" decoding="async" width="75" height="91" data-file-width="46" data-file-height="56"></noscript><span class="lazy-image-placeholder" style="width: 75px;height: 91px;" data-src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e0/EMVCoContactlessIndicator.svg/75px-EMVCoContactlessIndicator.svg.png" data-alt="EMVCoContactlessIndicator.svg" data-width="75" data-height="91" data-srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e0/EMVCoContactlessIndicator.svg/113px-EMVCoContactlessIndicator.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e0/EMVCoContactlessIndicator.svg/150px-EMVCoContactlessIndicator.svg.png 2x"> </span></a></div>
<p>In September 2007, Visa introduced <b>Visa payWave</b>, a <a href="/wiki/Contactless_payment" title="Contactless payment">contactless payment</a> technology feature that allows cardholders to wave their card in front of contactless payment terminals without the need to physically swipe or insert the card into a point-of-sale device.<sup id="cite_ref-104" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-104">[104]</a></sup> This is similar to the Mastercard <a href="/wiki/MasterCard#Mastercard_Contactless" class="mw-redirect" title="MasterCard">Contactless</a>  service and the <a href="/wiki/American_Express" title="American Express">American Express</a> <a href="/wiki/ExpressPay" class="mw-redirect" title="ExpressPay">ExpressPay</a>, with both using <a href="/wiki/Radio-frequency_identification" title="Radio-frequency identification">RFID</a> technology. All three use the same symbol as shown on the right.
</p><p>In Europe, Visa has introduced the <a href="/wiki/V_Pay" title="V Pay">V Pay</a> card, which is a <a href="/wiki/Smart_card" title="Smart card">chip</a>-only and <a href="/wiki/Personal_identification_number" title="Personal identification number">PIN</a>-only debit card.<sup id="cite_ref-105" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-105">[105]</a></sup> In Australia, take up has been the highest in the world, with more than 50% of in store Visa transactions now made via Visa payWave.<sup id="cite_ref-106" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-106">[106]</a></sup></p>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(7)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="mVisa">mVisa</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Visa_Inc.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: mVisa" data-section="24" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-7 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-7">
<p>mVisa is a mobile payment app allowing payment via <a href="/wiki/Smartphones" class="mw-redirect" title="Smartphones">smartphones</a> using <a href="/wiki/QR_code" title="QR code">QR code</a>. This <a href="/wiki/QR_code_payment" title="QR code payment">QR code payment</a> method was first introduced in India in 2015.  It was later expanded to a number of other countries, including in Africa and south east Asia.<sup id="cite_ref-107" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-107">[107]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-108" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-108">[108]</a></sup></p>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(8)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Visa_Checkout">Visa Checkout</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Visa_Inc.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Visa Checkout" data-section="25" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-8 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-8">
<p>In 2013 Visa launched Visa Checkout, an online payment system that removes the need to share card details with retailers. The Visa Checkout service allows users to enter all their personal details and card information, then use a single username and password to make purchases from online retailers. The service works with Visa credit, debit, and prepaid cards. On November 27, 2013 V.me went live in the UK, France, Spain and Poland, with Nationwide Building Society being the first financial institution in Britain to support it,<sup id="cite_ref-109" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-109">[109]</a></sup> although Nationwide subsequently withdrew this service in 2016.
</p>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(9)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Trademark_and_design">Trademark and design</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Visa_Inc.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Trademark and design" data-section="26" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-9 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-9">
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Logo_design">Logo design</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Visa_Inc.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: Logo design" data-section="27" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>The blue and gold in Visa's logo were chosen to represent the blue sky and gold-colored hills of <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a>, where the <a href="/wiki/Bank_of_America" title="Bank of America">Bank of America</a> was founded.
</p><p>In 2005, Visa changed its logo, removing the horizontal stripes in favor of a simple white background with the name Visa in blue with an orange flick on the 'V'.<sup id="cite_ref-110" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-110">[110]</a></sup> The orange flick was removed in favor of the logo being a solid blue gradient in 2014. In 2015, the gold and blue stripes were restored as card branding on <a href="/wiki/Visa_Debit" title="Visa Debit">Visa Debit</a> and <a href="/wiki/Visa_Electron" title="Visa Electron">Visa Electron</a>, although not as the company's logotype.<sup id="cite_ref-111" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-111">[111]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Card_design">Card design</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Visa_Inc.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: Card design" data-section="28" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:57px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Visa_holo.png" class="image"><noscript><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/c/cb/Visa_holo.png" decoding="async" width="55" height="41" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="55" data-file-height="41"></noscript><span class="lazy-image-placeholder" style="width: 55px;height: 41px;" data-src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/c/cb/Visa_holo.png" data-alt="" data-width="55" data-height="41" data-class="thumbimage"> </span></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Visa_holo.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The hologram</div></div></div><p>In 1984, most Visa cards around the world began to feature a <a href="/wiki/Hologram" class="mw-redirect" title="Hologram">hologram</a> of a <a href="/wiki/Dove" class="mw-redirect" title="Dove">dove</a> on its face, generally under the last four digits of the Visa number. This was implemented as a security feature – true holograms would appear three-dimensional and the image would change as the card was turned. At the same time, the Visa logo, which had previously covered the whole card face, was reduced in size to a strip on the card's right incorporating the hologram. This allowed issuing banks to customize the appearance of the card. Similar changes were implemented with MasterCard cards. Today, cards may be co-branded with various merchants, airlines, etc., and marketed as "reward cards".
</p><p>On older Visa cards, holding the face of the card under an <a href="/wiki/Ultraviolet_light" class="mw-redirect" title="Ultraviolet light">ultraviolet light</a> will reveal the dove picture, dubbed the Ultra-Sensitive Dove,<sup id="cite_ref-112" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-112">[112]</a></sup> as an additional security test. (On newer Visa cards, the UV dove is replaced by a small V over the Visa logo.)
</p><p>Beginning in 2005, the Visa standard was changed to allow for the hologram to be placed on the back of the card, or to be replaced with a holographic magnetic stripe ("HoloMag").<sup id="cite_ref-113" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-113">[113]</a></sup> The HoloMag card was shown to occasionally cause interference with card readers, so Visa eventually withdrew designs of HoloMag cards and reverted to traditional magnetic strips.<sup id="cite_ref-114" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-114">[114]</a></sup></p>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(10)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Signatures">Signatures</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Visa_Inc.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=29" title="Edit section: Signatures" data-section="29" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-10 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-10">
<p>Visa made a statement on January 12, 2018 that the signature requirement would become optional for all EMV contact or contactless chip-enabled merchants in North America starting in April 2018.  It was noted that the signatures are no longer necessary to fight fraud and the fraud capabilities have advanced allowing this elimination leading to a faster in-store purchase experience.<sup id="cite_ref-115" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-115">[115]</a></sup>  Visa was the last of the major credit card issuers to relax the signature requirements.  The first to eliminate the signature was MasterCard Inc. followed by Discover Financial Services and American Express Co.<sup id="cite_ref-116" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-116">[116]</a></sup></p>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(11)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Sponsorships">Sponsorships</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Visa_Inc.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=30" title="Edit section: Sponsorships" data-section="30" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-11 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-11">
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Olympics_and_Paralympics">Olympics and Paralympics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Visa_Inc.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=31" title="Edit section: Olympics and Paralympics" data-section="31" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
