<p><b>The Home Depot, Inc.</b> is the largest <a href="/wiki/Home_improvement" title="Home improvement">home improvement</a> retailer in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> supplying <a href="/wiki/Tool" title="Tool">tools</a>, <a href="/wiki/Construction" title="Construction">construction</a> products, and services. The company is headquartered at the Atlanta Store Support Center at 2455 Paces Ferry Road NW; Atlanta, GA, 30339<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> in unincorporated <a href="/wiki/Cobb_County,_Georgia" title="Cobb County, Georgia">Cobb County, Georgia</a> (with an <a href="/wiki/Atlanta" title="Atlanta">Atlanta</a> mailing address).

</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(1)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Home_Depot&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History" data-section="1" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-1 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-1">
<h3 class="in-block"><span id="1978.E2.80.931999"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="1978–1999">1978–1999</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Home_Depot&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: 1978–1999" data-section="2" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>The Home Depot was co-founded by <a href="/wiki/Bernard_Marcus" title="Bernard Marcus">Bernard Marcus</a>, <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Blank" title="Arthur Blank">Arthur Blank</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ron_Brill" title="Ron Brill">Ron Brill</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Pat_Farrah" title="Pat Farrah">Pat Farrah</a> in 1978.<sup id="cite_ref-Corporate_history_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Corporate_history-8">[8]</a></sup> The Home Depot's proposition was to build home-improvement <a href="/wiki/Big-box_store" title="Big-box store">superstores</a>, larger than any of their competitors' facilities.  Investment banker <a href="/wiki/Ken_Langone" class="mw-redirect" title="Ken Langone">Ken Langone</a> helped Marcus and Blank to secure the necessary <a href="/wiki/Financial_capital" title="Financial capital">capital</a>.
</p>
<table class="cquote pullquote" role="presentation" style="margin:auto; border-collapse: collapse; border: none; background-color: transparent; width: auto;"><tbody><tr><td style="width: 20px; vertical-align: top; border: none; color: #B2B7F2; font-size: 40px; font-family: 'Times New Roman', Times, serif; font-weight: bold; line-height: .6em; text-align: left; padding: 10px 10px;">“
</td>
<td style="vertical-align: top; border: none; padding: 4px 10px;">Bernie and I founded The Home Depot with a special vision – to create a company that would keep alive the values that were important to us.  Values like respect among all people, excellent customer service and giving back to communities and society<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></td>
<td style="width: 20px; vertical-align: bottom; border: none; color: #B2B7F2; font-size: 40px; font-family: 'Times New Roman', Times, serif; font-weight: bold; line-height: .6em; text-align: right; padding: 10px 10px;">”
</td></tr><tr><td colspan="3" class="cquotecite" style="border: none; padding-right: 4%; font-size: smaller; line-height:normal; text-align: right;"><cite>— <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Blank" title="Arthur Blank">Arthur Blank</a></cite>

<p>In 1979, the first two stores, built in spaces leased from <a href="/wiki/J._C._Penney" title="J. C. Penney">J. C. Penney</a> that were originally <a href="/wiki/The_Treasury_(retailer)" class="mw-redirect" title="The Treasury (retailer)">Treasure Island</a> "<a href="/wiki/Hypermarket" title="Hypermarket">hypermarket</a>" (<a href="/wiki/Discount_department_store" class="mw-redirect" title="Discount department store">discount department</a> and <a href="/wiki/Grocery_store" title="Grocery store">grocery</a>) stores, opened in <a href="/wiki/Metro_Atlanta" class="mw-redirect" title="Metro Atlanta">metro Atlanta</a> (in <a href="/wiki/Doraville" class="mw-redirect" title="Doraville">Doraville</a> and on <a href="/wiki/Memorial_Drive_(Atlanta)" title="Memorial Drive (Atlanta)">Memorial Drive</a> in <a href="/wiki/Decatur,_Georgia" title="Decatur, Georgia">Decatur</a>, both near <a href="/wiki/I-285_(GA)" class="mw-redirect" title="I-285 (GA)">I-285</a>)<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> on June 21. On September 22, 1981, The Home Depot went public on the <a href="/wiki/NASDAQ" class="mw-redirect" title="NASDAQ">NASDAQ</a> and raised $4.093 million. The Home Depot joined the <a href="/wiki/New_York_Stock_Exchange" title="New York Stock Exchange">New York Stock Exchange</a> on April 19, 1984.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Initial_public_offering" title="Initial public offering">initial public offering</a> of Home Depot stock was September 22, 1981.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p><p>The Home Depot began to branch out of Georgia to Florida in 1981 with stores opening in <a href="/wiki/Hollywood,_Florida" title="Hollywood, Florida">Hollywood</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ft._Lauderdale,_Florida" class="mw-redirect" title="Ft. Lauderdale, Florida">Ft. Lauderdale</a>. By 1984 The Home Depot was operating 19 stores with sales of over $256 million. To enter the <a href="/wiki/Dallas" title="Dallas">Dallas</a> market The Home Depot acquired Bowater Home Center from Bowater Inc. on October 31, 1984, for $40 million.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> The increased expansion of The Home Depot in the mid-1980s created financial difficulties with earnings falling at 42% and debt rising to $200 million. The financial difficulties of The Home Depot also caused the stock price to fall. To curb The Home Depot difficulties it opened only 10 stores in 1986 with a stock offering 2.99 million shares at $17 per share that helped The Home Depot to restructure its debts.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:282px;"><a href="/wiki/File:HomeDepotCanada2.jpg" class="image"><noscript><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6d/HomeDepotCanada2.jpg/280px-HomeDepotCanada2.jpg" decoding="async" width="280" height="185" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="4928" data-file-height="3264"></noscript><span class="lazy-image-placeholder" style="width: 280px;height: 185px;" data-src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6d/HomeDepotCanada2.jpg/280px-HomeDepotCanada2.jpg" data-alt="" data-width="280" data-height="185" data-class="thumbimage"> </span></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:HomeDepotCanada2.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The Home Depot store in <a href="/wiki/Markham,_Ontario" title="Markham, Ontario">Markham, Ontario</a>, <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>.</div></div></div>
<p>In 1989 The Home Depot became the largest home improvement store in the United States surpassing Lowe's. In the 1990s The Home Depot searched for ways to redefine its marketplace. An installation program for quality home improvement items such as windows or carpets was launched in 1991 called the EXPO with success. A 480-page book <i>Home Improvement 1-2-3</i> was published in 1995. The Canadian hardware chain <a href="/wiki/Aikenhead%27s_Hardware" title="Aikenhead's Hardware">Aikenhead's Hardware</a> was acquired by The Home Depot in 1994 for $150 million with a 75% share. All of the Aikenhead's Hardware stores were later converted to The Home Depot stores.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> By 1995, sales reached $10 billion while operating 350 stores.
</p><p>Former <a href="/wiki/General_Electric" title="General Electric">General Electric</a> executive <a href="/wiki/Robert_Nardelli" title="Robert Nardelli">Robert Nardelli</a> became CEO and president of The Home Depot in 2000.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span id="2000.E2.80.932007"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="2000–2007">2000–2007</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Home_Depot&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: 2000–2007" data-section="3" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/San_Diego" title="San Diego">San Diego</a> maintenance and repair supplies company Maintenance Warehouse was purchased by The Home Depot in 1997 for $245 million.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> Maintenance Warehouse was purchased because it was a leading direct-mail marketer of maintenance, repair and operations supplies that can reach customers out of reach by The Home Depot.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> Atlanta-based company Apex Supply was acquired by The Home Depot in 1999. Apex Supply is a wholesale distributor of plumbing, HVAC, industrial pipe and fittings.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> Apex Supply and Maintenance Warehouse were rebranded in 2004 as "The Home Depot Supply."<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p><p>In 2004, Home Depot employees at a suburban Detroit store in <a href="/wiki/Harper_Woods,_Michigan" title="Harper Woods, Michigan">Harper Woods, Michigan</a>, rejected a bid to be represented by a <a href="/wiki/Labor_union" class="mw-redirect" title="Labor union">labor union</a>, voting 115 to 42 against joining the <a href="/wiki/United_Food_and_Commercial_Workers" title="United Food and Commercial Workers">United Food and Commercial Workers</a>. If the union had won, the Michigan store would have been the first Home Depot to have union representation.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p><p>Your Other Warehouse, a large plumbing distributor with a focus on special order fulfillment, was acquired by The Home Depot in 2001. Your Other Warehouse also supplied two divisions of The Home Depot and the EXPO Design Centers.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> The "EXPO Design Center" division was reorganized in 2001 with three divisions based in the Northeast at <a href="/wiki/South_Plainfield,_New_Jersey" title="South Plainfield, New Jersey">South Plainfield, New Jersey</a>, the West at <a href="/wiki/Orange,_California" title="Orange, California">Orange, California</a>, and the Southeast at <a href="/wiki/Atlanta,_Georgia" class="mw-redirect" title="Atlanta, Georgia">Atlanta, Georgia</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p><p>The Home Depot entered the <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">Mexican</a> market in 2002 with the acquisition of the home improvement chain Del Norte. In addition, The Home Depot had begun construction of stores in <a href="/wiki/Mexicali" title="Mexicali">Mexicali</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tijuana" title="Tijuana">Tijuana</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> In the same year the <a href="/wiki/Home_Depot_Landscape_Supply" title="Home Depot Landscape Supply">Home Depot Landscape Supply</a> was launched to integrate professional landscapers and upscale plants into a <a href="/wiki/Plant_nursery" title="Plant nursery">plant nursery</a> retail chain.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> Home Depot Landscape Supply lasted only five years with only a few stores each in metro Atlanta and <a href="/wiki/Dallas/Fort_Worth" class="mw-redirect" title="Dallas/Fort Worth">Dallas/Fort Worth</a>. The Home Depot decided to close all Home Depot Landscape Supply stores in late 2007.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p><p>In September 2005, Home Depot Direct launched its online home-furnishings store, 10 Crescent Lane, shortly followed by the launch of "Paces Trading Company," its online lighting store. In mid 2006, the Home Depot acquired Home Decorators Collection, which was placed as an additional brand under its Home Depot Direct division.
</p><p>In 2006, the Home Depot acquired <a href="/wiki/Hughes_Supply" title="Hughes Supply">Hughes Supply</a> the largest home retailer in the United States for $3.2 billion.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> Hughes Supply was integrated into The Home Depot Supply to better serve business-to-business customers.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> The Home Depot Supply rebranded under the new name <a href="/wiki/HD_Supply" title="HD Supply">HD Supply</a> in January 2007.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> Five months later The Home Depot sold HD Supply to a consortium of three <a href="/wiki/Private_equity" title="Private equity">private equity</a> firms, <a href="/wiki/The_Carlyle_Group" title="The Carlyle Group">The Carlyle Group</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bain_Capital" title="Bain Capital">Bain Capital</a> and <a href="/wiki/Clayton,_Dubilier_and_Rice" class="mw-redirect" title="Clayton, Dubilier and Rice">Clayton, Dubilier and Rice</a> (with each agreeing to buy a one-third stake in the division).<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span id="2007.E2.80.93present"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="2007–present">2007–present</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Home_Depot&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: 2007–present" data-section="4" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>On January 2, 2007, the Home Depot and <a href="/wiki/Robert_Nardelli" title="Robert Nardelli">Robert Nardelli</a> mutually agreed on Nardelli's resignation as CEO after a six-year tenure.  Nardelli resigned amid complaints over his heavy-handed management and whether his pay package of $123.7 million (excluding <a href="/wiki/Stock_option" class="mw-redirect" title="Stock option">stock option</a> grants) over the previous five years was excessive, considering the stock's poor performance versus its competitor <a href="/wiki/Lowe%27s" title="Lowe's">Lowe</a>'s. His <a href="/wiki/Severance_package" title="Severance package">severance package</a> of $210 million was criticized because when the stock went down, his pay went up.<sup id="cite_ref-Corporate_news_release_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Corporate_news_release-32">[32]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Business_Week_article_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Business_Week_article-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Home_Depot,_center_aisle,_Natick_MA.jpg" class="image"><noscript><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/55/Home_Depot%2C_center_aisle%2C_Natick_MA.jpg/300px-Home_Depot%2C_center_aisle%2C_Natick_MA.jpg" decoding="async" width="300" height="225" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="3264" data-file-height="2448"></noscript><span class="lazy-image-placeholder" style="width: 300px;height: 225px;" data-src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/55/Home_Depot%2C_center_aisle%2C_Natick_MA.jpg/300px-Home_Depot%2C_center_aisle%2C_Natick_MA.jpg" data-alt="" data-width="300" data-height="225" data-class="thumbimage"> </span></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Home_Depot,_center_aisle,_Natick_MA.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Center aisle of a Home Depot store in 2014.</div></div></div>
<p>His successor, <a href="/wiki/Frank_Blake" title="Frank Blake">Frank Blake</a>, previously served as the company's vice chairman of the board and executive vice president. Blake agreed to a much more conservative compensation package than Nardelli, that is very heavily dependent upon the success of the company. Although a longtime deputy to Nardelli at GE and Home Depot, Blake was said to lack Nardelli's hard edge and instead preferred to make decisions by consensus. Indeed, Blake repudiated many of his predecessor's strategies, and it has been reported that the two men have not spoken since Nardelli departed Home Depot.<sup id="cite_ref-Corporate_news_release_32-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Corporate_news_release-32">[32]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></p><p>In 2008 and 2009, with the downturn in the housing market, The Home Depot announced the layoff of several thousand associates, as well as the closing of 54 stores nationwide, including the entire EXPO Design Center chain.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> Associates at EXPO were allowed to re-apply for Home Depot jobs after the <a href="/wiki/Layoff" title="Layoff">layoffs</a>, and did not lose any tenure if hired back. In the year of February 2009, sales totaled $71.288 billion, more than $20 billion down from the peak of two years earlier due to the sale of HD Supply and falling revenue at the retained business. In 2012, they proceeded to close the big-box style stores that they had in China, however smaller stores that specialized in custom products and focused on more intimate interactions between customers and associates remain open there.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></p><p>In 2013, The Home Depot established two large distribution centers in <a href="/wiki/Atlanta" title="Atlanta">Atlanta</a> and <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles" title="Los Angeles">Los Angeles</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a href="/wiki/File:InterlinebrandsJacksonville.jpg" class="image"><noscript><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0e/InterlinebrandsJacksonville.jpg/300px-InterlinebrandsJacksonville.jpg" decoding="async" width="300" height="197" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="2903" data-file-height="1904"></noscript><span class="lazy-image-placeholder" style="width: 300px;height: 197px;" data-src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0e/InterlinebrandsJacksonville.jpg/300px-InterlinebrandsJacksonville.jpg" data-alt="" data-width="300" data-height="197" data-class="thumbimage"> </span></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:InterlinebrandsJacksonville.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Interline_Brands" class="mw-redirect" title="Interline Brands">Interline Brands</a> headquarters in <a href="/wiki/Jacksonville,_Florida" title="Jacksonville, Florida">Jacksonville, Florida</a>.</div></div></div>
<p>In August 2014, it was announced that Frank Blake would step down as CEO and would be replaced by 57-year-old Craig Menear. The change occurred on November 1, 2014. Blake would continue with the company as chairman.
Menear joined The Home Depot in 1997, and served in various management and vice-presidential positions, until 2003, including merchandising vice president of hardware, merchandising vice president of the Southwest Division, and divisional merchandise manager of the Southwest Division. He subsequently served as senior vice president of merchandising from August 2003 to April 2007. He then served as an executive vice president of merchandising from April 2007 to February 2014. Until becoming CEO, Menear served as president of U.S. Retail from February 2014 to November 1, 2014.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup></p><p>The company had a data breach in September 2014.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> One major reason for the data breach was the practice of entering credit card numbers directly into computers at the service-desk and pro-desk, and in specialty departments including flooring, kitchen cabinets, appliances, and <a href="/wiki/Millwork_(building_material)" title="Millwork (building material)">millwork</a>, rather than using POS credit card terminals directly. The practice was stopped, and Home Depot offered a year of free credit monitoring through AllClearID for any customers who requested it. There were also reports of credit card numbers being stolen when used to make purchases on Homedepot.com.
</p><p>On July 22, 2015, Home Depot acquired <a href="/wiki/Interline_Brands" class="mw-redirect" title="Interline Brands">Interline Brands</a> from P2 Capital Partners, <a href="/wiki/Goldman_Sachs" title="Goldman Sachs">Goldman Sachs'</a> private equity arm, and the management of Interline Brands for $1.6 billion.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> Interline Brands became fully integrated with The Home Depot in August 2016 with the Interline Brands website merging with The Home Depot website. The subsidiaries of Interline Brands are now companies of The Home Depot.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup></p>

</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(3)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Mascot">Mascot</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Home_Depot&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Mascot" data-section="6" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-3 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-3">
<p>The Home Depot's mascot has been Homer D. Poe since 1981, when it was first used in advertising. Gwyn Raker, the illustrator, says, "I designed him to be a funny guy next door who wasn't intimidating." The Homer Fund, a for-associate charity, is named after the mascot, who since its creation, has been a part of Home Depot culture ever since. This includes signage, advertising, awards and even a life-size costume for stores to rent out.  Homer has a wife, named Daisy. 
<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup></p>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(4)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Operation">Operation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Home_Depot&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Operation" data-section="7" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-4 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-4">
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:402px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Homedepotstoremap.png" class="image"><noscript><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/85/Homedepotstoremap.png/400px-Homedepotstoremap.png" decoding="async" width="400" height="208" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="600" data-file-height="312"></noscript><span class="lazy-image-placeholder" style="width: 400px;height: 208px;" data-src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/85/Homedepotstoremap.png/400px-Homedepotstoremap.png" data-alt="" data-width="400" data-height="208" data-class="thumbimage"> </span></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Homedepotstoremap.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Map of Home Depot stores in the contiguous United States and Southern Canada, as of August 2011.</div></div></div>
<p>Home Depot stores average 105,000 ft<sup>2</sup> (9,755 m<sup>2</sup>) in size and are organized warehouse-style, stocking a large range of supplies.  Home Depot's two largest stores are located in <a href="/wiki/Vauxhall,_New_Jersey" title="Vauxhall, New Jersey">Vauxhall, New Jersey</a>, which encompasses 217,000 ft<sup>2</sup> of space, and in <a href="/wiki/Anaheim_Hills,_California" class="mw-redirect" title="Anaheim Hills, California">Anaheim Hills, California</a>, where it encompasses 204,000 ft<sup>2</sup>.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup>  The company color is a bright orange (PMS 165, CMYK 60M100Y, HEX FF6600), on signs, equipment and employee aprons. It was announced in August 2014 that Craig Menear will take over for Frank Blake as CEO while Blake will remain the chairman of the board.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Marketing">Marketing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Home_Depot&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Marketing" data-section="8" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>The Home Depot announced the new slogan "How Doers Get More Done" on December 5, 2019, replacing the previous slogan "More saving. More doing." which was introduced in the March 18, 2009 circular, replacing "You can do it. We can help." which had been used since 2003.<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> Other slogans used in the past 25 years include "The Home Depot, Low prices are just the beginning" in the early 1990s and "When you're at the Home Depot, You'll feel right at home" in the late 1990s and "The Home Depot: First In Home Improvement!" from 1999 to 2003.<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="US_Stores_division">US Stores division</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Home_Depot&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: US Stores division" data-section="9" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>Long-time employee Marc Powers became the head of Home Depot's US stores division in 2014. He took the place of <a href="/wiki/Marvin_Ellison" title="Marvin Ellison">Marvin Ellison</a>, who left to become the chief executive of <a href="/wiki/J.C._Penney" class="mw-redirect" title="J.C. Penney">J.C. Penney</a>. In January, 2016 Home Depot announced the departure of Powers as division head, to be replaced by another veteran employee, Ann-Marie Campbell, one of Powers' deputies. The change in leadership is effective as of February 1, 2016. Ms. Campbell has been employed by Home Depot for over 30 years, beginning as a cashier in a branch in <a href="/wiki/South_Florida" title="South Florida">South Florida</a>. Her most recent role was as president of Home Depot's Southern division.<sup id="cite_ref-Wall_Street_Journal-Home_Depot_Replaces_Head_of_U.S._Stores_Business_50-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wall_Street_Journal-Home_Depot_Replaces_Head_of_U.S._Stores_Business-50">[50]</a></sup></p>

<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Philanthropy">Philanthropy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Home_Depot&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Philanthropy" data-section="16" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>The Home Depot Foundation is the philanthropic arm of the company created in 2002. It has contributed over $200 million in time, labor, money, and supplies to a number of causes, including <a href="/wiki/Habitat_for_Humanity" title="Habitat for Humanity">Habitat for Humanity</a>, California-based <a href="/wiki/City_of_Hope_National_Medical_Center" title="City of Hope National Medical Center">City of Hope National Medical Center</a>, and playground construction organization <a href="/wiki/KaBOOM!_(non-profit_organization)" title="KaBOOM! (non-profit organization)">KaBOOM!</a><sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup> Home Depot supports the U.S. Military community with a <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://gruntroll.com/company/home-depot">10% military discount</a>. Since 1993, the "Team Depot" program has provided grants to veteran-based organizations and has workers from a local store do volunteer work that would benefit veterans.<sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-59" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup></p><p>The Home Depot has partnered with the <a href="/wiki/Georgia_Emergency_Management_Agency" title="Georgia Emergency Management Agency">Georgia Emergency Management Agency</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Ready_Georgia" title="Ready Georgia">Ready Georgia</a> campaign, leading both supplies and facility use to this statewide effort to increase emergency preparedness among Georgia's children.<sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup> The company also provided <a href="/wiki/Survival_kit" title="Survival kit">ready kits</a> and other prizes for an art and essay contest for Georgia elementary school students.<sup id="cite_ref-61" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup></p><p>In 2005, The Home Depot was among 53 entities that contributed the maximum of $250,000 to the second inauguration of President <a href="/wiki/George_W._Bush" title="George W. Bush">George W. Bush</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-62" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-63" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-64" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup></p><p>In March 2018, the company donated $50 million to train 20,000 people as construction workers over the next decade. The Home Builders Institute will use the money to train veterans and <a href="/wiki/United_States_Army" title="United States Army">U.S. Army</a> soldiers, high school students and disadvantaged youth. The financial support from Home Depot was to help address the needs of the shortage of construction workers.<sup id="cite_ref-65" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-66" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Environmental_record">Environmental record</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Home_Depot&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Environmental record" data-section="17" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>The Home Depot has stated on their website that they have a commitment "we continue to lead by example, demonstrating to the world that sustainability business practices are not only possible, they are good for business".<sup id="cite_ref-67" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup> The Home Depot introduced a label on nearly 3,000 products in 2007. The label promotes energy conservation, sustainable forestry and clean water. Home Depot executives said that as the world's-largest buyer of construction material, their company had the power to persuade thousands of suppliers, homebuilders and consumers to follow its lead on environment sustainability. "Who in the world has a chance to have a bigger impact on this sector than Home Depot?" asked Ron Jarvis, vice president for environmental innovation at Home Depot.<sup id="cite_ref-EnvLabel_68-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EnvLabel-68">[68]</a></sup> This program is following The Home Depot's promise in the late 1990s to eliminate the number of sales of lumber from endangered forests in countries including Chile and Indonesia.<sup id="cite_ref-69" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup> Home Depot has since worked with environmental groups to create a variety of green programs. For example,  Home Depot planted thousands of trees at its headquarters in Atlanta to offset carbon emissions. In 2007, The Home Depot Foundation (the company's charitable foundation) committed to investing $100 million over the next decade to build over 100,000 green affordable homes and plant 3,000,000 trees.
</p><p>Additionally, The Home Depot promotes <a href="/wiki/Compact_fluorescent" class="mw-redirect" title="Compact fluorescent">compact fluorescent</a> light bulbs in its stores. As part of this effort, the company created the largest <a href="/wiki/Recycling" title="Recycling">recycling</a> program in the United States for the bulbs.<sup id="cite_ref-CFL_Recycling_70-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CFL_Recycling-70">[70]</a></sup>
In March 2013, Home Depot locations in Canada stopped accepting compact fluorescent light bulbs for recycling.<sup id="cite_ref-71" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup></p><p><br></p>

<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Canada">Canada</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Home_Depot&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Canada" data-section="22" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>Home Depot Canada is the Canadian unit of the Home Depot and one of Canada's top home improvement retailers. The Canadian operation consists of 182 stores and employs over 28,000 people in Canada. Home Depot Canada has stores in all ten Canadian provinces and serves territorial Nunavut, Northwest Territories, and Yukon through electronic means (Online Sales). The Canadian head office is located in Toronto.
</p><p>The Canadian unit was created with the purchase of <a href="/wiki/Aikenhead%27s_Hardware" title="Aikenhead's Hardware">Aikenhead's Hardware</a>. Home Depot management had an ambitious plan to overtake its biggest competitor, <a href="/wiki/Rona,_Inc." title="Rona, Inc.">RONA</a>, which has about four times as many stores. However, some of RONA's stores are smaller than the typical Home Depot store. In terms of big box stores, the Home Depot has more stores than RONA (not including other Rona banners such as <a href="/wiki/R%C3%A9no-D%C3%A9p%C3%B4t" title="Réno-Dépôt">Réno-Dépôt</a> or Cashway). As of 2007, RONA pulled ahead of The Home Depot in total retail sales, due to aggressive consolidation efforts by RONA, combined with the loss of The Home Depot's industrial supply division, HD Supply, in July 2007. The Home Depot now faces competition from Lowe's as they have moved into the Canadian market effective the end of 2007; Lowe's now has 35 outlets in Canada.  In 2016, RONA was purchased by Lowes increasing its total store count to over 500 units.
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Quebec" title="Quebec">Quebec</a>, where it has 22 stores, The Home Depot is branded simply Home Depot (using English words but without the <a href="/wiki/Definite_article" class="mw-redirect" title="Definite article">definite article</a> "The").
</p>

<p>The Home Depot operates 120 stores and has become one of the largest retailers in Mexico since it entered the market in 2001. The Home Depot increased its presence in Mexico in 2004, with the acquisition of Home Mart, the second largest Mexican home improvement retailer.<sup id="cite_ref-homedepot.com.mx_81-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-homedepot.com.mx-81">[81]</a></sup></p><p>The Home Depot Mexico employs more than 15,000 associates throughout the country and as of the end of 2016, it had a record of 50 consecutive quarters with posted growth.<sup id="cite_ref-homedepot.com.mx_81-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-homedepot.com.mx-81">[81]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="China">China</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Home_Depot&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: China" data-section="24" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>In December 2006, the Home Depot announced its acquisition of the Chinese home improvement retailer The Home Way.<sup id="cite_ref-The_Home_Way_82-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-The_Home_Way-82">[82]</a></sup>  The acquisition gave the Home Depot an immediate presence in China, with 12 stores in six cities.
</p><p>In April 2011, Home Depot shut its last Beijing store, the fifth Home Depot to close in China in the previous two years. In September 2012, The Home Depot announced it was closing all big box stores in China. The Home Depot retained two specialty stores in China, a Home Decorators Collection Store and a paint and flooring store.<sup id="cite_ref-Burkitt_83-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Burkitt-83">[83]</a></sup></p><p>As of September 16, 2012, all seven of the box stores in China had been shut down.<sup id="cite_ref-84" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-84">[84]</a></sup> The Home Depot has no immediate plans to further expand its specialty stores in China. The company is taking a "wait-and-see" attitude towards the Chinese market, but does not want to completely pull out because re-entry into the market would be very costly.
</p><p>The Home Depot's lack of success in China has been attributed to the disconnect between The Home Depot's do-it-yourself ethos and Chinese culture. In 2012, the Home Depot conceded that it misread the country's appetite for do-it-yourself products. As a spokeswoman for the company said in an interview with the Wall Street Journal, "The market trend says this is more of a do-it-for-me culture".<sup id="cite_ref-Burkitt_83-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Burkitt-83">[83]</a></sup> Some have speculated that The Home Depot could have offered a do-it-for-me model to Chinese consumers. Chinese consumers prefer to see a finished product, such as a renovated room, rather than light bulbs and lumber.<sup id="cite_ref-85" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-85">[85]</a></sup> The same issue does not exist outside of China, especially in Canada, where Chinese Home Depot advertisements and store signage can be found in areas with large Chinese demographics.
</p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="United_Kingdom">United Kingdom</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Home_Depot&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: United Kingdom" data-section="25" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>There were reports that The Home Depot was interested in acquiring <a href="/wiki/B%26Q" title="B&amp;Q">B&amp;Q</a>, the largest <a href="/wiki/DIY" class="mw-redirect" title="DIY">DIY</a> retailer in the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a> and <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>. Speculation of a takeover began in 1999 when the retailer <a href="/wiki/Asda" title="Asda">Asda</a> was purchased by <a href="/wiki/Walmart" title="Walmart">Walmart</a>. The Home Depot would have to acquire <a href="/wiki/Kingfisher_plc" title="Kingfisher plc">Kingfisher plc</a>, B&amp;Q's parent company, to acquire B&amp;Q. Kingfisher consists of several European DIY chains; however, the Home Depot was only interested in B&amp;Q operations and says that it would dispose of the <a href="/wiki/Castorama" title="Castorama">Castorama</a> chain which operates in France, Italy, Poland and Russia. Talks ending in 2005 did not result in any takeover deal.<sup id="cite_ref-86" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-86">[86]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-87" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-87">[87]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-88" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-88">[88]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-89" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-89">[89]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="South_America">South America</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Home_Depot&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: South America" data-section="26" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>In 1997, Home Depot entered the Chilean and Argentine markets. While the venture was viewed with great optimism by founders <a href="/wiki/Bernard_Marcus" title="Bernard Marcus">Bernard Marcus</a> and <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Blank" title="Arthur Blank">Arthur Blank</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-Built_from_Scratch_90-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Built_from_Scratch-90">[90]</a></sup> it eventually proved unprofitable. In October 2001, Chilean partners <a href="/wiki/Falabella_(company)" class="mw-redirect" title="Falabella (company)">Falabella</a> bought out Home Depot's share of the five Chilean Home Center stores and rebranded them Home Store. In 2003, after merging with <a href="/wiki/Sodimac" title="Sodimac">Sodimac</a>, all stores adopted that brand. The company has since expanded across Latin America very profitably and successfully. It's currently the N°1 Home Improvement company in South America. In 2002, Argentina's Home Depots were bought out by Chilean company <a href="/wiki/Cencosud" title="Cencosud">Cencosud</a> and rebranded <a href="/wiki/Easy_(store)" title="Easy (store)">Easy</a> stores, a company that has also expanded across South America. It's currently the N°2 Home Improvement company in South America.
</p>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(7)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Controversy">Controversy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Home_Depot&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: Controversy" data-section="27" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-7 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-7">
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Whistleblower_case">Whistleblower case</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Home_Depot&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: Whistleblower case" data-section="28" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>The Home Depot was embroiled in <a href="/wiki/Whistleblower" title="Whistleblower">whistleblower</a> litigation brought under the <a href="/wiki/Sarbanes-Oxley_Act" class="mw-redirect" title="Sarbanes-Oxley Act">Sarbanes-Oxley Act</a> (SOX) law. In July 2005, former employee Michael Davis, represented by attorney <a href="/wiki/Mark_D._Schwartz" title="Mark D. Schwartz">Mark D. Schwartz</a> filed a whistleblower lawsuit against the Home Depot, alleging that his discharge was in retaliation for refusing to make unwarranted backcharges against vendors. Davis alleges that the Home Depot forced its employees to meet a set quota of backcharges to cover damaged or defective merchandise, forcing employees to make chargebacks to vendors for merchandise that was undamaged and not defective. The Home Depot alleges that it fired Davis for repeatedly failing to show up for work.
</p><p>The trial initially was concluded in June 2006, but in April 2007, U.S. Department of Labor Judge Pamela Lakes Wood ordered the case reopened after the Home Depot's law firm <a href="/wiki/Akin_Gump_Strauss_Hauer_%26_Feld" title="Akin Gump Strauss Hauer &amp; Feld">Akin Gump Strauss Hauer &amp; Feld</a> revealed that the retail giant's in-house counsel had told them that two Home Depot employees who testified at the trial had lied. Akin Gump sent Wood a letter on September 29, 2006, in which the law firm requested that the testimony be stricken. In response to Akin Gump's revelation, Davis' attorney Mark D. Schwartz asked for the case to be reopened to permit further questioning of the witnesses. On April 6, 2007, Wood ordered the case to be reopened.
</p><p>Schwartz was quoted by the <i><a href="/wiki/New_York_Post" title="New York Post">New York Post</a></i> as saying, "I have reason to believe these witnesses were intimidated into giving false testimony." The Home Depot called Schwartz's allegations "meritless".<sup id="cite_ref-91" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-91">[91]</a></sup></p><p>Home Depot has settled the dispute in a stipulation of settlement dated March 28, 2008. In the settlement, Home Depot changed some of its corporate governance provisions. Home Depot also agreed to pay the plaintiff's counsel $6 million in cash and $8.5 million in common stock.<sup id="cite_ref-92" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-92">[92]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Patent_law_controversy">Patent law controversy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Home_Depot&amp;action=edit&amp;section=29" title="Edit section: Patent law controversy" data-section="29" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Powell_v._Home_Depot" class="mw-redirect" title="Powell v. Home Depot">Powell v. Home Depot</a> USA, Inc. (2008cv61862) (2011) was a decision by the <a href="/wiki/United_States_District_Court_for_the_Southern_District_of_Florida" title="United States District Court for the Southern District of Florida">United States District Court for the Southern District of Florida</a> concerning <a href="/wiki/Patent_infringement" title="Patent infringement">patent infringement</a> on a "safe hands" device that Michael Powell, an independent contractor for Home Depot, created in response to injuries to the hands of associates using in-store radial arm saws. The district court jury returned a verdict in favor of Powell.
</p><p>In 2011, Home Depot appealed against the decision to the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Court_of_Appeals_for_the_Federal_Circuit" title="United States Court of Appeals for the Federal Circuit">United States Court of Appeals for the Federal Circuit</a>, challenging the district court's denial of its renewed motion for judgment as a matter of law on the issues of infringement, willfulness, and damages.<sup id="cite_ref-93" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-93">[93]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-94" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-94">[94]</a></sup> They also challenged the district court's claim construction, inequitable conduct, and attorney fees determinations. The appellate court found no inequitable conduct and insufficiently egregious misconduct on the part of Powell's attorney.<sup id="cite_ref-95" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-95">[95]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Tilt-up_construction_design_of_stores">Tilt-up construction design of stores</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Home_Depot&amp;action=edit&amp;section=30" title="Edit section: Tilt-up construction design of stores" data-section="30" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>In the wake of the <a href="/wiki/2011_Joplin_tornado" title="2011 Joplin tornado">2011 Joplin tornado</a> in which the walls of a Home Depot collapsed after being hit by an <a href="/wiki/EF5_tornado" class="mw-redirect" title="EF5 tornado">EF5 tornado</a>, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Kansas_City_Star" title="The Kansas City Star">The Kansas City Star</a></i>, citing engineers, criticized Home Depot's practice of using <a href="/wiki/Tilt-up" class="mw-redirect" title="Tilt-up">tilt-up</a> construction in hundreds of its big-box stores (other nearby big-box stores in Joplin, including a <a href="/wiki/Walmart" title="Walmart">Walmart</a> and <a href="/wiki/Academy_Sports" class="mw-redirect" title="Academy Sports">Academy Sports</a>, which had <a href="/wiki/Concrete_masonry_unit" title="Concrete masonry unit">concrete block</a> construction, lost their roofs but the walls remained intact). In tilt-up construction the concrete is poured onsite, lifted into place, and then attached to the roof. The engineers told the <i>Star</i> that the practice, while normally safe and efficient, is dangerous in major storms because once the roof is lifted (as happened in Joplin) the walls collapse in a <a href="/wiki/Domino_effect" title="Domino effect">domino effect</a>.  Seven people were killed in the front of the store when the 100,000-pound walls collapsed on them, while 28 people in the back of the store survived when those walls collapsed outward. Only two of the slab walls in the Home Depot survived. In contrast, 3 people died in the Walmart but 200 survived. Engineers noted that when concrete block construction fails, structural elements break in pieces and usually not in huge slabs.  Home Depot said it fundamentally disagreed with the engineers quoted by the <i>Star</i> and said it would use tilt-up construction when it rebuilds the Joplin store.<sup id="cite_ref-96" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-96">[96]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Payment-system_breach">Payment-system breach</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Home_Depot&amp;action=edit&amp;section=31" title="Edit section: Payment-system breach" data-section="31" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/List_of_data_breaches_in_the_United_States" class="mw-redirect" title="List of data breaches in the United States">List of data breaches in the United States</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:HomeDepotPinOakHoustonselfcheckout.jpg" class="image"><noscript><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b9/HomeDepotPinOakHoustonselfcheckout.jpg/170px-HomeDepotPinOakHoustonselfcheckout.jpg" decoding="async" width="170" height="226" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="1625" data-file-height="2165"></noscript><span class="lazy-image-placeholder" style="width: 170px;height: 226px;" data-src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b9/HomeDepotPinOakHoustonselfcheckout.jpg/170px-HomeDepotPinOakHoustonselfcheckout.jpg" data-alt="" data-width="170" data-height="226" data-class="thumbimage"> </span></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:HomeDepotPinOakHoustonselfcheckout.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Self-checkout system</div></div></div>
<p>On September 2, 2014, security news reporter <a href="/wiki/Brian_Krebs" title="Brian Krebs">Brian Krebs</a>  reported that he was seeing evidence of credit card numbers linked to Home Depot purchases being sold online, which he concluded to suggest that The Home Depot's payment systems were breached by hackers.<sup id="cite_ref-97" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-97">[97]</a></sup> On September 8, 2014, Home Depot confirmed that their payment systems were compromised.<sup id="cite_ref-98" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-98">[98]</a></sup> According to their press release, this breach affected any customers who made purchases at any Home Depot store from April 2014 to September 2014. Home Depot offered their affected customers free one-year credit monitoring service from <a href="/wiki/AllClear_ID" title="AllClear ID">AllClear ID</a>. Also in their press release, they made sure to emphasize that there was no evidence to suggest that online customers were affected by the breach. On September 18, 2014, Home Depot released a statement saying that the hackers obtained a total of <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://krebsonsecurity.com/2014/09/home-depot-56m-cards-impacted-malware-contained/">56 million credit card numbers</a> as a result of the breach. Since the breach, Home Depot has rolled out new encryption technology for their cash registers and self-checkout systems to protect customers. A class action lawsuit was filed against the company.
</p><p>In March 2016 Home Depot agreed to pay at least $19.5 million to compensate the more than 50 million consumers affected. The settlement terms included a $13 million fund to reimburse shoppers and a $6.5 million fund for cardholder identity protection services.<sup id="cite_ref-99" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-99">[99]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="EEOC_disability_discrimination_suit">EEOC disability discrimination suit</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Home_Depot&amp;action=edit&amp;section=32" title="Edit section: EEOC disability discrimination suit" data-section="32" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>In September 2012, Home Depot agreed to pay $100,000 and furnish other relief to settle a <a href="/wiki/Disability" title="Disability">disability</a> discrimination lawsuit<sup id="cite_ref-100" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-100">[100]</a></sup> filed by the U.S <a href="/wiki/Equal_Employment_Opportunity_Commission" title="Equal Employment Opportunity Commission">Equal Employment Opportunity Commission</a>, for the alleged failure to provide a <a href="/wiki/Reasonable_accommodation" title="Reasonable accommodation">reasonable accommodation</a> for a cashier with cancer at its <a href="/wiki/Towson,_Maryland" title="Towson, Maryland">Towson, Maryland</a>, store and then for purportedly firing her because of her condition.<sup id="cite_ref-101" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-101">[101]</a></sup></p>
