</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(1)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=CenterPoint_Energy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History" data-section="1" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-1 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-1">
<p>When the state of Texas deregulated the electricity market, the former <a href="/wiki/Houston_Lighting_%26_Power" title="Houston Lighting &amp; Power">Houston Lighting &amp; Power</a> (HL&amp;P) was split into several companies.<sup id="cite_ref-Exelonbids_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Exelonbids-4">[4]</a></sup> In 2003 HL&amp;P was split into <a href="/wiki/Reliant_Energy" title="Reliant Energy">Reliant Energy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Texas_Genco" title="Texas Genco">Texas Genco</a>, and CenterPoint Energy.<sup id="cite_ref-Fowler_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fowler-5">[5]</a></sup></p><p>Until December 15, 2004, CenterPoint Energy and its predecessors operated in its various markets under these names; they were used separately prior to Reliant Energy, and later in conjunction with the Reliant Energy and CenterPoint Energy names:
</p>
<ul><li>Minnegasco (Natural gas throughout parts of Minnesota)</li>
<li>Houston Lighting and Power (or HL&amp;P) Houston-Galveston electric provider</li>
<li>Entex (Natural gas throughout South and East Texas, Southern Louisiana and Mississippi)</li>
<li>Arkla (Natural gas throughout Northern Louisiana, Northeast Texas,<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Oklahoma, and Arkansas)</li></ul><p>In late 2004, four private equity firms—the <a href="/wiki/Texas_Pacific_Group" class="mw-redirect" title="Texas Pacific Group">Texas Pacific Group</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Blackstone_Group" class="mw-redirect" title="Blackstone Group">Blackstone Group</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kohlberg_Kravis_Roberts" title="Kohlberg Kravis Roberts">Kohlberg Kravis Roberts</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Hellman_%26_Friedman" title="Hellman &amp; Friedman">Hellman &amp; Friedman</a>—combined forces to purchase <a href="/wiki/Texas_Genco" title="Texas Genco">Texas Genco</a> from Centerpoint. Later in 2006, Texas Genco was sold to <a href="/wiki/NRG_Energy" title="NRG Energy">NRG Energy</a> of Princeton, N.J.
</p><p>On April 23, 2018 CenterPoint Energy and <a href="/wiki/Vectren" title="Vectren">Vectren Corporation</a> announced they have entered into a definitive merger agreement.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<dl><dt>September 2008 power outage</dt></dl><div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:CenterPoint_Energy_CIMG0887.JPG" class="image"><noscript><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bd/CenterPoint_Energy_CIMG0887.JPG/220px-CenterPoint_Energy_CIMG0887.JPG" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="1600" data-file-height="1200"></noscript><span class="lazy-image-placeholder" style="width: 220px;height: 165px;" data-src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bd/CenterPoint_Energy_CIMG0887.JPG/220px-CenterPoint_Energy_CIMG0887.JPG" data-alt="" data-width="220" data-height="165" data-class="thumbimage"> </span></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:CenterPoint_Energy_CIMG0887.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A CenterPoint Energy facility in Downtown Houston.</div></div></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Ike" title="Hurricane Ike">Hurricane Ike</a> caused great disruption of service in the Greater Houston Area, wiping out 2.1 million of CenterPoint Energy's 2.26 million clients' electricity.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> This was the largest power outage in the company's 130-year history, and the largest in the state's history.
</p>
<dl><dt>Smart meter pilot program</dt></dl><p>In March 2009, the company’s five-year smart meter deployment began, delivering enhanced <a href="/wiki/Smart_meter#United_States" title="Smart meter">smart meter</a> functionality to Retail Electric Providers (REPs).
The company worked with the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Energy" title="United States Department of Energy">Department of Energy</a> for a pilot program in Texas centered on energy consumption.<sup id="cite_ref-pilot_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pilot-8">[8]</a></sup> After working with 500-residential electricity customers in the Houston area, it was found that by using a smart meter, consumers cut down on energy use for the home.<sup id="cite_ref-pilot_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pilot-8">[8]</a></sup> The pilot program was funded in part by the $200 million it received from the federal stimulus act.<sup id="cite_ref-pilot_8-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pilot-8">[8]</a></sup></p><p>CenterPoint Energy has been working with IBM. In 2013, IBM helped CenterPoint Energy develop the  Customer Vision Platform. The Customer Vision Platform will give CenterPoint a 360-degree view of their 5 million metered customers, incorporating their gas, electric and home services customers. It’s going to give customers a range of choices around how they want to interact with CenterPoint while also providing a consistent experience across the board.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p><p><b>Electric vehicles</b>
</p><p>In 2010, CenterPoint Energy and <a href="/wiki/Ford_Motor_Company" title="Ford Motor Company">Ford Motor Company</a> formed a partnership to promote adoption of <a href="/wiki/Electric_vehicle" title="Electric vehicle">electric vehicles</a>. The two companies teamed up to work together to create electric vehicle consumer outreach and education programs; they also distributed details to various stakeholders about charging needs and the requirements needed to "ensure the electrical infrastructure can support the necessary demand." Additionally: "The Ford and CenterPoint Energy collaboration also includes developing strategies to minimize the emissions and distribution impacts of charging electric vehicles by using statewide system renewable energy resources and more efficient use of household electricity."<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(2)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Criticism">Criticism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=CenterPoint_Energy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Criticism" data-section="2" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-2 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-2">
<p>On December 16, 2005, CenterPoint Energy Inc. said it would restate its finances for 2004 and the first three quarters of 2005 to correct accounting errors that overstated revenue and natural gas expenses.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p><p>In December 2011, the non-partisan organization <a href="/wiki/Public_Campaign#Reports" class="mw-redirect" title="Public Campaign">Public Campaign</a> criticized CenterPoint Energy for spending $2.65 million on <a href="/wiki/Lobbying" title="Lobbying">lobbying</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tax_avoidance" title="Tax avoidance">not paying any taxes</a> during 2008-2010, instead getting $284 million in tax rebates, despite making a profit of $1.9 billion, and having an executive pay between $12 and $13 million for its top 5 executives.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
