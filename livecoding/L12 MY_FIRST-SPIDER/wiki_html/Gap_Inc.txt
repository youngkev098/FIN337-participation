Gap Inc. is the largest specialty retailer in the United States, and is 3rd in total international locations, behind <a href="/wiki/Inditex" title="Inditex">Inditex</a> Group and <a href="/wiki/H%26M" title="H&amp;M">H&amp;M</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> As of September 2008, the company has approximately 135,000 employees and operates 3,727 stores worldwide, of which 2,406 are located in the U.S.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p><p>The Fisher family remains deeply involved in the company, collectively owning much of its stock.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Donald_Fisher" title="Donald Fisher">Donald Fisher</a> served as <a href="/wiki/Chairman_of_the_board" class="mw-redirect" title="Chairman of the board">chairman of the board</a> until 2004, playing a role in the ouster of then-CEO <a href="/wiki/Millard_Drexler" class="mw-redirect" title="Millard Drexler">Millard Drexler</a> in 2002, and remained on the board until his death on September 27, 2009. Fisher's wife and their son, <a href="/wiki/Robert_J._Fisher" title="Robert J. Fisher">Robert J. Fisher</a>, also serve on Gap's board of directors. Robert succeeded his father as chairman in 2004 and also served as <a href="/wiki/Chief_Executive_Officer" class="mw-redirect" title="Chief Executive Officer">CEO</a> on an interim basis following the resignation of <a href="/wiki/Paul_Pressler" title="Paul Pressler">Paul Pressler</a> in 2007, before being succeeded by Glenn K. Murphy up until 2014. From February  2015 to November 2019, <a href="/wiki/Art_Peck" title="Art Peck">Art Peck</a> was CEO of Gap Inc,<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> until he was replaced by Sonia Syngal in March 2020.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>

</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(1)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gap_Inc.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History" data-section="1" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-1 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-1">
<p>In 1969, Don Fisher, a California commercial real estate broker specializing in retail store location, was a social friend of Walter "Wally" Haas Jr, President of <a href="/wiki/Levi_Strauss_%26_Co." title="Levi Strauss &amp; Co.">Levi Strauss &amp; Co.</a>  Fisher was inspired by the sudden success of 'The Tower of Shoes' in an old Quonset Hut in a non-retail industrial area of <a href="/wiki/Sacramento,_California" title="Sacramento, California">Sacramento, California</a>.,<sup id="cite_ref-AMAZON_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AMAZON-11">[11]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-The_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-The-12">[12]</a></sup> that drew crowds by advertising that no matter what brand, style or size of shoes a woman could want it was at The Tower of Shoes. And knowing that even <a href="/wiki/Macy%27s" title="Macy's">Macy's</a>, the biggest Levi's customer, was constantly running out of the best selling Levi's sizes, and colors, Fisher asked Haas to let him copy The Tower of Shoes' business model and apply it to Levi's products. Haas referred Fisher to Bud Robinson, his Director of Advertising, for what Haas assumed would be a quick refusal; but instead Robinson and Fisher carefully worked out a legal test plan for what was to become The Gap (named by Don's wife Doris Fisher).
</p><p>Fisher agreed to stock only Levi's apparel in every style and size, all grouped by size, and Levi's guaranteed The Gap to be never out of stock by overnight replenishment from Levi's <a href="/wiki/San_Jose,_California" title="San Jose, California">San Jose, California</a> warehouse. And finally, Robinson offered to pay 50% of The Gap's radio advertising upfront and avoided antitrust laws by offering the same marketing package to any store that agreed to sell nothing but Levi's products.
</p><p>Fisher opened the first Gap store on Ocean Avenue in <a href="/wiki/San_Francisco" title="San Francisco">San Francisco</a> on August 21, 1969; its only merchandise consisted of Levi's and LP records to attract teen customers.
</p><p>In 1970, Gap opened its second store in San Jose. In 1971, Gap established its corporate headquarters in <a href="/wiki/Burlingame,_California" title="Burlingame, California">Burlingame, California</a> with four employees. By 1973, the company had over 25 locations and had expanded into the East Coast market with a store in the Echelon Mall in <a href="/wiki/Voorhees,_New_Jersey" class="mw-redirect" title="Voorhees, New Jersey">Voorhees, New Jersey</a>. In 1974, Gap began to sell private-label merchandise.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2020)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p><p>In the 1990s, Gap assumed an upscale identity and revamped its inventory under the direction of <a href="/wiki/Millard_Drexler" class="mw-redirect" title="Millard Drexler">Millard Drexler</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> However, Drexler was removed from his position after 19 years of service in 2002 after over-expansion, a 29-month slump in sales, and tensions with the Fisher family. Drexler refused to sign a non-compete agreement and eventually became CEO of <a href="/wiki/J._Crew" class="mw-redirect" title="J. Crew">J. Crew</a>. One month after his departure, merchandise that he had ordered was responsible for a strong rebound in sales.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Robert_J._Fisher" title="Robert J. Fisher">Robert J. Fisher</a> recruited <a href="/wiki/Paul_Pressler" title="Paul Pressler">Paul Pressler</a> as the new CEO; he was credited with closing under-performing locations and paying off debt. However, his focus groups failed to recover the company's leadership in its market.
</p><p>In 2007, Gap announced that it would "focus [its] efforts on recruiting a chief executive officer who has deep retailing and merchandising experience ideally in apparel, understands the creative process and can effectively execute strategies in large, complex environments while maintaining strong financial discipline". That January, Pressler resigned after two disappointing holiday sales seasons and was succeeded by Robert J. Fisher on an interim basis.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> He began working with the company in 1980 and joined the board in 1990, and would later assume several senior executive positions, including president of Banana Republic and the Gap units.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> The board's search committee was led by Adrian Bellamy, chairman of <a href="/wiki/The_Body_Shop" title="The Body Shop">The Body Shop International</a> and included founder Donald Fisher. On February 2, Marka Hansen, the former head of the Banana Republic division, replaced <a href="/wiki/Cynthia_Harriss" title="Cynthia Harriss">Cynthia Harriss</a> as the leader of the Gap division. The executive president for marketing and merchandising Jack Calhoun became interim president of Banana Republic.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> In May, Old Navy laid off approximately 300 managers in lower volume locations to help streamline costs. That July, Glenn Murphy, previously CEO of <a href="/wiki/Shoppers_Drug_Mart" title="Shoppers Drug Mart">Shoppers Drug Mart</a> in Canada, was announced as the new CEO of Gap, Inc. New lead designers were also brought on board to help define a fashionable image, including <a href="/wiki/Patrick_Robinson_(fashion_designer)" title="Patrick Robinson (fashion designer)">Patrick Robinson</a> for Gap Adult, Simon Kneen for Banana Republic, and <a href="/wiki/Todd_Oldham" title="Todd Oldham">Todd Oldham</a> for Old Navy. Robinson was hired as chief designer in 2007, but was dismissed in May 2011 after sales failed to increase. However, he enjoyed commercial success in international markets.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> In 2007, <a href="/wiki/Ethisphere_Magazine" class="mw-redirect" title="Ethisphere Magazine">Ethisphere Magazine</a> chose Gap from among thousands of companies evaluated as one of 100 "World's Most Ethical Companies".<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p><p>In October 2011, Gap Inc. announced plans to close 189 US stores, nearly 21 percent, by the end of 2013; however, it also plans to expand its presence in <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> The company announced it would open its first stores in <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">Brazil</a> in the Fall of 2013.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p><p>In January 2015, Gap Inc announced plans to close their subsidiary <a href="/wiki/Piperlime" title="Piperlime">Piperlime</a> in order to focus on their core brands. The first and only Piperlime store, based in <a href="/wiki/SoHo" class="mw-redirect" title="SoHo">SoHo</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a>, closed in April.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p><p>In September 2018, Gap Inc began publicizing Hill City, a men's athletic apparel brand that launched in October 2018.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(2)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Corporate_identity">Corporate identity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gap_Inc.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Corporate identity" data-section="2" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-2 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-2">
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Logo">Logo</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gap_Inc.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Logo" data-section="3" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>Gap Inc. owns a <a href="/wiki/Trademark" title="Trademark">trademark</a> to its name, "Gap". The Gap's original trademark was a <a href="/wiki/Service_mark" title="Service mark">service mark</a> for <i>retail clothing store services</i>. The application was filed with the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Patent_and_Trademark_Office" title="United States Patent and Trademark Office">United States Patent and Trademark Office</a> on February 29, 1972, by The Gap Stores; registration was granted on October 10, 1972. The first use of the trademark was on August 23, 1969, and expanded to commercial usage on October 17, 1969. A second application was filed by Gap Stores, Inc. on September 12, 1974, this time for a trademark filed for <a href="/wiki/Shirt" title="Shirt">shirts</a>. The first usage for shirts and clothing products was on June 25, 1974. Trademark registration was granted on December 28, 1976. Both the service mark and trademark are registered and owned by Gap (Apparel), LLC of San Francisco, California.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2020)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p><p>On October 4, 2010, in an effort to establish a contemporary presence, Gap introduced a new logo. It was designed with the <a href="/wiki/Helvetica" title="Helvetica">Helvetica</a> font and reduced the prominence of the brand's iconic blue box. After much public outcry, the company reverted to its previous "blue box" logo on October 12, after less than a week in use.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> Marka Hansen, the executive who oversaw the logo change, resigned February 1, 2011.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p>

<h4 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Banana_Republic">Banana Republic</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gap_Inc.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Banana Republic" data-section="5" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h4>
<p><a href="/wiki/Banana_Republic" title="Banana Republic">Banana Republic</a>, a small safari-themed clothing retailer, was purchased by Gap in 1983 and was rebranded as an upscale clothing retailer in the late 1980s.
</p>
<h4 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Old_Navy">Old Navy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gap_Inc.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Old Navy" data-section="6" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h4>
<p><a href="/wiki/Old_Navy" title="Old Navy">Old Navy</a> was launched in 1994 as a value chain with a specialty flair. On February 28, 2019, Gap Inc., announced that Old Navy will <a href="/wiki/Corporate_spin-off" title="Corporate spin-off">spin-off</a> from the company, making Old Navy independent from Gap Inc.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> This was reversed on January 16, 2020, when Gap Inc. announced that the separation had been called off.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<h4 class="in-block"><span id="Forth_.26_Towne"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Forth_&amp;_Towne">Forth &amp; Towne</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gap_Inc.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Forth &amp; Towne" data-section="7" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h4>
<p><a href="/wiki/Forth_%26_Towne" title="Forth &amp; Towne">Forth &amp; Towne</a>, the company's fourth traditional retail concept, was launched on August 24, 2005, featuring apparel targeted toward women 35 years and older.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> On February 26, 2007, after an 18-month trial period, it was discontinued, and the 19 stores were closed.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></p>

<p>The Gap originally targeted the younger generation when it opened, with its name referring to the <a href="/wiki/Generation_gap" title="Generation gap">generation gap</a> of the time.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> It originally sold everything that Levi Strauss &amp; Co made in every style, size, and color, and organized the stock by size. The Gap was the first of many shops that carried only Levi's. In 1973, Gap started making their own jeans as a way to differentiate themselves from department stores.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> Gap's current marketing works to appeal to a broad demographic of customers, whereas <a href="/wiki/Banana_Republic" title="Banana Republic">Banana Republic</a> presents a sophisticated image with a self expressing easygoing personality and <a href="/wiki/Old_Navy" title="Old Navy">Old Navy</a> focuses "fun, fashion, and value" for families and younger customers. While the company has been criticized for blandness and uniformity in its selling environments, it maintains that it tailors its stores "to appeal to unique markets" by developing multiple formats and designs.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> The domain <i>www.gap.com</i> attracts over 18 million visitors annually, according to a 2008 <a href="/wiki/Compete.com" title="Compete.com">Compete.com</a> survey.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup>  The brand is being criticized in the UK because the merchandise that is offered to the UK customers cost double the prices (or even a direct $/£ swap) found in the United States. Gap also does not offer XXL or larger sizes in the UK stating the UK market does not require them in contrast to market leader NEXT who offer a variety of larger sizes in the UK. In 2018, a Gap ad campaign featuring a young girl wearing a <a href="/wiki/Hijab" title="Hijab">hijab</a> has been stirring up controversy in <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup></p>

</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(3)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Labor_controversies">Labor controversies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gap_Inc.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Labor controversies" data-section="17" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-3 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-3">
<p>Reports from news outlets of <a href="/wiki/Sweatshop" title="Sweatshop">sweatshop</a> workers in <a href="/wiki/Saipan" title="Saipan">Saipan</a> not being paid for overtime work, being subjected to forced <a href="/wiki/Abortion" title="Abortion">abortion</a>, and being required to work in unsafe working conditions surfaced in 1999.<sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup> In 2003, a <a href="/wiki/Class_action" title="Class action">class action</a> lawsuit against Gap and 21 other companies was started which ended when a <a href="/wiki/Settlement_(litigation)" title="Settlement (litigation)">settlement</a> of 20 million dollars was reached.<sup id="cite_ref-61" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup></p><p>In May 2006, adult and child employees of Western Factory, a supplier in <a href="/wiki/Irbid" title="Irbid">Irbid</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jordan" title="Jordan">Jordan</a>, were found to have worked up to 109 hours per week and to have gone six months without being paid. Some employees claimed they had been raped by managers.<sup id="cite_ref-NLCNET_62-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NLCNET-62">[62]</a></sup> The government of Jordan launched an investigation into the supplier and other textile factories and announced actions to prevent future abuses.<sup id="cite_ref-63" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Walmart" title="Walmart">Walmart</a>, who also sources from the supplier, confirmed "serious problems with working conditions" at Western Factory and other Jordanian suppliers. As of May 2018<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Gap_Inc.&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, Gap had ceased their relationship with Western Factory.<sup id="cite_ref-64" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup></p><p>On October 28, 2007, <a href="/wiki/BBC" title="BBC">BBC</a> footage showed <a href="/wiki/Child_labor" class="mw-redirect" title="Child labor">child labor</a> in Indian Gap factories.<sup id="cite_ref-65" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup> The company denied knowledge of the happenings; it subsequently removed and destroyed the single piece of clothing in question, a smock blouse, from a British store. Gap promised to investigate breaches in its ethical policy.<sup id="cite_ref-66" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup></p><p>On February 19, 2014, Glenn Murphy, CEO of Gap Inc., announced Gap will raise the minimum wages for its 65,000 U.S. store employees.<sup id="cite_ref-Gap_Readies_Minimum_Wage_Increase_67-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gap_Readies_Minimum_Wage_Increase-67">[67]</a></sup></p>
