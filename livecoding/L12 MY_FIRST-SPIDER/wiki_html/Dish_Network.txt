<p><b>DISH Network Corporation</b> is a <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">U.S.</a> <a href="/wiki/Multichannel_television" title="Multichannel television">television provider</a> based in Englewood,<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Colorado" title="Colorado">Colorado</a> and a Soon to be Prepaid Phone Company (With the Pending Purchase of <a href="/wiki/Boost_Mobile" title="Boost Mobile">Boost Mobile</a>) It is the owner of the <a href="/wiki/Satellite_television" title="Satellite television">direct-broadcast satellite</a> provider <b>DISH</b>, also still commonly known as <b>DISH Network</b>, and the <a href="/wiki/Over-the-top_media_services" class="mw-redirect" title="Over-the-top media services">over-the-top</a> <a href="/wiki/IPTV" class="mw-redirect" title="IPTV">IPTV</a> service <a href="/wiki/Sling_TV" title="Sling TV">Sling TV</a>.  The company has approximately 16,000 employees. Like many other providers, DISH is being affected by the <a href="/wiki/Cord-cutting" title="Cord-cutting">cord-cutting</a> trend where people are shifting towards internet based <a href="/wiki/Streaming_television" title="Streaming television">streaming television</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-auto_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-auto-7">[7]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>  Ending 2018, DISH lost 381,000 subscribers in its Q4 2018.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> DISH had 9.9 million <a href="/wiki/Satellite_television" title="Satellite television">satellite</a> subscribers, down from 11 million year the previous year,<sup id="cite_ref-auto_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-auto-7">[7]</a></sup> and 14 million subscribers in 2014.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>  Its primary competitors are AT&amp;T's satellite service known as <a href="/wiki/DirecTV" title="DirecTV">DirecTV</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cable_television" title="Cable television">cable television</a> providers.  The company revenues for FY 2018 were $13.6 billion.

</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(1)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dish_Network&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History" data-section="1" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-1 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-1">
<p>In January 2008, EchoStar Communications Corporation, which was founded by <a href="/wiki/Charlie_Ergen" title="Charlie Ergen">Charlie Ergen</a> as a satellite television equipment distributor in 1980,<sup id="cite_ref-dishnetworkprofile_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dishnetworkprofile-5">[5]</a></sup>  changed its name to DISH Network Corporation<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Corporate_spin-off" title="Corporate spin-off">spun off</a> its technology arm as a new company, named <a href="/wiki/EchoStar" title="EchoStar">EchoStar Corporation</a>. The company began using DISH Network as its consumer brand in 1996<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> after the successful launch of its first satellite, <a href="/wiki/EchoStar_I" title="EchoStar I">EchoStar I</a>, in December 1995.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> That launch marked the beginning of its <a href="/wiki/Subscription_business_model" title="Subscription business model">subscription</a> television services.
</p><p>Joseph Clayton became president and chief executive officer of the company in June 2011,<sup id="cite_ref-Avery_2011_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Avery_2011-16">[16]</a></sup> while Charlie Ergen remained chairman. Clayton remained in the position until March 31, 2015 when he retired leaving Ergen to resume the post.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> Ergen has said diversifying and updating technology for the company will be a high priority, with an expectation that, over the coming decade, the company will provide internet, video, and telephone service for both home and mobile applications.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> In December 2017, DISH Network announced that Ergen will step down and be replaced by Erik Carlson.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p><p>As of November 2016<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Dish_Network&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, the company provided services to 13.7 million television and 580,000 broadband subscribers.<sup id="cite_ref-2016results_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2016results-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Founding_and_early_growth">Founding and early growth</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dish_Network&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Founding and early growth" data-section="2" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:127px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Dish_Network_Logo_1996.gif" class="image"><noscript><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/9/95/Dish_Network_Logo_1996.gif" decoding="async" width="125" height="108" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="125" data-file-height="108"></noscript><span class="lazy-image-placeholder" style="width: 125px;height: 108px;" data-src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/9/95/Dish_Network_Logo_1996.gif" data-alt="" data-width="125" data-height="108" data-class="thumbimage"> </span></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Dish_Network_Logo_1996.gif" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Dish Network logo from March 4, 1996-April 2000.</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Original_Dish_Network_logo.svg" class="image"><noscript><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/a/a8/Original_Dish_Network_logo.svg/220px-Original_Dish_Network_logo.svg.png" decoding="async" width="220" height="220" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="706" data-file-height="706"></noscript><span class="lazy-image-placeholder" style="width: 220px;height: 220px;" data-src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/a/a8/Original_Dish_Network_logo.svg/220px-Original_Dish_Network_logo.svg.png" data-alt="" data-width="220" data-height="220" data-srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/a/a8/Original_Dish_Network_logo.svg/330px-Original_Dish_Network_logo.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/a/a8/Original_Dish_Network_logo.svg/440px-Original_Dish_Network_logo.svg.png 2x" data-class="thumbimage"> </span></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Original_Dish_Network_logo.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Dish Network logo from April 2000<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup>—2006</div></div></div>

<p>DISH Network officially began operations on March 4, 1996, as a service of EchoStar. EchoStar was formed in 1980 by its <a href="/wiki/Chairman" class="mw-redirect" title="Chairman">chairman</a> and chief executive officer, Charlie Ergen along with colleagues Candy Ergen and Jim DeFranco,<sup id="cite_ref-dishnetworkprofile_5-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dishnetworkprofile-5">[5]</a></sup> as a distributor of <a href="/wiki/C_band_(IEEE)" title="C band (IEEE)">C-band</a> satellite television systems. In 1987, EchoStar applied for a <a href="/wiki/Satellite_television" title="Satellite television">direct-broadcast satellite</a> <a href="/wiki/Broadcast_license" title="Broadcast license">broadcast license</a> with the FCC and was granted access to orbital slot 119° west <a href="/wiki/Longitude" title="Longitude">longitude</a> in 1992.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Dish_Network_logo_2012.svg" class="image"><noscript><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1e/Dish_Network_logo_2012.svg/220px-Dish_Network_logo_2012.svg.png" decoding="async" width="220" height="100" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="95" data-file-height="43"></noscript><span class="lazy-image-placeholder" style="width: 220px;height: 100px;" data-src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1e/Dish_Network_logo_2012.svg/220px-Dish_Network_logo_2012.svg.png" data-alt="" data-width="220" data-height="100" data-srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1e/Dish_Network_logo_2012.svg/330px-Dish_Network_logo_2012.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1e/Dish_Network_logo_2012.svg/440px-Dish_Network_logo_2012.svg.png 2x" data-class="thumbimage"> </span></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Dish_Network_logo_2012.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>DISH Network logo from February 1, 2012-2019</div></div></div>
<p>In 1998 EchoStar purchased the broadcasting assets of a satellite broadcasting joint venture of <a href="/wiki/News_Corporation_(1980%E2%80%932013)" title="News Corporation (1980–2013)">News Corporation</a> and <a href="/wiki/MCI_Communications" title="MCI Communications">MCI</a> Worldcom, called ASkyB (for <i><a href="/wiki/Sky_plc" class="mw-redirect" title="Sky plc">American Sky Broadcasting</a></i>, named after News Corp's <a href="/wiki/Sky_UK" title="Sky UK">BSkyB</a> service in Britain); the two companies had nearly merged (which called for DISH Network being renamed Sky) before it was called off due to Charlie Ergen's clashes with News Corp. executives. With this purchase EchoStar obtained 28 of the 32 transponder licenses in the 110° West orbital slot, more than doubling existing continental United States broadcasting capacity at a value of $682.5 million; some of the other assets were picked up by rival <a href="/wiki/PrimeStar" title="PrimeStar">PrimeStar</a>, which was sold to DirecTV in 1999. The acquisition (which also included an uplink center in <a href="/wiki/Gilbert,_Arizona" title="Gilbert, Arizona">Gilbert, Arizona</a>) inspired the company to introduce a multi satellite system called DISH 500, theoretically capable of receiving more than 500 channels on one DISH. In the same year, EchoStar, partnering with <a href="/wiki/Bell_Canada" title="Bell Canada">Bell Canada</a>, launched <a href="/wiki/Bell_TV" title="Bell TV">DISH Network Canada</a>.
</p><p>On December 7, 2007, EchoStar announced that it would spin off its technology and infrastructure assets into a separate company under the EchoStar name, after which the remainder of the company would be renamed DISH Network Corporation. The spun-out EchoStar began trading on January 3, 2008.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Acquisitions_and_expansion">Acquisitions and expansion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dish_Network&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Acquisitions and expansion" data-section="3" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>In 2011, DISH Network (DISH, an acronym for Digital Sky Highway<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup>) spent over $3 billion in acquisitions of companies in bankruptcy,<sup id="cite_ref-Sherman_&amp;_Grover_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sherman_&amp;_Grover-27">[27]</a></sup> which <a href="/wiki/The_Motley_Fool" title="The Motley Fool">The Motley Fool</a>'s Anders Bylund described as "a veritable buying rampage in the bargain bin."<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> This includes the April 6, 2011, purchase of <a href="/wiki/Blockbuster_LLC" title="Blockbuster LLC">Blockbuster Inc.</a> in a bankruptcy auction in New York, agreeing to pay $322 million in cash and assume $87 million in liabilities and other obligations for the nationwide video-rental company.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> DISH Network also acquired the defunct companies DBSD and <a href="/wiki/TerreStar_Corporation" title="TerreStar Corporation">Terrestar</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Sherman_&amp;_Grover_27-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sherman_&amp;_Grover-27">[27]</a></sup> DISH Network also made a bid to purchase <a href="/wiki/Hulu" title="Hulu">Hulu</a> on October 2011, but Hulu's owners chose not to sell the company.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> There was also speculation that DISH Network might purchase <a href="/wiki/Sprint_Corporation" title="Sprint Corporation">Sprint Nextel</a> or <a href="/wiki/Clearwire" title="Clearwire">Clearwire</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> In 2013, DISH made a bid for both companies. CEO Charles Ergen plans on adding wireless internet and mobile video services<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Chronological_items" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers"><span title="The time period mentioned near this tag is ambiguous. (June 2018)">when?</span></a></i>]</sup> that can compete with <a href="/wiki/Netflix" title="Netflix">Netflix</a> and cable companies.<sup id="cite_ref-Sherman_&amp;_Grover_27-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sherman_&amp;_Grover-27">[27]</a></sup> About the new markets, Ergen said, "Given the assets we've been accumulating, I don't think it's hard to see we're moving in a different direction from simply pay-TV, which is a market that's becoming increasingly saturated."<sup id="cite_ref-Sherman_&amp;_Grover_27-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sherman_&amp;_Grover-27">[27]</a></sup></p><p>DISH Network put its Blockbuster acquisition to work by making available DISH Movie Pack for DISH Network subscribers and <a href="/wiki/Sling_TV" title="Sling TV">Sling TV</a> for non-DISH Network subscribers. Blockbuster also has agreements that allow it to receive movies 28 days before Netflix and <a href="/wiki/Redbox" title="Redbox">Redbox</a> which could encourage customers to use these services.<sup id="cite_ref-Sherman_&amp;_Grover_27-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sherman_&amp;_Grover-27">[27]</a></sup></p><p>DISH Network also plans on offering high-speed internet.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Chronological_items" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers"><span title="The time period mentioned near this tag is ambiguous. (June 2018)">when?</span></a></i>]</sup> The company plans a hybrid satellite/terrestrial mobile broadband service. In 2011, it petitioned the <a href="/wiki/Federal_Communications_Commission" title="Federal Communications Commission">FCC</a> to combine the S-Band spectrum it acquired from DBSD and Terrestar, and combine this spectrum with <a href="/wiki/LTE_(telecommunication)" title="LTE (telecommunication)">LTE</a>. Unlike <a href="/wiki/LightSquared" class="mw-redirect" title="LightSquared">LightSquared</a>, DISH's spectrum has minimal risk of disrupting <a href="/wiki/Global_Positioning_System" title="Global Positioning System">Global Positioning Systems</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p><p>At the 2012 <a href="/wiki/Consumer_Electronics_Show" title="Consumer Electronics Show">Consumer Electronics Show</a>, DISH Network announced a corporate rebranding, under which the company would publicly refer to itself as just "DISH" rather than "DISH Network".<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p><p>After changing the position of a satellite orbital position from being over Mexico to Brazil in 2011, DISH Network sought companies that could make a deal, among them <a href="/wiki/Telef%C3%B3nica" title="Telefónica">Telefónica</a>. However, nothing ever came of this, and DISH decided to enter the country itself. According to the <a href="/wiki/National_Telecommunications_Agency_(Brazil)" title="National Telecommunications Agency (Brazil)">Brazilian Agency of Telecommunications</a> (Anatel), they awaited the authorization of the application.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> In June 2019, nonetheless, DISH TV accepted to resign its satellite exploration rights assigned to EchoStar and thus ending the possibility of entering the Brazilian market.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> On July 26, 2019, DISH announced it had reached an agreement with <a href="/wiki/T-Mobile_US" class="mw-redirect" title="T-Mobile US">T-Mobile US</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sprint_Corporation" title="Sprint Corporation">Sprint Corporation</a> to sell Boost Mobile and Virgin Mobile, Sprint's prepaid businesses, for $1.4 billion to DISH Network. They will also sell DISH $3.6 billion of 800 MHz spectrum, Sprint's entire 800 MHz portfolio. DISH customers will be able to use the New T-Mobile Network for seven years. DISH and T-Mobile are currently negotiating the lease of 20,000 cell sites and hundreds of retail stores being decommissioned by the New T-Mobile.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(2)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Services_and_devices">Services and devices</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dish_Network&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Services and devices" data-section="4" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-2 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-2">
<p>DISH's main service is satellite television. Its offerings are similar to other satellite and cable companies. Viewers can choose from a series of service bundles, paying more money for more channels. A la carte programming is available, however limited other than premium channels. The company is currently working on diversifying its offerings. With its purchase of <a href="/wiki/Blockbuster_LLC" title="Blockbuster LLC">Blockbuster LLC</a>, it now owns the Blockbuster trademarks and has used its intellectual property agreement to offer streaming and mail-order video services.
</p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="DishNET">DishNET</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dish_Network&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: DishNET" data-section="5" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Satellite_Internet_access" title="Satellite Internet access">Satellite Internet access</a></div>
<p>On September 27, 2012, DISH Network announced a satellite broadband service called DishNET, aimed at rural areas.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(3)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Technical_information">Technical information</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dish_Network&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Technical information" data-section="6" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-3 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-3">
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Broadcast_technology">Broadcast technology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dish_Network&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Broadcast technology" data-section="7" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>While for years DISH Network has used standard <a href="/wiki/MPEG-2" title="MPEG-2">MPEG-2</a> for broadcasting, the addition of bandwidth-intensive HDTV in a limited-bandwidth world has called for a change to an <a href="/wiki/H.264/MPEG-4_AVC" class="mw-redirect" title="H.264/MPEG-4 AVC">H.264/MPEG-4 AVC</a> system. Dish Network announced as of February 1, 2006, that all new HDTV channels would be available in H.264 format only, while maintaining the current lineup as MPEG-2. DISH Network intends to eventually convert the entire platform to H.264 in order to provide more channels to subscribers. In 2007, DISH Network reduced the resolution of 1080-line channels from 1920x1080 to 1440x1080. Reducing horizontal resolution and/or data rate of HD video is known as <a href="/wiki/HD_Lite" title="HD Lite">HD Lite</a> and is practiced by other TV providers as well.
</p><p>Both a standard receiver and a receiver with built-in <a href="/wiki/Digital_video_recorder" title="Digital video recorder">digital video recorder</a> (DVR) were available to subscribers.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> The DISH Network ViP722 HD DVR (Record up to 350 hours of standard-definition (SD), up to 55 hours of high-definition (HD)) replacement to the ViP622 has received generally positive reviews<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> from <a href="/wiki/CNET" title="CNET">CNET</a> and others. These Set Top Boxes (STBs) allow for HD on the Primary TV and SD on the secondary TV (TV2) without a secondary box on TV2.
</p>
<h4 class="in-block"><span id="High-definition_television_.28HDTV.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="High-definition_television_(HDTV)">High-definition television (HDTV)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dish_Network&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: High-definition television (HDTV)" data-section="8" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h4>
<p>In January 1999, the company released the industry's first <a href="/wiki/High-definition_television" title="High-definition television">high-definition television</a> (HDTV) <a href="/wiki/Tuner_(television)" class="mw-redirect" title="Tuner (television)">tuner</a>. In August 2003, the company launched EchoStar IX, the first satellite equipped with commercial Ka band payload for broadband service over the United States. This led the company in 2004 to be the first satellite TV service to offer local channels to all 50 states. In that year, the company also introduced the nation's first interactive TV multiple picture-in picture application for the Olympic Games, offering coverage from multiple channels at once. This year the company also acquired its ten millionth customer.
</p><p>In January 2005, EchoStar bought the broadcasting assets of the troubled HDTV satellite provider <a href="/wiki/Voom" class="mw-redirect" title="Voom">Voom</a>, including its Rainbow 1 satellite co-located with EchoStar 3 at 61.5° West. On April 29, EchoStar announced that it would expand its HDTV programming by adding the first 10 of 21 original Voom channels and mirror the channels on a CONUS slot. DISH Network added CNN HD in Spanish along with other packages in its Latino HD lineup.
</p>

<h4 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Satellite_antennas">Satellite antennas</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dish_Network&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Satellite antennas" data-section="10" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h4>
<p>DISH Network offers different types of satellite receiving equipment for obtaining <a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/signal" class="extiw" title="wikt:signal">signals</a> from its diverse satellite fleet. Most of their consumer boxes are manufactured by <a href="/wiki/Sanmina-SCI_Corporation" class="mw-redirect" title="Sanmina-SCI Corporation">Sanmina-SCI Corporation</a> to DISH specifications. Prior to the December 2001 merger of SCI Systems and Sanmina, DISH Network receivers were produced at factories in <a href="/wiki/Huntsville,_Alabama" title="Huntsville, Alabama">Huntsville</a>, Alabama and <a href="/wiki/Fountain,_Colorado" title="Fountain, Colorado">Fountain</a>, Colorado. Currently,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Chronological_items" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers"><span title="The time period mentioned near this tag is ambiguous. (November 2009)">when?</span></a></i>]</sup> receiver assembly takes place in <a href="/wiki/Guadalajara" title="Guadalajara">Guadalajara</a>, Mexico and India.
</p>
<h4 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Earlier_satellite_dishes">Earlier satellite dishes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dish_Network&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Earlier satellite dishes" data-section="11" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h4>
<p>DISH Network's first satellite antenna was simply called the "DISH Network" dish. It was retroactively named the "DISH 300" when legal and satellite problems forced delays of the forthcoming DISH 500 systems. It uses one <a href="/wiki/Low-noise_block_downconverter" title="Low-noise block downconverter">LNB</a> to obtain signals from the 119°W orbital location,<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> and was commonly used as a second dish to receive additional high-definition or international programming from either the 148°W or 61.5°W orbital locations.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> The 119°W slot is one of two primary orbital locations, the other being 110°W, that provide core services.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup></p><p>After EchoStar obtained the broadcasting assets of a failed joint venture between ASkyB and <a href="/wiki/MCI_Inc." title="MCI Inc.">MCI WorldCom</a>, it had more than doubled its capacity by adding 28 transponders at the 110°W orbital location. Since EchoStar also owned the adjacent 119°W orbital location it developed the DISH 500 to receive the signals of both orbital locations using one dish and an innovative dual-<a href="/wiki/Low-noise_block_downconverter" title="Low-noise block downconverter">LNB</a> assembly. Although the new 20-inch DISH 500 was slightly larger than the then-current 18-inch DISH 300 and DirecTV dishes it had the distinct advantage of obtaining signals from EchoStar's two adjacent satellite locations for a theoretical 500-channel capacity. The DISH 500, as a result, provided very large capacity for local-into-local service, nationwide programming, and business services. In order to migrate existing customers to DISH 500, DISH Network provides value-added channels in addition to local channels that can only be received with the DISH 500 and newer systems. Some of the channels exclusive to these newer systems are <a href="/wiki/H2_(TV_channel)" class="mw-redirect" title="H2 (TV channel)">H<sub>2</sub></a>, <a href="/wiki/Boomerang_(TV_network)" title="Boomerang (TV network)">Boomerang</a>, <a href="/wiki/The_Science_Channel" class="mw-redirect" title="The Science Channel">Science</a>, <a href="/wiki/Destination_America" title="Destination America">Planet Green</a>, <a href="/wiki/Universal_Kids" title="Universal Kids">PBS Kids Sprout</a> and <a href="/wiki/Comedy_Central" title="Comedy Central">Comedy Central</a>.
</p>
<h4 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Tailgater">Tailgater</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dish_Network&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Tailgater" data-section="12" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h4>
<p>Tailgater is a portable satellite antenna; the tailgater can be purchased as a standalone device for $350. The Tailgater is now being supported by a Wally receiver, to replace the still supported 211k model. Customers only need pay for the period of time where the receiver is active on the account, monthly cost for a Vip211k or Wally is $7 per month, if the receiver is the only one on the account, there is no charge.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> It weighs ten pounds, is protected from weather, and automatically searches for a signal. The only satellites that are currently compatible with the Tailgater are at DISH's 119 (SD/HD TV), 110 (SD/HD TV), and 129 (SD/HD TV) orbital slots.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup></p>

<h4 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Hopper_and_Joey">Hopper and Joey</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dish_Network&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Hopper and Joey" data-section="14" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h4>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Hopper_(DVR)" title="Hopper (DVR)">Hopper (DVR)</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:DishHD2012.jpg" class="image"><noscript><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a9/DishHD2012.jpg/220px-DishHD2012.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="251" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="1301" data-file-height="1485"></noscript><span class="lazy-image-placeholder" style="width: 220px;height: 251px;" data-src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a9/DishHD2012.jpg/220px-DishHD2012.jpg" data-alt="" data-width="220" data-height="251" data-class="thumbimage"> </span></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:DishHD2012.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>DISH HD, newest version used with the Hopper and Joey system</div></div></div>
<p>Hopper is a line of multi-tuner set-top boxes first introduced in 2012; they are digital video recorders that can be networked with accompanying "Joey" set-top boxes for multi-room access to recordings. DISH Network subsequently introduced updated versions of the Hopper, including Hopper with Sling (which adds integrated <a href="/wiki/Placeshifting" class="mw-redirect" title="Placeshifting">placeshifting</a> capabilities), and the Hopper 3, which features <a href="/wiki/4K_resolution" title="4K resolution">4K</a> support and 16 tuners. Hopper supports a voice-activated remote,<sup id="cite_ref-pcmag-hopperreview_47-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pcmag-hopperreview-47">[47]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-engadget-review_48-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-engadget-review-48">[48]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-pcmag-hopperslingreview_49-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pcmag-hopperslingreview-49">[49]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-engadget-hopper4k_50-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-engadget-hopper4k-50">[50]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-mcn-4kjoey_51-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mcn-4kjoey-51">[51]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-verge-hopper3_52-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-verge-hopper3-52">[52]</a></sup> as well as <a href="/wiki/Amazon_Echo" title="Amazon Echo">Amazon Echo</a> and <a href="/wiki/Google_Home" title="Google Home">Google Home</a> integration.<sup id="cite_ref-engadget-alexahopper_53-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-engadget-alexahopper-53">[53]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Apps">Apps</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dish_Network&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Apps" data-section="15" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>DISH Anywhere is DISH's subscriber-only streaming video service, which includes <a href="/wiki/HBO" title="HBO">HBO</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cinemax" title="Cinemax">Cinemax</a> programming. As of late 2018, HBO and Cinemax are no longer available for DISH customers due to Contract disputes.<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup></p>
<h4 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Sling_TV">Sling TV</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dish_Network&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Sling TV" data-section="16" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h4>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Sling_TV" title="Sling TV">Sling TV</a></div>
<p>In May 2012, DISH launched DISHWorld, a subscription-based <a href="/wiki/Over-the-top_content" class="mw-redirect" title="Over-the-top content">over-the-top</a> <a href="/wiki/Internet_television" class="mw-redirect" title="Internet television">streaming</a> <a href="/wiki/IPTV" class="mw-redirect" title="IPTV">IPTV</a> service, as an app on <a href="/wiki/Roku" title="Roku">Roku</a> devices, offering access to over 50 international television channels via <a href="/wiki/Broadband" title="Broadband">broadband</a> streaming.<sup id="cite_ref-bandc-dishworld_56-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bandc-dishworld-56">[56]</a></sup></p><p>In 2014, DISH Network began to reach carriage deals with broadcasters for a new over-the-top service that would be aimed towards <a href="/wiki/Cord_cutter" class="mw-redirect" title="Cord cutter">cord cutters</a> as a low-cost alternative to traditional pay television.<sup id="cite_ref-variety-scrippsott_57-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-variety-scrippsott-57">[57]</a></sup> On January 5, 2015, DISH Network officially unveiled <a href="/wiki/Sling_TV" title="Sling TV">Sling TV</a>, an over-the-top IPTV service designed to complement subscription video on-demand services such as Hulu and Netflix.<sup id="cite_ref-variety-slingunveil_58-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-variety-slingunveil-58">[58]</a></sup></p><p>Some broadcasters have been hesitant about over-the-top services such as Sling TV, showing concern that they may undermine their carriage deals with larger conventional <a href="/wiki/Cable_television" title="Cable television">cable</a>, <a href="/wiki/Direct_broadcast_satellite" class="mw-redirect" title="Direct broadcast satellite">satellite</a> and Internet TV providers. Time Warner initially noted that the carriage of its channels on the service was only for a "trial" basis, while both Time Warner's CEO <a href="/wiki/Jeffrey_Bewkes" class="mw-redirect" title="Jeffrey Bewkes">Jeffrey Bewkes</a> and an analyst from the firm <a href="/wiki/Macquarie_Group" title="Macquarie Group">Macquarie Capital</a> disclosed that current contract language in DISH's OTT carriage deals with the service's content distributors would cap the number of subscribers that the service is allowed to have at any given time to 5 million. Neither DISH Network or its content providers have confirmed any such cap.<sup id="cite_ref-SlingCap_59-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SlingCap-59">[59]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-mcn-unveil_60-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mcn-unveil-60">[60]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-thestreet-toopopular_61-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-thestreet-toopopular-61">[61]</a></sup> As of May 2019<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Dish_Network&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, the service has 2.4 million subscribers.<sup id="cite_ref-62" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup></p>

<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Criticism_of_Dish_Network" title="Criticism of Dish Network">Criticism of Dish Network</a></div>
<p>Since the early 2000s, DISH Network has received criticism regarding controversial technology and carriage disputes with programming providers. Most notably, when the Hopper digital video recorder provided an easy way for viewers to watch certain programming without commercials, major television networks sued DISH Network.<sup id="cite_ref-NYTimes_65-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NYTimes-65">[65]</a></sup> Dish has also been involved in <a href="/wiki/Carriage_dispute" title="Carriage dispute">carriage disputes</a> with various providers.
</p><p>DISH has been sued and countersued dozens of times. DISH argues that effective litigation is important to corporate operations. <a href="/wiki/Charlie_Ergen" title="Charlie Ergen">Charlie Ergen</a> said, "I may be the only CEO who likes to go to depositions. You can live in a bubble, and you're probably not going to get a disease. But you can play in the mud and the dirt, and you're probably not going to get a disease either, because you get immune to it. You pick your poison, and I think we choose to go play in the mud." In 2001, DISH disclosed using over 100 law firms over a ten-year period.<sup id="cite_ref-Bloomie_66-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bloomie-66">[66]</a></sup></p><p>DISH Network independent dealers have repeatedly been charged and fined for employing illegal telemarketing tactics, such as violating do not call lists and making calls in which a live telemarketer does not connect promptly after the call is answered. DISH Network terminated agreements with some independent dealers in relation to these charges.<sup id="cite_ref-67" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-68" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-69" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup></p><p>In January 2004, thirteen states charged that EchoStar, then the parent company of DISH Network, had not disclosed termination fees to potential customers and had debited customers' bank accounts for hidden fees. The company settled the lawsuit, paid $5 million, and promised to disclose all terms prior to signing contracts with potential customers.<sup id="cite_ref-wisc_70-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wisc-70">[70]</a></sup></p>
