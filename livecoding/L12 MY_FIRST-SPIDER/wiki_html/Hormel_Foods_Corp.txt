<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">For other uses, see <a href="/wiki/Hormel_(disambiguation)" class="mw-disambig" title="Hormel (disambiguation)">Hormel (disambiguation)</a>.</div>
<p><b>Hormel Foods Corporation</b> is an American company founded in 1891 in <a href="/wiki/Austin,_Minnesota" title="Austin, Minnesota">Austin, Minnesota</a>, by <a href="/wiki/George_A._Hormel" title="George A. Hormel">George A. Hormel</a> as George A. Hormel &amp; Company. Originally focusing on the packaging and selling of ham, <a href="/wiki/Spam_(food)" title="Spam (food)">Spam</a>, sausage and other pork, chicken, beef and lamb products to consumers; by the 1980s, Hormel began offering a wider range of <a href="/wiki/Packaged_food" class="mw-redirect" title="Packaged food">packaged</a> and refrigerated foods. The company changed its name to <i>Hormel Foods</i> in 1993. <b>Hormel</b> serves 80 countries with brands such as Applegate, <a href="/wiki/Columbus_Salame" title="Columbus Salame">Columbus Craft Meats</a>, Dinty Moore, <a href="/wiki/Jennie-O" title="Jennie-O">Jennie-O</a> and <a href="/wiki/Skippy_(peanut_butter)" title="Skippy (peanut butter)">Skippy</a>.

</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(1)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hormel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History" data-section="1" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-1 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-1">
<h3 class="in-block"><span id="1890.E2.80.931920"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="1890–1920">1890–1920</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hormel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: 1890–1920" data-section="2" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>The company was founded as George A. Hormel &amp; Company in Austin, Minnesota by George A. Hormel in 1891. It changed its name to Hormel Foods in 1993.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:212px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Old_Hormel_Building.jpg" class="image"><noscript><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b1/Old_Hormel_Building.jpg/210px-Old_Hormel_Building.jpg" decoding="async" width="210" height="140" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="1532" data-file-height="1024"></noscript><span class="lazy-image-placeholder" style="width: 210px;height: 140px;" data-src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b1/Old_Hormel_Building.jpg/210px-Old_Hormel_Building.jpg" data-alt="" data-width="210" data-height="140" data-class="thumbimage"> </span></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Old_Hormel_Building.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The building in which George A. Hormel started his business. Preserved at the <a href="/wiki/Mower_County,_Minnesota" title="Mower County, Minnesota">Mower County</a> Fairgrounds in Austin</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:HormelObelisk.jpg" class="image"><noscript><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0f/HormelObelisk.jpg/220px-HormelObelisk.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="146" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="221" data-file-height="147"></noscript><span class="lazy-image-placeholder" style="width: 220px;height: 146px;" data-src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0f/HormelObelisk.jpg/220px-HormelObelisk.jpg" data-alt="" data-width="220" data-height="146" data-class="thumbimage"> </span></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:HormelObelisk.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The Hormel <a href="/wiki/Obelisk" title="Obelisk">obelisk</a> in <a href="/wiki/Austin,_Minnesota" title="Austin, Minnesota">Austin's</a> <a href="/wiki/Lansing_Township,_Minnesota#Cemeteries" class="mw-redirect" title="Lansing Township, Minnesota">Oakwood Cemetery</a></div></div></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/George_A._Hormel" title="George A. Hormel">George A. Hormel</a> (born 1860 in <a href="/wiki/Buffalo,_New_York" title="Buffalo, New York">Buffalo, New York</a>) worked in a <a href="/wiki/Chicago" title="Chicago">Chicago</a> <a href="/wiki/Slaughterhouse" title="Slaughterhouse">slaughterhouse</a> before becoming a traveling wool and <a href="/wiki/Rawhide_(textile)" class="mw-redirect" title="Rawhide (textile)">hide</a> buyer. His travels took him to Austin and he decided to settle there. He borrowed $500 and opened a meat business.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Hormel handled the production side of the business and his partner, Albert Friedrich, handled the retail. Their partnership dissolved in 1891 as Hormel started his own meat packing operation in northeast Austin in a creamery building on the Cedar River.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Garrison1998_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Garrison1998-8">[8]</a></sup></p><p>To make ends meet in those early days, Hormel continued to trade in hides, eggs, wool, and poultry. The name <span class="plainlinks"><a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://tarr.uspto.gov/servlet/tarr?regser=serial&amp;entry=71203817">Dairy Brand</a></span> was first used in 1903.<sup id="cite_ref-dough_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dough-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>68</span></sup> In the first decade of the 20th century distribution centers were opened in <a href="/wiki/St._Paul,_Minnesota" class="mw-redirect" title="St. Paul, Minnesota">St. Paul</a>, <a href="/wiki/Minneapolis,_Minnesota" class="mw-redirect" title="Minneapolis, Minnesota">Minneapolis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Duluth,_Minnesota" title="Duluth, Minnesota">Duluth</a>, <a href="/wiki/San_Antonio,_Texas" class="mw-redirect" title="San Antonio, Texas">San Antonio</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dallas,_Texas" class="mw-redirect" title="Dallas, Texas">Dallas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chicago,_Illinois" class="mw-redirect" title="Chicago, Illinois">Chicago</a>, <a href="/wiki/Atlanta,_Georgia" class="mw-redirect" title="Atlanta, Georgia">Atlanta</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Birmingham,_Alabama" title="Birmingham, Alabama">Birmingham</a>.
</p><p>In 1915, Hormel began selling dry sausages under the names of Cedar Cervelat, Holsteiner and Noxall Salami.<sup id="cite_ref-dough_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dough-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>79</span></sup> Hormel products began appearing in national magazines such as <i>Good Housekeeping</i> as early as 1916.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span id="1920.E2.80.931950"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="1920–1950">1920–1950</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hormel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: 1920–1950" data-section="3" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:HormelPlantAustinMNpostcard.jpg" class="image"><noscript><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/96/HormelPlantAustinMNpostcard.jpg/220px-HormelPlantAustinMNpostcard.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="123" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="1467" data-file-height="820"></noscript><span class="lazy-image-placeholder" style="width: 220px;height: 123px;" data-src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/96/HormelPlantAustinMNpostcard.jpg/220px-HormelPlantAustinMNpostcard.jpg" data-alt="" data-width="220" data-height="123" data-class="thumbimage"> </span></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:HormelPlantAustinMNpostcard.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The Hormel plant circa 1920</div></div></div>
<p>In 1921, when George's son Jay Hormel returned from service in <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">the First World War</a>, he uncovered that assistant controller <a href="/wiki/Cy_Thomson" title="Cy Thomson">Cy Thomson</a> had embezzled $1,187,000 from the company over the previous ten years.<sup id="cite_ref-levin_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-levin-11">[11]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Embezzlement" title="Embezzlement">embezzlement</a> scandal provided George Hormel with additional incentive to professionalize his company. He did so by arranging for more reliable capital management, by dismissing unproductive employees, and by continuing to develop new products,<sup id="cite_ref-lund_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-lund-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>90–103</span></sup> reportedly with the mantra “Originate, don't imitate."<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> In 1926, the company introduced Hormel Flavor-Sealed Ham, America's first <a href="/wiki/Canning" title="Canning">canned</a> ham,<sup id="cite_ref-BusinessOfFood_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BusinessOfFood-14">[14]</a></sup> and added a canned chicken product line in 1928.<sup id="cite_ref-levin_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-levin-11">[11]</a></sup> Throughout the 1930s, Hormel ads were featured on the radio program <i>The George Burns and Gracie Allen Show</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-AlmironCole2015_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AlmironCole2015-15">[15]</a></sup></p><p>Hormel <a href="/wiki/Chili_con_carne" title="Chili con carne">Chili</a> and <a href="/wiki/Spam_(food)" title="Spam (food)">Spam</a> were introduced in 1936 and 1937 respectively.<sup id="cite_ref-pop_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pop-16">[16]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-levin_11-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-levin-11">[11]</a></sup> In 1938, Jay C. Hormel introduced the "Joint Savings Plan" which allowed employees to share in the profits of the company.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> 
</p><p>In 1933, workers, led by itinerant butcher Frank Ellis, formed the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Independent_Union_of_All_Workers&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Independent Union of All Workers (page does not exist)">Independent Union of All Workers</a> and conducted one of the nation's first successful <a href="/wiki/Sitdown_strike" title="Sitdown strike">sit-down strikes</a>; the union would later join the <a href="/wiki/Congress_of_Industrial_Organizations" title="Congress of Industrial Organizations">Congress of Industrial Organizations</a> (CIO, later AFL-CIO).<sup id="cite_ref-MNSM_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MNSM-18">[18]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Cornell85_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cornell85-19">[19]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-MPR85_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MPR85-20">[20]</a></sup></p><p>By 1942, George and Jay established <a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Hormel_Foundation&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="The Hormel Foundation (page does not exist)">The Hormel Foundation</a> to act as trustees of the family trusts.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> The Foundation funded the <a href="/wiki/Hormel_Institute" title="Hormel Institute">Hormel Institute</a> at the University of Minnesota, initially started with a study of the food value of soybeans.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> The Institute's scope later grew towards studying nutrition, animal diseases and food technology. Hormel's production increased to aid in <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a> and 65% of its products were purchased by the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Federal_Government" class="mw-redirect" title="United States Federal Government">U.S. government</a> by 1945.<sup id="cite_ref-dough_9-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dough-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>77–78</span></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span id="1950.E2.80.932000"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="1950–2000">1950–2000</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hormel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: 1950–2000" data-section="4" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>In 1959, Hormel was the first meatpacker to receive the Seal of Approval of the <a href="/wiki/Humane_Society" class="mw-redirect" title="Humane Society">American Humane Society</a> for its practice of <a href="/wiki/Anesthesia" title="Anesthesia">anesthetizing</a> animals before slaughter.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-dough_9-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dough-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>270</span></sup></p>

<p>Little Sizzlers sausages were introduced in 1961 and Cure 81 hams were introduced in 1963. Unfortunately Little Sizzler's sausage has been discontinued per 10/10/19 email from Hormel's Customer Service. <sup id="cite_ref-pop_16-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pop-16">[16]</a></sup></p><p>Not-So-Sloppy-Joe Sloppy Joe sauce made its debut in 1985.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> In 1986, Hormel Foods acquired <a href="/wiki/Jennie-O_Foods" class="mw-redirect" title="Jennie-O Foods">Jennie-O Foods</a><sup id="cite_ref-BusinessOfFood_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BusinessOfFood-14">[14]</a></sup> and also began an exclusive licensing arrangement to produce <a href="/wiki/Chi-Chi%27s" title="Chi-Chi's">Chi-Chi's</a> brand products.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> The following year, Hormel Foods introduced the Top Shelf line of microwavable non-frozen products. The company added to their poultry offerings by purchasing Chicken by George, created by former <a href="/wiki/Miss_America" title="Miss America">Miss America</a> <a href="/wiki/Phyllis_George" title="Phyllis George">Phyllis George</a>, in 1988.<sup id="cite_ref-prospers_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-prospers-26">[26]</a></sup> That same year, Hormel Foods also introduced microwave bacon.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> In 1984, Hormel introduced the <a href="/wiki/Frank_%27n_Stuff" title="Frank 'n Stuff">Frank 'n Stuff</a> brand of stuffed <a href="/wiki/Hot_dog" title="Hot dog">hot dogs</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-lund_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-lund-12">[12]</a></sup></p><p>In August 1985, Hormel workers went on strike at the Hormel headquarters in <a href="/wiki/Austin,_Minnesota" title="Austin, Minnesota">Austin, Minnesota</a>. In the early 1980s, recession impacted several meatpacking companies, decreasing demand and increasing competition which led smaller and less-efficient companies to go out of business. In an effort to keep plants from closing, many instituted wage cuts. Wilson Food Company declared bankruptcy in 1983, allowing them to cut wages from $10.69 to $6.50 and significantly reduce benefits. Hormel Foods had avoided such drastic action, but by 1985, pressure to stay competitive remained.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p><p>Workers had already labored under a wage freeze and dangerous working conditions, leading to many cases of <a href="/wiki/Repetitive_strain_injury" title="Repetitive strain injury">repetitive strain injury</a>. When management demanded a 23% wage cut from the workers they decided to begin the strike.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> It became one of the longest strikes of the 1980s. 
</p><p>The local chapter of the <a href="/wiki/United_Food_and_Commercial_Workers_Union" class="mw-redirect" title="United Food and Commercial Workers Union">United Food and Commercial Workers Union</a>, Local P-9, led the strike, but was not supported by their parent union. A commemorative mural painted by <a href="/wiki/Mike_Alewitz" title="Mike Alewitz">Mike Alewitz</a> and P-9 workers during the strike was destroyed by the UFCW months after the strike ended.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> The strike gained national attention and led to a widely publicized boycott of Hormel products. The strike ended in June 1986, after lasting 10 months.
</p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span id="2000.E2.80.932010"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="2000–2010">2000–2010</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hormel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: 2000–2010" data-section="5" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Spam_Museum" title="Spam Museum">Spam Museum</a> in Austin, Minnesota, was opened in 2001.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> That same year, Hormel Foods acquired The Turkey Store, the business was combined with Jennie-O Foods to form Jennie-O Turkey Store.<sup id="cite_ref-jenreut_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-jenreut-32">[32]</a></sup></p><p>According to Triple Pundit, Hormel Foods began CSR reporting in 2006.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> The company has been included in <i>Corporate Responsibility</i> magazine's list of the "100 best corporate citizens" for 10 consecutive years.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></p><p>In 2008 an article in the <i>New York Times</i>, "SPAM Turns Serious and Hormel Turns Out More", detailed an overwhelming spike in the demand for SPAM, perhaps due to the flagging economy.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> In 2009 Hormel and Herdez del Fuerte created the joint venture MegaMex Foods to market and distribute Mexican food in the United States.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> Brands included in the venture include Herdez, La Victoria, <a href="/wiki/Chi_Chi%27s" class="mw-redirect" title="Chi Chi's">Chi Chi's</a>, <a href="/wiki/El_Torito" title="El Torito">El Torito</a>, Embasa, Wholly Guacamole, Del Fuerte, Dona Maria, Bufalo, and Don Miguel.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></p><p>In September 2008, animal rights organization <a href="/wiki/PETA" class="mw-redirect" title="PETA">PETA</a> released a video recorded over the course of three months showing workers at a pig factory farm in Iowa abusing pigs.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> The factory farm was owned by Natural Pork Production II LLP of Iowa until August 18, 2008, at which point ownership had transferred to MowMar LLP.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> Hormel spokeswoman Julie Henderson Craven, who responded to the PETA video, called the videotaped abuses "completely unacceptable."<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> In their 2007 Corporate Responsibility Report, Hormel Foods stated that all suppliers are expected to comply with several welfare programs to ensure that the hogs purchased are treated humanely. Because of the investigation, several employees of the farm were fired and six individuals faced charges due to the abuse.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span id="2010.E2.80.93present"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="2010–present">2010–present</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hormel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: 2010–present" data-section="6" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>In 2011, Hormel Foods announced a two-for-one stock split.<sup id="cite_ref-secapril_42-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-secapril-42">[42]</a></sup> In 2013, Hormel Foods purchased <a href="/wiki/Skippy_(peanut_butter)" title="Skippy (peanut butter)">Skippy</a>—the best-selling brand of <a href="/wiki/Peanut_butter" title="Peanut butter">peanut butter</a> in China and the second-best-selling brand in the world—from <a href="/wiki/Unilever" title="Unilever">Unilever</a> for $700 million; the sale included Skippy's American and Chinese factories.<sup id="cite_ref-cnnmoney_43-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cnnmoney-43">[43]</a></sup></p><p>In May 2015, Hormel revealed it would acquire meat processing firm Applegate Farms for around $775 million, expanding its range of meat products.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup></p><p>In 2015, the Hormel Health Labs division of Hormel Foods launched its Hormel Vital Cuisine line of packaged ready to eat meals, nutrition shakes and whey protein powders geared towards cancer patients and made available for home delivery. The line was developed in concert with three parties, as "Hormel brought food formulation, packaging and shelf stability knowledge, (<a href="/wiki/Chef_de_cuisine" title="Chef de cuisine">chef de cuisine</a>) Ron DeSantis brought taste and texture expertise, and the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Cancer_Nutrition_Consortium&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Cancer Nutrition Consortium (page does not exist)">Cancer Nutrition Consortium</a> offered the nutritional framework."<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup></p><p>In 2015, SPAMMY became available for purchase under Title I for U.S. Department of Agriculture (USDA) feeding programs and Title II for U.S. Agency for International Development (USAID) programs under the name fortified poultry-based spread (FPBS).<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> Four years earlier, the company had made an initial three-year commitment to deliver 1 million cans of this product to in-need families in Guatemala.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup></p><p>Also in 2015, after an undercover investigation by a group known as <a href="/wiki/Compassion_Over_Killing" class="mw-redirect" title="Compassion Over Killing">Compassion Over Killing</a> at an Austin, Minnesota processing plant, Hormel Foods announced it was "bringing humane handling officers to a Quality Pork Processors Incorporated facility to ensure compliance with its own animal welfare standards."<sup id="cite_ref-reut_48-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-reut-48">[48]</a></sup> It has also told QPP to provide extra training, enhance compliance oversight and increase third-party auditing.<sup id="cite_ref-reut_48-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-reut-48">[48]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> According to Reuters, "in one scene of the video, pigs covered in feces or pus-filled abscesses are sent down the plant’s conveyor belt. At one point, a knife is used to cut open abscesses on dead pigs."<sup id="cite_ref-reut_48-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-reut-48">[48]</a></sup></p><p>In 2016, Peak Rock Capital purchased the Diamond Crystal Brands Inc. unit, purchased by Hormel in 2002 for $155 million from <a href="/wiki/Imperial_Sugar" title="Imperial Sugar">Imperial Sugar</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup> Also in 2016, Hormel acquired the nut butter producer Justin’s for 280 million.<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup></p><p>In 2016, 2017, and 2018, the company was named as one of the <a href="/wiki/Human_Rights_Campaign" title="Human Rights Campaign">Human Rights Campaign</a>'s Best Places to Work for LGBT equality.<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup> In 2019, Hormel was again named to one of the best places to work for LGBT equality, as the company obtained a 95 out of 100 score in the Human Rights Campaign's <i>Corporate Equality Index</i>. The almost-perfect score was due to the area of "equivalency in same and different-sex domestic partner medical and soft benefits", where Hormel received half-credit for providing parity in some, but not all, benefits.<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup></p><p>In 2017, Hormel sold Clougherty Packing, owner of the Farmer John and Saag's brands, to <a href="/wiki/Smithfield_Foods" title="Smithfield Foods">Smithfield Foods</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup></p><p>In October 2017, Hormel announced it would acquire deli meat company Columbus Manufacturing for $850 million.<sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup></p><p>In 2017, <a href="/wiki/Mercy_For_Animals" class="mw-redirect" title="Mercy For Animals">Mercy For Animals</a> released undercover video footage of pigs being abused at The Maschhoffs LLC., a Hormel pork supplier in <a href="/wiki/Hinton,_Oklahoma" title="Hinton, Oklahoma">Hinton, Oklahoma</a>, with piglets being castrated and having their tails cut off without any anesthetic, piglets left to suffer from untreated illness or injuries, and mother pigs crammed into gestation crates unable to move.<sup id="cite_ref-leigh_57-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-leigh-57">[57]</a></sup> In response, Hormel temporarily suspended its buying from the supplier.<sup id="cite_ref-leigh_57-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-leigh-57">[57]</a></sup></p><p>According to the Military Times, the company has been listed in the top 100 Best for Vets Employers category consistently since 2013.<sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup></p><p>In April 2019, together with Harvard University Dining Services, the company hosted the Small Change Big Impact Food Summit at Harvard University.<sup id="cite_ref-59" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup></p><p>In September 2019, Hormel Foods announced that they had achieved their non-renewable energy use reduction goal a year ahead of schedule.<sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup></p><p>Also in September 2019, the company launched a vegetarian meat alternative called Happy Little Plants for foodservice and retail customers.<sup id="cite_ref-61" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup></p>
