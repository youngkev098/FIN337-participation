			<div id="bodyContent" class="content"><div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><script>function mfTempOpenSection(id){var block=document.getElementById("mf-section-"+id);block.className+=" open-block";block.previousSibling.className+=" open-block";}</script><div class="mw-parser-output"><section class="mf-section-0" id="mf-section-0"><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">For people named Charles Schwab, including the founder of this company, see <a href="/wiki/Charles_Schwab_(disambiguation)" class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" title="Charles Schwab (disambiguation)">Charles Schwab (disambiguation)</a>.</div>
<p>The <b>Charles Schwab Corporation</b> is an American multinational <a href="/wiki/Financial_institution" title="Financial institution">financial services company</a> founded and based in <a href="/wiki/San_Francisco" title="San Francisco">San Francisco, California</a>. Headquartered in <a href="/wiki/Financial_District,_San_Francisco" title="Financial District, San Francisco">Financial District, San Francisco</a>, Charles Schwab is the <a href="/wiki/List_of_largest_banks_in_the_United_States" title="List of largest banks in the United States">14th largest banking institution</a> in the United States with over <a href="/wiki/United_States_dollar" title="United States dollar">US$</a>3.3 trillion in client assets (as of 2019). It is the <a href="/wiki/List_of_asset_management_firms" title="List of asset management firms">third largest asset manager</a> in the country, behind <a href="/wiki/BlackRock" title="BlackRock">BlackRock</a> and <a href="/wiki/The_Vanguard_Group" title="The Vanguard Group">Vanguard</a>. The firm is known for its <a href="/wiki/Electronic_trading_platform" title="Electronic trading platform">electronic trading platforms</a>, investor education, and <a href="/wiki/Broker" title="Broker">discount brokerage</a> services, particularly reduced and free <a href="/wiki/Stock_trader" title="Stock trader">trading commission</a> charges.

class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></td></tr></tbody></table><p>Founded as <b>Charles Schwab &amp; Co.</b> in 1971 by its namesake <a href="/wiki/Charles_R._Schwab" title="Charles R. Schwab">Charles Schwab</a>, the broker-dealer capitalized on the financial <a href="/wiki/Deregulation#United_States" title="Deregulation">deregulation of the 1970s</a> to pioneer <a href="/wiki/Discounts_and_allowances" title="Discounts and allowances">discount sales</a> of equity securities. After a flagship opening in <a href="/wiki/Sacramento,_California" title="Sacramento, California">Sacramento</a>, the bank expanded into <a href="/wiki/Seattle" title="Seattle">Seattle</a> before the <a href="/wiki/Economy_of_the_United_States#History" title="Economy of the United States">1980s economic expansion</a> financed the bank's investments in technology, automation, and digital record keeping. The first to offer round-clock order entry and quotation, it was purchased by <a href="/wiki/Bank_of_America" title="Bank of America">Bank of America</a> in 1983 for $55 million. Three years later, the profitability of the bank's no-charge <a href="/wiki/Mutual_fund" title="Mutual fund">mutual funds</a> prompted the founder to buy his company back for $280 million. In line with the <a href="/wiki/Digital_Revolution" title="Digital Revolution">Digital Revolution</a>, Charles Schwab offered online trading in 1994 which allowed them to drop and even eliminate account fees by 2005, and introduce commission-free funds by 2011.
</p><p>Charles Schwab offers commercial banking, stock brokerage, and <a href="/wiki/Wealth_management" title="Wealth management">wealth management advisory</a> services to both retail and institutional clients. Despite their online presence, the company also has a large network of physical branches mostly in metropolitan <a href="/wiki/Financial_centre" title="Financial centre">financial centers</a>. Their total national footprint includes 345 branches, primarily in the United States and Britain. It is known for its corporate marketing programs, especially their <a href="/wiki/Electric_blue_(color)" title="Electric blue (color)">electric blue</a> nameplate, television commercials, and slogans. In 2020, the bank announced their intention to move to <a href="/wiki/Texas" title="Texas">Texas</a> in conjunction with their large-scale mergers with and acquisitions of <a href="/wiki/USAA" title="USAA">USAA</a> (for $1.8 billion) and <a href="/wiki/TD_Ameritrade" title="TD Ameritrade">TD Ameritrade</a> (for $26 billion), respectively. 
</p>

</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(1)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Charles_Schwab_Corporation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History" data-section="1" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-1 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-1">
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Charles_Schwab_on_Nassau_Street.jpg" class="image"><noscript><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/72/Charles_Schwab_on_Nassau_Street.jpg/220px-Charles_Schwab_on_Nassau_Street.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="3264" data-file-height="2448"></noscript><span class="lazy-image-placeholder" style="width: 220px;height: 165px;" data-src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/72/Charles_Schwab_on_Nassau_Street.jpg/220px-Charles_Schwab_on_Nassau_Street.jpg" data-alt="" data-width="220" data-height="165" data-class="thumbimage"> </span></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Charles_Schwab_on_Nassau_Street.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A Charles Schwab location in <a href="/wiki/Princeton,_New_Jersey" title="Princeton, New Jersey">Princeton, New Jersey</a>.</div></div></div>
<p>In 1963, <a href="/wiki/Charles_R._Schwab" title="Charles R. Schwab">Charles R. Schwab</a> and two other partners launched <i>Investment Indicator</i>, an investment newsletter.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> At its height, the newsletter had 3,000 subscribers, each paying $84 a year to subscribe. In April 1971, the firm was incorporated in California as First Commander Corporation, a wholly owned subsidiary of Commander Industries, Inc., for traditional brokerage services and to publish the Schwab investment newsletter. In November of that year, Schwab and four others purchased all the stock from Commander Industries, Inc., and in 1972, Schwab bought all the stock from what was once Commander Industries. In 1973, the company name changed to Charles Schwab &amp; Co., Inc.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p><p>In 1975, the <a href="/wiki/U.S._Securities_and_Exchange_Commission" title="U.S. Securities and Exchange Commission">U.S. Securities and Exchange Commission</a> allowed for negotiated commission rates and Schwab set up a <a href="/wiki/Stockbroker" title="Stockbroker">stockbrokerage</a>. In September 1975, Schwab opened its first branch in Sacramento, CA, and started offering discount brokerage services. In 1977, Schwab began offering seminars to clients, and by 1978, Schwab had 45,000 client accounts total, doubling to 84,000 in 1979. In 1979, Schwab risked $500,000 on a back-office settlement system called BETA (which was short for Brokerage Execution and Transaction Analysis), enabling Schwab to become the first discount broker to bring automation inhouse. In 1980, Schwab established the industry's first 24-hour quotation service, and the total of client accounts grew to 147,000. In 1981, Schwab became a member of the <a href="/wiki/NYSE" class="mw-redirect" title="NYSE">NYSE</a>, and the total of client accounts grew to 222,000. In 1982, Schwab became the first to offer 24/7 order entry and quote service, its first international office was opened in Hong Kong, and the number of client accounts totaled 374,000.
</p><p>In 1983, <a href="/wiki/Stephen_McLin" title="Stephen McLin">Stephen McLin</a> purchased the company for <a href="/wiki/Bank_of_America" title="Bank of America">Bank of America</a> for $55 million. In 1984, the company launched 140 no-load <a href="/wiki/Mutual_fund" title="Mutual fund">mutual funds</a>. In 1987, management, including Charles R. Schwab, bought the company from Bank of America for $280 million.<sup id="cite_ref-selling_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-selling-6">[6]</a></sup> In 1991, the company acquired Mayer &amp; Scweitzer, a market making firm, allowing Schwab to execute its customers' orders without sending them to an exchange.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> In 1997, it was fined $200,000 for failing to arrange the best trades for its customers.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> The unit was renamed Schwab Capital Markets in 2000.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> In 1993, the company opened an office in <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">London</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p><p>In 1995, the company acquired The Hampton Company, founded by Walter W. Bettinger, who became CEO of Schwab in 2008.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> In 1996, Web trading goes live. Customers can trade listed and OTC stocks, or check balances and the status of orders on the schwab.com website.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> In 1998, dissatisfied by the in-house results, the company hired interactive firm Razorfish to redesign the website. Years later the website would be entered in the Cooper-Hewitt Museum's inaugural National Design Triennial.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> In 2000, Schwab purchased <a href="/wiki/U.S._Trust" class="mw-redirect" title="U.S. Trust">U.S. Trust</a> for $2.73 billion.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> In 2001, less than a year after the acquisition of U.S. Trust, the U.S. Trust subsidiary was fined $10 million in a bank secrecy law case. It was ordered to pay $5 million to the New York State Banking Department and $5 million to the Federal Reserve Board.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> On November 20, 2006, Schwab announced an agreement to sell U.S. Trust to <a href="/wiki/Bank_of_America" title="Bank of America">Bank of America</a> for $3.3 billion in cash.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> The deal closed in the second quarter of 2007.
</p><p>In January 2004, Schwab acquired <a href="/wiki/SoundView_Technology_Group" title="SoundView Technology Group">SoundView Technology Group</a> for $345 million to add <a href="/wiki/Equity_research" class="mw-redirect" title="Equity research">equity research</a> capabilities.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> David S. Pottruck, who had spent the majority of his 20 years at the brokerage as Charles R. Schwab's right-hand man, shared the CEO title with the company's founder from 1998 to 2003. In May 2003, Mr. Schwab stepped down, and gave Pottruck sole control as CEO. On July 24, 2004, the company's board fired Pottruck, replacing him with its founder and namesake. News of Pottruck's removal came as the firm had announced that overall profit had dropped 10%, to $113 million, for the second quarter, driven largely by a 26% decline in revenue from customer stock trading.
</p><p>After coming back into control, Mr. Schwab conceded that the company had "lost touch with our heritage", and quickly refocused the business on providing financial advice to individual investors. He also rolled back Pottruck's fee hikes. The company rebounded, and earnings began to turn around in 2005, as did the stock. The share price was up as high as 151% since Pottruck's removal, ten times since the return of Charles Schwab.<sup id="cite_ref-down_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-down-18">[18]</a></sup> The company's net transfer assets, or assets that come from other firms, quadrupled from 2004 to 2008. Schwab's YieldPlus fund drew controversy during the <a href="/wiki/Financial_crisis_of_2007%E2%80%9308" title="Financial crisis of 2007–08">2007 financial crisis</a> because of its -31.7% return.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> Investors in the Schwab YieldPlus Fund, including Charles Schwab himself, lost $1.1 billion.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> Schwab closed the YieldPlus funds in 2011.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> In April 2007, the company acquired The 401(k) Company.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> On July 22, 2008, Walter W. Bettinger, the previous <a href="/wiki/Chief_operating_officer" title="Chief operating officer">chief operating officer</a>, was named chief executive, succeeding the company's namesake. Charles R. Schwab remained <a href="/wiki/Executive_chairman" class="mw-redirect" title="Executive chairman">executive chairman</a> of the company and said in a statement that he would "continue to serve as a very active chairman".<sup id="cite_ref-down_18-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-down-18">[18]</a></sup> In 2011, the company acquired OptionsXpress.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> The company also acquired Compliance11, Inc., a provider of compliance software.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> In 2012, it expanded again by acquiring ThomasPartners, an <a href="/wiki/Asset_management" title="Asset management">asset management</a> firm.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> On July 26, 2019, the company announced it would acquire <a href="/wiki/USAA" title="USAA">USAA</a>’s investment management accounts for $1.8 billion in cash.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p><p>On November 25, 2019, the company announced its intent to acquire <a href="/wiki/TD_Ameritrade" title="TD Ameritrade">TD Ameritrade</a> for approximately $26 billion in stock.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> On February 24, 2020, the company announced it would acquire Wasmer, Schroeder &amp; Company in an all cash purchase. <sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup>.
</p>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(2)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Marketing">Marketing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Charles_Schwab_Corporation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Marketing" data-section="2" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-2 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-2">
<p>In 2004, Charles Schwab chose <a href="/wiki/Havas_Worldwide" class="mw-redirect" title="Havas Worldwide">Havas Worldwide</a> (then called Euro RSCG) as its full-service <a href="/wiki/Advertising_agency" title="Advertising agency">advertising agency</a>. In February 2013, Schwab hired <a href="/wiki/Crispin_Porter_%2B_Bogusky" title="Crispin Porter + Bogusky">Crispin Porter + Bogusky</a> (CP+B) as its lead creative agency with Havas Worldwide remaining to create ads for ActiveTrader and optionsXpress.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> In March 2015, <a href="/wiki/Adweek" title="Adweek">Adweek</a> reported on marketing material created by CP+B for Schwab's Intelligent Portfolio service.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p><p>In 2005, the company launched a series of television ads featuring the slogan <i>Talk to Chuck</i> by Euro RSCG and directed/animated by <a href="/wiki/Bob_Sabiston" title="Bob Sabiston">Bob Sabiston</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Rotoshop" title="Rotoshop">Flat Black Films</a>. "Talk to Chuck" ads appeared in print media, online, billboards, and branch offices.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> A blog post in <a href="/wiki/The_Wall_Street_Journal" title="The Wall Street Journal">The Wall Street Journal</a> described the ads as effective because they included a single memorable phrase.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p><p>In 2013, the company launched an advertising campaign with the slogan <i>Own Your Tomorrow</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(3)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Schwab_Charitable_Fund">Schwab Charitable Fund</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Charles_Schwab_Corporation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Schwab Charitable Fund" data-section="3" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-3 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-3">
<p>The <b>Schwab Charitable Fund</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Donor_advised_fund" class="mw-redirect" title="Donor advised fund">donor advised fund</a> which preserves the anonymity of donors by not disclosing individual donor names. Professionally managed accounts are only available through independent investment advisors working with Schwab Advisor Services, a business segment of The Charles Schwab Corporation. It accepts contributions of real estate, private equity or other non-cash assets via a charitable intermediary, with proceeds of the donation transferred to a donor-advised account upon liquidation. This intermediary considers donations on a case-by-case basis, with a typical requirement that assets be valued at $250,000 or more.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
