</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(1)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cerner&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History" data-section="1" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-1 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-1">
<p>Cerner was founded in 1979 by <a href="/wiki/Neal_Patterson" title="Neal Patterson">Neal Patterson</a>, Paul Gorup, and Cliff Illig, who were colleagues at <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Andersen" title="Arthur Andersen">Arthur Andersen</a>. Its original name was PGI &amp; Associates but was renamed Cerner in 1984 when it rolled out its first system, PathNet.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> It went public in 1986.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Cerner's client base grew steadily in the late 1980s, reaching 70 sites in 1987, 120 sites in 1988, 170 sites in 1989, and reaching 250 sites in 1990. Installations were primarily of PathNet systems.<sup id="cite_ref-fundinguniverse.com_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fundinguniverse.com-11">[11]</a></sup></p><p>During this time, Cerner was developing components of a Health Network Architecture (HNA), an integrated IT system designed to automate health care processes. Clients could purchase individual components or the whole system at one time. By 1994, more than 30 clients had purchased the full HNA system, while 100 clients had purchased multiple components of the system.<sup id="cite_ref-fundinguniverse.com_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fundinguniverse.com-11">[11]</a></sup></p><p>In 1997, the company introduced Cerner Millennium, an upgrade to its HNA system which incorporated all of the company's software offerings into one unified architecture. The introduction of Millennium contributed to significant growth for the company, with revenue increasing to $1.1 billion in 2005<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> from $245.1 million in 1997.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p><p>Cerner acquired IMC Health Care, Inc. in early 2010 to continue expanding its wellness services to outside commercial employers, pharmacies, and wellness programs.<sup id="cite_ref-Healthcare_IT_News_12.09_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Healthcare_IT_News_12.09-14">[14]</a></sup></p><p>In July 2010, president Trace Devanny left the company<sup id="cite_ref-Cerner-Jul-2010-8-K_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cerner-Jul-2010-8-K-15">[15]</a></sup> and Patterson became the company's president, in addition to his roles as chairman and chief executive officer.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>  In September 2013, Zane Burke was named president, assuming the title from Patterson.<sup id="cite_ref-Kansas_City_Star_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kansas_City_Star-17">[17]</a></sup></p><p>On August 5, 2014, Cerner announced its intent to purchase Siemens Health Services, the health information technology business of Germany's <a href="/wiki/Siemens_AG" class="mw-redirect" title="Siemens AG">Siemens AG</a>, for $1.3 billion.<sup id="cite_ref-Modern_Healthcare_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Modern_Healthcare-18">[18]</a></sup> The acquisition was completed on February 2, 2015.<sup id="cite_ref-Modern_Healthcare_8.14_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Modern_Healthcare_8.14-19">[19]</a></sup></p><p>On July 29, 2015, Leidos Partnership for Defense Health, which includes Cerner, <a href="/wiki/Accenture" title="Accenture">Accenture</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Leidos" title="Leidos">Leidos</a>, was awarded a 10-year, $4.3 billion contract to overhaul and manage the electronic health records for the Department of Defense.<sup id="cite_ref-The_Washington_Post_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-The_Washington_Post-20">[20]</a></sup></p><p>CEO and co-founder <a href="/wiki/Neal_Patterson" title="Neal Patterson">Neal Patterson</a> died July 9, 2017.<sup id="cite_ref-Patterson_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Patterson-1">[1]</a></sup></p><p>On January 10, 2018, Brent Shafer was named Chairman and CEO and took over leadership responsibilities in February 2018.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p><p>On September 4, 2019, Cerner laid off 255 employees as part of an ongoing cost-cutting effort and reorganization.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p><p>In February 2020, Cerner sells off parts of healthcare IT business in Germany and Spain to German company CompuGroup Medical SE for EUR 225 million (USD 247.5M).<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(2)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Products">Products</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cerner&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Products" data-section="2" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-2 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-2">
<p>The company's products include:<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<ul><li>Cerner <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://www.cerner.com/solutions/health-systems">Millennium</a>, an EHR system built on the IBM WebSphere Application Server<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> based on the Java Enterprise Edition (EE) runtime environment</li>
<li>Cerner <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://www.cerner.com/gb/en/solutions/pas">PAS</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Patient_administration_system" title="Patient administration system">Patient Administration System</a></li>
<li>Cerner <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://www.cerner.com/gb/en/solutions/health-information-exchange">Health Information Exchange (HIE)</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Health_information_exchange" title="Health information exchange">Health information exchange</a></li>
<li>Millenium+, a cloud hosted EHR</li>
<li>Cerner FirstNet, emergency department system</li>
<li>Cerner RadNet Radiology Information System (RIS)</li>
<li>Cerner Learning Framework<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></li></ul><p>The <a href="/wiki/Cerner_CCL" title="Cerner CCL">Cerner CCL</a> (Cerner Command Language), is a SQL-like programming language developed by Cerner.
</p>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(3)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Controversy">Controversy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cerner&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Controversy" data-section="3" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-3 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-3">
<p>In 2001, a memo authored by CEO Patterson and sent to about 400 managers was leaked online.  The memo, written in harsh language, was meant to motivate the managers to get more productivity out of employees and promised layoffs, a hiring freeze, closing of an "Associate Center," and the implementation of a punch-card system if Patterson did not see evidence of changes.  Patterson's metric was the fullness of the company's Kansas City office lot at the hours of 8 a.m. and 5 p.m.  The memo was widely seen as inflammatory and poor management, and Cerner's stock price fell 22% over three days.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p><p>In 2005, Cerner and other companies paid for a report by the <a href="/wiki/RAND_Corporation" title="RAND Corporation">RAND Corporation</a> which predicted great efficiencies from <a href="/wiki/Electronic_health_record" title="Electronic health record">electronic health records</a>, including savings of $81 billion a year or more, which RAND now says is overstated. This report helped drive growth in the electronic health record and billions of dollars in federal incentives to hospitals and doctors. Cerner's revenue tripled from $1 billion in 2005 to a projected $3 billion in 2013. The study was criticized by the <a href="/wiki/Congressional_Budget_Office" title="Congressional Budget Office">Congressional Budget Office</a> for overstating potential savings. A 2013 reassessment of the 2005 report by the RAND Corporation said that the conversion had failed to produce savings and had mixed results in efficiency and patient care.<sup id="cite_ref-Abelson_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Abelson-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Canada">Canada</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cerner&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Canada" data-section="4" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>In 2016, the <a href="/wiki/Nanaimo" title="Nanaimo">Nanaimo</a> <a href="/wiki/British_Columbia" title="British Columbia">BC</a> hospital ER began using the system. Physicians and nurses have called it a "huge failure" with it increasing some types of errors and slowing down the emergency department.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> The system cost <a href="/wiki/CAN%24" class="mw-redirect" title="CAN$">CAN$</a> 230 million dollars.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="United_States">United States</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cerner&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: United States" data-section="5" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>In 2002, the installation of a <a href="/wiki/Computerized_physician_order_entry" title="Computerized physician order entry">computerized health system</a> by Cerner in the <a href="/wiki/UPMC_Children%27s_Hospital_of_Pittsburgh" title="UPMC Children's Hospital of Pittsburgh">UPMC Children's Hospital of Pittsburgh</a>  made it harder for the doctors and nurses to do their jobs in emergency situations and resulted in a "disaster," according to <a href="/wiki/Phillip_Longman" title="Phillip Longman">Phillip Longman</a>, a senior fellow at the <a href="/wiki/New_America_Foundation" class="mw-redirect" title="New America Foundation">New America Foundation</a>. Longman wrote, "According to a study conducted by the hospital and published in the journal <i><a href="/wiki/Pediatrics_(journal)" title="Pediatrics (journal)">Pediatrics</a></i>, mortality rates for one vulnerable patient population—those brought by emergency transport from other facilities—more than doubled, from 2.8 percent before the installation to almost 6.6 percent afterward."<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> Defenders of Cerner in the study charged that the <a href="/wiki/Pittsburgh" title="Pittsburgh">Pittsburgh</a> hospital did not adequately prepare for the transition to the Computerized Provider Order Entry (CPOE) system because it had simultaneously modified its pharmacy process, did not provide adequate wireless bandwidth, and did not have order sets pre-programmed on day one. They stated that other hospitals that more carefully planned the implementation did not experience the same problems.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p><p>In 2010, Girard Medical Center, <a href="/wiki/Crawford_County,_Kansas" title="Crawford County, Kansas">Crawford County</a>, Kansas, hired Cerner to install an electronic records system. But after receiving $1.3 million, Cerner employees failed to get the system running in time to qualify for federal incentive payments, and in September 2011, notified the hospital that it was abandoning the project, according to a lawsuit Girard filed against Cerner. Cerner and executives at Girard agreed that Girard did not have adequate staff to manage the acquisition and implementation of the system.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup>  As of June 2014, the case remained in arbitration.<sup id="cite_ref-Abelson_29-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Abelson-29">[29]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-HealthIT2014_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HealthIT2014-35">[35]</a></sup> The outcome of the Girard case will likely be kept confidential due to contract provisions.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></p><p>In 2012, <a href="/wiki/Trinity_Health_(Minot,_North_Dakota)" title="Trinity Health (Minot, North Dakota)">Trinity Health</a>, a large hospital in <a href="/wiki/North_Dakota" title="North Dakota">North Dakota</a>, sued Cerner, claiming that Cerner's patient accounting solution didn't work correctly; the parties settled for $106M in 2014.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></p><p>In 2014, a grand jury in <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a> found that Cerner knew that the Ventura County healthcare agency was unprepared to complete a $32 million installation. Also in 2014, a $31 million Cerner implementation at the <a href="/wiki/Athens_Regional_Medical_Center" class="mw-redirect" title="Athens Regional Medical Center">Athens Regional Health System</a> in <a href="/wiki/Georgia_(U.S._state)" title="Georgia (U.S. state)">Georgia</a> had many problems, leading to forced resignations by the CEO and the CIO.<sup id="cite_ref-HealthIT2014_35-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HealthIT2014-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(4)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Locations">Locations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cerner&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Locations" data-section="6" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-4 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-4">
<p>Cerner's world headquarters campus is at 2800 Rockcreek Parkway, North Kansas City, Missouri.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> It acquired additional space in <a href="/wiki/Kansas_City,_Missouri" title="Kansas City, Missouri">Kansas City, Missouri</a> in 2005<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> and in 2006 it acquired another location in Kansas City.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup>  In 2013, Cerner announced plans to redevelop 236 acres in south Kansas City, Missouri into an office park. The site was previously occupied by <a href="/wiki/Bannister_Mall" title="Bannister Mall">Bannister Mall</a>, which was demolished in 2009.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> Cerner broke ground on the new campus on November 11, 2014. The $4.45 billion project intends to employ 16,000 new Cerner workers within the decade.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup></p><p>Cerner has offices in about 25 countries worldwide.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup></p>
