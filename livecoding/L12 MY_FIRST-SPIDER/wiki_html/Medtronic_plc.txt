<p class="mw-empty-elt">

</p>
<p><b>Medtronic plc</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Medical_device" title="Medical device">medical device</a> company that generates the majority of its sales and profits from the U.S. healthcare system but is headquartered in the Republic of Ireland for tax purposes.<sup id="cite_ref-largest_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-largest-3">[3]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-wsj1_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wsj1-4">[4]</a></sup> Medtronic has an operational and executive headquarters in <a href="/wiki/Fridley,_Minnesota" title="Fridley, Minnesota">Fridley, Minnesota</a> in the U.S.<sup id="cite_ref-star_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-star-5">[5]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-rules2_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rules2-6">[6]</a></sup> In 2015, Medtronic acquired Irish–tax registered <a href="/wiki/Covidien" title="Covidien">Covidien</a> (a U.S. <a href="/wiki/Tax_inversion" title="Tax inversion">tax inversion</a> to Ireland from 2007), in the largest U.S. <a href="/wiki/Corporation_tax_in_the_Republic_of_Ireland#List_of_inversions" title="Corporation tax in the Republic of Ireland">corporate tax inversion</a> in history,<sup id="cite_ref-bbb_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bbb-7">[7]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-medtronic_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-medtronic-8">[8]</a></sup> which enabled Medtronic to move its legal registration from the U.S. to Ireland.<sup id="cite_ref-cov_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cov-9">[9]</a></sup> Medtronic operates in 140 countries and employs over 86,000 people.<sup id="cite_ref-ft_10-0" class="reference"><a </section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(1)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Medtronic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History" data-section="1" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-1 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-1">
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_history">Early history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Medtronic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Early history" data-section="2" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:292px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Medtronic_OHQ_Fridley_MN_2017.jpg" class="image"><noscript><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fb/Medtronic_OHQ_Fridley_MN_2017.jpg/290px-Medtronic_OHQ_Fridley_MN_2017.jpg" decoding="async" width="290" height="164" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="2688" data-file-height="1520"></noscript><span class="lazy-image-placeholder" style="width: 290px;height: 164px;" data-src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fb/Medtronic_OHQ_Fridley_MN_2017.jpg/290px-Medtronic_OHQ_Fridley_MN_2017.jpg" data-alt="" data-width="290" data-height="164" data-class="thumbimage"> </span></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Medtronic_OHQ_Fridley_MN_2017.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Medtronic operational headquarters in <a href="/wiki/Fridley,_Minnesota" title="Fridley, Minnesota">Fridley, Minnesota</a>, a suburb of Minneapolis</div></div></div>
<p>Medtronic was founded in 1949 in <a href="/wiki/Minneapolis" title="Minneapolis">Minneapolis</a> by <a href="/wiki/Earl_Bakken" title="Earl Bakken">Earl Bakken</a> and his brother-in-law, Palmer Hermundslie, as a medical equipment repair shop.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p><p>Through his repair business, Bakken came to know <a href="/wiki/C._Walton_Lillehei" title="C. Walton Lillehei">C. Walton Lillehei</a>, a doctor of <a href="/wiki/Heart_surgery" class="mw-redirect" title="Heart surgery">heart surgery</a> at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Minnesota_Medical_School" title="University of Minnesota Medical School">University of Minnesota Medical School</a>. The deficiencies of the <a href="/wiki/Artificial_cardiac_pacemaker" title="Artificial cardiac pacemaker">artificial pacemakers</a> of the day were made painfully obvious following a <a href="/wiki/Power_outage" title="Power outage">power outage</a> over <a href="/wiki/Halloween" title="Halloween">Halloween</a> in 1957, which affected large sections of <a href="/wiki/Minnesota" title="Minnesota">Minnesota</a> and western <a href="/wiki/Wisconsin" title="Wisconsin">Wisconsin</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> A pacemaker-dependent <a href="/wiki/Paediatrics" class="mw-redirect" title="Paediatrics">paediatric</a> patient of Lillehei died because of the blackout. The next day, Lillehei spoke with Bakken about developing some form of <a href="/wiki/Battery_(electricity)" class="mw-redirect" title="Battery (electricity)">battery</a>-powered pacemaker. Bakken modified a design for a <a href="/wiki/Transistor" title="Transistor">transistorized</a> <a href="/wiki/Metronome" title="Metronome">metronome</a> and created the first battery-powered external  pacemaker.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:2012-0907-OldMedtronicHQ.jpg" class="image"><noscript><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/98/2012-0907-OldMedtronicHQ.jpg/220px-2012-0907-OldMedtronicHQ.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="5184" data-file-height="3456"></noscript><span class="lazy-image-placeholder" style="width: 220px;height: 147px;" data-src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/98/2012-0907-OldMedtronicHQ.jpg/220px-2012-0907-OldMedtronicHQ.jpg" data-alt="" data-width="220" data-height="147" data-class="thumbimage"> </span></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:2012-0907-OldMedtronicHQ.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Medtronic's original headquarters in St. Anthony, Minnesota</div></div></div>
<p>The company expanded through the 1950s, selling equipment built by other companies but also developing custom-made devices. Bakken built a small pacemaker that could be strapped to the body and powered by batteries. Work in the new field later produced an <a href="/wiki/Implant_(medicine)" title="Implant (medicine)">implantable</a> pacemaker in 1960. The company built headquarters in the Minneapolis suburb of <a href="/wiki/St._Anthony,_Minnesota" title="St. Anthony, Minnesota">St. Anthony, Minnesota</a>, in 1960<sup id="cite_ref-TCBiz090612_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TCBiz090612-13">[13]</a></sup> and the company moved to Fridley in the 1970s. Medtronic's main competitors in the cardiac rhythm field include <a href="/wiki/Boston_Scientific" title="Boston Scientific">Boston Scientific</a> and <a href="/wiki/St._Jude_Medical" title="St. Jude Medical">St. Jude Medical</a>. In 1998, Medtronic acquired <a href="/wiki/Physio-Control" title="Physio-Control">Physio-Control</a> for $538 million.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span id="Animal_rights_.282005_to_2010.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Animal_rights_(2005_to_2010)">Animal rights (2005 to 2010)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Medtronic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Animal rights (2005 to 2010)" data-section="3" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>In 2005, 2008, and 2010 <a href="/wiki/PETA" class="mw-redirect" title="PETA">PETA</a> threatened to submit a <a href="/wiki/Shareholder_resolution" title="Shareholder resolution">shareholder resolution</a> to improve <a href="/wiki/Animal_welfare" title="Animal welfare">animal welfare</a> standards in the company. In 2005, PETA attempted to stop five specific animal experiments that it deemed "crude and cruel". In 2008, PETA protested the outsourcing of animal testing to countries with lax animal welfare laws, such as <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>. In 2010, PETA attempted to stop Medtronic's reported use of live animals in testing and training. In response, Medtronic conducted a feasibility study that found that banning the use of live animals was impractical. Medtronic continues to use live animals for testing and training but has said that it will look for alternatives in the future. In each case, PETA withdrew its shareholder resolution after talks with Medtronic's leadership.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span id="Tax_inversion_to_Ireland_.282014.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Tax_inversion_to_Ireland_(2014)">Tax inversion to Ireland (2014)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Medtronic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Tax inversion to Ireland (2014)" data-section="4" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Corporation_tax_in_the_Republic_of_Ireland#Corporate_tax_inversions" title="Corporation tax in the Republic of Ireland">Corporation tax in the Republic of Ireland § Corporate tax inversions</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Medtronic_plc_Headquarters_Ireland.jpg" class="image"><noscript><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/49/Medtronic_plc_Headquarters_Ireland.jpg/220px-Medtronic_plc_Headquarters_Ireland.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="124" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="750" data-file-height="423"></noscript><span class="lazy-image-placeholder" style="width: 220px;height: 124px;" data-src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/49/Medtronic_plc_Headquarters_Ireland.jpg/220px-Medtronic_plc_Headquarters_Ireland.jpg" data-alt="" data-width="220" data-height="124" data-class="thumbimage"> </span></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Medtronic_plc_Headquarters_Ireland.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Medtronic legal headquarters, 20 Lower Hatch Street, Dublin 2, Ireland</div></div></div>
<p>In June 2014, Medtronic announced it would execute a <a href="/wiki/Tax_inversion" title="Tax inversion">tax inversion</a> to Ireland by acquiring Irish-based <a href="/wiki/Covidien_plc" class="mw-redirect" title="Covidien plc">Covidien plc</a> (a previous U.S. tax inversion to Ireland in 2007), for $42.9 billion in cash and stock.<sup id="cite_ref-cov_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cov-9">[9]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> The tax inversion enabled Medtronic to move its legal headquarters to Ireland, while maintaining its operational and executive headquarters in the U.S., thus allowing it to avoid taxation on more than $14 billion held overseas, and avail of Ireland's beneficial <a href="/wiki/Corporation_tax_in_the_Republic_of_Ireland" title="Corporation tax in the Republic of Ireland">low corporation tax regime</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-bbb_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bbb-7">[7]</a></sup> Medtronic's tax inversion is the largest tax inversion in history,<sup id="cite_ref-wsj1_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wsj1-4">[4]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-bbb_7-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bbb-7">[7]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-medtronic_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-medtronic-8">[8]</a></sup> and given the changes in the U.S. tax-code in 2016 to block the Pfizer-Allergan Irish tax inversion, is likely to remain the largest.<sup id="cite_ref-rules2_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rules2-6">[6]</a></sup> Medtronic CEO Omar Ishrak defended the tax inversion in a 2015 interview to the <i><a href="/wiki/Financial_Times" title="Financial Times">Financial Times</a></i> saying, "We just followed the rules and the deal was done based on strategic merits".<sup id="cite_ref-ft_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ft-10">[10]</a></sup> Ireland is less than 0.1% of Medtronic (or Covidien) sales, and the majority of Medtronic's sales, and an even greater percentage of Medtronic profits (due to the higher margins on U.S. medical devices), are from the U.S. healthcare system.<sup id="cite_ref-wsj1_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wsj1-4">[4]</a></sup> In 2016, the <i><a href="/wiki/Star_Tribune" title="Star Tribune">Star Tribune</a></i> reported that Medtronic was still winning U.S. Federal contracts and attending U.S. trade-missions as a U.S. company.<sup id="cite_ref-star_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-star-5">[5]</a></sup></p><p>In terms of scale, on 23 November 2018, Ireland's largest stockbrokers, <i><a href="/wiki/Davy_Stockbrokers" class="mw-redirect" title="Davy Stockbrokers">Davy Stockbrokers</a></i> reported that the total capitalization of the Irish stock market was €104 billion (this does not include Medtronic, which Davy do not consider an Irish company).<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> In contrast, on the same day Medtronic's capitalisation was listed on Bloomberg at just over $130 billion (or €115 billion).
</p><p>Medtronic's acquisition of Covidien plc made Medtronic the world's largest medical device company by revenues.<sup id="cite_ref-largest_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-largest-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span id="Post_tax_inversion_to_Ireland_.282014.E2.80.932020.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Post_tax_inversion_to_Ireland_(2014–2020)">Post tax inversion to Ireland (2014–2020)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Medtronic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Post tax inversion to Ireland (2014–2020)" data-section="5" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>In February 2016, the company announced that it would acquire Bellco from <a href="/wiki/Private_equity" title="Private equity">private equity</a> firm Charme Capital Partners.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> In June, the company announced its acquisition of HeartWare International Inc. for $1.1 billion.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> In December 2017, Medtronic acquired Crospon in €38 million ($45 million).<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> In September 2018 the company acquired <a href="/wiki/Mazor_Robotics" title="Mazor Robotics">Mazor Robotics</a> for $1.64 billion ($58.50 per American Depository Share or $29.25 per ordinary share.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> In late November, Medtronic acquired Nutrino Health Ltd boosting the companies nutrition-related data services and analytics.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p><p>In May 2019, Medtronic announced it would acquire Titan Spine, a technology company focussed on titanium spine implants.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup>
Chief Executive Officer Omar Ishrak will retire in April 2020 and will be succeeded by senior executive Geoff Martha.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p><p>In January 2020, the company announced its intention to acquire Stimgenics, LLC and their primary therapy, differential target multiplexed spinal cord stimulation.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>

<p><b>Vitatron</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Netherlands</a>-based European subsidiary of Medtronic, a worldwide leader in medical technology. It is focused on development and manufacturing of <a href="/wiki/Artificial_cardiac_pacemaker" title="Artificial cardiac pacemaker">cardiac pacing technology</a>. Once an independently operating Dutch medical company, it was acquired by Medtronic in 1986.<sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup>
Vitatron pacemakers are interrogated and programmed by Medtronic Carelink Model 2090 Programmer for Medtronic and Vitatron Devices, using a separate interface.<sup id="cite_ref-59" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="History_2">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Medtronic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: History" data-section="9" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<ul><li>1956: Vitatron founded</li>
<li>1962: First Implantable pacemakers</li>
<li>1981: Microprocessor-driven, software-based pacemaker (DPG1)</li>
<li>1982: Rate Responsive pacemaker (TX1)</li>
<li>1984: Quintech DDD with automatic upper rate behavior ("mode switch")</li>
<li>1988: Daily Learn algorithm (Rhythmyx)</li>
<li>1997: First upgradeable pacemaker system with dedicated AF diagnostics, rate and rhythm control therapy.</li>
<li>2003: Vitatron goes digital: 1st Vitatron C-Series, the world's first fully digital pacemaker.<sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup></li>
<li>2004: 2nd Vitatron C-Series, digital, fast pacemaker.</li>
<li>2004: Vitatron T-Series: The full picture, digital pacemaker system.</li>
<li>2005: Vitatron C-Series, A3 models, a new top line range of digital pacemakers for bradycardic patients.</li></ul></section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(4)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Business_units">Business units</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Medtronic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Business units" data-section="10" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-4 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-4">
<p>Medtronic has four main business units: the Minimally Invasive Therapies Group, the Diabetes Group, the Restorative Therapies Group, and the Cardiac and Vascular Group. Medtronic develops and manufactures devices and therapies to treat more than 30 chronic diseases, including <a href="/wiki/Heart_failure" title="Heart failure">heart failure</a>, <a href="/wiki/Parkinson_disease" class="mw-redirect" title="Parkinson disease">Parkinson disease</a>, <a href="/wiki/Urinary_incontinence" title="Urinary incontinence">urinary incontinence</a>, <a href="/wiki/Down_syndrome" title="Down syndrome">Down syndrome</a>, <a href="/wiki/Obesity" title="Obesity">obesity</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chronic_pain" title="Chronic pain">chronic pain</a>, spinal disorders and <a href="/wiki/Diabetes" title="Diabetes">diabetes</a>.
</p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="CRDM">CRDM</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Medtronic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: CRDM" data-section="11" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>Cardiac rhythm disease management (CRDM) is the oldest and largest of Medtronic's business units. Its work in heart rhythm therapies dates back to 1957, when Bakken developed the first wearable heart pacemaker to treat abnormally-slow heart rates. Since then, it has expanded its expertise in electrical stimulation to treat other cardiac rhythm diseases. It has also made an effort to address overall disease management by adding diagnostic and monitoring capabilities to many of its devices. An independently-operating Dutch pacemaker manufacturer, <a href="/wiki/Vitatron" class="mw-redirect" title="Vitatron">Vitatron</a>, acquired by Medtronic in 1986, is now a European subsidiary of the unit.<sup id="cite_ref-61" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup> Medtronic and Vitatron pacemakers are interrogated and programmed by Medtronic Carelink Model 2090 Programmer for Medtronic and Vitatron Devices; they use separate interfaces.<sup id="cite_ref-62" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup></p><p>In 2007, Medtronic recalled its Sprint Fidelis product, the flexible wires, or leads, which connect a defibrillator to the interior of the heart. The leads were found to be failing at an unacceptable rate, resulting in unnecessary shocks or no shocks when needed; either can be lethal. The scope of the problem continues to be a matter of research. Studies since the recall, disputed by Medtronic, suggest that the failure rate of already-implanted Sprint Fidelis leads is increasing exponentially. Medtronic's liability is limited by various court decisions.<sup id="cite_ref-WSJ2010_63-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WSJ2010-63">[63]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Spinal_and_biologics">Spinal and biologics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Medtronic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Spinal and biologics" data-section="12" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>Spinal and biologics is Medtronic's second-largest business. Medtronic is the world leader in spinal and musculoskeletal therapies. In 2007, Medtronic purchased Kyphon, a manufacturer and seller of spinal implants that are necessary for procedures like <a href="/wiki/Kyphoplasty" class="mw-redirect" title="Kyphoplasty">kyphoplasty</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-64" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup></p><p>In May 2008, Medtronic Spine agreed to pay the <a href="/wiki/US_government" class="mw-redirect" title="US government">US government</a> $75 million to settle a <a href="/wiki/Qui_tam" title="Qui tam">qui tam</a> lawsuit after a <a href="/wiki/Whistleblower" title="Whistleblower">whistleblower</a> alleged that Medtronic committed Medicare fraud. The company was charged with illegally convincing healthcare providers to offer kyphoplasty, a spinal fracture repair surgery, as an inpatient, not an outpatient, procedure to make thousands of dollars more in profits per surgery.<sup id="cite_ref-65" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup></p><p>A "special report" by writer <a href="/wiki/Steven_Brill_(journalist)" title="Steven Brill (journalist)">Steven Brill</a> in <i><a href="/wiki/Time_(magazine)" title="Time (magazine)">Time</a></i> showed that according to Medtronic's quarterly <a href="/wiki/SEC_filing" title="SEC filing">SEC filing</a> of October 2012, the company had, on average, a 75.1% profit margin on its spine products and therapies.<sup id="cite_ref-66" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Cardiovascular">Cardiovascular</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Medtronic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Cardiovascular" data-section="13" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>Medtronic's cardiovascular therapies span the major specialities of interventional cardiology, cardiac surgery and vascular surgery. The products are used to reduce the potentially debilitating effects of coronary, aortic and structural heart disease.
</p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Neuromodulation">Neuromodulation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Medtronic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Neuromodulation" data-section="14" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>Neuromodulation is the second-oldest and third-largest department of Medtronic. Its products include <a href="/wiki/Neuromodulation_(medicine)" title="Neuromodulation (medicine)">neurostimulation</a> systems and implantable drug delivery systems for chronic pain, common movement disorders and urologic and gastrointestinal disorders. The department's revenues in 2014 amounted to $1.9 billion, or 11% of Medtronic's total revenues.<sup id="cite_ref-67" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Diabetes">Diabetes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Medtronic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Diabetes" data-section="15" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Diabetes" title="Diabetes">diabetes</a> management manufacturing and sales division of Medtronic is based in <a href="/wiki/Northridge,_California" class="mw-redirect" title="Northridge, California">Northridge, California</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-68" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup> The original company, Minimed Technologies, was founded in the early 1980s by <a href="/wiki/Alfred_E._Mann" title="Alfred E. Mann">Alfred E. Mann</a> and spun off from <a href="/wiki/Pacesetter_Systems" title="Pacesetter Systems">Pacesetter Systems</a> to design a practical <a href="/wiki/Insulin_pump" title="Insulin pump">insulin pump</a> for lifelong wear.<sup id="cite_ref-69" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup> Most devices then were either too large or impossible to program and were extremely unreliable. The release of the lightweight, menu-driven MiniMed 500 series changed the landscape, and it was a major factor in bringing insulin pump usage to the mainstream.
</p><p>In 1996, the MiniMed was redesigned by the innovation consulting RKS Design to look both more flashy and more elegant and to resemble a beeper. The friendliness of the device boosted adoption rate, and sales increased by 357%.<sup id="cite_ref-70" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup> In the early 2000s, Medtronic purchased Minimed, to form Medtronic Minimed.<sup id="cite_ref-71" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup></p><p>On 11 May 2009, Medtronic announced it had chosen <a href="/wiki/San_Antonio,_Texas" class="mw-redirect" title="San Antonio, Texas">San Antonio, Texas</a>, for the location of its new Diabetes Therapy Management and Education Center. The company announced that it expected 1400 new jobs would be created to staff the 150,000-square-foot (14,000 m<sup>2</sup>) facility.<sup id="cite_ref-72" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-72">[72]</a></sup></p><p>In September 2016, the <a href="/wiki/FDA" class="mw-redirect" title="FDA">FDA</a> approved a device, <a href="/wiki/MiniMed_670G" class="mw-redirect" title="MiniMed 670G">MiniMed 670G</a>, which automatically pumps insulin to a diabetic patient's body on sensing its absence or reduction.<sup id="cite_ref-73" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-73">[73]</a></sup></p>

<p>The surgical technologies business group designed and manufactured products for the diagnosis and treatment of ear, nose, and throat (ENT) diseases and cranial, spinal, and neurologic conditions. It also encompassed a surgical navigation division to design "StealthStation" systems, software and instruments for <a href="/wiki/Computer-assisted_surgery" title="Computer-assisted surgery">Computer Assisted Surgery (CAS)</a> and a special intraoperative X-ray imaging system (3D fluoroscopy), known as the O-arm Imaging System. Many of the products are used for minimally-invasive surgical procedures. In 2016, the business unit was dissolved, and each site folded into new business groups.
</p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Technology_safety">Technology safety</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Medtronic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Technology safety" data-section="17" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>In 2011, an independent security researcher, Jay Radcliffe, revealed a security vulnerability in a Medtronic insulin pump at the Computer Security conference <a href="/wiki/Black_Hat_Briefings" title="Black Hat Briefings">Black Hat Briefings</a>, allowing an attacker to take control of the pump. Medtronic responded by assuring users of the full safety of its devices.<sup id="cite_ref-74" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-74">[74]</a></sup></p><p>In 2008, a team of computer security researchers was able to take remote control of a Medtronic cardiac implant. The team, using an unused implant in a lab, was able to control the electrical shocks delivered by the defibrillator component and even glean patient data from the device.<sup id="cite_ref-75" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-75">[75]</a></sup></p><p>In February 2020, Medtronic recalled around 322,000 MiniMed insulin pumps with faulty pump retainer rings, which had been correlated to a death and around 2,000 injuries.<sup id="cite_ref-wcpo_76-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wcpo-76">[76]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Portable_ventilators">Portable ventilators</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Medtronic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Portable ventilators" data-section="18" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>On 30 March 2020, Medtronic shared its portable <a href="/wiki/Ventilator" title="Ventilator">ventilator</a> design specifications during the outbreak of <a href="/wiki/2019%E2%80%9320_coronavirus_pandemic" title="2019–20 coronavirus pandemic">2019–20 coronavirus pandemic</a>, in order to accelerate global ventilator production.<sup id="cite_ref-77" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-77">[77]</a></sup></p>
