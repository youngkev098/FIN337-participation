<p><b>NRG Energy, Inc.</b> is a large American energy company, dual-headquartered in Princeton, New Jersey<sup id="cite_ref-:0_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-4">[4]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Houston" title="Houston">Houston</a>, <a href="/wiki/Texas" title="Texas">Texas</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-yahoo_genon_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-yahoo_genon-1">[1]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> It was formerly the wholesale arm of <a href="/wiki/Northern_States_Power_Company" title="Northern States Power Company">Northern States Power Company</a> (NSP), which became <a href="/wiki/Xcel_Energy" title="Xcel Energy">Xcel Energy</a>, but became independent in 2000. NRG Energy is involved in energy generation and retail electricity.<sup id="cite_ref-:2_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:2-8">[8]</a></sup> Their portfolio includes nuclear generation, coal generation, wind generation, utility scale generation, distributed solar generation, and oil generation.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> NRG serves 2.9 million retail customers in Texas, Connecticut, Delaware, Illinois, Maryland, Massachusetts, New Jersey, New York, Pennsylvania, Ohio, and the District of Columbia.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_4-1" </section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(1)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=NRG_Energy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History" data-section="1" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-1 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-1">
<h4 class="in-block"><span id="1980s.E2.80.931990s"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="1980s–1990s">1980s–1990s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=NRG_Energy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: 1980s–1990s" data-section="2" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h4>
<p>NRG Energy was formed in 1989 as one of NSP’s wholly owned subsidiaries. In 1997, NRG Energy, Inc. had 2,650 MW of generation and operational responsibility for a supplementary 5,374 MW. By 1998, the company began an aggressive acquisition campaign. It bought plants from Niagara Mohawk, San Diego Gas and Electric, Consolidated Edison, Montaup Electric, Rochester Gas and Electric, and Connecticut Light &amp; Power. They continued to grow through acquisitions in 1999 and<ins>,</ins> in 2000, acquired Cajun Electric Power Cooperative’s facilities.<sup id="cite_ref-:2_8-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:2-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h4 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="2000s">2000s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=NRG_Energy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: 2000s" data-section="3" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h4>
<p>In 2001, NRG Energy had a net ownership of 24,357 MW of generation globally, with 19,077 MW in the United States. From 1996 to 2001, the operating revenue increased from $104 million to $3 billion, and the debt increased from $212 million to $8.3 billion. By 2002, the debt had reached $9.4 billion and NRG Energy sold their powerplants in Hungary and Czech Republic. To avert default by NRG, Xcel sold $500 million in stock in July 2002. In 2003, Xcel Energy paid NRG Energy $752 million for the benefit of NRG Energy’s creditors and took a $2 billion write-off.<sup id="cite_ref-:2_8-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:2-8">[8]</a></sup></p><p>On May 14, 2003, NRG Energy filed for chapter 11 bankruptcy. In the reorganization of the company, Excel Energy relinquished their ownership interest and NRG Energy became an independent, public company after bankruptcy. David W. Crane joined NRG as the chief executive in December.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> The reorganized NRG Energy eliminated about $5.2 billion of corporate debt along with $1.2 billion of additional claims by giving equity and cash to unsecured creditors.<sup id="cite_ref-:2_8-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:2-8">[8]</a></sup></p><p>By 2005, NRG Energy was expanding again and added 7,600 MW of domestic capacity to its portfolio. The company acquired Dynegy’s 50% of 1,800 MW of generation in California. They also acquired Texas GenCo in 2006, Reliant Energy in 2009, and Green Mountain Energy in 2010. The company began to focus on domestic markets and retreated from international electricity markets.<sup id="cite_ref-:2_8-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:2-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h4 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="2010s">2010s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=NRG_Energy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: 2010s" data-section="4" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h4>
<p>By 2011, NRG Energy’s generation portfolio had 25,135 MW, with only 1,000 MW outside of the United States. In 2012, they added GenOn Energy for $1.7 billion and in 2013, they added Edison Mission for $2.6 billion. This gave the company 46,000 MW total of generation capacity. In 2013, they also added a demand response company called Energy Curtailment Specialists.<sup id="cite_ref-:2_8-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:2-8">[8]</a></sup></p><p>During December 2015, NRG Energy released David Crane of his duty<sup id="cite_ref-:4_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:4-15">[15]</a></sup> and Mauricio Gutierrez, the Chief Operating Officer<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> at the time, was brought on as Chief Executive and President of NRG Energy.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> That same month, NRG’s shares rose by 63% and closed at $17.90 after it had fallen by 60% in 2015. For the first three months of 2016, the company posted a net income of $47 million in comparison to the net loss of $136 million the first quarter of 2015. Gutierrez stated that paying down debt was a top priority.<sup id="cite_ref-:4_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:4-15">[15]</a></sup></p><p>NRG Energy announced in February 2018 the sale of the company’s stake in NRG Yield, a tax-advantaged renewable energy investment pass-through vehicle, to Global Energy Infrastructure Partners (GIP)<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:5_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:5-19">[19]</a></sup> and the sale of its Louisiana assets to Cleco Corporate Holdings. This included the 1,300 MW Cottonwood natural gas plant (1.3 GW), the 1,500 MW Big Cajun II coal and gas-fired plant (1.5 GW), and three other gas-fired peaking plants.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> In August, NRG Yield changed its name to Clearway Energy and began trading under the new name and ticker symbol (CWEN and CWEN.A) in the New York Stock Exchange on September 17.<sup id="cite_ref-:5_19-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:5-19">[19]</a></sup> These sales are estimated to reduce NRG Energy’s debt by $7 billion and reduce their energy generation portfolio from 50,000 MW (50 GW) to 24,000 MW (24 GW).<sup id="cite_ref-:5_19-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:5-19">[19]</a></sup> In March 2018, the company planned to hasten its transition from an independent power producer model (IPP) to a customer driven integrated power model that favored retail businesses. After GenOn’s exit in 2018,<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> the NRG fleet shrunk to 23 GW but retained 2.9 million retail customers. The new fleet has 11.5 GW of generation (46% natural gas generation, 31% coal generation, and 15% oil generation) in Texas, 9.7 GW of generation in the East, and 2.6 GW of generation in the West.<sup id="cite_ref-:1_10-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-10">[10]</a></sup> In March 2018, NRG Energy acquired XOOM Energy, a retail electricity provider, for $210 million. XOOM Energy served 300,000 customers in the East, which was added to NRG Energy’s retail profile.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p><p>In a partnership with Cypress Creek Renewables, NRG is offering a long-term, fixed price, consumer solar energy project in June 2018. The first customer of the project is Sysco, who has signed a 10-year renewable energy agreement . Cypress Creek Renewables will build, own, and operate three solar gardens in Texas that will have a combined power capacity of 25 MW. NRG Energy will buy the energy and schedule, distribute and manage the energy for Sysco. The solar project is expected to come online by the first quarter of 2019.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> The solar installations are expected to provide 10 percent of Sysco’s electricity nationwide by generating 25 megawatts of power.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> NRG Energy is offering solar renewable program contracts for 7–10 years, with the ability to customize the program. The company also assumes the risk of wholesale pricing fluctuation.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p><p>In July 2018, NRG and GenOn consummated the NRG settlement resulting in NRG Energy paying GenOn Energy $125 million in a net payment during GenOn’s chapter 11 bankruptcy agreement.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> The deal would help GenOn revamp its capital structure and reemerge with new debt instruments.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(2)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Acquisitions">Acquisitions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=NRG_Energy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Acquisitions" data-section="5" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-2 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-2">
<p>When the state of Texas deregulated the electricity market, <a href="/wiki/GenOn_Energy" title="GenOn Energy">Houston Industries</a>, the parent company of <a href="/wiki/Houston_Lighting_%26_Power" title="Houston Lighting &amp; Power">Houston Lighting &amp; Power</a> (HL&amp;P) was broken up.<sup id="cite_ref-Exelonbids_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Exelonbids-28">[28]</a></sup> In 2003 Houston Industries was split into three companies. The power plants went to <a href="/wiki/Texas_Genco" title="Texas Genco">Texas Genco</a>, <a href="/wiki/CenterPoint_Energy" title="CenterPoint Energy">CenterPoint Energy</a> took over the distribution system, and the retail and wholesale electricity business became <a href="/wiki/Reliant_Energy" title="Reliant Energy">Reliant Energy</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Fowler_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fowler-29">[29]</a></sup></p><p>In 2006, NRG Energy bought Texas Genco from a group of private equity firms for roughly $5.9 billion.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> Afterwards, in May 2009, NRG Energy acquired the retail operations of <a href="/wiki/Reliant_Energy" title="Reliant Energy">Reliant Energy</a>. With those two moves, NRG's holdings represented most of the former HL&amp;P and today serve 1.6 million customers in Texas. The retail operations continue to operate under the Reliant Energy name while old Reliant's wholesale operations became <a href="/wiki/RRI_Energy" class="mw-redirect" title="RRI Energy">RRI Energy</a>.
</p><p>Following the acquisition of Reliant, NRG extended its retail footprint with the acquisition of <a href="/wiki/Green_Mountain_Energy" title="Green Mountain Energy">Green Mountain Energy</a> in November 2010.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> In doing so, NRG also became the largest retailer of green power in the nation, providing all of its Green Mountain and many of its Reliant customers with energy derived from 100% renewable resources.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p><p>NRG Energy completed its acquisition of <a href="/wiki/GenOn_Energy" title="GenOn Energy">GenOn Energy</a> in December 2012<sup id="cite_ref-yahoo_genon_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-yahoo_genon-1">[1]</a></sup> for $1.7 billion in stock and cash.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> The GenOn name was retired in the merger, but the combined company retained GenOn's Houston headquarters to coordinate operations.<sup id="cite_ref-yahoo_genon_1-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-yahoo_genon-1">[1]</a></sup> That company, in turn, had been formed out of the merger of RRI Energy and <a href="/wiki/GenOn_Energy_Holdings" title="GenOn Energy Holdings">Mirant Corporation</a> in 2010.
</p><p>In August 2013, NRG acquired Energy Curtailment Specialists, a Buffalo, New York based <a href="/wiki/Demand_response" title="Demand response">Demand response</a> company.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> The terms of the deal were not disclosed.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a href="/wiki/File:CES_2012_-_Goal_Zero_(6791752228).jpg" class="image"><noscript><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8f/CES_2012_-_Goal_Zero_%286791752228%29.jpg/200px-CES_2012_-_Goal_Zero_%286791752228%29.jpg" decoding="async" width="200" height="267" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="1200" data-file-height="1600"></noscript><span class="lazy-image-placeholder" style="width: 200px;height: 267px;" data-src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8f/CES_2012_-_Goal_Zero_%286791752228%29.jpg/200px-CES_2012_-_Goal_Zero_%286791752228%29.jpg" data-alt="" data-width="200" data-height="267" data-class="thumbimage"> </span></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:CES_2012_-_Goal_Zero_(6791752228).jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>a Goal Zero battery, lamp and solar panel</div></div></div>
<p>During March 2014, NRG Energy acquired Roof Diagnostics Solar (RDS), a residential solar power installation company, for an undisclosed amount. RDS headquarters remained in Wall Township, New Jersey while they operated under NRG Residential Solar Solutions.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> That same month, NRG also acquired the retail power business of Dominion Resources Inc., which included Texas-based Cirro Energy and added 600,000 customer accounts to NRG Energy’s retail business. Cirro Energy has continued to operate under the Cirro name.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> In September 2014, NRG acquired Goal Zero, a manufacturer of personal solar power products.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> The following month, NRG Energy acquired the residential solar company Pure Energies Group, which focused on web-based customer acquisition. This provided a simplified solar adoption process as well as a sales channel for Goal Zero.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup></p><p>In March 2018 NRG acquired XOOM Energy, a mainly residential focused, retail energy supplier with 300,000 RCE customers. The sale price was $210 million, which includes working capital and $6 million in transaction costs. It was an all-cash transaction that was funded with $75 Million from excess cash and $135 million debt. NRG Energy stated that the acquisition would serve to balance NRG’s generation portfolio in the east.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> In May 2019, NRG agreed to purchase <a href="/wiki/Stream_Energy" title="Stream Energy">Stream Energy</a> for $300 million. The deal was finalized that August.<sup id="cite_ref-StreamEnergy_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-StreamEnergy-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Naming_rights">Naming rights</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=NRG_Energy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Naming rights" data-section="6" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>NRG Energy holds the naming rights to the <a href="/wiki/NRG_Park" title="NRG Park">NRG Park</a> campus (formerly Reliant Park) in <a href="/wiki/Houston" title="Houston">Houston</a>, <a href="/wiki/Texas" title="Texas">Texas</a>, home to the <a href="/wiki/NRG_Astrodome" class="mw-redirect" title="NRG Astrodome">NRG Astrodome</a>, <a href="/wiki/NRG_Stadium" title="NRG Stadium">NRG Stadium</a>, <a href="/wiki/NRG_Arena" title="NRG Arena">NRG Arena</a> and <a href="/wiki/NRG_Center" title="NRG Center">NRG Center</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> NRG Energy also holds the naming rights for a <a href="/wiki/NRG_station" title="NRG station">NRG Station</a> (formerly AT&amp;T Station), a rapid transit station  in <a href="/wiki/Philadelphia" title="Philadelphia">Philadelphia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pennsylvania" title="Pennsylvania">Pennsylvania</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup></p>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(3)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Products_and_Services">Products and Services</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=NRG_Energy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Products and Services" data-section="7" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-3 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-3">
<p>NRG Energy has multiple products and services, including generation facilities, operations and management services (O&amp;M), retail electricity, commercial industrial backup generators, and an internal data analytics engine called SpaceTag.
</p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span id="Operations_.26_Management_.28O.26M.29_Services"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Operations_&amp;_Management_(O&amp;M)_Services">Operations &amp; Management (O&amp;M) Services</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=NRG_Energy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Operations &amp; Management (O&amp;M) Services" data-section="8" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>In 2014, NRG Energy helped restart Aspen Power plant, the first wood-based biomass plant in Texas. The plant is overseen by InventivEnergy LLC, but NRG operates and maintains the facility, including hiring the management team and operating staff. The plant consumes 525,000 tons of logging debris and municipal wood waste a year and can produce 50 MW of power. Aspen Power uses a stoker-type boiler that is made to reduce the air pollution from the plant. <sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> NRG Energy took over the O&amp;M for two more Texas plants in November 2016. The two facilities, Mustang and Antelope Elk Energy Center, are owned by Golden Spread Electric Cooperative Inc. Mustang is a natural gas turbine plant in Denver City that produces 958 MW of power. Antelope Elk Energy Center is also a natural gas facility located in Abernathy that creates 744 MW of power. In 2016, NRG Energy oversaw O&amp;M for nine other generation facilities and had 9.2 GW portfolio.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> By 2018, NRG Energy maintained 2.4 GW of renewable power through their O&amp;M operations in 17 different states. They sold the renewable O&amp;M business, along with NRG Yield and NRG’s renewable energy development and operations, to Global Infrastructure Partners (GIP) for $1.375 million in cash.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Retail_Electricity">Retail Electricity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=NRG_Energy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Retail Electricity" data-section="9" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>NRG's Retail Power services provide electricity services to more than 3 million customers, mostly in Texas and the Northeast.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup>   
</p><p>In March 2014, NRG Energy acquired Dominion Resources Inc. and Cirro Energy, growing its retail electric customer base by 30 percent. It adds about 600,000 accounts to its existing 2 million residential and commercial customers. Dominion’s retail electricity customers were mainly in Connecticut, Illinois, Massachusetts, Maryland, New York, New Jersey, Ohio, and Pennsylvania; Cirro mainly served residential and business customers in Texas. NRG Energy operates Cirro Energy under Cirro’s name. In 2014, NRG Energy had two other retail electricity services, Reliant Energy and Green Mountain.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup></p><p>By 2018, NRG Energy also added Pennywise Power to its retail electricity portfolio. They picked up a number of customers from Pioneer Energy of Sugar Land and Stat Energy of Richardson, as well. In July, the prices for day-ahead pricing reached $2,172.70 per MW hour, compared to $28 per MW hour of July 2017.<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> 
</p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Commercial_Industrial_Backup_Generators">Commercial Industrial Backup Generators</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=NRG_Energy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Commercial Industrial Backup Generators" data-section="10" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>NRG Energy partnered with Cummins in December 2017 to offer backup generators to run as part of an asset backed demand response system. The Cummins natural-gas-fired genset would be used when the grid is down, and it could shave 10 to 15 percent off energy bills by running it as part of an aggregated fleet of an “estimated hundreds of megawatts capacity." <sup id="cite_ref-:3_49-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:3-49">[49]</a></sup> The backup generators help gas stations, supermarkets, ATMs, and other everyday life necessities during power outages from hurricanes or other natural disasters.<sup id="cite_ref-:3_49-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:3-49">[49]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="SpaceTag">SpaceTag</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=NRG_Energy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: SpaceTag" data-section="11" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>In April 2017, NRG Energy publicly mentioned its in-house analytics platform SpaceTag. The analytics engine found the best combination of distributed resources for every building in a given territory as well as showing what it was worth to each customer. NRG tested SpaceTag to gather “60 megawatts of flexible clean energy capacity for key parts of SCE’s [Southern California Edison] Orange County and Los Angeles power grid." <sup id="cite_ref-:6_50-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:6-50">[50]</a></sup> The platform, developed by NRG’s Station A research team in San Francisco, was originally intended to help with customer acquisition but has since evolved. SpaceTag used data about the physical attributes of a building, how it was used, and existing energy data to create an energy performance portfolio and then assessed its location in the power grid. It also used data from the distributed energy equipment, including its capital cost, associated costs of providing generation, the demand-reduction attributes over time, and the best mix of technology on the building and portfolio level. After SpaceTag is deployed, it can then monitor operations and performance of the building. NRG Energy mapped 24 different utility territories outside of SCE.<sup id="cite_ref-:6_50-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:6-50">[50]</a></sup></p><p>By 2018, NRG Energy no longer owned SpaceTag as Station A became a standalone company. NRG Energy owns a minority stake in the company and is a customer of Station A.<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup>   
</p>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(4)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Wholesale_generation">Wholesale generation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=NRG_Energy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Wholesale generation" data-section="12" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-4 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-4">
<p>After the GenOn merger in 2012, NRG had 47,000 MW of total generation capacity, enough to power approximately 40 million homes.<sup id="cite_ref-combo_52-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-combo-52">[52]</a></sup> Its nearly 100 power plants were located in 18 states in the Northeast, Chicago area, Gulf Coast, Southwest, Nevada, and California.<sup id="cite_ref-combo_52-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-combo-52">[52]</a></sup>  Generation facilities include mostly <a href="/wiki/Fossil_fuel_power_plant" class="mw-redirect" title="Fossil fuel power plant">fossil fuel power plants</a> powered by natural gas, oil, and coal; plus four <a href="/wiki/Wind_farm" title="Wind farm">wind farms</a> (in Texas) and six <a href="/wiki/Solar_farm" class="mw-redirect" title="Solar farm">solar farms</a> (in California, Arizona, and New Mexico).<sup id="cite_ref-projectlist_53-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-projectlist-53">[53]</a></sup>  NRG also has a 44% ownership stake in the <a href="/wiki/South_Texas_Nuclear_Generating_Station" title="South Texas Nuclear Generating Station">South Texas Nuclear Generating Station</a> and a 37.5% stake in a coal power plant in <a href="/wiki/Gladstone,_Queensland" title="Gladstone, Queensland">Gladstone, Queensland</a>, Australia.<sup id="cite_ref-projectlist_53-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-projectlist-53">[53]</a></sup>  Some facilities use <a href="/wiki/Cogeneration" title="Cogeneration">cogeneration</a> and the company also owns 28 MW of solar <a href="/wiki/Distributed_generation" title="Distributed generation">distributed generation</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-projectlist_53-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-projectlist-53">[53]</a></sup></p><p>NRG Energy owns the Indian River Power Plant near Millsboro, Delaware. The plant is a 784 MW facility that produces electricity from the combustion of coal.<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup></p><p>During 2018, NRG Energy sold four of their wholesale generation platforms: NRG Yield, Operations &amp; Management (O&amp;M) business, development business, and NRG’s South Central business. <a href="/wiki/Global_Infrastructure_Partners" title="Global Infrastructure Partners">Global Infrastructure Partners</a> (GIP) bought NRG Yield, the O&amp;M business, and the development business for $1.375 billion in cash proceeds. NRG Yield had a diverse portfolio of energy generation including wind, solar, and natural gas, with a total operating capacity of 5,100 MW. The O&amp;M platform managed 2,400 MW of renewable energy in 17 different states, and the development business had 6,400 MW of renewable generation opportunities in the project pipeline. GIP and NRG Yield purchased the Carlsbad Energy Center natural gas project (527 MW) and the Buckthorn Solar project (154 MW) from NRG Energy. <a href="/wiki/Cleco_Holdings" title="Cleco Holdings">Cleco Corporate Holdings</a> purchased NRG’s South Central business for $1 billion in price and cash proceeds. The South Central business had 3,500 MW generation capacity, including the natural gas facilities Bayou Cove plant (225 MW), Sterlington plant (176 MW), and the Cottonwood plant (1,263,000 MW). NRG Energy also sold 1,891 MW of coal-fired capacity to Cleco from their Big Cajun-I and Big Cajun-II plants.<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup> In July 2018, NRG Energy released GenOn and paid $125 million in net payment during GenOn’s bankruptcy settlement.<sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup> In 2018, NRG Energy reduced its generation capacity to 23,000 MW. They also reduced their power plants from 100 to 40, but they retained 2.9 million retail customers.<sup id="cite_ref-:1_10-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(5)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Green_energy_initiatives">Green energy initiatives</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=NRG_Energy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Green energy initiatives" data-section="13" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-5 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-5">
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Nissan_LEAF_got_thirsty.jpg" class="image"><noscript><img alt="Nissan LEAF charging on an EVgo charger" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/93/Nissan_LEAF_got_thirsty.jpg/220px-Nissan_LEAF_got_thirsty.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="4320" data-file-height="2880"></noscript><span class="lazy-image-placeholder" style="width: 220px;height: 147px;" data-src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/93/Nissan_LEAF_got_thirsty.jpg/220px-Nissan_LEAF_got_thirsty.jpg" data-alt="Nissan LEAF charging on an EVgo charger" data-width="220" data-height="147" data-class="thumbimage"> </span></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Nissan_LEAF_got_thirsty.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Nissan_Leaf" title="Nissan Leaf">Nissan LEAF</a> recharging from an EVgo network charging station in Houston, Texas</div></div></div>
<p>Beginning in 2009, NRG began an initiative to become a <a href="/wiki/Green_energy" class="mw-redirect" title="Green energy">green energy</a> producer in the United States and started investing money in <a href="/wiki/Clean_energy" class="mw-redirect" title="Clean energy">clean energy</a> projects.<sup id="cite_ref-nrgenergy.com_57-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nrgenergy.com-57">[57]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup> They include onshore and offshore wind power, <a href="/wiki/Solar_thermal_energy" title="Solar thermal energy">solar thermal energy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Photovoltaic" class="mw-redirect" title="Photovoltaic">photovoltaic</a>, and distributed solar power facilities, and repowering of some of their traditional coal plants with <a href="/wiki/Biomass" title="Biomass">biomass</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-nrgenergy.com_57-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nrgenergy.com-57">[57]</a></sup> In late 2010, NRG launched the "EVgo" network, the first completely private public car <a href="/wiki/Charging_station" title="Charging station">charging station</a> network for electric power vehicles.<sup id="cite_ref-59" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup> NRG Energy sold the EVgo charging network to Vision Ridge Partners, a Colorado-based sustainable<s>-</s>energy investment firm, in 2016 for an undisclosed amount.<sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup></p><p>Green Mountain Energy, one of NRG Energy's retail subsidiaries, signed a two-year agreement beginning in January 2011 to provide <a href="/wiki/100%25_renewable_energy" title="100% renewable energy">100% renewable energy</a> for the <a href="/wiki/Empire_State_Building" title="Empire State Building">Empire State Building</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-61" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-62" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup></p><p>In 2012, NRG Energy partnered with the DLR Group to design an open-air, solar system for Patriot Place in Massachusetts. The solar array produces an estimated 30% of the power for Patriot Place and provides renewable energy education for visitors at kiosks, electric vehicle charging stations, and off-grid solar “Helios” at the main entrance.<sup id="cite_ref-63" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup> The solar panel canopies also offer weather protection and reduce carbon emissions by an estimated 800 metric tons a year.<sup id="cite_ref-64" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup></p><p>In 2013, NRG Energy helped the <a href="/wiki/San_Francisco_49ers" title="San Francisco 49ers">San Francisco 49ers</a> Stadium gain LEED certification by installing three solar array-covered bridges, a solar canopy over the green roof on the suite tower, and solar panels over the 49ers training center. The solar arrays have a peak capacity of 400 kW and provide enough power during the year to offset the cost of power consumed at 49ers home games.<sup id="cite_ref-65" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup> NRG Energy provided sustainable energy for <a href="/wiki/Super_Bowl_LI" title="Super Bowl LI">Super Bowl LI</a> in 2017 at <a href="/wiki/NRG_Stadium" title="NRG Stadium">NRG Stadium</a>. The stadium received power from 600 solar panels and used only LEDs to light up the field, using 60% less energy than previously. NRG Energy also agreed to provide renewable energy credits for any venue associated with the Super Bowl, such as the <a href="/wiki/George_R._Brown_Convention_Center" title="George R. Brown Convention Center">George R. Brown Convention Center</a> and the hotels where the teams were staying.<sup id="cite_ref-66" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup> NRG Energy installed 11,000 solar panels and 14 wind turbines at the Eagles’ <a href="/wiki/Lincoln_Financial_Field" title="Lincoln Financial Field">Lincoln Financial Field</a> stadium. These renewable energy installations made the stadium the largest solar power plant in the NFL. The solar power plant produces 40% of the Eagles’ own energy and feed the energy back into the grid, which they buy back from NRG at a discounted rate. The <a href="/wiki/Philadelphia_Eagles" title="Philadelphia Eagles">Eagles</a> and NRG Energy are in a multi-year corporate sponsorship agreement that helps the Eagles regulate their energy costs.<sup id="cite_ref-67" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup></p><p>New York State Assemblywoman <a href="/wiki/Aravella_Simotas" title="Aravella Simotas">Aravella Simotas</a> has been chair of a coalition to support the utility in their plan to replace its <a href="/wiki/Power_plant" class="mw-redirect" title="Power plant">power plant</a> in Astoria with a newer generator.<sup id="cite_ref-NRG_68-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NRG-68">[68]</a></sup> The company stated its intention in 2012 to replace 31 older oil generators with new gas generators that will increase the <a href="/wiki/Megawatt" class="mw-redirect" title="Megawatt">megawatts</a> of power while reducing emissions.<sup id="cite_ref-NRG_68-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NRG-68">[68]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-repowering_69-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-repowering-69">[69]</a></sup> As of 2018, of the 19 Astoria facilities listed in the 2018 NYISO Gold Book as being owned by NRG, 7 of the facilities are on the deactivated list (at a total of 140 MW of capacity rights), and 12 of the facilities (at a total of 558 MW in nameplate capacity) have each consistently produced less than 15 <a href="/wiki/Watt" title="Watt">GWh</a> a year since 2011. This is equivalent to running at full capacity for less than 4% of the year. These 12 units still collect annual revenues from the <a href="/wiki/New_York_energy_law#NYISO" title="New York energy law">NYISO's</a> capacity market for not producing energy. For example, at 6.40 ($/kW – Month), the 12 actively listed facilities would produce an annual capacity market revenue of $42.8 million for NRG. It is unclear if the 7 deactivated units still collect capacity market revenues.<sup id="cite_ref-70" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-71" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-72" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-72">[72]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-73" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-73">[73]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-74" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-74">[74]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-75" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-75">[75]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-76" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-76">[76]</a></sup> In July 2017, NRG filed a request with the New York State Public Service Commission to avoid Article 10 siting procedures for a proposed turbine replacement project which would represent a total proposed capacity of 579 MW. The turbine upgrades listed in the filing are new simple-cycle turbines. The filing states that since the proposed capacity is not 25 MW greater than the existing facility, Article 10 regulation is not required. As of November 2018, no ruling has been issued by the NYSPSC.<sup id="cite_ref-77" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-77">[77]</a></sup></p><p><a href="/wiki/JPMorgan_Chase" title="JPMorgan Chase">JPMorgan Chase</a> announced their plans in 2017 to power all their facilities in Texas through 100% renewable power by 2020. By the end of 2017, 75% of JPMorgan Chase’s facilities ran on wind energy provided through NRG Energy’s Buckthorn wind farm in <a href="/wiki/Erath_County,_Texas" title="Erath County, Texas">Erath County</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-78" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-78">[78]</a></sup> In 2018, NRG Energy was recognized for supporting the environment in the <a href="/wiki/Hudson_Valley" title="Hudson Valley">Hudson Valley</a> through NRG Community Solar. The community solar company builds <a href="/wiki/Community_solar_farm" title="Community solar farm">solar farms</a> with industrial grade solar panels that produces and delivers clean energy directly to the power grid. This allows residents who cannot purchase rooftop solar to invest in solar energy generation and receive solar credits on their utility bills. The solar farms are maintained year-round, creating local jobs in the Hudson Valley.<sup id="cite_ref-79" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-79">[79]</a></sup> In January 2019, NRG Community Solar became Clearway Community Solar under the parent company Clearway Energy Group.<sup id="cite_ref-80" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-80">[80]</a></sup></p>
