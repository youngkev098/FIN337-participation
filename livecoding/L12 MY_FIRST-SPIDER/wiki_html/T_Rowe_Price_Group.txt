</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(1)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Business_Philosophy">Business Philosophy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=T._Rowe_Price&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Business Philosophy" data-section="1" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-1 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-1">
<p>Thomas Rowe Price Jr. started in finance in the 1920s as an entry-level researcher and <a href="/wiki/Account_manager" title="Account manager">account manager</a> at Baltimore-area <a href="/wiki/Brokerage_firm" class="mw-redirect" title="Brokerage firm">brokerages</a>, but disliked the operating models of sales-oriented firms at the time. When he founded T. Rowe Price &amp; Associates in 1937, his firm diverged from the norm in three major ways: charging fees based on <a href="/wiki/Assets_under_management" title="Assets under management">assets under management</a> rather than sales volume, <a href="/wiki/Active_management" title="Active management">actively managing</a> his clients' accounts strictly as a <a href="/wiki/Fiduciary" title="Fiduciary">fiduciary</a>, and investing in <a href="/wiki/Growth_stocks" class="mw-redirect" title="Growth stocks">growth stocks</a> instead of <a href="/wiki/Value_stocks" class="mw-redirect" title="Value stocks">value stocks</a>. He became well known as the "father of growth investing" and was nicknamed the "Sage of Baltimore" by <i><a href="/wiki/Forbes" title="Forbes">Forbes</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Investopedia_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Investopedia-4">[4]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(2)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=T._Rowe_Price&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History" data-section="2" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-2 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-2">
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="1937-1986">1937-1986</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=T._Rowe_Price&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: 1937-1986" data-section="3" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>Thomas Rowe Price, Jr. founded T. Rowe Price &amp; Associates in Baltimore in 1937. The firm was originally headquartered at <a href="/wiki/Bank_of_America_Building_(Baltimore)" title="Bank of America Building (Baltimore)">10 Light Street</a> and staffed by a small pool of associates, many of whom left <a href="/wiki/Legg_Mason" title="Legg Mason">Legg Mason</a>'s precursor, MacKubin, Legg and Co. along with Mr. Price.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Initially a very small firm focused on <a href="/wiki/Wealth_management" title="Wealth management">wealth management</a> and private investing accounts for Baltimore-area families, the company struggled through the financial turbulence of the <a href="/wiki/Great_Depression" title="Great Depression">Great Depression</a> and <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a> before gaining solid footing at the end of the 1940s. By 1950, its clientele grew too large for the staff to manage accounts individually, so the firm <a href="/wiki/Incorporation_(business)" title="Incorporation (business)">incorporated</a> and launched its first mutual fund, the T. Rowe Price Growth Stock Fund.<sup id="cite_ref-Investopedia_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Investopedia-4">[4]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Encyclopedia_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Encyclopedia-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:100_East_Pratt_Street.jpg" class="image"><noscript><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8d/100_East_Pratt_Street.jpg/220px-100_East_Pratt_Street.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="329" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="1555" data-file-height="2323"></noscript><span class="lazy-image-placeholder" style="width: 220px;height: 329px;" data-src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8d/100_East_Pratt_Street.jpg/220px-100_East_Pratt_Street.jpg" data-alt="" data-width="220" data-height="329" data-class="thumbimage"> </span></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:100_East_Pratt_Street.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>100 East Pratt Street</div></div></div>
<p>Gaining traction in Baltimore and along the U.S. eastern seaboard, the firm continued a steady expansion of clientele, staff, and geographic reach. By 1960, Mr. Price felt the need to open a second fund, named the New Horizons Fund, focused on growth investment opportunities, and especially technological firms like <a href="/wiki/Xerox" title="Xerox">Xerox</a>, <a href="/wiki/IBM" title="IBM">IBM</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Boeing" title="Boeing">Boeing</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Encyclopedia_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Encyclopedia-8">[8]</a></sup> In need of more room, headquarters were moved in 1962 to the new <a href="/wiki/One_Charles_Center" title="One Charles Center">One Charles Center</a> building designed by <a href="/wiki/Ludwig_Mies_van_der_Rohe" title="Ludwig Mies van der Rohe">Ludwig Mies van der Rohe</a> nearby in downtown Baltimore.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> At this same juncture, Mr. Price began to prepare for retirement, resigning as president of the firm in 1963, delegating some responsibilities, and selling his shares in the company.<sup id="cite_ref-Encyclopedia_8-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Encyclopedia-8">[8]</a></sup> Despite this, Mr. Price maintained an active presence in the firm for several years, and urged the opening of the New Era Fund in 1969 as a response to the rapid <a href="/wiki/Inflation" title="Inflation">inflation</a> he predicted would <a href="/wiki/1973%E2%80%9375_recession" class="mw-redirect" title="1973–75 recession">dominate the 1970s</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> In 1971, the year Mr. Price completely retired, T. Rowe Price opened its <a href="/wiki/Fixed_income" title="Fixed income">Fixed Income Division</a>, and began to modernize and diversify its operations.
</p><p>In the 1970s and early 1980s, T. Rowe Price kicked off more assertive growth than before, moving to its current location at <a href="/wiki/100_East_Pratt_Street" title="100 East Pratt Street">100 East Pratt Street</a> and opening its first international office. In 1979, T. Rowe Price launched a <a href="/wiki/Joint_venture" title="Joint venture">joint venture</a> with British asset manager <a href="/wiki/Robert_Fleming_%26_Co." title="Robert Fleming &amp; Co.">Robert Fleming &amp; Co.</a> named Rowe Price-Fleming International. The venture, which managed $39 billion at its height in 2000, allowed T. Rowe Price to offer a broader range of services and expertise internationally.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="1986-2010">1986-2010</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=T._Rowe_Price&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: 1986-2010" data-section="4" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>T. Rowe Price held its <a href="/wiki/Initial_public_offering" title="Initial public offering">initial public offering</a>, valued at nearly $200 million, in 1986.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Shortly thereafter, the firm began establishing larger office complexes in the U.S. and research offices around the world, beginning with a <a href="/wiki/Jardine_House" title="Jardine House">Hong Kong office</a> in 1987. Retirement Plan Services were launched in the 1990s alongside additional new services and funds, including mutual funds acquired from other companies such as <a href="/wiki/USF%26G" title="USF&amp;G">USF&amp;G</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> This momentum, and the firm reaching $100 billion assets under management, pushed T. Rowe Price to create an asset management partnership with <a href="/wiki/Sumitomo_Bank" class="mw-redirect" title="Sumitomo Bank">Sumitomo Bank</a> and <a href="/wiki/Daiwa_Securities" class="mw-redirect" title="Daiwa Securities">Daiwa Securities</a> in Tokyo in 1999, and to purchase 100% interest of the London-based Rowe Price-Fleming International, which was renamed T. Rowe Price International.<sup id="cite_ref-Encyclopedia_8-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Encyclopedia-8">[8]</a></sup> Also in 1999, T. Rowe Price was added to the <a href="/wiki/S%26P_500_Index" title="S&amp;P 500 Index">S&amp;P 500 Index</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-S&amp;P_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-S&amp;P-14">[14]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p><p>On the strength of its ethic of <a href="/wiki/Risk_aversion" title="Risk aversion">risk aversion</a>, fundamental research, and active management approach, T. Rowe Price somewhat predicted and largely avoided the <a href="/wiki/Dot-com_bubble" title="Dot-com bubble">dot-com bubble</a> of 2000. Its ethic of caution was so different from the norm at the time that <i>The <a href="/wiki/Wall_Street_Journal" class="mw-redirect" title="Wall Street Journal">Wall Street Journal</a></i> expressed surprise at the firm's moderation with then-profitable technology stocks just a week before the markets began to crash in March 2000.<sup id="cite_ref-Dotcom_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dotcom-16">[16]</a></sup> In 2001, the company launched T. Rowe Price Funds SICAV, <a href="/wiki/Domicile_(law)" title="Domicile (law)">domiciled</a> in Luxembourg, for non-U.S. institutional investors and <a href="/wiki/Financial_intermediary" title="Financial intermediary">financial intermediaries</a>. Two years later it created <a href="/wiki/Target_date_fund" title="Target date fund">target-date retirement funds</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> In 2010, T. Rowe Price bought a significant interest in <a href="/wiki/Unit_Trust_of_India" class="mw-redirect" title="Unit Trust of India">Unit Trust of India</a>, India's oldest mutual fund company and one of its five largest.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> Since 2000, T. Rowe Price has opened global offices in locations ranging from Madrid and Dubai to Stockholm and Sydney.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="2010-present">2010-present</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=T._Rowe_Price&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: 2010-present" data-section="5" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>As of 2019, T. Rowe Price has continued to focus on <a href="/wiki/Active_management" title="Active management">active management</a> rather than <a href="/wiki/Passive_management" title="Passive management">passive management</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-3">[3]</a></sup> In the decade from 2010 to 2019, T. Rowe Price was able to increase assets under management from $400 billion to over $1 trillion while major competitors focused on active management such as Franklin Templeton experienced net outflows.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
