</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(1)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Waters_Corporation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History" data-section="1" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-1 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-1">
<p>The business was started by James (Jim) Logan Waters as Waters Associates in an office in the basement of a police station in <a href="/wiki/Framingham,_Massachusetts" title="Framingham, Massachusetts">Framingham, Massachusetts</a>, in 1958.<sup id="cite_ref-McDonald_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-McDonald-3">[3]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Ettre_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ettre-4">[4]</a></sup></p><p>Waters enrolled in the <a href="/wiki/V-12_Navy_College_Training_Program" title="V-12 Navy College Training Program">V-12 Navy College Training Program</a>, an officer training program, and graduated from <a href="/wiki/Columbia_University" title="Columbia University">Columbia University</a> as an ensign with a B.S. degree in Physics in 1946. After stints as a university math teacher, Naval officer, project engineer, and entrepreneur, Waters formed Waters Associates in 1958. The fledgling firm’s first offices were in the rented basement of the Framingham, Mass. police station.
During these years, Waters Associates was what is now referred to as a research boutique. Companies would contract Waters and his five employees to build one-of-a-kind instruments for various purposes. Early products included a <a href="/wiki/Boiler_feedwater" title="Boiler feedwater">boiler feedwater</a> flame <a href="/wiki/Photometer" title="Photometer">photometer</a>, a balloon <a href="/wiki/Hydrometer" title="Hydrometer">hydrometer</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Nerve_gas" class="mw-redirect" title="Nerve gas">nerve gas</a> detector, a lab <a href="/wiki/Refractometer" title="Refractometer">refractometer</a> and process control refractometers. While from its start the company had been self-financed, with proceeds from an earlier business sale, Waters opened Waters Associates to external ownership in 1962.
</p><p>The company’s first major break came when <a href="/wiki/Dow_Chemical" class="mw-redirect" title="Dow Chemical">Dow Chemical</a> bought one of Waters’ first <a href="/wiki/Gel_permeation_chromatography" title="Gel permeation chromatography">gel permeation chromatography</a> instruments, Dow Chemical made an additional investment of $400,000. By 1979, Dow Chemical had attained nearly 25 percent ownership in Waters.
</p><p>In 1967, the ALC 100, the first Waters LC system, was brought to market. According to Leslie S. Ettre in a review about Jim Waters, the LC system was formally introduced at the 1968 Pittsburgh Conference. It was a benchtop system equipped with a Milton Roy pump, syringe injection, and two detectors: a Waters differential refractometer and a UV detector from the Laboratory Data Control (LDC) Co.
</p><p>In 1969, Dimitri D’Arbeloff, then president of <a href="/wiki/Millipore_Corporation" class="mw-redirect" title="Millipore Corporation">Millipore Corporation</a>, joined the corporation's board of directors. Millipore’s venture capital subsidiary made a $600,000 equity investment in Waters.
</p><p>Waters next big break came in 1972 when Dr. Helmut Hamberger, chief post-doc for Nobel Laureate Dr. Robert Woodward of Harvard University, sought Jim Waters’ help to the first synthesis of vitamin B<sub>12</sub>. Dr. Hamberger wanted to purify the positional isomers, which were needed to give him the right compound for the final stages of the synthesis.  Working with Dr. Hamberger, the pair took two days to develop a separation, five more days to obtain larger columns to scale up the separation, and three more days to prep his material. In the end, the two had isolated and purified 200 mg of the precursor compound.<sup id="cite_ref-McDonald_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-McDonald-3">[3]</a></sup></p><p>In 1972, Waters Associates appointed Frank Zenie president. A year later, headquarters moved from Framingham to a semi-rural 26-acre (110,000 m<sup>2</sup>) site in <a href="/wiki/Milford,_Massachusetts" title="Milford, Massachusetts">Milford, Massachusetts</a>. Waters became chairman, and continued in that role until the company merged with Millipore in 1980, and was rechristened the Waters Chromatograph Division.
</p><p>The sought-for synergies between the two companies never materialized, however. And, in 1993, Waters returned to independence under the leadership of Chairman, President, and Chief Executive Officer Douglas A. Berthiaume. In 1997 Waters entered mass spectrometry market with acquisition of Micromass for $176 million.
On 11 June 2012, Waters India celebrated its silver jubilee anniversary in India.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>

<p>A majority of the company's revenue is derived from the life science market, a market comprising <a href="/wiki/Drug_discovery" title="Drug discovery">drug discovery</a>, drug development, quality control, and the emerging sciences of <a href="/wiki/Genomics" title="Genomics">genomics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Proteomics" title="Proteomics">proteomics</a> and <a href="/wiki/Biopharmaceuticals" class="mw-redirect" title="Biopharmaceuticals">biopharmaceuticals</a>. Waters products are also sold into the food and beverage, <a href="/wiki/Natural_environment" title="Natural environment">environmental</a>, fine chemical, personal care product, university, government, <a href="/wiki/Semiconductor" title="Semiconductor">semiconductor</a>, clinical and plastics markets sectors.
