</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(1)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Broadridge_Financial_Solutions&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History" data-section="1" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-1 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-1">
<p>Legislation passed in the mid-1970s in the United States mandates two processes affecting <a href="/wiki/Security_(finance)" title="Security (finance)">securities</a> and their transfer: <a href="/wiki/Settlement_(finance)#Immobilisation" title="Settlement (finance)">immobilisation</a> and later, <a href="/wiki/Dematerialization_(securities)" title="Dematerialization (securities)">dematerialisation</a> - the first requires paper securities to be kept in one central location and their ownership to be changed through transactions, rather than the piece of paper itself being transferred, and the second allows ownership to be simply recorded electronically without any paper certificate being created at all. These changes were made to allow more trades to happen, and for them to happen faster, in response to demand from the markets.  These changes also had the effect of separating companies from their shareholders, and putting intermediaries between them.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>1–2</span></sup></p><p>Immobilization led to greater need for such intermediaries and created a new situation where shareholders voted by <a href="/wiki/Proxy_voting#Corporate_settings" title="Proxy voting">proxy</a> instead of directly, and this in turn led a new industry to manage the shareholder voting process.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>1–2,48</span></sup>  Prior to this industry transformation, banks and brokers typically maintained in-house proxy departments for handling these processes.<sup id="cite_ref-:2_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:2-5">[5]</a></sup>  Even after the move to electronic certificates eliminated the need for intermediaries, they have persisted.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>1–2</span></sup></p><p>The proxy voting and shareholder communications services industry was, by the mid-1990s, dominated by <a href="/wiki/Automatic_Data_Processing" class="mw-redirect" title="Automatic Data Processing">Automatic Data Processing</a> (ADP).<sup id="cite_ref-:0_4-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>48</span></sup>  At the end of March, 2007, ADP spun-out the entirety of their shareholder communications activities, resulting in the formation of Broadridge Financial Solutions.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_4-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>27</span></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:2_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:2-5">[5]</a></sup></p><p>In the wake of the <a href="/wiki/Financial_crisis_of_2007%E2%80%932008" class="mw-redirect" title="Financial crisis of 2007–2008">financial crisis of 2007–2008</a>, Congress evaluated many aspects of <a href="/wiki/Corporate_governance" title="Corporate governance">corporate governance</a>, including shareholder communications and proxy voting.<sup id="cite_ref-2010Cong_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2010Cong-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>1–2</span></sup> In 2010 testimony by a vice president of <a href="/wiki/NERA_Economic_Consulting" title="NERA Economic Consulting">NERA Economic Consulting</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jonathan_R._Macey" title="Jonathan R. Macey">Jonathan R. Macey</a> noted with alarm the near <a href="/wiki/Monopoly" title="Monopoly">monopoly</a> position of Broadridge in handling proxy voting.<sup id="cite_ref-2010Cong_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2010Cong-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>249</span></sup>  In 2010, the company processed about 350 billion shares for the companies it provided services for.<sup id="cite_ref-:1_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>2</span></sup>  By 2013 the company had retained its predominant position in the proxy processing market.<sup id="cite_ref-:1_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>17</span></sup></p><p>In the summer of 2016, Broadridge acquired the North America Customer Communications (NACC) unit of <a href="/wiki/DST_Systems" title="DST Systems">DST Systems</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Kansas_City,_Missouri" title="Kansas City, Missouri">Kansas City</a>-based business services provider, which provided the company with addressing information for about 75% of all public company shareholders in the United States and Canada.<sup id="cite_ref-Madore16_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Madore16-1">[1]</a></sup>  Later in 2016, Broadridge bought M&amp;O Systems, a small <a href="/wiki/Manhattan" title="Manhattan">Manhattan</a>-based financial services company.<sup id="cite_ref-Madore16_1-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Madore16-1">[1]</a></sup> In 2016, the company acquired Spence Johnson, an institutional financial flow data intelligence firm co-founded by CEO at the time, Magnus Spence.<sup id="cite_ref-Baker17_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Baker17-8">[8]</a></sup> This was a strategic investment by Broadridge, allowing for the combination of Broadridge's retail data and analytics with Spence Johnson's data and analytics focused on money flows between firms, two distinct intelligence dimensions.<sup id="cite_ref-Baker17_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Baker17-8">[8]</a></sup>. In November 2019, Broadridge announced their acquisition of ClearStructure Financial Technology, a global provider of portfolio management solutions for the private debt markets.<sup id="cite_ref-BroadridgePressRelease_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BroadridgePressRelease-9">[9]</a></sup> In March 2020, Broadridge completed its acquisition of FundsLibrary, a leader in fund document and data dissemination in the European market.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
