<p>The <b>Campbell Soup Company</b>, also known as just <b>Campbell's</b>, is an American <a href="/wiki/Processed_food" class="mw-redirect" title="Processed food">processed food</a> and snack company.  As the name implies, the company is most closely associated with its flagship <a href="/wiki/Canned_soup" class="mw-redirect" title="Canned soup">canned soup</a> products, however it has through <a href="/wiki/Mergers_and_acquisitions" title="Mergers and acquisitions">mergers and acquisitions</a> grown to become one of the largest processed food companies in the U.S., with a wide variety of products under its flagship Campbell's brand as well as such well-known brands as <a href="/wiki/Pepperidge_Farm" title="Pepperidge Farm">Pepperidge Farm</a>, <a href="/wiki/Snyder%27s_of_Hanover" title="Snyder's of Hanover">Snyder's of Hanover</a>, <a href="/wiki/V8_(beverage)" title="V8 (beverage)">V8</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Swanson" title="Swanson">Swanson</a>.  Under these brands and many others, Campbell's produces not only soups and <a href="/wiki/Broth" title="Broth">broths</a>, but other canned meals such as <a href="/wiki/SpaghettiOs" title="SpaghettiOs">SpaghettiOs</a>, baked goods such as <a href="/wiki/Cookie" title="Cookie">cookies</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cracker_(food)" title="Cracker (food)">crackers</a>, beverages, and salty snacks such as <a href="/wiki/Pretzel" title="Pretzel">pretzels</a> and <a href="/wiki/Potato_chips" class="mw-redirect" title="Potato chips">potato chips</a>. </section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(1)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Campbell_Soup_Company&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History" data-section="1" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-1 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-1">
<p>The company was started in 1869 by <a href="/wiki/Joseph_A._Campbell" title="Joseph A. Campbell">Joseph A. Campbell</a>, a fruit merchant from <a href="/wiki/Bridgeton,_New_Jersey" title="Bridgeton, New Jersey">Bridgeton</a>, New Jersey, and <a href="/wiki/Abraham_Anderson" title="Abraham Anderson">Abraham Anderson</a>, an <a href="/wiki/Icebox" title="Icebox">icebox</a> manufacturer from <a href="/wiki/South_Jersey" title="South Jersey">South Jersey</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-arcadia_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-arcadia-4">[4]</a></sup> They produced canned <a href="/wiki/Tomato" title="Tomato">tomatoes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vegetable" title="Vegetable">vegetables</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fruit_preserves" title="Fruit preserves">jellies</a>, soups, <a href="/wiki/Condiment" title="Condiment">condiments</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Ground_meat" title="Ground meat">minced meats</a>.
</p><p>In 1876, Anderson left the partnership and the company became the "Joseph A. Campbell Preserve Company".<sup id="cite_ref-gilman_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gilman-5">[5]</a></sup> Anderson's son, Campbell Speelman, split paths with his father and continued to work at Campbell's as a creative director, originally designing the <a href="/wiki/Campbell%27s_Soup_Cans" title="Campbell's Soup Cans">Campbell's Soup Cans</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p><p>Campbell reorganized into "Joseph Campbell &amp; Co." in 1896. In 1897, <a href="/wiki/John_T._Dorrance" class="mw-redirect" title="John T. Dorrance">John T. Dorrance</a>, a nephew of the general manager Arthur Dorrance, began working for the company at a wage of $7.50 a week.<sup id="cite_ref-gilman_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gilman-5">[5]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Dorrance, a chemist with degrees from <a href="/wiki/MIT" class="mw-redirect" title="MIT">MIT</a> and <a href="/wiki/G%C3%B6ttingen_University" class="mw-redirect" title="Göttingen University">Göttingen University</a>, <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a>, developed a commercially viable method for condensing soup by halving the quantity of its heaviest ingredient: <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">water</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-arcadia_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-arcadia-4">[4]</a></sup> He went on to become president of the company from 1914 to 1930, eventually buying out the Campbell family.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p><p>In 1898, Herberton Williams, a Campbell's executive, convinced the company to adopt a <a href="/wiki/Carnelian_(color)" title="Carnelian (color)">carnelian red</a> and bright white color scheme, because he was taken by the crisp <a href="/wiki/Carnelian" title="Carnelian">carnelian</a> red color of the <a href="/wiki/Cornell_University" title="Cornell University">Cornell University</a> <a href="/wiki/Football_(American)" class="mw-redirect" title="Football (American)">football</a> team's uniforms.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> To this day, the layout of the can, with its red and white design and the metallic bronze medal seal from the <a href="/wiki/Exposition_Universelle_(1900)" title="Exposition Universelle (1900)">1900 Paris Exhibition</a>, has changed very little, with the exception of the French phrase on the top of the bronze seal that said "Exposition-Universelle-Internationale" which was changed to the English name of the exhibition as "Paris International Exposition".
</p><p>Campbell Soup became one of the largest food companies in the world under the leadership of <a href="/wiki/William_Beverly_Murphy" title="William Beverly Murphy">William Beverly Murphy</a>. He was elected executive vice president of Campbell Soup in 1949 and was President and <a href="/wiki/CEO" class="mw-redirect" title="CEO">CEO</a> from 1953 to 1972. While at Campbell's Soup Company, he took the corporation public and increased its brand portfolio to include Pepperidge Farm's breads, cookies, and crackers, Franco-American's gravies and pastas, V8 vegetable juices, Swanson broths, and Godiva's chocolates. <a href="/wiki/David_Johnson_(company_director)" title="David Johnson (company director)">David Johnson</a> was President and CEO from 1990 until 1997.
</p><p>Campbell Soup has invested heavily in <a href="/wiki/Advertising" title="Advertising">advertising</a> since its inception, and many of its promotional campaigns have proven value in the <a href="/wiki/Americana" title="Americana">Americana</a> collectible advertising market. Perhaps best known are the "<a href="/wiki/Campbell%27s_Kids" title="Campbell's Kids">Campbell's Kids</a>" designed by illustrator <a href="/wiki/Grace_Drayton" title="Grace Drayton">Grace Drayton</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-arcadia_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-arcadia-4">[4]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Ronald_Reagan" title="Ronald Reagan">Ronald Reagan</a> was a spokesman for <a href="/wiki/V8_(beverage)" title="V8 (beverage)">V8</a> when Campbell's acquired the brand in 1948.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p><p>In addition to collectible advertising, the company has had notable commercial sponsorships. Among these was <a href="/wiki/Orson_Welles" title="Orson Welles">Orson Welles</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/The_Campbell_Playhouse_(radio_series)" title="The Campbell Playhouse (radio series)">The Campbell Playhouse</a></i>, which had previously been <i><a href="/wiki/The_Mercury_Theatre_on_the_Air" title="The Mercury Theatre on the Air">The Mercury Theatre on the Air</a></i>.  After the program's adaptation of <i><a href="/wiki/The_War_of_the_Worlds_(radio_drama)" class="mw-redirect" title="The War of the Worlds (radio drama)">The War of the Worlds</a></i> became a sensation for accidentally starting a mass panic due to its realism, Campbell's took over as sponsor of the radio theater program in December 1938.
</p><p>The shutdown of Campbell's <a href="/wiki/Camden,_New_Jersey" title="Camden, New Jersey">Camden, New Jersey</a>, plant No. 1 was announced in 1989 and production stopped on the night of March 1, 1991. The following day, the plant was officially closed. It was demolished on November 1, 1991. Plants in <a href="/wiki/Pocomoke_City,_Maryland" title="Pocomoke City, Maryland">Pocomoke City (Maryland)</a>, <a href="/wiki/Crisfield,_Maryland" title="Crisfield, Maryland">Crisfield (Maryland)</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Smyrna,_Tennessee" title="Smyrna, Tennessee">Smyrna (Tennessee)</a> also shut down.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p><p>Plant No. 2, originally a tomato-processing plant, shut down in 1980. It was responsible for about 35% of all Campbell's products in the 1950s. Products included <a href="/wiki/Pork_and_beans" title="Pork and beans">pork and beans</a>, tomato juice, <a href="/wiki/V8_(beverage)" title="V8 (beverage)">V-8 vegetable juice</a>, <a href="/wiki/Franco-American_(brand)" title="Franco-American (brand)">Franco-American</a> spaghetti, <a href="/wiki/Macaroni_and_cheese" title="Macaroni and cheese">macaroni and cheese</a>, and soups (notably: beans and bacon, <a href="/wiki/Cream_of_mushroom_soup" title="Cream of mushroom soup">cream of mushroom</a>, cream of celery, and <a href="/wiki/Cream_of_asparagus_soup" title="Cream of asparagus soup">cream of asparagus</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Courier_Post_1900_End_of_an_Era_in_Camden.jpeg" class="image"><noscript><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/7/7a/Courier_Post_1900_End_of_an_Era_in_Camden.jpeg/220px-Courier_Post_1900_End_of_an_Era_in_Camden.jpeg" decoding="async" width="220" height="178" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="3861" data-file-height="3120"></noscript><span class="lazy-image-placeholder" style="width: 220px;height: 178px;" data-src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/7/7a/Courier_Post_1900_End_of_an_Era_in_Camden.jpeg/220px-Courier_Post_1900_End_of_an_Era_in_Camden.jpeg" data-alt="" data-width="220" data-height="178" data-class="thumbimage"> </span></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Courier_Post_1900_End_of_an_Era_in_Camden.jpeg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Article in the <i>Courier Post</i> (1990) announcing the shutdown of Campbell's Camden factory</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Campbell%27s_Chicken_Noodle_Soup_(16184220183).jpg" class="image"><noscript><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0d/Campbell%27s_Chicken_Noodle_Soup_%2816184220183%29.jpg/220px-Campbell%27s_Chicken_Noodle_Soup_%2816184220183%29.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="330" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="772" data-file-height="1158"></noscript><span class="lazy-image-placeholder" style="width: 220px;height: 330px;" data-src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0d/Campbell%27s_Chicken_Noodle_Soup_%2816184220183%29.jpg/220px-Campbell%27s_Chicken_Noodle_Soup_%2816184220183%29.jpg" data-alt="" data-width="220" data-height="330" data-class="thumbimage"> </span></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Campbell%27s_Chicken_Noodle_Soup_(16184220183).jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A can of Campbell's Chicken Noodle Soup featuring the <a href="/wiki/1900_Paris_International_Exposition" class="mw-redirect" title="1900 Paris International Exposition">1900 Paris International Exposition</a></div></div></div>
<p>A total of 2,800 jobs were lost, 940–1,000 of those jobs from the Camden plant. Campbell's agreed to give workers one week's payment for each year of employment as well as paying in full for six months of medical benefits, and paying half the cost for another six months. Salaried workers received one week's pay for each year of employment.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Production was moved to plants in <a href="/wiki/Napoleon,_Ohio" title="Napoleon, Ohio">Napoleon (Ohio)</a>, <a href="/wiki/Paris,_Texas" title="Paris, Texas">Paris (Texas)</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Maxton,_North_Carolina" title="Maxton, North Carolina">Maxton (North Carolina)</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> <sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p><p>In the UK and Ireland, Campbell Soup was rebranded as Batchelors Condensed Soup (UK) and Erin (Ireland) in March 2008, when the license to use the brand name expired. <a href="/wiki/Premier_Foods" title="Premier Foods">Premier Foods</a>, <a href="/wiki/St._Albans" class="mw-redirect" title="St. Albans">St. Albans</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hertfordshire" title="Hertfordshire">Hertfordshire</a> bought the Campbell Soup Company in the UK and Ireland, for £450 million ($830 million) in 2006, but was licensed to use the brand only until 2008. Under this agreement, the US-based Campbell Soup Company continued to produce Campbell's Condensed Soup but could not sell the product in the UK for a further five years.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p><p>Campbell's continues to be a major part of Camden, regularly participating in charity events<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> in the community. In 2009, Campbell's completed the building of a new and expanded headquarters<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> in the city.
</p><p>In January 2010, Campbell's Canadian subsidiary began selling a line of soups that are certified by the <a href="/wiki/Islamic_Society_of_North_America" title="Islamic Society of North America">Islamic Society of North America</a> as being <a href="/wiki/Halal" title="Halal">halal</a> (prepared in accordance with <a href="/wiki/Sharia" title="Sharia">Islamic law</a>). Although Campbell does not have any plans to sell its halal soups in the United States, the move has drawn criticism from anti-Muslim critics in the United States. Blogger <a href="/wiki/Pamela_Geller" title="Pamela Geller">Pamela Geller</a> called for a boycott of the company.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p><p>In November 2007, Campbell's Soup sold Godiva to Yildiz Holding.
</p><p>In July 2011, Campbell's Soup decided to once again sell its product in the UK after being absent since 2008.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> Symingtons began manufacturing the brand under license. The new line-up comprised twelve cup soups, five simmer soups designed to be cooked in a pot of water, four savoury rice lines, and four savory pasta and sauce packets. The new range were not sold in cans, but instead in packets and boxes. Later in 2011, the canned varieties also returned to supermarket shelves with refreshed labels and new lines.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p><p>In 2012, Campbell announced plans to buy <a href="/wiki/Bolthouse_Farms" title="Bolthouse Farms">Bolthouse Farms</a>, a maker of juices, salad dressings and baby carrots, for $1.55 billion. Analysts saw this as an attempt to reach younger, more affluent consumers.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p><p>From 2012, Campbell Soup has been focused on updating their image and digital marketing to increase visibility among younger generations. They hired <a href="/w/index.php?title=Umang_Shah&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Umang Shah (page does not exist)">Umang Shah</a> to lead global social and digital marketing. He led record social engagement campaigns including #DeclareRecess and #BIGFiveO.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p><p>In June 2013, Campbell acquired the Danish multinational baked goods company Kelsen Group for an undisclosed amount.<sup id="cite_ref-vanallen2013_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-vanallen2013-25">[25]</a></sup> Kelsen has an 85-country distribution network and is seen as providing Campbell with opportunities for international expansion, particularly into China and other Asian markets.<sup id="cite_ref-vanallen2013_25-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-vanallen2013-25">[25]</a></sup></p><p>In June 2015, Campbell Soup acquired <a href="/wiki/Salsa_(sauce)" title="Salsa (sauce)">salsa</a> maker Garden Fresh Gourmet for $231 million as it looked to expand into the fresh and organic packaged foods business.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p><p>In December 2017, Campbell's completed the acquisition<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> of Pacific Foods of Oregon, LLC<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> for $700 million and announced the agreement to acquire the snack company <a href="/wiki/Snyder%27s-Lance" title="Snyder's-Lance">Snyder's-Lance</a> for $4.87 billion in cash.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> The latter deal is the largest in the company's history.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p><p>The company announced in January 2018 that their only Canadian factory, in Toronto, would close. Production would shift to three existing facilities within the U.S. It was reported that the expected loss of jobs, as a result of the closing, would be 380.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-31">[31]</a></sup></p><p><a href="/wiki/Denise_Morrison" title="Denise Morrison">Denise Morrison</a> served as the company's president and CEO from 2011 through 2018.<sup id="cite_ref-retire_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-retire-32">[32]</a></sup></p><p>On December 21, 2018, former <a href="/wiki/Pinnacle_Foods" title="Pinnacle Foods">Pinnacle Foods</a> CEO <a href="/wiki/Mark_Clouse" title="Mark Clouse">Mark Clouse</a> was named the next Campbell's CEO, effective January 22, 2019.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p><p>In July 2019, Campbell's agreed to sell its stake in the Kelsen Group for <a href="/wiki/United_States_dollar" title="United States dollar">$</a>300 million to a subsidiary of <a href="/wiki/Ferrero_SpA" title="Ferrero SpA">Ferrero SpA</a>. The transfer will be completed sometime early 2020.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(2)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Pop_art">Pop art</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Campbell_Soup_Company&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Pop art" data-section="2" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-2 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-2">
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Campbell%27s_Soup_Cans" title="Campbell's Soup Cans">Campbell's Soup Cans</a></div>
<p>In 1962, artist <a href="/wiki/Andy_Warhol" title="Andy Warhol">Andy Warhol</a> took the familiar look of the Campbell's soup can and integrated it into a series of <a href="/wiki/Pop_art" title="Pop art">pop art</a> <a href="/wiki/Screen_printing" title="Screen printing">silkscreens</a>, a theme he would return to off and on through the 1960s and 1970s. The first batch in 1962 were <a href="/wiki/Campbell%27s_Soup_Cans" title="Campbell's Soup Cans">a series of 32 canvases</a>. At first, the cans were accurate representations of actual Campbell's cans, but as his series progressed, they became more surrealistic, with Warhol experimenting with negative-reversed color schemes and other varied techniques (many of these which would be used on other Warhol paintings of the period, such as his celebrity silkscreens of the 1960s). The silkscreens themselves have become iconic pieces of pop art, with one in particular, <i>Small Torn Campbell Soup Can (Pepper Pot)</i> (1962), commanding a price of $11.8 million at auction in 2006.
</p><p>In 2004, Campbell's themselves recognized Warhol's art by releasing in the eastern United States a limited-edition series of cans that were inspired by the coloring and silkscreen effects of Warhol's pieces. This marked one of the few times in the company's history that they would change the trade dress for their main canned-soup line in any substantial manner. In 2012, the Warhol-inspired cans returned to shelves, in an exclusive retail partnership with <a href="/wiki/Target_Stores" class="mw-redirect" title="Target Stores">Target</a>.
</p>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(3)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Slogans">Slogans</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Campbell_Soup_Company&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Slogans" data-section="3" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-3 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-3">

<li>Made for real, Real life (2015–present)</li></ul></section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(4)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Health_controversies">Health controversies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Campbell_Soup_Company&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Health controversies" data-section="4" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-4 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-4">
<p>Many canned soups, including Campbell's condensed and chunky varieties, contain relatively high quantities of <a href="/wiki/Sodium" title="Sodium">sodium</a> and thus are not desirable for those on low-sodium diets. However, Campbell's Chunky, Healthy Request and other soups, as well as their V-8 and Tomato juices, are claimed by Campbell's to contain reduced sodium levels.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></p><p>In fall 2007, Campbell's was awarded a Certificate of Excellence, for their efforts in lowering sodium levels, from <i>Blood Pressure Canada</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></p><p>By autumn 2009, Campbell's claimed it had lowered the sodium content in 50% of its soups range.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup>
In March 2010, this claim was challenged. ABC News reported that the low-sodium variety of Campbell soup in fact contains the same amount of sodium as the regular variety, and that Campbell's Healthy Request soup contains more fat than the regular variety.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup></p><p>In December 2009, <i><a href="/wiki/Consumer_Reports" title="Consumer Reports">Consumer Reports</a></i> found that major canned food companies including Campbell's Soup had tinned products which had <a href="/wiki/Bisphenol_A" title="Bisphenol A">bisphenol A</a> (BPA) levels over 100 ppb in some cases;<sup id="cite_ref-cpbpa_40-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cpbpa-40">[40]</a></sup> the testing revealed that just one serving of canned food would exceed an expert's recommendation for daily exposure (0.2 micrograms per kg body weight per day).<sup id="cite_ref-cpbpa_40-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cpbpa-40">[40]</a></sup></p><p>In July 2011, citing sinking sales, and a combination of: "consumer views and choices" and having "found no connection between sodium consumption and negative health outcomes"<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> they increased the salt contents again.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup></p>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(5)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="GMO">GMO</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Campbell_Soup_Company&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: GMO" data-section="5" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-5 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-5">
<p>Throughout 2012, Campbell's contributed $500,000 to a $46 million political campaign known as "The Coalition Against The Costly Food Labeling Proposition, sponsored by Farmers and Food Producers"<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> This organization was set up to oppose a citizens' initiative, known as <a href="/wiki/California_Proposition_37,_2012" class="mw-redirect" title="California Proposition 37, 2012">Proposition 37</a>, demanding mandatory labeling of foods containing genetically modified ingredients.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup></p><p>In January 2016, they decided to support mandatory labeling and announced they would label their products that contained GMO additives.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup></p>
