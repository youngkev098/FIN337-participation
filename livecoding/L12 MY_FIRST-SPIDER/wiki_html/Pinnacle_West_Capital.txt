</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(1)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pinnacle_West_Capital&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History" data-section="1" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-1 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-1">
<p>In February 1985, Arizona Public Service Company reorganized as a holding company, AZP Group Inc., with APS as the leading subsidiary.<sup id="cite_ref-appa_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-appa-2">[2]</a></sup> It traded under the ticker symbol AZP. 1985 earnings reached a new record high at almost US$290 million, and by the end of the year the stock price had doubled from its 1984 low.<sup id="cite_ref-stockguide_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-stockguide-3">[3]</a></sup></p><p>In 1987, AZP Group changed its name to Pinnacle West Capital Corporation, and began trading under the new ticker symbol PNW. The utility continued to operate as its principal subsidiary.<sup id="cite_ref-appa_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-appa-2">[2]</a></sup> By then Pinnacle's long-term debt had grown to almost US$2.4 billion. Earnings had declined to US$260 million in 1986, but were recovering by the end 1987. However, the stock price reached an all-time peak that year, which was not surpassed for the next decade, as the company ran into troubles through the early 1990s.<sup id="cite_ref-stockguide_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-stockguide-3">[3]</a></sup>  In 1986, Pinnacle acquired MeraBank, a savings and loan.  It grew to become Arizona's largest thrift, with $6.5 billion in assets.  However, the thrift was seized by federal regulators in January 1990, due in part to the deterioration in prices in the Arizona real estate market.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p><p>By 1990, the company's earnings had fallen to under US$100 million, as it struggled through the <a href="/wiki/1990-1991_recession" class="mw-redirect" title="1990-1991 recession">1990-1991 recession</a>, as well as a series of failed attempts to diversify. By 1991, the company was facing a loss of almost US$190 million, and discontinued paying its <a href="/wiki/Dividend" title="Dividend">dividend</a>. APS and its predecessors had paid a dividend without interruption since 1920. The stock price plunged 85% from its 1987 peak, to its lowest level in over twenty years. Its long-term debt by then reached over US$2.5 billion.<sup id="cite_ref-stockguide_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-stockguide-3">[3]</a></sup></p><p>However, by 1992, the company returned to profitability, as earnings  recovered to over US$150 million. The stock price tripled from its 1991 low by the end of 1993. The company reinstated its dividend that year.<sup id="cite_ref-stockguide_3-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-stockguide-3">[3]</a></sup></p><p>By 1995, Pinnacle West Capital had been added to the S&amp;P MidCap 400 index. That year, earnings surpassed US$200 million again, and by early the next year the stock price began approaching its old 1987 high. Also in 1995, the utility subsidiary Arizona Public Services issued 30-year <a href="/w/index.php?title=Monthly_Income_Debt_Securities&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Monthly Income Debt Securities (page does not exist)">Monthly Income Debt Securities</a> paying 10%.<sup id="cite_ref-stockguide_3-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-stockguide-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
