</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(1)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span></h2><section class="mf-section-1 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-1">
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Founding_and_growth">Founding and growth</span></h3>
<p>In 1983, <a href="/wiki/Gary_Burrell" title="Gary Burrell">Gary Burrell</a> recruited <a href="/wiki/Min_Kao" title="Min Kao">Min H. Kao</a> from the defense contractor <a href="/wiki/Magnavox" title="Magnavox">Magnavox</a> while working for the former <a href="/wiki/King_Radio_(company)" title="King Radio (company)">King Radio</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Forbes_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Forbes-7">[7]</a></sup>  They founded Garmin in 1989 in <a href="/wiki/Lenexa,_Kansas" title="Lenexa, Kansas">Lenexa, Kansas</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-:0_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-2">[2]</a></sup> as "ProNav".  ProNav's first product was a GPS unit which sold for <a href="/wiki/United_States_dollar" title="United States dollar">US$</a>2,500.  The company was later renamed "Garmin", a <a href="/wiki/Portmanteau" title="Portmanteau">portmanteau</a> of its two founders, <b>Gar</b>y Burrell and <b>Min</b> H. Kao.<sup id="cite_ref-Huffpost_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Huffpost-8">[8]</a></sup>  In 1991, the <a href="/wiki/U.S._Army" class="mw-redirect" title="U.S. Army">U.S. Army</a> became their first customer.<sup id="cite_ref-Johnson_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Johnson-9">[9]</a></sup></p><p>By 1995, Garmin's sales had reached $105 million, and it had achieved a <a href="/wiki/Profit_(accounting)" title="Profit (accounting)">profit</a> of $23 million. By 1999, sales had reached $233 million and a profit of $64 million. Garmin reported a 2006 total revenue of $1.77 billion, up 73% from $1.03 billion in 2005.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p><p>By 2000 Garmin had sold three million GPS devices, and was producing 50 different models. Its products were sold in 100 countries and carried by 2,500 independent distributors. As of 22 August 2000, the company held 35 <a href="/wiki/Patent" title="Patent">patents</a> on GPS technology. By the end of June 2000, the company employed 1,205 people: 541 in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, 635 in <a href="/wiki/Taiwan" title="Taiwan">Taiwan</a>, and 29 in the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> In April 2018, Garmin launched Connect IQ 3.0  along with new apps—MySwim Pro, <a href="/wiki/Yelp" title="Yelp">Yelp</a>, Trailforks and <a href="/wiki/IHeartRadio" title="IHeartRadio">iHeartRadio</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> In May 2018, Garmin partnered with the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Kansas_Medical_Center" title="University of Kansas Medical Center">University of Kansas Medical Center</a> to tackle <a href="/wiki/Sleep_apnea" title="Sleep apnea">sleep apnea</a> and <a href="/wiki/Atrial_fibrillation" title="Atrial fibrillation">atrial fibrillation</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Public_offering">Public offering</span></h3>
<p>The company began public trading on <a href="/wiki/NASDAQ" class="mw-redirect" title="NASDAQ">NASDAQ</a> on 8 December 2000. At that time Burrell owned 19,911,155 shares. Kao owned 20,352,803 shares. Together their holdings accounted for 45 percent of the company's stock. Kao's brother, Ruey-Jeng Kao, an <a href="/wiki/Lawyer" title="Lawyer">attorney</a> in <a href="/wiki/Taipei" title="Taipei">Taipei</a>, owned another 7,984,109 shares, which when combined with Burrell's and Kao's shares constituted 54.22 percent of the shares outstanding.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Acquisitions">Acquisitions</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:20070513_Allerton_Hotel_and_Michigan_Ave.JPG" class="image"><noscript><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/63/20070513_Allerton_Hotel_and_Michigan_Ave.JPG/220px-20070513_Allerton_Hotel_and_Michigan_Ave.JPG" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="3072" data-file-height="2304"></noscript><span class="lazy-image-placeholder" style="width: 220px;height: 165px;" data-src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/63/20070513_Allerton_Hotel_and_Michigan_Ave.JPG/220px-20070513_Allerton_Hotel_and_Michigan_Ave.JPG" data-alt="" data-width="220" data-height="165" data-class="thumbimage"> </span></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:20070513_Allerton_Hotel_and_Michigan_Ave.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>From 2006 to 2015 Garmin operated a retail store on Michigan Ave. in Chicago.</div></div></div>
<p>In August 2003, Garmin completed acquisition of UPS Aviation Technologies, Inc. based in <a href="/wiki/Salem,_Oregon" title="Salem, Oregon">Salem, Oregon</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> a subsidiary of United Parcel Service, Inc., expanding its product line of panel-mounted <a href="/wiki/Global_Positioning_System" title="Global Positioning System">GPS/NAV/COMM</a> units and integrated <a href="/wiki/Cockpit_display_system" title="Cockpit display system">cockpit systems</a> for private and commercial aircraft. The acquired company changed its name to Garmin AT, Inc. and continued operations as a wholly owned subsidiary of Garmin International, Inc.<sup id="cite_ref-garminat_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-garminat-17">[17]</a></sup></p><p>Garmin has acquired Dynastream Innovations,<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> EME Tec Sat SAS (<a href="/wiki/Emergency_position-indicating_radiobeacon_station" title="Emergency position-indicating radiobeacon station">EME</a>),<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> and Digital Cyclone.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> Dynastream,  in <a href="/wiki/Cochrane,_Alberta" title="Cochrane, Alberta">Cochrane, Alberta</a>, produces personal monitoring technology (<a href="/wiki/ANT%2B" class="mw-redirect" title="ANT+">ANT+</a>)—such as foot pods and heart rate monitors for sports and fitness products—and also ultra-low-power and low-cost wireless connectivity devices for a wide range of applications (<a href="/wiki/ANT_(network)" title="ANT (network)">ANT</a>). EME Tec Sat SAS (<a href="/wiki/Emergency_position-indicating_radiobeacon_station" title="Emergency position-indicating radiobeacon station">EME</a>) is the distributor of Garmin's consumer products in France; following the acquisition, EME changed its name to Garmin France SAS. Digital Cyclone Inc (DCI), located in <a href="/wiki/Chanhassen,_Minnesota" title="Chanhassen, Minnesota">Chanhassen, Minnesota</a>, provides mobile <a href="/wiki/Weather" title="Weather">weather</a> solutions for <a href="/wiki/Consumer" title="Consumer">consumers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Aircraft_pilot" title="Aircraft pilot">pilots</a>, and outdoor enthusiasts. Garmin also bought Nautamatic Marine Systems,<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> an Oregon-based company that makes <a href="/wiki/Autopilot" title="Autopilot">autopilot</a> <a href="/wiki/System" title="System">systems</a> for <a href="/wiki/Boat" title="Boat">boats</a>.  In July 2011, Garmin finished its acquisition of the German <a href="/wiki/Satellite_navigation" title="Satellite navigation">satellite navigation</a> company Navigon.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p><p>In 2006, Garmin introduced a new corporate logo,<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> and opened its first retail store, located on <a href="/wiki/Michigan_Avenue_(Chicago)" title="Michigan Avenue (Chicago)">Michigan Avenue</a> in <a href="/wiki/Chicago" title="Chicago">Chicago</a>, Illinois.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p><p>In 2015, Garmin acquired South Africa's iKubu Ltd. for its Backtracker on-bicycle low power radar system.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p><p>In 2018, it was reported that the Garmin subsidiary <a href="/wiki/Navionics" title="Navionics">Navionics</a> had exposed hundreds of thousands of customer records, when its MongoDB database wasn't secured with a password.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Corporate_governance">Corporate governance</span></h3>
<p>Burrell retired in 2003 as Garmin's <a href="/wiki/Chief_executive_officer" title="Chief executive officer">chief executive officer</a> and in 2004 retired as chairman of its <a href="/wiki/Board_of_directors" title="Board of directors">board of directors</a>. He remained chairman <a href="/wiki/Emeritus" title="Emeritus">emeritus</a> until his death in 2019. Kao became CEO in 2003, and chairman in 2004.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p><p>In 2005, <i><a href="/wiki/Forbes" title="Forbes">Forbes</a></i> estimated Kao's <a href="/wiki/Net_worth" title="Net worth">net worth</a> at $1.5 billion. He has donated $17.5 million to the University of Tennessee. The same year <i>Forbes</i> estimated Burrell's net worth as $940 million.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> Cliff Pemble is the current CEO of Garmin.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p>

<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Marine_GPS">Marine GPS</span></h3>
<p>The company's first product was the GPS 100, a panel-mounted GPS receiver aimed at the marine market, priced at $2,500. It made its debut at the 1990 International Marine Technology Exposition in <a href="/wiki/Chicago" title="Chicago">Chicago</a>.
</p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Handheld_GPS">Handheld GPS</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Garmin_Etrex_H_7262.jpg" class="image"><noscript><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/62/Garmin_Etrex_H_7262.jpg/150px-Garmin_Etrex_H_7262.jpg" decoding="async" width="150" height="215" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="2091" data-file-height="3000"></noscript><span class="lazy-image-placeholder" style="width: 150px;height: 215px;" data-src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/62/Garmin_Etrex_H_7262.jpg/150px-Garmin_Etrex_H_7262.jpg" data-alt="" data-width="150" data-height="215" data-class="thumbimage"> </span></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Garmin_Etrex_H_7262.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A Garmin eTrex H, an early, basic GPS model</div></div></div>
<p>Another early product, a handheld GPS receiver, was sold to military personnel serving in <a href="/wiki/Kuwait" title="Kuwait">Kuwait</a> and <a href="/wiki/Saudi_Arabia" title="Saudi Arabia">Saudi Arabia</a> during the 1991 <a href="/wiki/Gulf_War" title="Gulf War">Gulf War</a>.
In the early 2000s (decade), Garmin launched a series of personal GPS devices aimed at recreational <a href="/wiki/Road_running" title="Road running">runners</a> called the <a href="/wiki/Garmin_Forerunner" title="Garmin Forerunner">Forerunner</a>. The Garmin Foretrex is a similar wrist-worn GPS device with two-dimensional <a href="/wiki/GPS_tracking" class="mw-redirect" title="GPS tracking">GPS tracking</a> and <a href="/wiki/Waypoint" title="Waypoint">waypoint</a> projection called .
</p>
<h4 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="eTrex">eTrex</span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Garmin_eTrex10.jpg" class="image"><noscript><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/44/Garmin_eTrex10.jpg/150px-Garmin_eTrex10.jpg" decoding="async" width="150" height="267" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="2988" data-file-height="5312"></noscript><span class="lazy-image-placeholder" style="width: 150px;height: 267px;" data-src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/44/Garmin_eTrex10.jpg/150px-Garmin_eTrex10.jpg" data-alt="" data-width="150" data-height="267" data-class="thumbimage"> </span></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Garmin_eTrex10.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Garmin eTrex10 Handheld</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Garmin_eTrex30_close.JPG" class="image"><noscript><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/06/Garmin_eTrex30_close.JPG/150px-Garmin_eTrex30_close.JPG" decoding="async" width="150" height="238" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="1562" data-file-height="2482"></noscript><span class="lazy-image-placeholder" style="width: 150px;height: 238px;" data-src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/06/Garmin_eTrex30_close.JPG/150px-Garmin_eTrex30_close.JPG" data-alt="" data-width="150" data-height="238" data-class="thumbimage"> </span></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Garmin_eTrex30_close.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A Garmin eTrex 30 simultaneously connects to both GPS and GLONASS satellites.</div></div></div>
<p>The compact eTrex was introduced in 2000; several models with different features have been released since. The original eTrex, commonly nicknamed "eTrex Yellow", offered a lightweight (5.3 oz/150 g), waterproof, palm-sized 12-channel GPS receiver, along with a battery life of up to 22 hours on two AA-size batteries.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> It was replaced in 2007 by the eTrex H, which added a high-sensitivity receiver.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> Other eTrex models include the Summit, Venture, Legend, and Vista, each with various additional features such as <a href="/wiki/WAAS" class="mw-redirect" title="WAAS">WAAS</a>, altimeter, digital compass, city database, and highway maps. Many of these models come in color and expandable-memory versions.<sup id="cite_ref-etrex_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-etrex-33">[33]</a></sup></p><p>In May 2011 Garmin refreshed the eTrex product line with new mechanical design and support for advances in cartography and hardware technology with its release of the eTrex 10, eTrex 20, and eTrex 30, Garmin became the first company to manufacture and distribute a worldwide consumer navigation product supporting both GPS and <a href="/wiki/GLONASS" title="GLONASS">GLONASS</a> satellite constellations.<sup id="cite_ref-etrex102030_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-etrex102030-34">[34]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> On May 13, 2015 Garmin released the eTrex 20x and 30x, which succeeded the eTrex 20 and 30. The main upgrade was a higher resolution screen and 4GB storage, double of the previous models.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></p><p>On July 2, 2015 Garmin introduced its eTrex Touch line, releasing three models (25, 35 and 35t), all featuring a 2.6" touch screen.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> The 35t model designation is not used in Europe, but the European market 35 is essentially the 35t, and both the European 25 and 35 include Garmin TopoActive Europe maps and 8GB of internal storage.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></p>

<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="iQue_PDA_receivers">iQue PDA receivers</span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Garmin_iQue" title="Garmin iQue">Garmin iQue</a></div>
<p>In 2003, Garmin launched the <a href="/wiki/Garmin_iQue" title="Garmin iQue">iQue</a> line of integrated <a href="/wiki/Personal_digital_assistant" title="Personal digital assistant">PDA</a>-<a href="/wiki/GPS" class="mw-redirect" title="GPS">GPS</a> receivers. On 31 October 2005, the iQue M4 became the first PDA that did not require a PC to preload the maps. The American version came with built-in maps of North America, while the UK version was supplied pre-loaded with maps of Western Europe.
</p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Dog_tracking_and_training">Dog tracking and training</span></h3>
<p>On June 2, 2011, Garmin introduced Astro 320.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup></p><p>On June 12, 2014, Garmin introduced T5 and TT15 Collars for Astro and Alpha.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup></p><p>On July 31, 2015, T5 mini and T15 mini collars were added.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup></p><p>On June 16, 2016, Garmin introduced the Astro 430.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup></p><p>On October 10, 2016, Garmin added pairing of the Astro 430 and Alpha 100 to Garmin Smart Watches.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Fishfinders">Fishfinders</span></h3>
<p>Garmin also manufactures a line of <a href="/wiki/Sonar" title="Sonar">sonar</a> <a href="/wiki/Fishfinder" title="Fishfinder">fishfinders</a>, including some units that also have GPS capability, and some that use spread spectrum technology.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Laptop_GPS_and_mobile_apps">Laptop GPS and mobile apps</span></h3>
<p>In April 2008, Garmin launched Garmin Mobile PC, a GPS navigation software program for laptop PCs and other computers, based on the Microsoft Windows operating system, now discontinued.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup></p><p>Garmin offers mobile apps for various purposes for Android, Windows Phone, and for iPhone.
</p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span id="N.C3.BCvifone"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Nüvifone">Nüvifone</span></h3>

</p>

</p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Avionics"><span id="Avionics">Avionics</span></span></h3>
<p>Garmin offers integrated cockpits, panel mount displays, multi-function displays (MFD), transponders, radar and other <a href="/wiki/Avionics" title="Avionics">avionics</a>. 
Garmin entered this market in 1991 with the <i>GPS-100AVD</i> panel-mounted receiver. Its first portable unit, the <i>GPS-95</i>, was introduced in 1993. In 1994, the <i>GPS-155</i> panel-mounted unit was the first GPS receiver on the market to receive <a href="/wiki/Type_certificate" title="Type certificate">full FAA certification</a> for <a href="/wiki/Instrument_approach" title="Instrument approach">instrument approaches</a>. In 1998, Garmin introduced the <i>GNS-430</i>, an integrated GPS navigation receiver/communications transceiver. in 1998 rolled out its first integrated GPS, COM, VOR, LOC and glideslope product, the GNS 430. More than 125,000 GNS navigators are now installed in aircraft. Garmin reached its one millionth delivery in November 2017.<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup></p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Garmin_G1000" title="Garmin G1000">G1000</a> is an all-glass avionics suite for OEM aircraft, the similar G950 is used in <a href="/wiki/Experimental_aircraft" title="Experimental aircraft">experimental aircraft</a>, and the G600 is a <a href="/wiki/Retrofit" class="mw-redirect" title="Retrofit">retrofit</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup></p><p>On 30 October 2019, Garmin announced that the <a href="/wiki/Piper_M600" class="mw-redirect" title="Piper M600">Piper M600</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cirrus_Vision_Jet" class="mw-redirect" title="Cirrus Vision Jet">Cirrus Vision Jet</a> would become the first <a href="/wiki/General_aviation" title="General aviation">general aviation</a> aircraft certified with the company's <a href="/wiki/Autoland#emergency_autoland" title="Autoland">emergency autoland</a> system, which is capable of automatically landing the aircraft with the push of a button and will be a part of both aircraft's <a href="/wiki/Garmin_G3000" title="Garmin G3000">G3000</a> integrated avionics suite in 2020.<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup> Garmin calls the new technology "Autonomí".<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup>
Garmin plans to equip other platforms in 2020, like the <a href="/wiki/SOCATA_TBM" title="SOCATA TBM">TBM 940</a>, and could expand its offer to the G1000 avionics suite.<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup></p>

</ul><h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Garmin-AT_subsidiary"><span id="Garmin-AT_subsidiary">Garmin-AT subsidiary</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2003, Garmin acquired UPS Aviation Technologies, including that firm's II Morrow Apollo line of aircraft MFD/GPS/NAV/COMM units. II Morrow had been founded in <a href="/wiki/Salem,_Oregon" title="Salem, Oregon">Salem, Oregon</a> in 1982 as a manufacturer of <a href="/wiki/LORAN" title="LORAN">LORAN C</a> marine and general aviation products. In 1982 its aircraft navigator 602 LORAN C receiver permitted point to point navigation. Some examples of its LORAN units are Apollo II 616B Aviation LORAN panel mount (1986), II Morrow Apollo 604 Loran Navigator (1987) and Apollo 820 GPS Flybuddy (1991). In 1986, <a href="/wiki/United_Parcel_Service" title="United Parcel Service">United Parcel Service</a> (UPS) purchased the company to expand the use of electronic technology in the package delivery and tracking business.
</p><p>II Morrow shifted focus from marine business to development of package process automation technology for UPS such as vehicle management systems, automated high speed package sorting systems, as well as delivery and tracking systems. In 1999, II Morrow was renamed to UPS Aviation Technologies, and re-focused towards modernizing UPS' Boeing 7xx series Heavy Iron Transport Category Aircraft fleet, as well they also re-entered the general aviation marketplace. It certified the first Gamma 3 WAAS GPS engine for FAA Certified Precision GPS approaches.<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup> The new certified WAAS engine yielded vertical and horizontal accuracy of one meter RMS in guidance into airports without existing ILS approaches. This GPS technology met the FAA's TSO-C146a primary navigation standards for en route, terminal and approach phases of flight—with WAAS augmentation as the sole means of navigation.  This primary GPS "sole source" navigation capability was integrated into the CNX-80.  The CNX-80 WAAS GPS/COM/NAV integrated navigator was the first product in the industry approved for primary GPS navigation. It also enabled LPV "glidescope" approaches without requiring ground nav aids. New LNAV (GPS) approaches provide the accuracy and safety of an ILS—without the ground-based localizer and glideslope equipment.<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup> Later, the CNX-80 was renamed the GNS-480, under Garmin.<sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup></p><p>In 1999: <i>Flight International</i> magazine presented UPS Aviation Technologies with its Aerospace Industry Award for the development of ADS-B, a surveillance technology intended to reduce aviation delays while improving safety.<sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup></p>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(4)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Wristwear">Wristwear</span></h2><section class="mf-section-4 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-4">
<p>Garmin produces activity trackers and sports watches, aimed at activities such as running, watersports, golf, cycling and swimming with sensors such as heart rate and gps. Some recent models add Bluetooth music playback and puls-oximetry.
</p><p>The vivofit and vivosmart ranges are activity trackers. The Garmin Vivofit 3 measures the wearer's duration and quality of sleep, quantifies body movement, records heart rate, counts steps and the number of stairs climbed.<sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup> Garmin produces the Vivosmart HR. It comes with the touch screen and includes heart rate monitoring, media player controls, smart notifications and phone finder features.
</p><p>The Forerunner series is aimed at running primarily, but the watches are more broadly focussed, especially in the higher end. The 735 XT has multi-sport tracking capabilities (automatically switching between sports, for example in a triathlon) and a variety of special profiles for jogging, swimming, cycling, skiing, paddle sports, a variety of weight loss activities, and hiking. It comes with built-in heart rate sensor and GPS.<sup id="cite_ref-59" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup></p><p>The Fenix range, such as the Fenix 6 released in August 2019, is a more rugged, multisport range<sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup> that now offers a solar charging model<sup id="cite_ref-61" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup>,
</p><p>The Vivomove is a traditionally-styled watch with activity tracking capabilities. It has a built-in accelerometer (calculates distance during indoor workouts, without the need for a foot pod), step counter, auto goal (learns the wearer's activity level and assigns a daily step goal), move bar, and sleep-monitoring capabilities.<sup id="cite_ref-62" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup></p><p>Other series include the Quatix aimed at water sports, the D2 aviator watches, the Approach golf watches.
</p><p>In 2018, Garmin introduced functionality such as maps, Bluetooth music playback, NFC payment and pulse-oximetry for its wristwear.<sup id="cite_ref-63" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup></p><p>In October 2018, Garmin announced it has integrated Spotify to its Garmin Fenix 5 Plus and Garmin Forerunner 645 Music GPS sports watch.<sup id="cite_ref-64" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup> Garmin was the second smartwatch maker to do so, after Spotify was exclusive to <a href="/wiki/Samsung" title="Samsung">Samsung</a> smartwatches since 2016. <sup id="cite_ref-65" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup></p>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(5)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Maps">Maps</span></h2><section class="mf-section-5 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-5">
<p>Most current Garmin devices can display its current location on a map, which is vector-based and stored in the built-in memory or loaded from additional flash media. The built-in (or 'basemap') displays all country borders and major cities. Garmin offers different maps for purchase, including detailed road maps, topographic maps and nautical maps.  Non-commercial maps are also available and can be displayed on most Garmin GPS devices.
</p><p>The maps used by Garmin products are currently provided by <a href="/wiki/Navteq" title="Navteq">Navteq</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-66" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup> Map errors are handled using Navteq Map Reporter.<sup id="cite_ref-67" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup> Errors can be reported using Garmin's report a map error page,<sup id="cite_ref-68" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup> or by using the Navteq map reporter.<sup id="cite_ref-69" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup></p>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(6)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Sport_sponsorship">Sport sponsorship</span></h2><section class="mf-section-6 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-6">
<p>In 2007 Garmin began sponsorship of <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">English</a> <a href="/wiki/Premier_League" title="Premier League">Premier League</a> football club <a href="/wiki/Middlesbrough_F.C." title="Middlesbrough F.C.">Middlesbrough</a> in a one-year deal that was carried into a second year for the 2008/09 season.<sup id="cite_ref-70" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup>  In 2008 Garmin began sponsorship of <a href="/wiki/Cycling_team" title="Cycling team">cycling team</a> <a href="/wiki/EF_Pro_Cycling" title="EF Pro Cycling">Garmin–Chipotle p/b H30</a> to promote its <i>Edge</i> line of bicycle computers.<sup id="cite_ref-71" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup> In 2015, the team became <a href="/wiki/Cannondale-Garmin" class="mw-redirect" title="Cannondale-Garmin">Cannondale-Garmin</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-72" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-72">[72]</a></sup>
In 2014 Garmin paired up with <a href="/wiki/Premier_League" title="Premier League">Premier League</a> side <a href="/wiki/Southampton_FC" class="mw-redirect" title="Southampton FC">Southampton FC</a> in a global partnership. Garmin's European head office is located in <a href="/wiki/Southampton" title="Southampton">Southampton</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-73" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-73">[73]</a></sup></p>
