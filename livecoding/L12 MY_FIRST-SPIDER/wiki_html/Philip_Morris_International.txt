It is headquartered in <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York</a>, <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>. International">Papastratos Philip Morris International</a></td></tr><tr><th scope="row" style="padding-right: 0.5em;">Website</th><td style="line-height: 1.35em;"><span class="url"><a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://www.pmi.com">www<wbr></wbr>.pmi<wbr></wbr>.com</a></span></td></tr><tr><td colspan="2" style="text-align:center;line-height: 1.35em;"><b>Footnotes / title="Corporate spin-off">spin-off</a> in March 2008, Philip Morris International was an operating company of <a href="/wiki/Altria" title="Altria">Altria</a>. Altria explained the spin-off, arguing PMI would have more "freedom" i.e. leeway outside the responsibilities and standards of American corporate ownership in terms of potential litigation and legislative restrictions to "pursue sales growth in emerging markets", while Altria focuses on the American domestic market.<sup id="cite_ref-MSNBC_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MSNBC-3">[3]</a></sup> The shareholders in Altria at the time were given shares in PMI, which was listed on the London Stock Exchange and other markets.
</p><p>The company's headquarters are in New York City, yet it does not operate in the United States of America. <a href="/wiki/Philip_Morris_USA" title="Philip Morris USA">Philip Morris USA</a>, a subsidiary of PMI's former owner American parent <a href="/wiki/Altria" title="Altria">Altria</a> group owns the Philip Morris brands there.
</p><p>With <a href="/wiki/Tobacco_smoking" title="Tobacco smoking">tobacco</a> being <a href="/wiki/Addiction" title="Addiction">addictive</a> and <a href="/wiki/Health_effects_of_tobacco" title="Health effects of tobacco">the single greatest cause of preventable death globally</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> the company is highly controversial, not least because of its history of obfuscating scientific evidence around the health impacts of smoking. It has been the subject of litigation and restrictive legislation from governments.
</p><p>In response to burgeoning awareness of the harm to health of cigarettes, PMI has declared on its homepage the intention to replace cigarettes with smoke-free products, and to "switch ... adult smokers" to these products as the first phase of a business strategy, as a responsible decision for the benefit of "adult smokers" and the companies shareholders and employees.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p><p>With the world-wide decrease in smoking in the 21st century, shares of Philip Morris were no longer considered the "safe haven" they once were.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> The company ranked No. 108 in the 2018 <a href="/wiki/Fortune_500" title="Fortune 500">Fortune 500</a> list of the largest corporations by total revenue.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>

</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(1)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Philip_Morris_International&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History" data-section="1" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-1 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-1">
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_development">Early development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Philip_Morris_International&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Early development" data-section="2" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>The company states its history is traced to a London tobacconist, <a href="/wiki/Philip_Morris_(tobacconist)" title="Philip Morris (tobacconist)">Philip Morris</a>, opening a single shop on London’s <a href="/wiki/Bond_Street" title="Bond Street">Bond Street</a> in 1847 which sold tobacco and cigarettes.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> In 1881, Philip Morris' son, Leopard Morris, established "Philip Morris &amp; Company and Grunebaum Ltd" with Joseph Grunebaum.<sup id="cite_ref-:1_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-2">[2]</a></sup> In 1885, the company changed its name to "Philip Morris &amp; Co. Ltd." <sup id="cite_ref-:1_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-2">[2]</a></sup></p><p>In 1894, William Curtis Thomson and his family began to control the company, and in 1902 the company was incorporated in <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-:1_2-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-2">[2]</a></sup> In 1919, the American business was acquired and incorporated as "Philip Morris &amp; Co. Ltd., Inc." in <a href="/wiki/Virginia" title="Virginia">Virginia</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-:1_2-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Later_development">Later development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Philip_Morris_International&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Later development" data-section="3" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>In 1954, Philip Morris (Australia) became the first affiliate of Philip Morris &amp; Co., Ltd, Inc. outside the U.S. In 1972, the company's <a href="/wiki/Marlboro_(cigarette)" title="Marlboro (cigarette)">Marlboro</a> became the world's top-selling cigarette brand.<sup id="cite_ref-:1_2-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-2">[2]</a></sup></p><p>In 1987, Philip Morris International (PMI) was incorporated as an operating company of Philip Morris Companies Inc.<sup id="cite_ref-:1_2-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-2">[2]</a></sup> In 2001, the operations center of the company was transferred from <a href="/wiki/Rye_Brook,_New_York" title="Rye Brook, New York">Rye Brook, New York</a>, to <a href="/wiki/Lausanne" title="Lausanne">Lausanne</a>, <a href="/wiki/Switzerland" title="Switzerland">Switzerland</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-:1_2-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-2">[2]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> On January 27, 2003, Philip Morris Companies Inc. formally changed its name to <a href="/wiki/The_Altria_Group" class="mw-redirect" title="The Altria Group">the Altria Group</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> In March 2008, Philip Morris International was spun off from Altria.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p><p>In April 2014, Philip Morris International announced that it would close its <a href="/wiki/Moorabbin" class="mw-redirect" title="Moorabbin">Moorabbin</a> plant in <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a> by the end of 2014 after operating for 60 years, due to the gradual decline of sales in the last ten years and difficulties conforming to 2010 Australian government regulation about reducing fire risks.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> In 2015, the company sold 850 billion cigarettes.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p><p>In August 2018 Reuters reported that Philip Morris "has been among foreign companies with exposure to <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a>’s tobacco market. The company’s sales exposure to Russia is 7 percent, according to a note from Goldman Sachs."<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>

<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Heat-not-burn_product#IQOS" class="mw-redirect" title="Heat-not-burn product">Heat-not-burn product § IQOS</a></div>
<p>Philip Morris International's research center is located in Neuchatel, Switzerland and houses Philip Morris International's product <a href="/wiki/Research_and_development" title="Research and development">research and development</a> program.<sup id="cite_ref-ForbesFisher_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ForbesFisher-23">[23]</a></sup> As of April 2018, earnings reports showed the company had spent $4.5 billion on four products: two that heat rather than burn tobacco, and two other nicotine products.<sup id="cite_ref-BloombergKaplan_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BloombergKaplan-24">[24]</a></sup> One of these heat-not-burn tobacco products is <a href="/wiki/Heat-not-burn_product#IQOS" class="mw-redirect" title="Heat-not-burn product">IQOS</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p><p>It is funding the <b>Foundation for a Smoke-Free World</b> (founded in 2017) to purportedly fund scientific research for the global elimination of  tobacco smoking and has adopted a goal of making cigarettes obsolete by 2035. Its claims to independence have been challenged<sup id="cite_ref-not_independent_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-not_independent-26">[26]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-guardian_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-guardian-27">[27]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> and it has been criticised by the <a href="/wiki/Campaign_for_Tobacco-Free_Kids" title="Campaign for Tobacco-Free Kids">Campaign for Tobacco-Free Kids</a>, <a href="/wiki/Action_on_Smoking_and_Health" title="Action on Smoking and Health">Action on Smoking and Health</a> (ASH), and <a href="/wiki/Corporate_Accountability_International" title="Corporate Accountability International">Corporate Accountability International</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-guardian_27-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-guardian-27">[27]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/American_Cancer_Society" title="American Cancer Society">American Cancer Society</a> stated, "This attempt by Philip Morris International to paint itself as a public health partner is manipulative and dangerous. It is a new twist out of the tobacco industry’s deadly playbook, but nobody should be fooled. It’s a continuation of a decades-long effort to paint over tobacco’s role in spreading death and misery around the globe."<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(6)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Controversies">Controversies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Philip_Morris_International&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Controversies" data-section="8" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-6 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-6">
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Australia">Australia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Philip_Morris_International&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Australia" data-section="9" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>The Australian Government announced it would introduce "<a href="/wiki/Tobacco_Plain_Packaging_Laws" class="mw-redirect" title="Tobacco Plain Packaging Laws">Tobacco Plain Packaging Laws</a>" on 29 April 2010. Philip Morris International (PMI), arranged for its wholly owned Hong Kong subsidiary Philip Morris Asia (PMA) to 'takeover' two Australian subsidiaries - Philip Morris Australia Limited and Philip Morris Limited on 23 February 2011. In June 2011, Philip Morris International announced that it was using <a href="/wiki/Investor_State_Dispute_Settlement" class="mw-redirect" title="Investor State Dispute Settlement">ISDS</a> provisions in the Australia-Hong Kong Bilateral Investment treaty (BIT) to demand compensation for Australia's <a href="/wiki/Plain_cigarette_packaging" class="mw-redirect" title="Plain cigarette packaging">plain cigarette packaging</a> anti-smoking legislation.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> It was one of several tobacco companies to launch legal action against the <a href="/wiki/Australian_Government" class="mw-redirect" title="Australian Government">Australian Government</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup>
British American Tobacco, Philip Morris, Imperial Tobacco and Japan Tobacco International are taking the Australian government to the <a href="/wiki/High_Court_of_Australia" title="High Court of Australia">High Court of Australia</a> to try to stop the government of Australia from introducing plain packaging for tobacco products.
</p><p>Two challenges to the tobacco plain packaging legislation were heard by the High Court of Australia between 17 and 19 April 2012: 'British American Tobacco Australasia Limited and Ors v. Commonwealth of Australia' and 'J T International SA v. Commonwealth of Australia'.
</p><p>On 15 August 2012, the High Court handed down orders for these matters, and found that the Tobacco Plain Packaging Act 2011 is not contrary to s 51(xxxi) of the Constitution. On 5 October 2012, the Court handed down its reasons for the decision. By a 6:1 majority (Heydon J in dissent) the Court held that there had been no acquisition of property that would have required provision of 'just terms' under s51(xxxi) of the Constitution.<sup id="cite_ref-tobaccoplainpackaging_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-tobaccoplainpackaging-32">[32]</a></sup></p><p>On 18 December 2015, the Tribunal instituted by the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Commission_on_International_Trade_Law" title="United Nations Commission on International Trade Law">United Nations Commission on International Trade Law</a> (UNCITRAL) issued a unanimous decision (3-0) agreeing with Australia's position that the Tribunal has no jurisdiction to hear PMA's claim. This was due to the fact that PMI used its wholly owned subsidiary PMA to takeover the Australian-based PM subsidiaries in order to specifically sue the Australian Government for bringing in plain packaging laws. PMI was unable to do this itself as the Australia–United States free-trade agreement signed in 2004 did not have any <a href="/wiki/Investor-state_dispute_settlement" title="Investor-state dispute settlement">investor-state dispute settlement</a> clauses included—by design.<sup id="cite_ref-tobaccoplainpackaging_32-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-tobaccoplainpackaging-32">[32]</a></sup></p><p>In 2017, the <a href="/wiki/Dispute_Settlement_Body" title="Dispute Settlement Body">Dispute Settlement Body</a> of the <a href="/wiki/World_Trade_Organization" title="World Trade Organization">World Trade Organization</a> supported Australia's right to enforce plain packaging.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> In 2017, PMI was instructed to pay the Australia government's legal costs, an estimated 50 million dollars.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></p><p>In March 2018, the Tobacco giant announced that it will cut 150 jobs as part of a major restructure. Tammy Chan, the managing director in Australia, said more efficient ways to deal with retailers were introduced based on digital technology development.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="European_Union">European Union</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Philip_Morris_International&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: European Union" data-section="10" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>In 2004, Philip Morris and the European Union reached an agreement according to which Philip Morris would pay $1.25bn until 2016 to end a lawsuit over smuggling charges.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Norway">Norway</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Philip_Morris_International&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Norway" data-section="11" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>Philip Morris also sued Norway over the country's ban on displaying tobacco products in stores. It lost the case in 2012.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Uruguay">Uruguay</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Philip_Morris_International&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Uruguay" data-section="12" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>In 2010, the company lobbied against <a href="/wiki/Uruguay" title="Uruguay">Uruguay</a>'s strong anti-smoking laws<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> and filed a complaint against the country (<i><a href="/wiki/Philip_Morris_v._Uruguay" title="Philip Morris v. Uruguay">Philip Morris v. Uruguay</a></i>) under the Switzerland-Uruguay <a href="/wiki/Bilateral_investment_treaty" title="Bilateral investment treaty">bilateral investment treaty</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-bbc_39-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bbc-39">[39]</a></sup> On 8 July 2016, the <a href="/wiki/International_Centre_for_Settlement_of_Investment_Disputes" title="International Centre for Settlement of Investment Disputes">International Centre for Settlement of Investment Disputes</a> ruled in favour of Uruguay.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="United_Kingdom">United Kingdom</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Philip_Morris_International&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: United Kingdom" data-section="13" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>In August 2014, the company foreshadowed legal action against the <a href="/wiki/Government_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Government of the United Kingdom">Government of the United Kingdom</a> if it went ahead with plans to introduce plain packaging. In a submission to the government, Philip Morris International said it would seek compensation running into "billions of pounds," if the proposed legislation went ahead.<sup id="cite_ref-PMIpackaging_41-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PMIpackaging-41">[41]</a></sup></p><p>In 2018, an advertising campaign was criticized as hypocritical for urging smokers to quit while promoting other products such as heated tobacco.<sup id="cite_ref-UK_anti-smoking_ad_42-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-UK_anti-smoking_ad-42">[42]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="IQOS">IQOS</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Philip_Morris_International&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: IQOS" data-section="14" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Heat-not-burn_product#IQOS" class="mw-redirect" title="Heat-not-burn product">Heat-not-burn product § IQOS</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Girl_smoking_tobacco_device_IQOS.jpg" class="image"><noscript><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7a/Girl_smoking_tobacco_device_IQOS.jpg/220px-Girl_smoking_tobacco_device_IQOS.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="5021" data-file-height="3346"></noscript><span class="lazy-image-placeholder" style="width: 220px;height: 147px;" data-src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7a/Girl_smoking_tobacco_device_IQOS.jpg/220px-Girl_smoking_tobacco_device_IQOS.jpg" data-alt="" data-width="220" data-height="147" data-class="thumbimage"> </span></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Girl_smoking_tobacco_device_IQOS.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A person using the IQOS device.</div></div></div>
<p>In 2017, according to two editors of the journal <i><a href="/wiki/JAMA_Internal_Medicine" title="JAMA Internal Medicine">JAMA Internal Medicine</a></i>, after publication of a research letter describing harmful chemicals in <a href="/wiki/Heat-not-burn_product" class="mw-redirect" title="Heat-not-burn product">heat-not-burn tobacco products</a>, people from Philip Morris International contacted the institutions where the researchers worked and questioned the methods used in the study; the editors described this as a form of "pressure to suppress discourse that could harm commercial interests".<sup id="cite_ref-Katz2017_43-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Katz2017-43">[43]</a></sup></p><p>In December 2017, <a href="/wiki/Reuters" title="Reuters">Reuters</a> published documents and testimonies of former employees detailing irregularities in the clinical trials conducted by Philip Morris International for the approval of the IQOS product by the FDA.<sup id="cite_ref-Lasseter_44-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lasseter-44">[44]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Other">Other</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Philip_Morris_International&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Other" data-section="15" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Advancement_of_Sound_Science_Center" title="Advancement of Sound Science Center">Advancement of Sound Science Center</a></div>
<p>From the 1970s to the late 1990s, Phillip Morris along with <a href="/wiki/British_American_Tobacco" title="British American Tobacco">British American Tobacco</a>, was involved in campaigns to undermine bans against smoking in <a href="/wiki/Muslim_majority_countries" class="mw-redirect" title="Muslim majority countries">Muslim majority countries</a> by branding Muslims who opposed smoking as a "'fundamentalist’ who wishes to return to <a href="/wiki/Shariah" class="mw-redirect" title="Shariah">sharia law</a>," and be "a threat to existing government as” according to leaked documents.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_45-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-45">[45]</a></sup> A 1985 report from Philip Morris squarely blamed the <a href="/wiki/World_Health_Organization" title="World Health Organization">World Health Organization</a>: “This ideological development has become a threat to our business because of the interference of the WHO [...] The WHO has not only joined forces with Muslim fundamentalists who view smoking as evil, but has gone yet further by encouraging religious leaders previously not active anti-smokers to take up the cause."<sup id="cite_ref-:0_45-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-45">[45]</a></sup> Philip Morris has refused to comment on these findings.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_45-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-45">[45]</a></sup></p><p>Philip Morris International has announced an overhaul of its human rights protections of tobacco workers in Kazakhstan and 30 other countries after critical reports.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup></p><p>The company runs an information web site outlining the health issues of tobacco. However, it has been criticised in an article in the journal Public Health Nursing as merely a "public relations effort" intended to "undermine public health".<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup></p><p>In the 1930s, the company's tobacco advertisements were a steady source of income for numerous medical organizations and journals, including the <i><a href="/wiki/New_England_Journal_of_Medicine" class="mw-redirect" title="New England Journal of Medicine">New England Journal of Medicine</a></i> (NEJM) and the <i><a href="/wiki/Journal_of_the_American_Medical_Association" class="mw-redirect" title="Journal of the American Medical Association">Journal of the American Medical Association</a></i> (JAMA).<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup></p><p>In February 2015, <a href="/wiki/John_Oliver_(comedian)" class="mw-redirect" title="John Oliver (comedian)">John Oliver</a> highlighted the company's many international legal cases on an <a href="/wiki/Tobacco_(Last_Week_Tonight)" title="Tobacco (Last Week Tonight)">episode</a> of his television show <i><a href="/wiki/Last_Week_Tonight" class="mw-redirect" title="Last Week Tonight">Last Week Tonight</a></i>. He also attempted to raise awareness for his campaign using the hashtag #<a href="/wiki/JeffWeCan" class="mw-redirect" title="JeffWeCan">JeffWeCan</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup></p>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(7)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Sponsorship">Sponsorship</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Philip_Morris_International&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Sponsorship" data-section="16" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-7 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-7">
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Marlboro-Ferrari.jpg" class="image"><noscript><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/de/Marlboro-Ferrari.jpg/220px-Marlboro-Ferrari.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="199" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="1743" data-file-height="1580"></noscript><span class="lazy-image-placeholder" style="width: 220px;height: 199px;" data-src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/de/Marlboro-Ferrari.jpg/220px-Marlboro-Ferrari.jpg" data-alt="" data-width="220" data-height="199" data-class="thumbimage"> </span></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Marlboro-Ferrari.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Marlboro_(cigarette)" title="Marlboro (cigarette)">Marlboro</a> sponsored Ferrari Racing Team on <a href="/wiki/2006_Bahrain_Grand_Prix" title="2006 Bahrain Grand Prix">2006 Bahrain Grand Prix</a>.</div></div></div>
<p>Philip Morris is a long-term main sponsor of the <a href="/wiki/Formula_One" title="Formula One">Formula One</a> team <a href="/wiki/Scuderia_Ferrari" title="Scuderia Ferrari">Scuderia Ferrari</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> The sponsorship is subliminal in the logo these days due to restriction in tobacco sponsorship, but Marlboro-branded Ferrari and <a href="/wiki/Team_McLaren" class="mw-redirect" title="Team McLaren">McLaren</a> cars won several world titles with famous drivers such as <a href="/wiki/Alain_Prost" title="Alain Prost">Alain Prost</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ayrton_Senna" title="Ayrton Senna">Ayrton Senna</a> and <a href="/wiki/Michael_Schumacher" title="Michael Schumacher">Michael Schumacher</a>. Philip Morris also sponsored several title winners in <a href="/wiki/MotoGP" class="mw-redirect" title="MotoGP">MotoGP</a> in <a href="/wiki/Road_racing" title="Road racing">road racing</a> and <a href="/wiki/Indy_Cars" class="mw-redirect" title="Indy Cars">Indy Cars</a>. The Ferrari Formula One deal before direct sponsorships was banned was estimated to be worth £45 million a year as well as paying the multi-million salary of Schumacher.<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup></p><p>Despite no longer being able to display the Marlboro logo on Ferrari cars, Philip Morris renewed its sponsorship deal with Ferrari in 2011,<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup> 2015,<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup> 2017,<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup> and 2018 up until 2021.<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup> The 2017 deal was reported to be worth $160 million a year.<sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup></p><p>Philip Morris' sponsorship of Ferrari was seen visually on the car again at the <a href="/wiki/2018_Japanese_Grand_Prix" title="2018 Japanese Grand Prix">2018 Japanese Grand Prix</a>, with the cigarette company's "Mission Winnow" branding. This branding has been seen by authorities as an attempt to flout laws and rules banning tobacco advertising, and it was removed by Ferrari for the <a href="/wiki/2019_Australian_Grand_Prix" title="2019 Australian Grand Prix">2019 Australian Grand Prix</a> after Australian authorities launched an investigation.<sup id="cite_ref-Scott_Mitchell_57-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Scott_Mitchell-57">[57]</a></sup> Ferrari also decided to remove the branding for the <a href="/wiki/2019_Canadian_Grand_Prix" title="2019 Canadian Grand Prix">2019 Canadian Grand Prix</a> and the <a href="/wiki/2019_French_Grand_Prix" title="2019 French Grand Prix">2019 French Grand Prix</a> to avoid problems with bans on tobacco advertising.<sup id="cite_ref-Scott_Mitchell_57-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Scott_Mitchell-57">[57]</a></sup></p>
