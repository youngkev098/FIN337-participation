<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">"Tiffany's" redirects here. For other uses, see <a href="/wiki/Tiffany_(disambiguation)" class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" title="Tiffany (disambiguation)">Tiffany (disambiguation)</a>.</div>
<p><b>Tiffany &amp; Co.</b> (colloquially known as <b>Tiffany's</b>)<sup id="cite_ref-10-K_FY_Jan._31,_2018_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10-K_FY_Jan._31,_2018-2">[2]</a></sup> is an American luxury <a href="/wiki/Jewellery" title="Jewellery">jewelry</a> and specialty retailer owned by <a href="/wiki/LVMH" title="LVMH">LVMH</a> headquartered in New York City.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> It sells jewelry, <a href="/wiki/Sterling_silver" title="Sterling silver">sterling silver</a>, china, crystal, stationery, fragrances, water bottles, watches, personal accessories, and leather goods.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Tiffany is known for its <a href="/wiki/Luxury_goods" title="Luxury goods">luxury goods</a>, particularly its diamond and sterling silver jewelry.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> It markets itself as an arbiter of taste and style.<sup id="cite_ref-fundinguniverse.com_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fundinguniverse.com-9">[9]</a></sup> These goods are sold at Tiffany stores, and through direct-mail and corporate merchandising.

The company operates retail outlets in the Americas, Asia-Pacific, Japan, Europe and the United Arab Emirates. In 2018, Tiffany had 93 stores in the US and 321 stores worldwide; net sales totaled US$4.44 billion.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>

</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(1)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tiffany_%26_Co.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History" data-section="1" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-1 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-1">
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Establishment">Establishment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tiffany_%26_Co.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Establishment" data-section="2" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Tiffany_Co_Union_Square_1887.png" class="image"><noscript><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a8/Tiffany_Co_Union_Square_1887.png/220px-Tiffany_Co_Union_Square_1887.png" decoding="async" width="220" height="173" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="798" data-file-height="629"></noscript><span class="lazy-image-placeholder" style="width: 220px;height: 173px;" data-src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a8/Tiffany_Co_Union_Square_1887.png/220px-Tiffany_Co_Union_Square_1887.png" data-alt="" data-width="220" data-height="173" data-class="thumbimage"> </span></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Tiffany_Co_Union_Square_1887.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Tiffany &amp; Company, Union Square, storage area with porcelain, c. 1887</div></div></div>
<p>Founded in 1837 by <a href="/wiki/Charles_Lewis_Tiffany" title="Charles Lewis Tiffany">Charles Lewis Tiffany</a> and John B. Young<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> in <a href="/wiki/Brooklyn,_Connecticut" title="Brooklyn, Connecticut">Brooklyn, Connecticut</a>, as a "stationery and fancy goods emporium", with the help of Charles Tiffany's father who financed the store for only $1,000.<sup id="cite_ref-Tiffany_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Tiffany-13">[13]</a></sup> The store initially sold a wide variety of stationery items, and operated as "Tiffany, Young and Ellis" as of 1838 at 259 Broadway in <a href="/wiki/Lower_Manhattan" title="Lower Manhattan">Lower Manhattan</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> The name was shortened to Tiffany &amp; Company in 1853, when Charles Tiffany took control and established the firm's emphasis on jewelry.<sup id="cite_ref-mention_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mention-15">[15]</a></sup> The company has since opened stores in major cities worldwide. Unlike other stores at the time in the 1830s, Tiffany clearly marked the prices on its goods to forestall any haggling over prices. In addition, against the social norm at the time, Tiffany only accepted cash payments, and did not allow purchases on credit.<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-16">[16]</a></sup> Such practices (fixed prices for ready money) were first introduced in 1750 by Palmer's of London Bridge.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span id=".22Blue_Book.22_and_the_Civil_War"></span><span class="mw-headline" id='"Blue_Book"_and_the_Civil_War'>"Blue Book" and the Civil War</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tiffany_%26_Co.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title='Edit section: "Blue Book" and the Civil War' data-section="3" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>The first Tiffany <a href="/wiki/Mail_order" title="Mail order">mail order</a> catalog, known as the "Blue Book," was published in 1845 in the United States (U.S.),<sup id="cite_ref-tiffany.com_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-tiffany.com-18">[18]</a></sup> and publishing of the catalog continues in the 21st century. In 1862, Tiffany supplied the <a href="/wiki/Union_Army" title="Union Army">Union Army</a> with swords (<a href="/wiki/Model_1840_Cavalry_Saber" title="Model 1840 Cavalry Saber">Model 1840 Cavalry Saber</a>), flags and surgical implements. In 1867, Tiffany was the first U.S. firm to win an award for excellence in silverware at the <i><a href="/wiki/Exposition_Universelle_(1867)" title="Exposition Universelle (1867)">Exposition Universelle</a></i> in Paris. In 1868, Tiffany was incorporated.<sup id="cite_ref-fundinguniverse.com_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fundinguniverse.com-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span id=".22Gilded_Age.22"></span><span class="mw-headline" id='"Gilded_Age"'>"Gilded Age"</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tiffany_%26_Co.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title='Edit section: "Gilded Age"' data-section="4" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>In 1870, the company built a new store building at <a href="/wiki/15_Union_Square_West" title="15 Union Square West">15 Union Square West</a>, Manhattan, designed by <a href="/wiki/John_Kellum" title="John Kellum">John Kellum</a> and cost $500,000. It was described by <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times" title="The New York Times">The New York Times</a></i> as a "palace of jewels".<sup id="cite_ref-Moved_uptown_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Moved_uptown-19">[19]</a></sup> Tiffany stayed at this site until 1906.<sup id="cite_ref-Moved_uptown_19-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Moved_uptown-19">[19]</a></sup></p><p>In 1877, an insignia that would become the <a href="/wiki/New_York_Yankees" title="New York Yankees">New York Yankees</a> "NY" logo was struck on a police medal of honor by Tiffany; the Yankees adopted the logo in 1909. In 1878, Tiffany won the gold medal for jewelry and a grand prize for silverware at the Paris Exposition. In 1879, Tiffany purchased one of  the world's largest yellow diamonds which became known as the Tiffany Diamond. The Tiffany Diamond was worn by only two people, one of which was Audrey Hepburn for the promotion of "Breakfast at Tiffany's".<sup id="cite_ref-Tiffany_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Tiffany-13">[13]</a></sup> In 1887, Tiffany bought the <a href="/wiki/French_Crown_Jewels" title="French Crown Jewels">French Crown Jewels</a>, which attracted publicity and further solidified the Tiffany brand's association with high-quality diamonds.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> The company revised the <a href="/wiki/Great_Seal_of_the_United_States" title="Great Seal of the United States">Great Seal of the United States</a> in 1885. In 1902, after the death of <a href="/wiki/Charles_Lewis_Tiffany" title="Charles Lewis Tiffany">Charles Lewis Tiffany</a>, his son, <a href="/wiki/Louis_Comfort_Tiffany" title="Louis Comfort Tiffany">Louis Comfort Tiffany</a>, became the company's first official design director.<sup id="cite_ref-tiffany.com_18-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-tiffany.com-18">[18]</a></sup>
In 1906, the Manhattan flagship store was relocated to a new location, at the corner of 37th Street and Fifth Avenue, where it would remain for 34 years.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span id="1900.E2.80.931999"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="1900–1999">1900–1999</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tiffany_%26_Co.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: 1900–1999" data-section="5" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Tiffany_Cross_Medal_of_Honor.jpg" class="image"><noscript><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/96/Tiffany_Cross_Medal_of_Honor.jpg/170px-Tiffany_Cross_Medal_of_Honor.jpg" decoding="async" width="170" height="213" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="2427" data-file-height="3036"></noscript><span class="lazy-image-placeholder" style="width: 170px;height: 213px;" data-src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/96/Tiffany_Cross_Medal_of_Honor.jpg/170px-Tiffany_Cross_Medal_of_Honor.jpg" data-alt="" data-width="170" data-height="213" data-class="thumbimage"> </span></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Tiffany_Cross_Medal_of_Honor.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Tiffany_Cross_Medal_of_Honor" title="Tiffany Cross Medal of Honor">"Tiffany Cross" version</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Medal_of_Honor" title="Medal of Honor">Medal of Honor</a></div></div></div>
<p>The company has not only sold the most brilliant diamonds but also introduced many previously unknown colored gems throughout history. In 1902, Tiffany unveiled <a href="/wiki/Kunzite" class="mw-redirect" title="Kunzite">kunzite</a>, a purplish pink gem discovered in California, named after Tiffany's notable gemologist <a href="/wiki/George_Frederick_Kunz" title="George Frederick Kunz">George Frederick Kunz</a>. In 1910, the <a href="/wiki/Morganite" class="mw-redirect" title="Morganite">morganite</a> was unearthed in Madagascar and was named after Tiffany's most loyal customer, banking tycoon <a href="/wiki/J._P._Morgan" title="J. P. Morgan">John Pierpont Morgan</a>. More discoveries came such as the <a href="/wiki/Tanzanite" title="Tanzanite">tanzanite</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Tsavorite" title="Tsavorite">tsavorite</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p><p>In 1919, the company made a revision to the <a href="/wiki/Medal_of_Honor" title="Medal of Honor">Medal of Honor</a> on behalf of the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_the_Navy" title="United States Department of the Navy">United States Department of the Navy</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> This <a href="/wiki/Tiffany_Cross_Medal_of_Honor" title="Tiffany Cross Medal of Honor">"Tiffany Cross" version</a> was rare because it was awarded only for combat, using the previous design for non-combat awards.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> In 1942, the Navy established the Tiffany version for <i>non</i>-combat heroism as well but, in August 1942, the Navy subsequently eliminated the Tiffany Cross and the two-medal system.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p><p>In 1956, legendary designer <a href="/wiki/Jean_Schlumberger_(jewelry_designer)" title="Jean Schlumberger (jewelry designer)">Jean Schlumberger</a> joined Tiffany, and <a href="/wiki/Andy_Warhol" title="Andy Warhol">Andy Warhol</a> collaborated with the company to create Tiffany holiday cards (circa 1956–1962).<sup id="cite_ref-tiffany.com_18-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-tiffany.com-18">[18]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> In 1968, <a href="/wiki/Lady_Bird_Johnson" title="Lady Bird Johnson">Lady Bird Johnson</a>, First Lady of the U.S. at the time, commissioned Tiffany to design a <a href="/wiki/White_House_china" title="White House china">White House china</a>-service that featured 90 flowers.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-nfll_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nfll-28">[28]</a></sup></p><p>In November 1978, Tiffany &amp; Co. was sold to <a href="/wiki/Avon_Products_Inc." class="mw-redirect" title="Avon Products Inc.">Avon Products Inc.</a> for about US$104 million in stock. However, in a 1984 <i><a href="/wiki/Newsweek" title="Newsweek">Newsweek</a></i> article, the <a href="/wiki/Fifth_Avenue" title="Fifth Avenue">Fifth Avenue</a> Tiffany store was likened to the <a href="/wiki/Macy%27s" title="Macy's">Macy's</a> department store during a <a href="/wiki/White_sale" title="White sale">white sale</a>, due to the high number of inexpensive items on sale;<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_16-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-16">[16]</a></sup> furthermore, customers complained about declining quality and service. In August 1984, Avon sold Tiffany to an investor group led by William R. Chaney for $135.5 million in cash. Tiffany <a href="/wiki/Went_public" class="mw-redirect" title="Went public">went public</a> again in 1987 and raised about $103.5 million from the sale of 4.5 million shares of <a href="/wiki/Common_stock" title="Common stock">common stock</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_16-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-16">[16]</a></sup></p><p>Due to the <a href="/wiki/1990%E2%80%931991_recession" class="mw-redirect" title="1990–1991 recession">1990–1991 recession</a> in the United States, Tiffany commenced an emphasis upon <a href="/wiki/Mass_merchandising" class="mw-redirect" title="Mass merchandising">mass merchandising</a>. A new campaign was launched that stressed how Tiffany could be affordable for all; for example, the company advertised that the price of diamond engagement rings started at $850. “How to Buy a Diamond” brochures were sent to 40,000 people, who called a toll-free number specifically set up to target the broader population.<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_16-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-16">[16]</a></sup> However, to maintain its image as a <a href="/wiki/Luxury_goods" title="Luxury goods">luxury goods</a> company, high-style images remained on display in Tiffany stores.<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_16-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span id="2000.E2.80.93present"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="2000–present">2000–present</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tiffany_%26_Co.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: 2000–present" data-section="6" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Tiffany_blue_box_2.jpg" class="image"><noscript><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f2/Tiffany_blue_box_2.jpg/170px-Tiffany_blue_box_2.jpg" decoding="async" width="170" height="227" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="2448" data-file-height="3264"></noscript><span class="lazy-image-placeholder" style="width: 170px;height: 227px;" data-src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f2/Tiffany_blue_box_2.jpg/170px-Tiffany_blue_box_2.jpg" data-alt="" data-width="170" data-height="227" data-class="thumbimage"> </span></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Tiffany_blue_box_2.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Tiffany &amp; Co. iconic <a href="/wiki/Tiffany_Blue" title="Tiffany Blue">blue</a> gift boxes</div></div></div>
<p>The Tiffany &amp; Co. Foundation was established in 2000 to provide grants to nonprofit organizations working in the areas of the environment and the arts.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> In June 2004, Tiffany sued <a href="/wiki/EBay" title="EBay">eBay</a>, claiming that the latter was making profits from the sale of counterfeit Tiffany products;<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> however, Tiffany lost both at <a href="/wiki/Tiffany_(NJ)_Inc._v._eBay_Inc." title="Tiffany (NJ) Inc. v. eBay Inc.">trial and on appeal</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p><p>In 2009, a collaboration between the Japanese mobile-phone operator <a href="/wiki/SoftBank" class="mw-redirect" title="SoftBank">SoftBank</a> and Tiffany &amp; Co. was announced. The two companies designed a cellphone, limited to ten copies, and containing more than 400 diamonds, totaling more than 20 carats (4.0 g). Each cellphone cost more than 100 million yen (<a href="/wiki/Pound_sterling" title="Pound sterling">£</a>781,824).<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p><p>A media report in early July 2013 revealed that former Tiffany &amp; Co. vice president Ingrid Lederhaas-Okun had been arrested and charged with stealing more than $1.3 million of diamond bracelets, drop earrings, and other jewelry. According to prosecutors from <a href="/wiki/Manhattan" title="Manhattan">Manhattan</a>, the official charges filed against Lederhaas-Okun accused her of "wire fraud and interstate transportation of stolen property."<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p><p>In February 2017, the company announced that <a href="/wiki/Chief_executive_officer" title="Chief executive officer">CEO</a> <a href="/wiki/Frederic_Cumenal" title="Frederic Cumenal">Frédéric Cuménal</a> was out of a job, effective immediately, after only 22 months, blaming weak sales results. He was replaced on an <a href="/wiki/Interim" title="Interim">interim</a> basis by the company's longtime former CEO, <a href="/wiki/Michael_J._Kowalski" title="Michael J. Kowalski">Michael Kowalski</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> Shortly before his abrupt departure, Cuménal had appointed former <a href="/wiki/Coach_New_York" title="Coach New York">Coach</a> designer <a href="/wiki/Reed_Krakoff" title="Reed Krakoff">Reed Krakoff</a> as the company's new chief artistic officer. Although Krakoff had had no previous experience with jewellery design, his previous success with Coach and "deep understanding of iconic American design" led to his appointment, with the hopes that Krakoff would be able to refresh the image of the brand.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></p><p>In July 2017, it was announced that <a href="/wiki/Bulgari" title="Bulgari">Bulgari</a> veteran Alessandro Bogliolo would be taking over as CEO. Under his leadership, it was hoped that Tiffany &amp; Co. could turn around slumping sales and capture a younger audience.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></p><p>In October 2019, the company removed an online advertisement after it was accused of portraying support for the <a href="/wiki/2019_Hong_Kong_protests" class="mw-redirect" title="2019 Hong Kong protests">2019 Hong Kong protests</a> and potentially jeopardizing its business in China.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></p><p>In November 2019, <a href="/wiki/LVMH" title="LVMH">LVMH</a> announced its purchase of Tiffany &amp; Co for $16.2 billion, $135 per share.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> The deal was expected to close by June 2020.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup></p><p>Tiffany opened its first store in New Delhi, India on 3 February 2020.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup></p>

<p>Since 1940, Tiffany's <a href="/wiki/Flagship_store" class="mw-redirect" title="Flagship store">flagship store</a> has operated at the corner of <a href="/wiki/Fifth_Avenue" title="Fifth Avenue">Fifth Avenue</a> and <a href="/wiki/57th_Street_(Manhattan)" title="57th Street (Manhattan)">57th Street</a> in <a href="/wiki/Manhattan" title="Manhattan">Manhattan</a>, New York City. The polished granite exterior is well known for its window displays, and the store has been the location for a number of films, including <i><a href="/wiki/Breakfast_at_Tiffany%27s_(film)" title="Breakfast at Tiffany's (film)">Breakfast at Tiffany's</a></i>, starring <a href="/wiki/Audrey_Hepburn" title="Audrey Hepburn">Audrey Hepburn</a>, and <i><a href="/wiki/Sweet_Home_Alabama_(film)" title="Sweet Home Alabama (film)">Sweet Home Alabama</a></i>, starring <a href="/wiki/Reese_Witherspoon" title="Reese Witherspoon">Reese Witherspoon</a>. The former <a href="/wiki/Tiffany_and_Company_Building" title="Tiffany and Company Building">Tiffany and Company Building</a> on 37th Street is on the U.S. <a href="/wiki/National_Register_of_Historic_Places" title="National Register of Historic Places">National Register of Historic Places</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup></p><p>When it opened in 1990, the Tiffany &amp; Co. store at <a href="/wiki/Fairfax_Square" title="Fairfax Square">Fairfax Square</a> in <a href="/wiki/Tysons_Corner,_Virginia" class="mw-redirect" title="Tysons Corner, Virginia">Tysons Corner, Virginia</a>, became the largest outside of New York City, with 14,500 sq ft (1,350 m<sup>2</sup>) of retail space.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup></p><p>In France, Tiffany stores are located in <a href="/wiki/Rue_de_la_Paix,_Paris" title="Rue de la Paix, Paris">Rue de la Paix</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Champs-%C3%89lys%C3%A9es" title="Champs-Élysées">Avenue des Champs Elysées</a> (the largest European store<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup>) in <a href="/wiki/Paris" title="Paris">Paris</a>, but also in the <a href="/wiki/Galeries_Lafayette" title="Galeries Lafayette">Galeries Lafayette</a> and <a href="/wiki/Printemps" title="Printemps">Printemps</a> <a href="/wiki/Department_store" title="Department store">department stores</a>, in Terminal 2 at Paris' <a href="/wiki/Charles_de_Gaulle_Airport" title="Charles de Gaulle Airport">Charles de Gaulle airport</a> (two stores), and on the <a href="/wiki/French_Riviera" title="French Riviera">French Riviera</a> in <a href="/wiki/Nice" title="Nice">Nice</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p><p>In the United Kingdom (UK), Tiffany stores are located in Terminal 5, at London's <a href="/wiki/Heathrow_airport" class="mw-redirect" title="Heathrow airport">Heathrow airport</a> (opened at the end of March 2008), in the <a href="/wiki/Westfield_London" title="Westfield London">Westfield London</a> shopping centre in Shepherd's Bush, in <a href="/wiki/Old_Bond_Street" class="mw-redirect" title="Old Bond Street">Old Bond Street</a>, opposite the entrance to <a href="/wiki/Burlington_Gardens" title="Burlington Gardens">Burlington Gardens</a>, and in Manchester, Selfridges Exchange Square. A flagship Irish store was opened in <a href="/wiki/Brown_Thomas" title="Brown Thomas">Brown Thomas</a> on Dublin's <a href="/wiki/Grafton_Street" title="Grafton Street">Grafton Street</a> in October 2008 and is the second largest of the company's European outlets. Also in October 2008, Tiffany's opened a store in <a href="/wiki/Madrid" title="Madrid">Madrid</a>, Spain, and brought the <a href="/wiki/Tiffany_Yellow_Diamond" title="Tiffany Yellow Diamond">Tiffany Yellow Diamond</a> (pictured at right) to the opening.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>, Tiffany's flagship store is located on <a href="/wiki/Collins_Street,_Melbourne" title="Collins Street, Melbourne">Collins Street</a> in <a href="/wiki/Melbourne" title="Melbourne">Melbourne</a>, first established in 1996.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> Other stores include <a href="/wiki/Chadstone_Shopping_Centre" title="Chadstone Shopping Centre">Chadstone Shopping Centre</a> (Melbourne); <a href="/wiki/Sydney" title="Sydney">Sydney</a> (<a href="/wiki/Castlereagh_Street,_Sydney" class="mw-redirect" title="Castlereagh Street, Sydney">Castlereagh Street</a>, <a href="/wiki/Westfield_Bondi_Junction" title="Westfield Bondi Junction">Westfield Bondi Junction</a> and <a href="/wiki/DFS_Galleria" class="mw-redirect" title="DFS Galleria">DFS Galleria</a> on <a href="/wiki/George_Street,_Sydney" title="George Street, Sydney">George Street</a>); <a href="/wiki/Brisbane" title="Brisbane">Brisbane</a> (<a href="/wiki/Queens_Plaza,_Brisbane" class="mw-redirect" title="Queens Plaza, Brisbane">Queens Plaza</a>); and <a href="/wiki/Perth,_Western_Australia" class="mw-redirect" title="Perth, Western Australia">Perth</a> (<a href="/wiki/King_Street,_Perth" title="King Street, Perth">King Street</a>).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a>、Tiffany's flagship store is located in <a href="/wiki/Ginza" title="Ginza">Ginza</a> and opened in 2009.  They also have a large store in <a href="/wiki/Shinjuku" title="Shinjuku">Shinjuku</a> (opened in 2014) and many other branches.
</p><p>On March 8, 2001, Tiffany launched its first Latin American store in <a href="/wiki/S%C3%A3o_Paulo" title="São Paulo">São Paulo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">Brazil</a>, located in the <a href="/wiki/Iguatemi_S%C3%A3o_Paulo" title="Iguatemi São Paulo">Iguatemi São Paulo shopping center</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> The company opened a second store in the city on October 20, 2003,<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> near the famous <a href="/wiki/Rua_Oscar_Freire" title="Rua Oscar Freire">Oscar Freire Street</a>. The last store opened was <a href="/wiki/Curitiba" title="Curitiba">Curitiba</a> in September 2013, now Tiffany has stores in <a href="/wiki/S%C3%A3o_Paulo" title="São Paulo">São Paulo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rio_de_Janeiro" title="Rio de Janeiro">Rio de Janeiro</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bras%C3%ADlia" title="Brasília">Brasília</a> e <a href="/wiki/Curitiba" title="Curitiba">Curitiba</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p><p>In 2004, Tiffany created "Iridesse", a chain of stores dedicated to pearl-only jewelry. The company operated 16 stores in <a href="/wiki/Florida" title="Florida">Florida</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_Jersey" title="New Jersey">New Jersey</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_York_(state)" title="New York (state)">New York</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pennsylvania" title="Pennsylvania">Pennsylvania</a>, <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a>, <a href="/wiki/Illinois" title="Illinois">Illinois</a>, <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts" title="Massachusetts">Massachusetts</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Virginia" title="Virginia">Virginia</a>. However, the chain operated at a loss since its founding and the company announced in early 2009 that, despite its continued belief in the concept, it would discontinue Iridesse due to the <a href="/wiki/Financial_crisis_of_2007-2008" class="mw-redirect" title="Financial crisis of 2007-2008">financial crisis of 2007-2008</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup></p><p>Tiffany &amp; Co. reported in 2006 that its location at <a href="/wiki/South_Coast_Plaza" title="South Coast Plaza">South Coast Plaza</a> in <a href="/wiki/Costa_Mesa,_California" title="Costa Mesa, California">Costa Mesa, California</a>, was its most profitable location, followed by the New York City flagship store, the <a href="/wiki/Boston" title="Boston">Boston</a>, Massachusetts outlet in <a href="/wiki/Copley_Place" title="Copley Place">Copley Place</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Ala_Moana_Shopping_Center" class="mw-redirect" title="Ala Moana Shopping Center">Ala Moana Shopping Center</a> in <a href="/wiki/Honolulu" title="Honolulu">Honolulu</a>, Hawaii.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p><p>Tiffany &amp; Co. announced its second store opening at <a href="/wiki/Pavilion_Kuala_Lumpur" title="Pavilion Kuala Lumpur">Pavilion Kuala Lumpur</a> in <a href="/wiki/Kuala_Lumpur,_Malaysia" class="mw-redirect" title="Kuala Lumpur, Malaysia">Kuala Lumpur, Malaysia</a> in September 2007 to coincide with the shopping mall's opening. The store consists of 1,700 sq ft (160 m<sup>2</sup>) retail space and features the same decor elements as the New York City flagship store. Later that year, other stores were opened in the U.S., such as the <a href="/wiki/Natick_Collection" class="mw-redirect" title="Natick Collection">Natick Collection</a> in <a href="/wiki/Natick,_MA" class="mw-redirect" title="Natick, MA">Natick</a>, Massachusetts, which opened in September 2007, <a href="/wiki/Mohegan_Sun" title="Mohegan Sun">Mohegan Sun</a> Erika's casino in <a href="/wiki/Connecticut" title="Connecticut">Connecticut</a>, and the Providence Place mall in <a href="/wiki/Providence,_Rhode_Island" title="Providence, Rhode Island">Providence, Rhode Island</a>, both of which opened in November 2007.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p><p>As of January 31, 2007, the company operated 64 Tiffany &amp; Co. stores in the U.S., with a total physical area of approximately 486,000 gross square feet, as well as 103 international stores that measure approximately 306,000 gross square feet in total.
</p><p>The company's expansion continued in 2011 with the opening of a store at the <a href="/wiki/Multiplaza" title="Multiplaza">Multiplaza</a> complex in <a href="/wiki/Escaz%C3%BA,_Costa_Rica" class="mw-redirect" title="Escazú, Costa Rica">Escazú, Costa Rica</a>,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> and a <a href="/wiki/Richmond,_Virginia" title="Richmond, Virginia">Richmond, Virginia</a>, location in <a href="/wiki/Stony_Point_Fashion_Park" title="Stony Point Fashion Park">Stony Point Fashion Park</a> on September 9, 2011.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p><p>In November 2012, the company operated in 22 countries, and its worldwide net sales reached $3.6 billion in 2011. Over 50% of the company's 2011 sales occurred in the U.S.<sup id="cite_ref-Rt_49-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rt-49">[49]</a></sup></p><p>As of January 31, 2014, the company operated 121 stores in the Americas, 72 in the Asia-Pacific region, 54 in Japan, 37 in Europe and 5 in "emerging markets".  The 298<sup id="cite_ref-NYT41415_50-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NYT41415-50">[50]</a></sup> stores have 1,165,700 gross retail square footage.  The company's flagship store in New York had 45,500 gross retail square footage and accounted for 8% of the company's net sales in the fiscal year ended January 31, 2014. The company planned to continue expanding in 2014 with the opening of a flagship store at the <a href="/wiki/Centro_Andino" title="Centro Andino">Centro Andino</a> complex in <a href="/wiki/Bogota,_Colombia" class="mw-redirect" title="Bogota, Colombia">Bogota, Colombia</a>.
</p><p>As of 2018, Tiffany operated 93 stores in the US and 321 stores worldwide, including (as of 31 January 2017) 55 locations in Japan and 85 in the Asia-Pacific region. Net sales in 2018 totaled US$4.44 billion.<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup></p>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(3)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Manufacturing">Manufacturing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tiffany_%26_Co.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Manufacturing" data-section="8" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-3 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-3">
<p>The company's manufacturing facilities produce approximately 60% of the merchandise sold<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>—the balance, including rose-gold and almost all non-jewelry items, coming from third parties overseas. Tiffany's oversees a significant U.S. manufacturing base, with jewelry and silver goods produced in Mount Vernon, New York; majority in <a href="/wiki/Cumberland,_Rhode_Island" title="Cumberland, Rhode Island">Cumberland, Rhode Island</a>; and Lexington, Kentucky, while silver <a href="/wiki/Holloware" title="Holloware">hollow-ware</a> is produced in Rhode Island. The company's other subsidiaries, located in facilities outside the U.S., process, cut and polish the diamonds.
</p><p>The company may increase the percentage of internally manufactured jewelry in the future, but it is not expected<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag may use weasel words or too-vague attribution. (August 2015)">by whom?</span></a></i>]</sup> that Tiffany will ever manufacture all of its needs. Some of the key factors which management considered<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Chronological_items" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers"><span title="The time period mentioned near this tag is ambiguous. (August 2015)">when?</span></a></i>]</sup> prior to its decision to outsource manufacturing included: product quality; gross margin; access to or mastery of various jewelry-making skills and technology; support for alternative capacity; and the cost of capital investments.<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup></p>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(4)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Advertising">Advertising</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tiffany_%26_Co.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Advertising" data-section="9" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-4 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-4">
<p>After the initial publication of the "Blue Book" Tiffany catalog in 1845, Tiffany continued to use its catalog as part of its advertisement strategy. The Tiffany catalog, one of the first catalogs printed in full color, remained free until 1972. Tiffany's mail-order catalogs reached 15 million people in 1994. Tiffany also produces a corporate-gift catalog each year, and corporate customers purchase Tiffany products for <a href="/wiki/Trade" title="Trade">business</a> gift-giving, employee-service and achievement-recognition awards, and for customer incentives. Tiffany still produces a catalog for subscribers, but its advertisement strategy no longer focuses primarily on its catalog.<sup id="cite_ref-fundinguniverse.com_9-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fundinguniverse.com-9">[9]</a></sup></p><p>In addition to the mail-order catalog, Tiffany displays its advertisements in many locations, including at bus stops, in magazines and newspapers, and online. Tiffany routinely places ads in <i><a href="/wiki/Vanity_Fair_(magazine)" title="Vanity Fair (magazine)">Vanity Fair</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times_Magazine" title="The New York Times Magazine">The New York Times Magazine</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Architectural_Digest" title="Architectural Digest">Architectural Digest</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Money_(magazine)" title="Money (magazine)">Money</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Conde_Nast_Traveler" class="mw-redirect" title="Conde Nast Traveler">Conde Nast Traveler</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Black_Enterprise" title="Black Enterprise">Black Enterprise</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/Texas_Monthly" title="Texas Monthly">Texas Monthly</a></i>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> With the advent of new technologies, Tiffany places <a href="/wiki/Banner_advertisements" class="mw-redirect" title="Banner advertisements">banner advertisements</a> in the <i>New York Times'</i> <a href="/wiki/Mobile_app" title="Mobile app">mobile app</a> for the <a href="/wiki/IPhone" title="IPhone">iPhone</a>, whereby the user can download the Tiffany app free of charge.<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup> In January 2015, they launched their first ever same-sex couple campaign.
</p>

<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Diamonds">Diamonds</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tiffany_%26_Co.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Diamonds" data-section="11" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:182px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Tiffany_Diamond2.jpg" class="image"><noscript><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/ba/Tiffany_Diamond2.jpg/180px-Tiffany_Diamond2.jpg" decoding="async" width="180" height="221" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="726" data-file-height="891"></noscript><span class="lazy-image-placeholder" style="width: 180px;height: 221px;" data-src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/ba/Tiffany_Diamond2.jpg/180px-Tiffany_Diamond2.jpg" data-alt="" data-width="180" data-height="221" data-class="thumbimage"> </span></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Tiffany_Diamond2.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The <a href="/wiki/Tiffany_Yellow_Diamond" title="Tiffany Yellow Diamond">Tiffany Yellow Diamond</a>, a 128-carat stone cut in a modified cushion-shape featuring 90 facets instead of the 57 or 58 of a standard brilliant cut.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2020)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The stone, discovered in 1878, has never been sold.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2020)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></div></div></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/George_Frederick_Kunz" title="George Frederick Kunz">George Frederick Kunz</a> (1856-1932), a Tiffany's <a href="/wiki/Gemologist" class="mw-redirect" title="Gemologist">gemologist</a>, became instrumental in the international adoption of the metric <a href="/wiki/Carat_(unit)" class="mw-redirect" title="Carat (unit)">carat</a> as a weight standard for gems, and the Tiffany standards for sterling and platinum have been adopted<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag may use weasel words or too-vague attribution. (March 2019)">by whom?</span></a></i>]</sup> as U.S. standards.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The  Tiffany Yellow Diamond (128.54 carats (25.708 g)) is usually on display in the New York City flagship store.<sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup></p><p>Famous U.S. families such as the <a href="/wiki/Astor_family" title="Astor family">Astors</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vanderbilt_family" title="Vanderbilt family">Vanderbilts</a>, Posts, Huttons and Morgans have worn Tiffany designs.<sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup> Athletes, Hollywood stars, and European royalty also became Tiffany customers. However, like other similar diamond retailers, Tiffany's enacts a strict policy against the repurchasing of diamonds sold from its stores. In 1978, a female customer in New York City was denied after she attempted to sell back a diamond ring she had bought from Tiffany two years earlier for $100,000. Writing for <i><a href="/wiki/The_Atlantic" title="The Atlantic">The Atlantic</a></i> publication in 1982, <a href="/wiki/Edward_Jay_Epstein" title="Edward Jay Epstein">Edward Jay Epstein</a> explained the rationale for such a policy:
</p>
<blockquote><p>
Retail jewelers, especially the prestigious Fifth Avenue stores, prefer not to buy back diamonds from customers, because the offer they would make would most likely be considered ridiculously low ... Most jewelers would prefer not to make a customer an offer that might be deemed insulting and also might undercut the widely held notion that diamonds go up in value. Moreover, since retailers generally receive their diamonds for engagement rings from wholesalers on consignment, and need not pay for them until they are sold, they would not readily risk their own cash to buy diamonds from customers. Rather than offer customers a fraction of what they paid for diamonds, retail jewelers almost invariably recommend to their clients firms that specialize in buying diamonds "retail."<sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup></p></blockquote>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Colored_gemstones">Colored gemstones</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tiffany_%26_Co.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Colored gemstones" data-section="12" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>Tiffany offers jewelry incorporating a wide variety of colored gemstones including gems it played a role in popularizing such as <a href="/wiki/Tsavorite" title="Tsavorite">tsavorite</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-ICA_59-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ICA-59">[59]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Spodumene#Kunzite" title="Spodumene">Kunzite</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Morganite" class="mw-redirect" title="Morganite">morganite</a>. In February 2015, a <a href="/wiki/Turquoise" title="Turquoise">turquoise</a> and <a href="/wiki/Aquamarines" class="mw-redirect" title="Aquamarines">aquamarine</a> bib designed by Francesca Amfitheatrof, Tiffany's design director, worn by <a href="/wiki/Cate_Blanchett" title="Cate Blanchett">Cate Blanchett</a> at the <a href="/wiki/87th_Academy_Awards" title="87th Academy Awards">2015 Academy Awards</a>, contrasted favorably with the white–diamond encrusted jewelry worn by other stars.<sup id="cite_ref-NYT41415_50-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NYT41415-50">[50]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Fragrances">Fragrances</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tiffany_%26_Co.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Fragrances" data-section="13" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Tiffany4men.jpg" class="image"><noscript><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/50/Tiffany4men.jpg/220px-Tiffany4men.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="176" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="1122" data-file-height="897"></noscript><span class="lazy-image-placeholder" style="width: 220px;height: 176px;" data-src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/50/Tiffany4men.jpg/220px-Tiffany4men.jpg" data-alt="" data-width="220" data-height="176" data-class="thumbimage"> </span></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Tiffany4men.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Original 1989 sample bottle of "Tiffany for Men" fragrance</div></div></div>
<p>In the late 1980s, Tiffany &amp; Co. ventured into the fragrance business. "Tiffany" for women was launched in 1987, a floral perfume for women by perfumer Francois Demachy. At $220 per ounce, "Tiffany" was successfully marketed by major department stores across the United States.<sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup> Two years later, "Tiffany for Men" was launched in 1989 and developed by perfumer <a href="/wiki/Jacques_Polge" title="Jacques Polge">Jacques Polge</a>. The bottles for both the men's and women's fragrance were designed by Pierre Dinand.<sup id="cite_ref-61" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup> In 1995, Tiffany launched "Trueste" perfume for women, which was later discontinued. Currently, Tiffany continues to produce the core fragrance product for men and the product for women.
</p>
