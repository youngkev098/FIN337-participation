<p><b>Texas Instruments Incorporated</b> (<b>TI</b>) is an American technology company that designs and manufactures <a href="/wiki/Semiconductor" title="Semiconductor">semiconductors</a> and various <a href="/wiki/Integrated_circuit" title="Integrated circuit">integrated circuits</a>, which it sells to electronics designers and <a href="/wiki/Manufacturer" class="mw-redirect" title="Manufacturer">manufacturers</a> globally.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Its headquarters are in <a href="/wiki/Dallas" title="Dallas">Dallas</a>, Texas in the United States. TI is one of the top 10 semiconductor companies worldwide, based on sales volume.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> The company's focus is on developing <a href="/wiki/Analog_chip" class="mw-redirect" title="Analog chip">analog chips</a> and <a href="/wiki/Embedded_processor" class="mw-redirect" title="Embedded processor">embedded processors</a>, which account for more than 80% of its revenue.<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-8">[8]</a></sup> TI also produces TI <a href="/wiki/Digital_Light_Processing" title="Digital Light Processing">digital light processing</a> technology and education technology<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-8">[8]</a></sup> products including <a href="/wiki/Calculator" title="Calculator">calculators</a>, <a href="/wiki/Microcontroller" title="Microcontroller">microcontrollers</a> and <a href="/wiki/Multi-core_processor" title="Multi-core processor">multi-core processors</a>. text" href="http://ti.com">ti<wbr></wbr>.com</a></span></td></tr></tbody></table><p>Texas Instruments emerged in 1951 after a reorganization of <a href="/wiki/Geophysical_Service_Incorporated" class="mw-redirect" title="Geophysical Service Incorporated">Geophysical Service Incorporated</a>, a company founded in 1930 that manufactured equipment for use in the seismic industry, as well as defense electronics.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> TI produced the world's first commercial <a href="/wiki/Silicon_transistor" class="mw-redirect" title="Silicon transistor">silicon transistor</a> in 1954,<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> and the same year designed and manufactured the first <a href="/wiki/Transistor_radio" title="Transistor radio">transistor radio</a>. <a href="/wiki/Jack_Kilby" title="Jack Kilby">Jack Kilby</a> invented the <a href="/wiki/Integrated_circuit" title="Integrated circuit">integrated circuit</a> in 1958 while working at TI's Central Research Labs. TI also invented the hand-held <a href="/wiki/Calculator" title="Calculator">calculator</a> in 1967, and introduced the first single-chip microcontroller  in 1970, which combined all the elements of computing onto one piece of silicon.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-12">[12]</a></sup></p><p>In 1987, TI invented the digital light processing device (also known as the DLP chip), which serves as the foundation for the company's award-winning DLP technology and DLP Cinema.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-12">[12]</a></sup> TI released the popular <a href="/wiki/TI-81" title="TI-81">TI-81</a> calculator in 1990, which made it a leader in the graphing calculator industry. Its defense business was sold to <a href="/wiki/Raytheon" class="mw-redirect" title="Raytheon">Raytheon</a> in 1997; this allowed TI to strengthen its focus on digital solutions.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> After the acquisition of <a href="/wiki/National_Semiconductor" title="National Semiconductor">National Semiconductor</a> in 2011, the company had a combined portfolio of 45,000 analog products and customer design tools.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>

</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(1)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Texas_Instruments&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History" data-section="1" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-1 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-1">
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:North_Campus_Gate1.jpg" class="image"><noscript><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ee/North_Campus_Gate1.jpg/220px-North_Campus_Gate1.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="151" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="661" data-file-height="453"></noscript><span class="lazy-image-placeholder" style="width: 220px;height: 151px;" data-src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ee/North_Campus_Gate1.jpg/220px-North_Campus_Gate1.jpg" data-alt="" data-width="220" data-height="151" data-class="thumbimage"> </span></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:North_Campus_Gate1.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Entrance to Texas Instruments North Campus facility in Dallas, Texas</div></div></div>
<p>Texas Instruments was founded by <a href="/wiki/Cecil_H._Green" class="mw-redirect" title="Cecil H. Green">Cecil H. Green</a>, <a href="/wiki/J._Erik_Jonsson" title="J. Erik Jonsson">J. Erik Jonsson</a>, <a href="/wiki/Eugene_McDermott" title="Eugene McDermott">Eugene McDermott</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Patrick_E._Haggerty" title="Patrick E. Haggerty">Patrick E. Haggerty</a> in 1951.  McDermott was one of the original founders of <a href="/wiki/Geophysical_Service_Incorporated" class="mw-redirect" title="Geophysical Service Incorporated">Geophysical Service</a> Inc. (GSI) in 1930. McDermott, Green, and Jonsson were GSI employees who purchased the company in 1941. In November, 1945, Patrick Haggerty was hired as general manager of the Laboratory and Manufacturing (L&amp;M) division, which focused on electronic equipment.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> By 1951, the L&amp;M division, with its defense contracts, was growing faster than GSI's geophysical division. The company was reorganized and initially renamed General Instruments Inc. Because a firm named <a href="/wiki/General_Instrument" title="General Instrument">General Instrument</a> already existed, the company was renamed Texas Instruments that same year. From 1956 to 1961, <a href="/wiki/Fred_Agnich" title="Fred Agnich">Fred Agnich</a> of Dallas, later a <a href="/wiki/Republican_Party_(United_States)" title="Republican Party (United States)">Republican</a> member of the <a href="/wiki/Texas_House_of_Representatives" title="Texas House of Representatives">Texas House of Representatives</a>, was the Texas Instruments president. Geophysical Service, Inc. became a subsidiary of Texas Instruments. Early in 1988, most of GSI was sold to the <a href="/wiki/Halliburton" title="Halliburton">Halliburton</a> Company.
</p>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r886047036">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>Texas Instruments exists to create, make, and market useful products and services to satisfy the needs of its customers throughout the world.<sup id="cite_ref-Innovation_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Innovation-16">[16]</a></sup></p><div class="templatequotecite">— <cite>Patrick Haggerty, Texas Instruments Statement of Purpose</cite></div></blockquote>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Geophysical_Service_Incorporated">Geophysical Service Incorporated</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Texas_Instruments&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Geophysical Service Incorporated" data-section="2" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>In 1930, <a href="/wiki/J._Clarence_Karcher" title="J. Clarence Karcher">J. Clarence Karcher</a> and Eugene McDermott founded Geophysical Service, an early provider of <a href="/wiki/Reflection_seismology" title="Reflection seismology">seismic exploration</a> services to the petroleum industry. In 1939, the company reorganized as <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.ti.com/corp/docs/company/history/lowbandwidthtimeline.shtml">Coronado Corp</a>., an oil company with Geophysical Service Inc (GSI), now as a subsidiary. On December 6, 1941, McDermott along with three other GSI employees, J. Erik Jonsson, Cecil H. Green, and H.B. Peacock purchased GSI. During World War II, GSI expanded their services to include <a href="/wiki/Electronics" title="Electronics">electronics</a> for the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Army" title="United States Army">U.S. Army</a>, <a href="/wiki/United_States_Army_Signal_Corps" class="mw-redirect" title="United States Army Signal Corps">Signal Corps</a>, and <a href="/wiki/United_States_Navy" title="United States Navy">U.S. Navy</a>. In 1951, the company changed its name to Texas Instruments, spun off to build seismographs for oil explorations<sup id="cite_ref-TIcalcsMedium_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TIcalcsMedium-17">[17]</a></sup> and with GSI becoming a wholly owned subsidiary of the new company.
</p><p>An early success story for TI-GSI came in 1965 when GSI was able (under a <a href="/wiki/Security_clearance" title="Security clearance">Top Secret</a> government contract) to monitor the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a>'s underground <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_weapon" title="Nuclear weapon">nuclear weapons</a> <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_testing" class="mw-redirect" title="Nuclear testing">testing</a> under the ocean in <a href="/wiki/Vela_Uniform" title="Vela Uniform">Vela Uniform</a>, a subset of <a href="/wiki/Project_Vela" title="Project Vela">Project Vela</a>, to verify compliance of the <a href="/wiki/Partial_Nuclear_Test_Ban_Treaty" title="Partial Nuclear Test Ban Treaty">Partial Nuclear Test Ban Treaty</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p><p>Texas Instruments also continued to manufacture equipment for use in the seismic industry, and GSI continued to provide seismic services.  After selling (and repurchasing) GSI, TI finally sold the company to <a href="/wiki/Halliburton" title="Halliburton">Halliburton</a> in 1988, when GSI ceased to exist as a separate entity.
</p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Semiconductors">Semiconductors</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Texas_Instruments&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Semiconductors" data-section="3" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>In early 1952, Texas Instruments purchased a patent license to produce germanium transistors from <a href="/wiki/Western_Electric" title="Western Electric">Western Electric</a>, the manufacturing arm of AT&amp;T, for $25,000, beginning production by the end of the year.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p><p>On January 1, 1953, Haggerty brought <a href="/wiki/Gordon_Teal" class="mw-redirect" title="Gordon Teal">Gordon Teal</a> to the company as a research director. Gordon brought with him his expertise in growing semiconductor crystals. Teal's first assignment was to organize what became TI's Central Research Laboratories, which Teal based on his prior experience at Bell Labs.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p><p>Among his new hires was <a href="/wiki/Willis_Adcock" title="Willis Adcock">Willis Adcock</a>, who joined TI early in 1953. Adcock, who like Teal was a <a href="/wiki/Physical_chemist" class="mw-redirect" title="Physical chemist">physical chemist</a>, began leading a small research group focused on the task of fabricating "grown-junction, <i><a href="/wiki/Silicon" title="Silicon">silicon</a></i>, single-crystal, small-signal transistors. Adcock later became the first TI Principal Fellow.<sup id="cite_ref-Tribute_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Tribute-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<h4 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="First_silicon_transistor_and_integrated_circuits">First silicon transistor and integrated circuits</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Texas_Instruments&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: First silicon transistor and integrated circuits" data-section="4" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:102px;"><a href="/wiki/File:KL_TI_SN5451_Logic_IC.jpg" class="image"><noscript><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/66/KL_TI_SN5451_Logic_IC.jpg/100px-KL_TI_SN5451_Logic_IC.jpg" decoding="async" width="100" height="195" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="420" data-file-height="819"></noscript><span class="lazy-image-placeholder" style="width: 100px;height: 195px;" data-src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/66/KL_TI_SN5451_Logic_IC.jpg/100px-KL_TI_SN5451_Logic_IC.jpg" data-alt="" data-width="100" data-height="195" data-class="thumbimage"> </span></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:KL_TI_SN5451_Logic_IC.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Transistorized "<a href="/wiki/Transistor-transistor_logic" class="mw-redirect" title="Transistor-transistor logic">logic</a>" chip, an integrated circuit produced by TI</div></div></div>
<p>In January 1954, <a href="/wiki/Morris_Tanenbaum" title="Morris Tanenbaum">Morris Tanenbaum</a> at <a href="/wiki/Bell_Labs" title="Bell Labs">Bell Labs</a> created the first workable silicon transistor.<sup id="cite_ref-transistor_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-transistor-20">[20]</a></sup> This work was reported in the spring of 1954, at the IRE off-the-record conference on solid-state devices, and was later published in the <i>Journal of Applied Physics</i>. Working independently in April 1954, Gordon Teal at TI created the first commercial silicon transistor and tested it on April 14, 1954. On May 10, 1954, at the Institute of Radio Engineers  National Conference on Airborne Electronics in Dayton, Ohio, Teal presented a paper: "Some Recent Developments in Silicon and Germanium Materials and Devices,".<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p><p>In 1954, Texas Instruments designed and manufactured the first <a href="/wiki/Transistor_radio" title="Transistor radio">transistor radio</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Regency_TR-1" title="Regency TR-1">Regency TR-1</a> used germanium transistors, as silicon transistors were much more expensive at the time. This was an effort by Haggerty to increase market demand for transistors.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Jack_Kilby" title="Jack Kilby">Jack Kilby</a>, an employee at TI's Central Research Labs, invented the <a href="/wiki/Integrated_circuit" title="Integrated circuit">integrated circuit</a> in 1958.<a rel="nofollow" class="external autonumber" href="http://www.ti.com/corp/docs/company/factsheet.shtml">[1]</a> Kilby recorded his initial ideas concerning the integrated circuit in July 1958, and successfully demonstrated the world's first working integrated circuit on September 12, 1958.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> Six months later, <a href="/wiki/Robert_Noyce" title="Robert Noyce">Robert Noyce</a> of <a href="/wiki/Fairchild_Semiconductor" title="Fairchild Semiconductor">Fairchild Semiconductor</a> (who went on to co-found <a href="/wiki/Intel" title="Intel">Intel</a>) independently developed the integrated circuit with integrated interconnect, and is also considered an inventor of the integrated circuit.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> In 1969, Kilby was awarded the National Medal of Science, and in 1982 he was inducted into the National Inventor's Hall of Fame.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> Kilby also won the 2000 Nobel Prize in Physics for his part of the invention of the integrated circuit.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> Noyce's chip, made at Fairchild, was made of silicon, while Kilby's chip was made of <a href="/wiki/Germanium" title="Germanium">germanium</a>.  In 2008, TI named its new development laboratory "Kilby Labs" after Jack Kilby.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p><p>In 2011, Intel, Samsung, LG, ST-Ericsson, Huawei's HiSilicon Technologies subsidiary, Via Telecom, and three other undisclosed chipmakers licensed the C2C link specification developed by Arteris Inc. and Texas Instruments.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>

<p>The <a href="/wiki/7400_series" class="mw-redirect" title="7400 series">7400 series</a> of <a href="/wiki/Transistor-transistor_logic" class="mw-redirect" title="Transistor-transistor logic">transistor-transistor logic</a>  chips, developed by Texas Instruments in the 1960s, popularized the use of integrated circuits in computer logic. The military-grade version of this was the 5400 series.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<h4 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Microprocessor">Microprocessor</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Texas_Instruments&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Microprocessor" data-section="6" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h4>
<p>Texas Instruments invented the hand-held <a href="/wiki/Calculator" title="Calculator">calculator</a> (a prototype called "<a href="/wiki/Cal_Tech_(calculator)" class="mw-redirect" title="Cal Tech (calculator)">Cal Tech</a>") in 1967 and the single-chip <a href="/wiki/Microcomputer" title="Microcomputer">microcomputer</a> in 1971,  was assigned the first patent on a single-chip <a href="/wiki/Microprocessor" title="Microprocessor">microprocessor</a> (invented by <a href="/w/index.php?title=Gary_Boone&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Gary Boone (page does not exist)">Gary Boone</a>) on September 4, 1973.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> This was disputed by Gilbert Hyatt, formerly of the Micro Computer Company, in August 1990, when he was awarded a patent superseding TI's. This was overturned on June 19, 1996, in favor of TI<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> (note: <a href="/wiki/Intel" title="Intel">Intel</a> is usually given credit with Texas Instruments for the almost-simultaneous invention of the microprocessor).
</p>
<h4 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="First_speech_synthesis_chip">First speech synthesis chip</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Texas_Instruments&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: First speech synthesis chip" data-section="7" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h4>
<p>In 1978, Texas Instruments introduced the first single-chip <a href="/wiki/Linear_predictive_coding" title="Linear predictive coding">linear predictive coding</a> <a href="/wiki/Speech_synthesizer" class="mw-redirect" title="Speech synthesizer">speech synthesizer</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> In 1976, TI began a feasibility study of memory-intensive applications for bubble memory then being developed. They soon focused on speech applications. This resulted in the development the TMC0280 one-chip linear predictive coding  speech synthesizer, which was the first time a single silicon chip had electronically replicated the human voice.<sup id="cite_ref-TITimeLine_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TITimeLine-32">[32]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> This was used in several TI commercial products beginning with <a href="/wiki/Speak_%26_Spell_(toy)" title="Speak &amp; Spell (toy)">Speak &amp; Spell</a>, which was introduced at the Summer Consumer Electronics Show in June 1978. In 2001, TI left the speech synthesis business, selling it to <a href="/w/index.php?title=Sensory_Inc.&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Sensory Inc. (page does not exist)">Sensory Inc.</a> of Santa Clara, California.<sup id="cite_ref-TI_will_exit_dedicated_speech-synthesis_chips,_transfer_products_to_Sensory_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TI_will_exit_dedicated_speech-synthesis_chips,_transfer_products_to_Sensory-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Consumer_electronics_and_computers">Consumer electronics and computers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Texas_Instruments&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Consumer electronics and computers" data-section="8" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>In May 1954, Texas Instruments designed and built a prototype of the world's first <a href="/wiki/Transistor_radio" title="Transistor radio">transistor radio</a>, and, through a partnership with Industrial Development Engineering Associates  of Indianapolis, Indiana, the 100% solid-state radio was sold to the public beginning in October of that year.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></p><p>In the 1960s, company president Pat Haggerty had a team that included Jack Kilby to work on a handheld calculator project. Kilby and two other colleagues created the Cal-Tech, a three-pound battery-powered calculator that could do basic math and fit six-digit numbers on its display. This 4.25 x 6.15 x 1.75 inch calculator's processor would originate the vast majority of Texas Instruments’ revenue.<sup id="cite_ref-TIcalcsMedium_17-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TIcalcsMedium-17">[17]</a></sup></p><p>In 1973, the handheld calculator SR-10 (named after <a href="/wiki/Slide_rule" title="Slide rule">slide rule</a>) and in 1974 the handheld scientific calculator <a href="/wiki/TI_SR-50" title="TI SR-50">SR-50</a> were issued by TI. Both had red LED-segments numeric displays. The optical design of the SR-50 is somewhat similar to the <a href="/wiki/HP-35" title="HP-35">HP-35</a> edited by Hewlett Packard before in early 1972, but buttons for the operations "+", "–", ... are in the right of the number block and the decimal point lies between two neighboring digits.
</p><p>TI continued to be active in the consumer electronics market through the 1970s and 1980s. Early on, this also included two digital clock models - one for desk and the other a bedside alarm. From this sprang what became the Time Products Division, which made LED watches. Though these LED watches enjoyed early commercial success due to excellent quality, it was short-lived due to poor battery life. LEDs were replaced with LCD watches for a short time, but these could not compete because of styling issues, excessive makes and models, and price points. The watches were manufactured in Dallas and then Lubbock, Texas. Several spin-offs of the Speak &amp; Spell, such as the <a href="/wiki/Speak_%26_Read" title="Speak &amp; Read">Speak &amp; Read</a> and <a href="/wiki/Speak_%26_Math" title="Speak &amp; Math">Speak &amp; Math</a>, were introduced soon thereafter.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></p><p>In 1979, TI entered the <a href="/wiki/Home_computer" title="Home computer">home computer</a> market with the <a href="/wiki/Texas_Instruments_TI-99/4A" title="Texas Instruments TI-99/4A">TI-99/4</a>, a competitor to such entries as the <a href="/wiki/Apple_II" title="Apple II">Apple II</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tandy_Corporation" title="Tandy Corporation">Tandy</a>/<a href="/wiki/RadioShack" title="RadioShack">Radio Shack</a> <a href="/wiki/TRS-80" title="TRS-80">TRS-80</a>, and the later <a href="/wiki/Atari" title="Atari">Atari</a> <a href="/wiki/Atari_8-bit_family" title="Atari 8-bit family">400</a>/<a href="/wiki/Atari_8-bit_family" title="Atari 8-bit family">800</a> series and <a href="/wiki/Commodore_International" title="Commodore International">Commodore</a> <a href="/wiki/VIC-20" class="mw-redirect" title="VIC-20">VIC-20</a>. It discontinued the <a href="/wiki/Texas_Instruments_TI-99/4A" title="Texas Instruments TI-99/4A">TI-99/4A</a> (1981), the sequel to the 99/4, in late 1983 amid an intense <a href="/wiki/North_American_video_game_crash_of_1983#A_Savage_Price_War" class="mw-redirect" title="North American video game crash of 1983">price war</a> waged primarily against Commodore. At the 1983 Winter CES, TI showed models 99/2 and the <a href="/wiki/Texas_Instruments_Compact_Computer_40" title="Texas Instruments Compact Computer 40">Compact Computer 40 (CC-40)</a>, the latter aimed at professional users.  The <a href="/wiki/Texas_Instruments_Professional_Computer_and_Professional_Portable_Computer" title="Texas Instruments Professional Computer and Professional Portable Computer">TI Professional</a> (1983) ultimately joined the ranks of the many unsuccessful <a href="/wiki/DOS" title="DOS">DOS</a> and <a href="/wiki/X86" title="X86">x86</a>-based—but <a href="/wiki/PC_compatible#Compatibility_issues" class="mw-redirect" title="PC compatible">non-compatible</a><sup id="cite_ref-Inc.1984_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Inc.1984-37">[37]</a></sup>—competitors to the <a href="/wiki/IBM_PC" class="mw-redirect" title="IBM PC">IBM PC</a> (the founders of <a href="/wiki/Compaq" title="Compaq">Compaq</a>, an early leader in PC compatibles, all came from TI). The company for years successfully made and sold PC-compatible laptops before withdrawing from the market and selling its product line to <a href="/wiki/Acer_Inc." title="Acer Inc.">Acer</a> in 1998.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Defense_electronics">Defense electronics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Texas_Instruments&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Defense electronics" data-section="9" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Convair_240-1_N240HH_Texas_Inst_Chino_05.10.90R_edited-3.jpg" class="image"><noscript><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/70/Convair_240-1_N240HH_Texas_Inst_Chino_05.10.90R_edited-3.jpg/220px-Convair_240-1_N240HH_Texas_Inst_Chino_05.10.90R_edited-3.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="152" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="1024" data-file-height="706"></noscript><span class="lazy-image-placeholder" style="width: 220px;height: 152px;" data-src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/70/Convair_240-1_N240HH_Texas_Inst_Chino_05.10.90R_edited-3.jpg/220px-Convair_240-1_N240HH_Texas_Inst_Chino_05.10.90R_edited-3.jpg" data-alt="" data-width="220" data-height="152" data-class="thumbimage"> </span></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Convair_240-1_N240HH_Texas_Inst_Chino_05.10.90R_edited-3.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>TI operated this <a href="/wiki/Convair_240" class="mw-redirect" title="Convair 240">Convair 240</a> on experimental work in the 1980s fitted with a modified extended nose section.</div></div></div>
<p>TI entered the <a href="/wiki/Defense_(military)" class="mw-redirect" title="Defense (military)">defense</a> electronics market in 1942 with submarine detection equipment,<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> based on the seismic exploration technology previously developed for the oil industry. The division responsible for these products was known at different times as the Laboratory &amp; Manufacturing Division, the Apparatus Division, the Equipment Group, and the Defense Systems &amp; Electronics Group (DSEG).
</p><p>During the early 1980s, TI instituted a quality program which included <a href="/wiki/Joseph_M._Juran" title="Joseph M. Juran">Juran</a> training, as well as promoting <a href="/wiki/Statistical_process_control" title="Statistical process control">statistical process control</a>, <a href="/wiki/Taguchi_methods" title="Taguchi methods">Taguchi methods</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Design_for_Six_Sigma" title="Design for Six Sigma">Design for Six Sigma</a>. In the late '80s, the company, along with <a href="/wiki/Eastman_Kodak" class="mw-redirect" title="Eastman Kodak">Eastman Kodak</a> and <a href="/wiki/Allied_Signal" class="mw-redirect" title="Allied Signal">Allied Signal</a>, began involvement with <a href="/wiki/Motorola" title="Motorola">Motorola</a>, institutionalizing Motorola's <a href="/wiki/Six_Sigma" title="Six Sigma">Six Sigma</a> methodology.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> Motorola, which originally developed the Six Sigma methodology, began this work in 1982. In 1992, the DSEG division<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> of Texas Instruments' quality-improvement efforts were rewarded by winning the <a href="/wiki/Malcolm_Baldrige_National_Quality_Award" title="Malcolm Baldrige National Quality Award">Malcolm Baldrige National Quality Award</a> for manufacturing.
</p>
<dl><dt>Infrared and radar systems</dt></dl><p>TI developed the AAA-4 <a href="/wiki/Infra-red_search_and_track" class="mw-redirect" title="Infra-red search and track">infra-red search and track</a>  in the late '50s and early '60s for the <a href="/wiki/F-4B_Phantom" class="mw-redirect" title="F-4B Phantom">F-4B Phantom</a><sup id="cite_ref-Sweetmanp526_42-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sweetmanp526-42">[42]</a></sup> for passive scanning of jet-engine emissions, but it possessed limited capabilities and was eliminated on F-4Ds and later models.<sup id="cite_ref-Sweetmanp552_43-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sweetmanp552-43">[43]</a></sup></p><p>In 1956, TI began research on <a href="/wiki/Infrared" title="Infrared">infrared</a> technology that led to several line scanner contracts and with the addition of a second scan mirror the invention of the first <a href="/wiki/Forward_looking_infrared" class="mw-redirect" title="Forward looking infrared">forward looking infrared</a> (FLIR) in 1963 with production beginning in 1966. In 1972, TI invented the common module FLIR<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> concept, greatly reducing cost and allowing reuse of common components.
</p><p>TI went on to produce side-looking radar systems, the first <a href="/wiki/Terrain_following_radar" class="mw-redirect" title="Terrain following radar">terrain-following radar</a> and surveillance radar systems for both the military and FAA. TI demonstrated the first solid-state radar called Molecular Electronics for Radar Applications.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> In 1976, TI developed a <a href="/wiki/Microwave_landing_system" title="Microwave landing system">microwave landing system</a> prototype. In 1984, TI developed the first <a href="/wiki/Inverse_synthetic_aperture_radar" class="mw-redirect" title="Inverse synthetic aperture radar">inverse synthetic aperture radar</a>. The first single-chip <a href="/wiki/Gallium_arsenide" title="Gallium arsenide">gallium arsenide</a> radar module was developed. In 1991, the military microwave integrated circuit<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> program was initiated –  a joint effort with Raytheon.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<dl><dt>Missiles and laser-guided bombs</dt></dl><p>In 1961, TI won the guidance and control system contract for the defense suppression <a href="/wiki/AGM-45_Shrike" title="AGM-45 Shrike">AGM-45 Shrike</a> <a href="/wiki/Antiradiation_missile" class="mw-redirect" title="Antiradiation missile">antiradiation missile</a>. This led later to the prime on the <a href="/wiki/AGM-88_HARM" title="AGM-88 HARM">high-speed antiradiation missile</a> (AGM-88 HARM) development contract in 1974 and production in 1981.
</p><p>In 1964, TI began development of the first laser guidance system for <a href="/wiki/Precision-guided_munition" title="Precision-guided munition">precision-guided munitions</a>, leading to the <a href="/wiki/Paveway" title="Paveway">Paveway</a> series of <a href="/wiki/Laser-guided_bomb" title="Laser-guided bomb">laser-guided bombs</a> (LGBs). The first LGB was the <a href="/wiki/BOLT-117" title="BOLT-117">BOLT-117</a>.
</p><p>In 1969, TI won the Harpoon (missile) Seeker contract. In 1986, TI won the Army <a href="/wiki/FGM-148_Javelin" title="FGM-148 Javelin">FGM-148 Javelin</a> <a href="/wiki/Fire-and-forget" title="Fire-and-forget">fire-and-forget</a> man portable antitank guided missile in a joint venture with <a href="/wiki/Martin_Marietta" title="Martin Marietta">Martin Marietta</a>. In 1991, TI was awarded the contract for the <a href="/wiki/AGM-154_Joint_Standoff_Weapon" title="AGM-154 Joint Standoff Weapon">AGM-154 Joint Standoff Weapon</a>.
</p>
<dl><dt>Military computers</dt></dl><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Military_computers" title="Military computers">Military computers</a></div>
<p>Because of TI's research and development of military temperature-range silicon transistors and integrated circuits (ICs), TI won contracts for the first IC-based computer for the U.S. Air Force in 1961 (molecular electronic computer)<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> and for ICs for the Minuteman Missile the following year. In 1968, TI developed the data systems for <a href="/wiki/Mariner_Program" class="mw-redirect" title="Mariner Program">Mariner Program</a>. In 1991 TI won the F-22 Radar and Computer development contract.
</p>
<dl><dt>Divestiture to Raytheon</dt></dl><p>As the defense industry consolidated, TI sold its defense business to <a href="/wiki/Raytheon" class="mw-redirect" title="Raytheon">Raytheon</a> in 1997 for $2.95 billion. The Department of Justice required that Raytheon divest the TI <a href="/wiki/Monolithic_Microwave_Integrated_Circuit" class="mw-redirect" title="Monolithic Microwave Integrated Circuit">Monolithic Microwave Integrated Circuit</a> (MMIC) operations after closing the transaction.<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> The TI MMIC business accounted for less than $40 million in 1996 revenues, or roughly 2% of the $1.8 billion in total TI defense revenues, and was sold to <a href="/wiki/TriQuint_Semiconductor" title="TriQuint Semiconductor">TriQuint Semiconductor</a>, Inc. Raytheon retained its own existing <a href="/wiki/MMIC" class="mw-redirect" title="MMIC">MMIC</a> capabilities and has the right to license TI's MMIC technology for use in future product applications from TriQuint.<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup></p><p>Shortly after Raytheon acquired TI DSEG, Raytheon then acquired <a href="/wiki/Hughes_Aircraft" class="mw-redirect" title="Hughes Aircraft">Hughes Aircraft</a> from <a href="/wiki/General_Motors" title="General Motors">General Motors</a>. Raytheon then owned TI's <a href="/wiki/Mercury_cadmium_telluride" title="Mercury cadmium telluride">mercury cadmium telluride</a> detector business and <a href="/wiki/Infrared" title="Infrared">infrared</a> (IR) systems group. In California, it also had Hughes infrared detector and an IR systems business. When again the US government forced Raytheon to divest itself of a duplicate capability, the company kept the TI IR systems business and the Hughes detector business. As a result of these acquisitions, these former arch rivals of TI systems and Hughes detectors work together.<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup></p><p>Immediately after acquisition, DSEG was known as Raytheon TI Systems (RTIS).<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup> It is now fully integrated into Raytheon and this designation no longer exists.
</p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Artificial_intelligence">Artificial intelligence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Texas_Instruments&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Artificial intelligence" data-section="10" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>TI was active in the 1980s, in the area of <a href="/wiki/Artificial_intelligence" title="Artificial intelligence">artificial intelligence</a>. In addition to ongoing developments in speech and signal processing and recognition, it developed and sold the <a href="/wiki/TI_Explorer" title="TI Explorer">Explorer</a> computer family of <a href="/wiki/Lisp_machine" title="Lisp machine">Lisp machines</a>. For the Explorer, a special 32-bit Lisp microprocessor was developed, which was used in the Explorer II and the TI MicroExplorer (a Lisp Machine on a <a href="/wiki/NuBus" title="NuBus">NuBus</a> board for the Apple <a href="/wiki/Macintosh" title="Macintosh">Macintosh</a>). AI application software developed by TI for the Explorer included the gate assignment system for United Airlines, <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://articles.latimes.com/1989-11-07/business/fi-1112_1_expert-system">described as</a> "an artificial intelligence program that captures the combined experience and knowledge of a half-dozen United operations experts."  In software for the PC, they introduced "Personal Consultant", a rule-based <a href="/wiki/Expert_system" title="Expert system">expert system</a> development tool and runtime engine, followed by "Personal Consultant Plus" written in the Lisp-like language from MIT known as <a href="/wiki/Scheme_(programming_language)" title="Scheme (programming language)">Scheme</a>, and the natural language menu system NLMenu.<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Sensors_and_controls">Sensors and controls</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Texas_Instruments&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Sensors and controls" data-section="11" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>TI was a major <a href="/wiki/Original_equipment_manufacturer" title="Original equipment manufacturer">original-equipment manufacturer</a> of <a href="/wiki/Sensor" title="Sensor">sensor</a>, control, protection, and <a href="/wiki/RFID" class="mw-redirect" title="RFID">RFID</a> products for the automotive, appliance, aircraft, and other industries. The Sensors &amp; Controls division was headquartered in <a href="/wiki/Attleboro,_Massachusetts" title="Attleboro, Massachusetts">Attleboro, Massachusetts</a>.
</p><p>In 2006, <a href="/wiki/Bain_Capital_LLC" class="mw-redirect" title="Bain Capital LLC">Bain Capital LLC</a>, a private equity firm, purchased the Sensors &amp; Controls division for $3.0 billion in cash.<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup>  The RFID portion of the division remained part of TI, transferring to the Application Specific Products business unit of the Semiconductor division, with the newly formed independent company based in Attleboro taking the name <a href="/w/index.php?title=Sensata_Technologies&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Sensata Technologies (page does not exist)">Sensata Technologies</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Software">Software</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Texas_Instruments&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Software" data-section="12" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>In 1997, TI sold its software division, along with its main products such as the <a href="/wiki/CA_Gen" title="CA Gen">CA Gen</a>, to <a href="/wiki/Sterling_Software" title="Sterling Software">Sterling Software</a>, which is now part of <a href="/wiki/Computer_Associates" class="mw-redirect" title="Computer Associates">Computer Associates</a>. However, TI still owns small pieces of software, such as the software for calculators such as the  <a href="/wiki/TI_Interactive!" class="mw-redirect" title="TI Interactive!">TI Interactive!</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup> TI also creates a significant amount of target software for its digital signal processors, along with host-based tools for creating DSP applications.<sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span id="TI_store_.28eCommerce.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="TI_store_(eCommerce)">TI store (eCommerce)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Texas_Instruments&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: TI store (eCommerce)" data-section="13" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>In 2000, a team at TI had a desire to sell <a href="/wiki/Code_Composer_Studio" title="Code Composer Studio">Code Composer Studio</a> software to customers via the internet.  In response, an employee bought an off-the-shelf program and built an eCommerce platform over the course of one weekend –  the result was <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://store.ti.com/">TI store</a>. During the same year, a separate online integrated circuit sample-ordering system was launched to replace a physical room where orders were received by phone, fax, and email and then fulfilled by hand.
</p><p>In 2002, the TI store inventory was expanded to include paid evaluation modules (EVMs) and a separate home-grown online evaluation module sample system was launched. This resulted in three separate eCommerce systems for TI: one for paid EVMs, one for sample EVMs, and one for sample integrated circuits.
</p><p>In 2010, the TI store was completely redesigned using a new online platform. Additionally, sample EVMs were moved into the eStore from the home-grown application.
</p><p>In 2014, at the TI store, integrated circuit samples were moved into the store from the home-grown application and integrated circuit purchase options were added. These changes combined all evaluation and development modules, integrated circuits, and sample programs into one platform.
</p><p>In 2015, the TI store increased its maximum order quantity from 99 to 999.
</p><p>In December 2016, Code Composer Studio v7 was released at no cost, as it included a new licensing model: Technology Software Publicly Available.<sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup></p>

<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Analog_products">Analog products</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Texas_Instruments&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Analog products" data-section="17" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>Analog products connect the physical and the digital worlds –  turning signals such as sound, pressure, temperature, humidity, and light into digital information to be used by electronic devices –  and translating those data back to information to which to interact in the real world. These chips, including data converters and power management and other devices, can extend and optimize battery life, improve accuracy, sense conditions such as humidity and temperature, and much more.<sup id="cite_ref-ti.com_61-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ti.com-61">[61]</a></sup></p><p>TI's analog integrated-circuit portfolio includes both power-management and signal-chain devices, as well as integrated analog device and application solutions.<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceB_62-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceB-62">[62]</a></sup></p>

<p>DLP is a trademark under which Texas Instruments sells technology regarding TVs, video projectors, and <a href="/wiki/Digital_cinema" title="Digital cinema">digital cinema</a>.  On February 2, 2000, Philippe Binant, technical manager of Digital Cinema Project at <a href="/wiki/Gaumont_Film_Company" title="Gaumont Film Company">Gaumont</a> in France, realized the first digital cinema projection in Europe with the DLP CINEMA technology developed by TI. DLP technology enables a diverse range of display and advanced light control applications spanning industrial, enterprise, automotive, and consumer market segments.
</p>
<dl><dt>Custom application-specific integrated circuits (ASICs)</dt></dl><p>The ASICs business develops more complex integrated-circuit solutions for clients on a custom basis.

<h4 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Educational_technology">Educational technology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Texas_Instruments&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Educational technology" data-section="26" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h4>
<p>TI has produced educational toys for children, including the <a href="/wiki/Little_Professor" title="Little Professor">Little Professor</a> in 1976 and <a href="/wiki/Dataman" title="Dataman">Dataman</a> in 1977.<sup id="cite_ref-66" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-67" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup></p><p>TI produces a range of calculators, with the <a href="/wiki/TI-30" title="TI-30">TI-30</a> being one of the most popular early <a href="/wiki/Calculator" title="Calculator">calculators</a>. TI has also developed a line of <a href="/wiki/Graphing_calculator" title="Graphing calculator">graphing calculators</a>, the first being the <a href="/wiki/TI-81" title="TI-81">TI-81</a>, and most popular being the <a href="/wiki/TI-83_Plus" class="mw-redirect" title="TI-83 Plus">TI-83 Plus</a> (with the <a href="/wiki/TI-84_Plus" class="mw-redirect" title="TI-84 Plus">TI-84 Plus</a> being an updated equivalent).
</p><p>Many TI calculators are still sold without graphing capabilities.<sup id="cite_ref-68" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup> The TI-30 has been replaced by the <a href="/wiki/TI-30" title="TI-30">TI-30X IIS</a>. Also, some financial calculators are for sale on the TI website.
</p><p>In 2007, TI released the <a href="/wiki/TI-Nspire" class="mw-redirect" title="TI-Nspire">TI-Nspire</a> family of calculators and computer software that has similar capabilities to the calculators.
</p><p>Less than 3% of Texas Instruments’ overall revenue comes from calculators, part of the $1.43 billion revenue in the "Other" section in the company's 2018 annual report. The calculators are a lucrative product. For example, estimates have a $15 to $20 cost to produce TI-84 Plus which likely has a profit margin of at least 50%.
</p><p>Throughout the 1980s, Texas Instruments worked closely with National Council of Teachers of Mathematics (NCTM) to develop a calculator to become the educational standard. In 1986, Connecticut School Board became the first to require a graphing calculator on state-mandated exams. Chicago Public Schools gave a free calculator to every student, beginning in the fourth grade, in 1988. New York required the calculator in 1992 for its Regents exams after first allowing it the previous year. The College Board  required calculators on the Advanced Placement tests in 1993 and allowed calculators on the SAT a year later. Texas Instruments provides free services for free to the College Board, which administers AP tests and the SAT, and also has a group called Teachers Teaching for Technology (T3), which educates teachers on how to use its calculators.<sup id="cite_ref-TIcalcsMedium_17-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TIcalcsMedium-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<h5 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="TI_calculator_community">TI calculator community</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Texas_Instruments&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: TI calculator community" data-section="27" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h5>
<p>In the 1990s, with the advent of TI's graphing calculator series, programming became popular among some students. The TI-8x series of calculators (beginning with the TI-81) came with a built-in <a href="/wiki/BASIC" title="BASIC">BASIC</a> interpreter, through which simple programs could be created. The <a href="/wiki/TI-85" title="TI-85">TI-85</a> was the first TI calculator to allow <a href="/wiki/Assembly_language" title="Assembly language">assembly</a> programming (via a shell called "ZShell"), and the TI-83 was the first in the series to receive native assembly. While the earlier BASIC programs were relatively simple applications or small games, the modern assembly-based programs rival what one might find on a <a href="/wiki/Game_Boy" title="Game Boy">Game Boy</a> or <a href="/wiki/Personal_digital_assistant" title="Personal digital assistant">personal digital assistant</a>.
</p><p>Around the same time that these programs were first being written, personal <a href="/wiki/Web_page" title="Web page">web pages</a> were becoming popular (through services such as <a href="/wiki/Angelfire" title="Angelfire">Angelfire</a> and <a href="/wiki/GeoCities" class="mw-redirect" title="GeoCities">GeoCities</a>), and programmers began creating websites to host their work, along with tutorials and other calculator-relevant information. This led to the formation of TI calculator <a href="/wiki/Webrings" class="mw-redirect" title="Webrings">webrings</a> and eventually a few large communities, including the now-defunct TI-Files and still-active ticalc.org.<sup id="cite_ref-69" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup></p><p>The TI community reached the height of its popularity in the early 2000s, with new websites and programming groups being started almost daily. In fact, the aforementioned community sites were exploding with activity, with close to 100 programs being uploaded daily by users of the sites. Also, a competition existed between both sites to be the top site in the community, which helped increase interest and activity in the community.
</p><p>One of the common unifying forces that has united the community over the years has been the rather contentious relationship with TI regarding control over its graphing calculators. TI graphing calculators generally fall into two distinct groups - those powered by the <a href="/wiki/Zilog_Z80" title="Zilog Z80">Zilog Z80</a> and those running on the <a href="/wiki/Motorola_68000" title="Motorola 68000">Motorola 68000</a> series. Both lines of calculators are locked by TI with checks in the hardware and through the signing of software to disable use of custom flash applications and operating systems.
</p><p>However, users employed the <a href="/wiki/General_number_field_sieve" title="General number field sieve">general number field sieve</a> to find the keys and publish them in 2009. TI responded by sending invalid <a href="/wiki/DMCA" class="mw-redirect" title="DMCA">DMCA</a> <a href="/wiki/Takedown_notice" class="mw-redirect" title="Takedown notice">takedown notices</a>, causing the <a href="/wiki/Texas_Instruments_signing_key_controversy" title="Texas Instruments signing key controversy">Texas Instruments signing key controversy</a>. Enthusiasts had already been creating their own operating systems before the finding of the keys, which could be installed with other methods.<sup id="cite_ref-70" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup></p>

<p>On April 4, 2011, Texas Instruments announced that it had agreed to buy <a href="/wiki/National_Semiconductor" title="National Semiconductor">National Semiconductor</a> for $6.5 billion in cash. TI paid $25 per share of National Semiconductor stock, which was an 80% premium over the share price of $14.07 as of April 4, 2011 close. The deal made TI the world's largest maker of analog technology components.<sup id="cite_ref-BbBw2011_84-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BbBw2011-84">[84]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-85" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-85">[85]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-86" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-86">[86]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-87" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-87">[87]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-88" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-88">[88]</a></sup>
The companies formally merged on September 23, 2011.<sup id="cite_ref-89" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-89">[89]</a></sup></p>
