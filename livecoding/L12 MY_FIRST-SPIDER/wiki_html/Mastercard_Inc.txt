<p><b>Mastercard Incorporated</b> (stylized as <b>MasterCard</b> from 1979 to 2016 and <b>mastercard</b> from 2016) is an American <a href="/wiki/Multinational_corporation" title="Multinational corporation">multinational</a> <a href="/wiki/Financial_services" title="Financial services">financial services</a> corporation headquartered in the <a href="/wiki/Mastercard_International_Global_Headquarters" title="Mastercard International Global Headquarters">Mastercard International Global Headquarters</a> in <a href="/wiki/Purchase,_New_York" title="Purchase, New York">Purchase, New York</a>, United States.<sup id="cite_ref-10K_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10K-3">[3]</a></sup> The Global Operations Headquarters is located in <a href="/wiki/O%27Fallon,_Missouri" title="O'Fallon, Missouri">O'Fallon, Missouri</a>, United States, a municipality of <a href="/wiki/St._Charles_County,_Missouri" title="St. Charles County, Missouri">St. Charles County, Missouri</a>.  Throughout the world, its principal business is to process payments between the banks of merchants and the card issuing banks or credit unions of the purchasers who use the "Mastercard" brand <a href="/wiki/Debit_card" title="Debit card">debit</a>, <a href="/wiki/Credit_card" title="Credit card">credit</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pre-paid_debit_card" class="mw-redirect" title="Pre-paid debit card">prepaid</a> cards to make purchases. Mastercard Worldwide has been a <a href="/wiki/Public_company" title="Public company">publicly traded company</a> since 2006. Prior to its <a href="/wiki/Initial_public_offering" title="Initial public offering">initial public offering</a>, Mastercard Worldwide was a <a href="/wiki/Cooperative" title="Cooperative">cooperative</a> owned by the more than 25,000 <a href="/wiki/Financial_institution" title="Financial institution">financial institutions</a> that issue its branded cards.

"Master Charge" from 1969 to 1979,<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> was created by an alliance of several regional bankcard associations in response to the <a href="/wiki/Visa_Inc." title="Visa Inc.">BankAmericard</a> issued by <a href="/wiki/Bank_of_America" title="Bank of America">Bank of America</a>, which later became the Visa credit card issued by <a </p>

<p>Although BankAmericard's debut in 1958 had been a notorious disaster, it began to turn a profit by May 1961.<sup id="cite_ref-Stearns1_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Stearns1-5">[5]</a></sup>  Bank of America deliberately kept this information secret and allowed then-widespread negative impressions to linger in order to ward off competition.<sup id="cite_ref-Stearns2_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Stearns2-6">[6]</a></sup>   This strategy was successful until 1966, when BankAmericard's profitability had become far too big to hide.<sup id="cite_ref-Stearns2_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Stearns2-6">[6]</a></sup>  From 1960 to 1966, there were only 10 new credit cards introduced in the United States, but from 1966 to 1968, approximately 440 credit cards were introduced by banks large and small throughout the country.<sup id="cite_ref-Stearns2_6-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Stearns2-6">[6]</a></sup>  These newcomers promptly banded together into regional bankcard associations.<sup id="cite_ref-Stearns3_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Stearns3-7">[7]</a></sup></p><p>In 1966, several bankcard associations joined together to form the Interbank Card Association (ICA).<sup id="cite_ref-Stearns3_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Stearns3-7">[7]</a></sup>  The Interbank branding in 1966 initially consisted only of a small unobtrusive lowercase <i>i</i> inside a circle in the lower right hand corner of the front of each Interbank card; the rest of the card design was the prerogative of each issuing bank.<sup id="cite_ref-Stearns4_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Stearns4-8">[8]</a></sup>   This tiny logo proved to be entirely unsatisfactory for creating nationwide <a href="/wiki/Brand_awareness" title="Brand awareness">brand awareness</a> in order to compete against the established leader, BankAmericard.<sup id="cite_ref-Stearns4_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Stearns4-8">[8]</a></sup>  In 1969, Interbank developed a new national brand, "Master Charge: The Interbank Card" by combining the two overlapping yellow and orange circles of the Western States Bankcard Association with the "Master Charge" name coined by the First National Bank of Louisville, Kentucky.<sup id="cite_ref-Stearns4_8-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Stearns4-8">[8]</a></sup></p><p>That same year, <a href="/wiki/Citibank" title="Citibank">First National City Bank</a> joined Interbank and merged its proprietary <a href="/wiki/The_Everything_Card" title="The Everything Card">Everything Card</a> with Master Charge.
</p><p>In 1968, the ICA and <a href="/wiki/Eurocard_(payment_card)" title="Eurocard (payment card)">Eurocard</a> started a strategic alliance, which effectively allowed the ICA access to the European market, and for Eurocard to be accepted on the ICA network. The <a href="/wiki/Access_(credit_card)" title="Access (credit card)">Access</a> card system from the United Kingdom joined the ICA/Eurocard alliance in 1972.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2020)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p><p>In 1979, "Master Charge: The Interbank Card" was renamed "MasterCard". In 1997, Mastercard took over the Access card; the Access brand was then retired.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p><p>In 2002, MasterCard International merged with <a href="/wiki/Europay_International" title="Europay International">Europay International</a>, another large credit-card issuer association, of which Eurocard had become a part in 1992.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2020)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p><p>In mid-2006, MasterCard International changed its name to MasterCard Worldwide. This was to suggest a more global scale. In addition, the company introduced a new corporate logo adding a third circle to the two that had been used in the past (the familiar card logo, resembling a <a href="/wiki/Venn_diagram" title="Venn diagram">Venn diagram</a>, remained unchanged). A new corporate tagline was introduced at the same time: "The Heart of Commerce".<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p><p>In August 2010, MasterCard expanded its e-commerce offering with the acquisition of <a href="/wiki/DataCash" title="DataCash">DataCash</a>, a UK-based payment processing and fraud/risk management provider.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p><p>In March 2012, MasterCard announced the expansion of its mobile contactless payments program, including markets across the Middle East.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p><p>In spring 2014, MasterCard acquired Australia's leading rewards program manager company Pinpoint for an undisclosed amount.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p><p>Mastercard teamed with <a href="/wiki/Apple_Inc." title="Apple Inc.">Apple</a> in September 2014, to incorporate a new mobile wallet feature into Apple's new iPhone models, enabling users to more readily use their Mastercard, and other credit cards.<sup id="cite_ref-iPhoneWallet_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-iPhoneWallet-14">[14]</a></sup></p><p>In July 2016, Mastercard introduced their new rebranding, along with a new corporate logo. In addition, they changed their service name from "MasterCard" to "mastercard".<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p><p>In August 2017, Mastercard acquired <a href="/wiki/Brighterion" title="Brighterion">Brighterion</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Delaware_General_Corporation_Law" title="Delaware General Corporation Law">Delaware Corporation</a> headquartered in San Francisco, California that provides a portfolio of artificial intelligence and machine learning technologies.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Brighterion holds several <a href="/wiki/Patent" title="Patent">patents</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="IPO">IPO</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mastercard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: IPO" data-section="2" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>The company, which had been organized as a <a href="/wiki/Cooperative" title="Cooperative">cooperative</a> of banks, had an <a href="/wiki/Initial_public_offering" title="Initial public offering">initial public offering</a> on May 25, 2006, selling 95.5 million shares at <a href="/wiki/United_States_dollar" title="United States dollar">$</a>39 each.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> The stock is traded on the <a href="/wiki/New_York_Stock_Exchange" title="New York Stock Exchange">NYSE</a> under the symbol MA, with a market capitalization of $236.15 billion as of August 2016.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Litigation">Litigation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mastercard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Litigation" data-section="3" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<h4 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Antitrust_lawsuit_by_ATM_operators">Antitrust lawsuit by ATM operators</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mastercard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Antitrust lawsuit by ATM operators" data-section="4" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h4>
<p>Mastercard, along with <a href="/wiki/Visa_Inc." title="Visa Inc.">Visa</a>, has been sued in a class action by ATM operators that claims the credit card networks' rules effectively fix ATM access fees. The suit claims that this is a <a href="/wiki/Restraint_of_trade" title="Restraint of trade">restraint of trade</a> in violation of federal law. The lawsuit was filed by the National ATM Council and independent operators of automated teller machines. More specifically, it is alleged that Mastercard's and Visa's network rules prohibit ATM operators from offering lower prices for transactions over PIN-debit networks that are not affiliated with Visa or Mastercard. The suit says that this price fixing artificially raises the price that consumers pay using ATMs, limits the revenue that ATM operators earn, and violates the <a href="/wiki/Sherman_Act" class="mw-redirect" title="Sherman Act">Sherman Act</a>'s prohibition against unreasonable restraints of trade. Johnathan Rubin, an attorney for the plaintiffs said, "Visa and Mastercard are the ringleaders, organizers, and enforcers of a conspiracy among U.S. banks to fix the price of ATM access fees in order to keep the competition at bay."<sup id="cite_ref-ATM_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ATM-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<h4 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Debit_card_swipe_fee_price_fixing">Debit card swipe fee price fixing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mastercard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Debit card swipe fee price fixing" data-section="5" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h4>
<p>Both Mastercard and Visa have paid approximately $3 billion in damages resulting from a <a href="/wiki/Class-action_lawsuit" class="mw-redirect" title="Class-action lawsuit">class-action lawsuit</a> filed in January 1996.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> The litigation cites several retail giants as plaintiffs, including <a href="/wiki/Wal-Mart" class="mw-redirect" title="Wal-Mart">Wal-Mart</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sears_Holdings_Corporation" class="mw-redirect" title="Sears Holdings Corporation">Sears, Roebuck &amp; Co.</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Safeway_Inc." title="Safeway Inc.">Safeway</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<h4 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Antitrust_settlement_with_U.S._Justice_Department">Antitrust settlement with U.S. Justice Department</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mastercard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Antitrust settlement with U.S. Justice Department" data-section="6" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h4>
<p>In October 2010, Mastercard and Visa reached a settlement with the <a href="/wiki/U.S._Justice_Department" class="mw-redirect" title="U.S. Justice Department">U.S. Justice Department</a> in another antitrust case. The companies agreed to allow merchants displaying their logos to decline certain types of cards (because <a href="/wiki/Interchange_fee" title="Interchange fee">interchange fees</a> differ), or to offer consumers discounts for using cheaper cards.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<h4 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Payment_Card_Interchange_Fee_and_Merchant_Discount_Antitrust_Litigation">Payment Card Interchange Fee and Merchant Discount Antitrust Litigation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mastercard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Payment Card Interchange Fee and Merchant Discount Antitrust Litigation" data-section="7" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h4>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Payment_Card_Interchange_Fee_and_Merchant_Discount_Antitrust_Litigation" title="Payment Card Interchange Fee and Merchant Discount Antitrust Litigation">Payment Card Interchange Fee and Merchant Discount Antitrust Litigation</a></div>
<p>On November 27, 2012, a federal judge entered an order granting preliminary approval to a proposed settlement to a class-action lawsuit<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> filed in 2005 by merchants and trade associations against Mastercard and Visa. The suit was filed due to alleged price fixing practices employed by Mastercard and Visa. About one-fourth of the named class plaintiffs have decided to opt out of the settlement. Opponents object to provisions that would bar future lawsuits and prevent merchants from opting out of significant portions of the proposed settlement.<sup id="cite_ref-MasterCard_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MasterCard-25">[25]</a></sup></p><p>Plaintiffs allege that <a href="/wiki/Visa_Inc." title="Visa Inc.">Visa Inc.</a> and Mastercard fixed <a href="/wiki/Interchange_fees" class="mw-redirect" title="Interchange fees">interchange fees</a>, also known as swipe fees, that are charged to merchants for the privilege of accepting payment cards. In their complaint, the plaintiffs also alleged that the defendants unfairly interfere with merchants from encouraging customers to use less expensive forms of payment such as lower-cost cards, cash, and checks.<sup id="cite_ref-MasterCard_25-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MasterCard-25">[25]</a></sup></p><p>A settlement of $6.24 billion has been reached and a court is scheduled to approve or deny the agreement on November 7, 2019.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>

<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Antitrust_issues_in_the_United_States">Antitrust issues in the United States</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mastercard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Antitrust issues in the United States" data-section="10" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>Few companies have faced more antitrust lawsuits both in the US and abroad.<sup id="cite_ref-Unfair_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Unfair-29">[29]</a></sup></p><p>Mastercard, along with Visa, engaged in systematic parallel exclusion against <a href="/wiki/American_Express" title="American Express">American Express</a> during the 1980s and 1990s. Mastercard used exclusivity clauses in its contracts and blacklists to prevent banks from doing business with American Express. Such exclusionary clauses and other written evidence were used by the United States Department of Justice in regulatory actions against Mastercard and Visa.<sup id="cite_ref-NYO_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NYO-30">[30]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Discover_Financial" title="Discover Financial">Discover</a> has sued Mastercard for similar issues.<sup id="cite_ref-Unfair_29-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Unfair-29">[29]</a></sup></p><p>In 1996 about 4 million merchants sued Mastercard in federal court for making them accept debit cards if they wanted to accept credit cards and dramatically increasing credit card swipe fees. This case was settled with a multibillion-dollar payment in 2003. This was the largest antitrust award in history.<sup id="cite_ref-Unfair_29-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Unfair-29">[29]</a></sup></p><p>In 1998, the Department of Justice sued Mastercard over rules prohibiting their issuing banks from doing business with American Express or Discover. The Department of Justice won in 2001 and the verdict withstood appeal. American Express also filed suit.<sup id="cite_ref-Unfair_29-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Unfair-29">[29]</a></sup></p><p>On August 23, 2001, Mastercard International Inc. was sued for violating the Florida Deceptive and Unfair Trade Practices Act.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p><p>On November 15, 2004, Mastercard Inc. paid damages to <a href="/wiki/American_Express" title="American Express">American Express</a>, due to anticompetitive practices that prevented American Express from issuing cards through U.S. banks,<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> and paid $1.8 billion for settlement.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Antitrust_investigations_in_Europe">Antitrust investigations in Europe</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mastercard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Antitrust investigations in Europe" data-section="11" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a> has repeatedly criticised Mastercard for monopolistic trade practices. In April 2009, Mastercard reached a settlement with the European Union in an antitrust case, promising to reduce debit card swipe fees to 0.2 percent of purchases.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> In December 2010, a senior official from the European Central Bank called for a break-up of the Visa/Mastercard duopoly by creation of a new European debit card for use in the <a href="/wiki/Single_Euro_Payments_Area" title="Single Euro Payments Area">Single Euro Payments Area</a> (SEPA).<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></p><p><a href="/wiki/WikiLeaks" title="WikiLeaks">WikiLeaks</a> published documents showing that American authorities lobbied <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a> to defend the interests of Visa and Mastercard.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> In response Mastercard blocked payments to WikiLeaks. Members of the <a href="/wiki/European_Parliament" title="European Parliament">European Parliament</a> expressed concern that payments from European citizens to a European corporation could apparently be blocked by the United States, and called for a further reduction in the dominance of Visa and Mastercard in the European payment system.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></p><p>In 2013, Mastercard was under investigation by the European Union for the high fees it charged merchants to accept cards issued outside the EU, compared to cards issued in the EU, as well as other anti-competitive practices that could hinder electronic commerce and international trade, and high fees associated with premium credit cards. The EU's competition regulator said that these fees were of special concern because of the growing role of non-cash payments. Mastercard was banned from charging fees on cross-border transactions conducted wholly within the EU via a ruling by the European Commission in 2007.<sup id="cite_ref-Euro_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Euro-38">[38]</a></sup> The European Commission said that their investigation also included large differences in fees across national borders. For instance, a €50 payment might cost €0.10 in the Netherlands but eight times that amount in Poland. The Commission argues that Mastercard rules that prohibit merchants from enjoying better terms offered in other EU countries may be against antitrust law.
</p><p>The European Consumer Organisation (<a href="/wiki/BEUC" class="mw-redirect" title="BEUC">BEUC</a>) praised the action against Mastercard. BEUC said interbank fees push up prices and hurt consumers. BEUC Director General Monique Goyens said, "So in the end, all consumers are hit by a scheme which ultimately rewards the card company and issuing bank."<sup id="cite_ref-Euro_38-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Euro-38">[38]</a></sup></p><p>In January 2019, the European Commission imposed an antitrust fine of €570,566,000 to Mastercard for "obstructing merchants' access to cross-border card payment services", due to Mastercard's rules obliging acquiring banks to apply the <a href="/wiki/Interchange_fee" title="Interchange fee">interchange fees</a> of the country where a retailer was located. The Commission concluded that Mastercard's rules prevented retailers from benefitting from lower fees and restricted competition between banks cross border, in breach of <a href="/wiki/European_Union_competition_law" title="European Union competition law">EU antitrust rules</a>. The infringement of antitrust rules ended when Mastercard amended its rules due to the entering into force of the Interchange Fee Regulation in 2015, which introduced caps on interchange fees. The Commission did grant Mastercard a 10% reduction of the fine however, in return for Mastercard acknowledging the facts and cooperating with the antitrust investigation.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Sanctions_of_Russia">Sanctions of Russia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mastercard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Sanctions of Russia" data-section="12" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>On December 27, 2014, <a href="/wiki/Visa_Inc." title="Visa Inc.">Visa Inc.</a> and Mastercard suspended servicing some Russian banks in <a href="/wiki/Crimea" title="Crimea">Crimea</a>:<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Rossiya_Bank" title="Rossiya Bank">Rossiya Bank</a>, Sobinbank, SMP Bank and Investcapitalbank,<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> after the United States issued sanctions against the Russian government due to the <a href="/wiki/2014_Russian_military_intervention_in_Ukraine" class="mw-redirect" title="2014 Russian military intervention in Ukraine">2014 Russian military intervention in Ukraine</a> and <a href="/wiki/Annexation_of_Crimea_by_the_Russian_Federation" title="Annexation of Crimea by the Russian Federation">Crimea annexation</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Regulatory_action_in_Australia_and_New_Zealand">Regulatory action in Australia and New Zealand</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mastercard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Regulatory action in Australia and New Zealand" data-section="13" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>In 2003, the <a href="/wiki/Reserve_Bank_of_Australia" title="Reserve Bank of Australia">Reserve Bank of Australia</a> required that interchange fees be dramatically reduced, from about 0.95% of the transaction to approximately 0.5%.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> One notable result has been the reduced use of <a href="/wiki/Loyalty_program" title="Loyalty program">reward cards</a> and increased use of debit cards. Australia also prohibited the "no surcharge" rule, a policy established by credit card networks like Visa and Mastercard to prevent merchants from charging a credit card usage fee to the cardholder. A surcharge would mitigate or even exceed the merchant discount paid by a merchant, but would also make the cardholder more reluctant to use the card as the method of payment.  Australia has also made changes to the interchange rates on debit cards and has considered abolishing interchange fees altogether.
</p><p>As of November 2006, <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">New Zealand</a> was considering similar actions, following a <a href="/wiki/State_sector_organisations_in_New_Zealand#Crown_entities" class="mw-redirect" title="State sector organisations in New Zealand">Commerce Commission</a> lawsuit alleging price-fixing by Visa and Mastercard. In New Zealand, merchants pay a 1.8% fee on every credit card transaction.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2020)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Blocking_payments_to_WikiLeaks">Blocking payments to WikiLeaks</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mastercard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Blocking payments to WikiLeaks" data-section="14" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>In December 2010, Mastercard blocked all payments to <a href="/wiki/WikiLeaks" title="WikiLeaks">WikiLeaks</a> due to claims that they engage in illegal activity.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> In a response, a group of online activists calling themselves "<a href="/wiki/Anonymous_(group)" title="Anonymous (group)">Anonymous</a>" organised a <a href="/wiki/Denial-of-service_attack" title="Denial-of-service attack">denial-of-service attack</a>; as a result, the Mastercard website experienced <a href="/wiki/Downtime" title="Downtime">downtime</a> on December 8–9, 2010.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> On December 9, 2010 the servers of Mastercard underwent a massive attack<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> as part of an <a href="/wiki/Operation_Avenge_Assange" class="mw-redirect" title="Operation Avenge Assange">Operation Avenge Assange</a> for closing down payments of whistleblowing platform <a href="/wiki/WikiLeaks" title="WikiLeaks">WikiLeaks</a>. According to several news sites, security of thousands of credit cards was compromised during that attack due to a phishing-site set up by the attackers.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> However, Mastercard denied this, stating that "cardholder account data has not been placed at risk".<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> WikiLeaks spokesman said: "We neither condemn nor applaud these attacks."<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/United_Nations" title="United Nations">U.N.</a> <a href="/wiki/Office_of_the_United_Nations_High_Commissioner_for_Human_Rights" title="Office of the United Nations High Commissioner for Human Rights">High Commissioner for Human Rights</a>, <a href="/wiki/Navi_Pillay" title="Navi Pillay">Navi Pillay</a> said that closing down credit lines for donations to WikiLeaks "could be interpreted as an attempt to censor the publication of information, thus potentially violating WikiLeaks' right to freedom of expression".<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup></p><p>DataCell, the company that enables WikiLeaks to accept credit and debit card donations, said it would take legal action against Visa Europe and Mastercard.<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Iceland" title="Iceland">Iceland</a>-based IT firm DataCell said it would move immediately to try to force the two companies to resume allowing payments to the website.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> DataCell had earlier<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Chronological_items" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers"><span title="The time period mentioned near this tag is ambiguous. (August 2012)">when?</span></a></i>]</sup> said that suspension of payments towards WikiLeaks is a violation of the agreements with their customers. On July 14, 2011 DataCell announced they had filed a complaint with the <a href="/wiki/European_Commission" title="European Commission">European Commission</a> claiming the closure by Visa and Mastercard of Datcell's access to the payment card networks violated the competition rules of the European Community.<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup></p><p>On July 12, 2012 a <a href="/wiki/Reykjav%C3%ADk" title="Reykjavík">Reykjavík</a> court ruled that <a href="/wiki/Valitor" title="Valitor">Valitor</a>, Visa and Mastercard's partner in Iceland, had to start processing donations within fourteen days<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup> or pay daily fines to the amount of ISK 800,000 (some $6000) for each day after that time, to open the payment gateway. Valitor also had to pay DataCell's litigation costs of ISK 1,500,000.<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Corporate_branding_of_all_Nigerian_ID_Cards">Corporate branding of all Nigerian ID Cards</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mastercard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Corporate branding of all Nigerian ID Cards" data-section="15" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>In 2014, pursuant to an agreement between Mastercard and the <a href="/wiki/Nigerian_Government" class="mw-redirect" title="Nigerian Government">Nigerian Government</a>, acting through the National Identity Management Commission, the new Nigerian <a href="/wiki/ID_cards" class="mw-redirect" title="ID cards">ID cards</a> will bear the Mastercard logo, contain personal database data and double as <a href="/wiki/Payment_cards" class="mw-redirect" title="Payment cards">payment cards</a>, irrevocably linking such payments to the individuals,<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup> sparking criticism by the <a href="/wiki/Human_rights_in_Nigeria" title="Human rights in Nigeria">Civil Rights Congress</a> alleging that it "represents a stamped ownership of a Nigerian by an American company ... reminiscent of the logo pasted on the bodies of African slaves transported across the Atlantic."<sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup></p>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(3)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Prepaid_debit_cards">Prepaid debit cards</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mastercard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Prepaid debit cards" data-section="16" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-3 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-3">
<p>Mastercard, <a href="/wiki/Comerica_Bank" class="mw-redirect" title="Comerica Bank">Comerica Bank</a>, and the U.S. Treasury Department teamed up in 2008 to create the Direct Express Debit Mastercard <a href="/wiki/Prepaid_debit_card" class="mw-redirect" title="Prepaid debit card">prepaid debit card</a>. The federal government uses the Express Debit product to issue electronic payments to people who do not have bank accounts, who are often referred to collectively as the "<a href="/wiki/Unbanked" title="Unbanked">unbanked</a>". Comerica Bank is the issuing bank for the debit card.
</p><p>The Direct Express cards give recipients a number of <a href="/wiki/Consumer_protection" title="Consumer protection">consumer protections</a>.
</p><p>In June 2013, Mastercard announced a partnership with <a href="/wiki/British_Airways" title="British Airways">British Airways</a> to offer members the Executive Club Multi-currency Cash Passport, which will allow members to earn extra points and make multi-currency payments. The Passport card allows users to load up to ten currencies (<a href="/wiki/Euro" title="Euro">euro</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pound_sterling" title="Pound sterling">pound</a>, U.S. dollar, Turkish Lira, Swiss franc, Australian dollar, Canadian dollar, New Zealand dollar, U.A.E. dirham and South African rand) at a locked-in rate. When used, the card selects the local currency to ensure the best <a href="/wiki/Exchange_rate" title="Exchange rate">exchange rate</a>, and if the local currency is not already loaded onto the card, funds are used from other currencies.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p><p>In 2019, a study into foreign exchange rates by a New Zealand financial website concluded that MasterCard's foreign exchange rates outperformed Visa and American Express<sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup></p>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(4)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Advertising">Advertising</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mastercard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Advertising" data-section="17" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-4 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-4">
<p>Mastercard's current advertising campaign tagline is "<i>Priceless</i>". The slogan associated with the campaign is "There are some things money can't buy. For everything else, there's Mastercard." The Priceless campaign in more recent iterations has been applicable to both Mastercard's credit card and <a href="/wiki/Debit_card" title="Debit card">debit card</a> products. They also use the <i>Priceless</i> description to promote products such as their "priceless travel" site which features deals and offers for Mastercard holders,<sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup> and "priceless cities", offers for people in specified locations.<sup id="cite_ref-59" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup></p><p>The first of these <i>Priceless</i> ads was run during the <a href="/wiki/1997_World_Series" title="1997 World Series">1997 World Series</a> and there are numerous different TV, radio and print ads.<sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup> Mastercard registered <i>Priceless</i> as a trademark.<sup id="cite_ref-61" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup> Actor <a href="/wiki/Billy_Crudup" title="Billy Crudup">Billy Crudup</a> has been the voice in the US market; in the UK, actor <a href="/wiki/Jack_Davenport" title="Jack Davenport">Jack Davenport</a> is the voice. The original idea and concept of the campaign stems from the advertising agency of <a href="/wiki/McCann_Erickson" class="mw-redirect" title="McCann Erickson">McCann Erickson</a> (as it was named in 1997).<sup id="cite_ref-62" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup></p><p>The purpose of the campaign is to position Mastercard as a friendly credit card company with a sense of humor, as well as responding to the public's worry that everything is being commodified and that people are becoming too materialistic.<sup id="cite_ref-63" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup></p><p>Many parodies have been made using this same pattern, especially on Comedy Central, though Mastercard has threatened legal action,<sup id="cite_ref-64" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup> contending that Mastercard views such parodies as a violation of its rights under the federal and state trademark and unfair competition laws, under the federal and state anti-<a href="/wiki/Trademark_dilution" title="Trademark dilution">dilution</a> laws, and under the Copyright Act. Despite these claims, however, noted US consumer advocate and presidential candidate <a href="/wiki/Ralph_Nader" title="Ralph Nader">Ralph Nader</a> emerged victorious (after a four-year battle) in the suit Mastercard brought against him after he produced his own "Priceless" political commercials.<sup id="cite_ref-65" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup> In the election ads Nader had criticized the corporate financing of both the Bush and Gore campaigns. Using the theme and some of the language behind the Mastercard "Priceless" campaign the election specified the dollar amounts contributed by corporate interests to both candidates and then summed it up with "finding out the truth ... priceless". Mastercard sued Nader's campaign committee and filed a temporary restraining order to stop the ads. The TRO was not granted and Nader defended the ads by claiming they were protected under the fair use doctrine.<sup id="cite_ref-66" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span id="Litigation_of_.22Priceless.22_ad_campaign"></span><span class="mw-headline" id='Litigation_of_"Priceless"_ad_campaign'>Litigation of "Priceless" ad campaign</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mastercard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title='Edit section: Litigation of "Priceless" ad campaign' data-section="18" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>In 1994, Argentinian born Edgardo Apesteguía created an ad campaign in Paraguay for Bancard's credit card. Its slogan was "There are things money can't buy, but, for everything else, there is Bancard".<sup id="cite_ref-67" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup> Plagiarism lawsuits were filed in Paraguay and Chile against Mastercard and their publicist McCann, who registered the "priceless" slogan ads in the US in 1999 and was represented in Paraguay by Nafta and Biedermann publicists at the time.
</p>

Previously, it also sponsored the <a href="/wiki/FIFA_World_Cup" title="FIFA World Cup">FIFA World Cup</a> but withdrew its contract after a court settlement and its rival, Visa, took up the contract in 2007.<sup id="cite_ref-69" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup> In 1997, Mastercard was the main sponsor of the <a href="/wiki/MasterCard_Lola" title="MasterCard Lola">Mastercard Lola</a> <a href="/wiki/Formula_One" title="Formula One">Formula One</a> team,<sup id="cite_ref-70" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup> which withdrew from the <a href="/wiki/1997_Formula_One_season" class="mw-redirect" title="1997 Formula One season">1997 Formula One season</a> after <a href="/wiki/1997_Australian_Grand_Prix" title="1997 Australian Grand Prix">its first race</a> due to financial problems.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. <p>Mastercard Contactless (formerly branded PayPass<sup id="cite_ref-78" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-78">[78]</a></sup>) is an <a href="/wiki/EMV" title="EMV">EMV</a>-compatible, "contactless" payment feature similar to American Express' <a href="/wiki/ExpressPay" class="mw-redirect" title="ExpressPay">ExpressPay</a>, and Visa <a href="/wiki/PayWave" class="mw-redirect" title="PayWave">payWave</a>. All three use the same symbol as shown on the right. It is based on the <a href="/wiki/ISO/IEC_14443" title="ISO/IEC 14443">ISO/IEC 14443</a> standard that provides cardholders with a simpler way to pay by tapping a payment card or other payment device, such as a phone or key fob, on a point-of-sale terminal reader rather than swiping or inserting a card. Contactless can currently be used on transactions up to and including 45 <a href="/wiki/Pound_sterling" title="Pound sterling">GBP</a>, 25 <a href="/wiki/Euro" title="Euro">EUR</a>, 40 <a href="/wiki/Swiss_Francs" class="mw-redirect" title="Swiss Francs">CHF</a>, 50 <a href="/wiki/United_States_dollar" title="United States dollar">USD</a>, 100 <a href="/wiki/Canadian_dollar" title="Canadian dollar">CAD</a>, 200 <a href="/wiki/Swedish_krona" title="Swedish krona">SEK</a>, 200 <a href="/wiki/Norwegian_krone" title="Norwegian krone">NOK</a>, 50 <a href="/wiki/Polish_zloty" class="mw-redirect" title="Polish zloty">PLN</a>, 200 <a href="/wiki/Danish_krone" title="Danish krone">DKK</a>, 80 <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand_Dollar" class="mw-redirect" title="New Zealand Dollar">NZD</a>, 100 <a href="/wiki/Australian_dollar" title="Australian dollar">AUD</a>, 1000 <a href="/wiki/Russian_ruble" title="Russian ruble">RUB</a>, 500 <a href="/wiki/Ukrainian_hryvnia" title="Ukrainian hryvnia">UAH</a> or 2000 <a href="/wiki/Indian_rupee" title="Indian rupee">INR</a>.
</p><p>In 2003, Mastercard concluded a nine-month PayPass market trial in <a href="/wiki/Orlando,_Florida" title="Orlando, Florida">Orlando, Florida</a> with <a href="/wiki/JPMorgan_Chase" title="JPMorgan Chase">JPMorgan Chase</a>, <a href="/wiki/Citibank" title="Citibank">Citibank</a>, and <a href="/wiki/MBNA" title="MBNA">MBNA</a>. More than 16,000 cardholders and more than 60 retailer locations participated in the market trial.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Chronological_items" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers"><span title="The date of the event predicted near this tag has passed. (March 2016)">needs update</span></a></i>]</sup> In addition, Mastercard worked with <a href="/wiki/Nokia" title="Nokia">Nokia</a>, <a href="/wiki/AT%26T_Wireless" class="mw-redirect" title="AT&amp;T Wireless">AT&amp;T Wireless</a>, and <a href="/wiki/JPMorgan_Chase" title="JPMorgan Chase">JPMorgan Chase</a> to incorporate Mastercard PayPass into mobile phones using <a href="/wiki/Near-field_communication" title="Near-field communication">near-field communication</a> technology, in <a href="/wiki/Dallas,_Texas" class="mw-redirect" title="Dallas, Texas">Dallas, Texas</a>. In 2011, <a href="/wiki/Google" title="Google">Google</a> and Mastercard launched <a href="/wiki/Google_Wallet" class="mw-redirect" title="Google Wallet">Google Wallet</a>, an <a href="/wiki/Android_(operating_system)" title="Android (operating system)">Android</a> application which allows a mobile device to send credit/debit card information directly to a Paypass-enabled payment terminal, bypassing the need for a physical card, up until the creation of <a href="/wiki/Android_Pay" class="mw-redirect" title="Android Pay">Android Pay</a>.
</p><p>During late 2015, Citicards in the USA stopped issuing Paypass-enabled plastic, but the keyfob was still available upon request. Effective July 16, 2016, Citicards stopped supporting Paypass completely. While existing plastic and keyfobs continued to work until their expiration date, no new Paypass-enabled hardware was issued to US customers after that date.
</p>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(9)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="QkR">QkR</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mastercard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: QkR" data-section="26" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-9 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-9">
<p><b>QkR</b> is a mobile payment app developed by Mastercard, for the purpose of ordering products and services through a <a href="/wiki/Smartphone" title="Smartphone">smartphone</a> with payments charged to the associated <a href="/wiki/Credit_card" title="Credit card">credit card</a>. It is being deployed for use in large-scale events, such as sport events, concerts, or movie theaters. Unlike other Mastercard mobile payment apps such as Pay Pass, QkR does not use <a href="/wiki/Near_field_communication" class="mw-redirect" title="Near field communication">NFC</a> from the phone, but rather an Internet connection.
</p><p>Users can open the app, scan a <a href="/wiki/QR_code" title="QR code">QR code</a> located on the back of the seat in front of them, and place orders for refreshments of their choice.<sup id="cite_ref-79" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-79">[79]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-80" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-80">[80]</a></sup>  The order is dispatched to a nearby concession stand, from where a runner delivers the items to the patrons' seats. It is already deployed in Australian movie theaters and is being tested in <a href="/wiki/Yankee_Stadium" title="Yankee Stadium">Yankee Stadium</a>.
</p><p>QkR is being marketed to vendors as a replacement for other mobile payment apps and a mobile ordering app, either distributed by the vendor (such as <a href="/wiki/Starbucks" title="Starbucks">Starbucks</a>'s app, <a href="/wiki/McDonald%27s" title="McDonald's">McDonald's</a>' app, or <a href="/wiki/Chipotle_Mexican_Grill" title="Chipotle Mexican Grill">Chipotle</a>'s mobile ordering app) or by a third party, such as <a href="/wiki/Square,_Inc." title="Square, Inc.">Square</a>, headed by <a href="/wiki/Twitter" title="Twitter">Twitter</a> cofounder <a href="/wiki/Jack_Dorsey" title="Jack Dorsey">Jack Dorsey</a>.
</p>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(10)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Banknet">Banknet</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mastercard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: Banknet" data-section="27" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-10 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-10">
<p>Mastercard operates Banknet, a global <a href="/wiki/Telecommunications_network" title="Telecommunications network">telecommunications network</a> linking all Mastercard card issuers, acquirers, and data processing centers into a single financial network. The operations hub is located in <a href="/wiki/St._Louis,_Missouri" class="mw-redirect" title="St. Louis, Missouri">St. Louis, Missouri</a>. Banknet uses the <a href="/wiki/ISO_8583" title="ISO 8583">ISO 8583</a> protocol.
</p><p>Mastercard's network differs significantly from <a href="/wiki/Visa_Inc." title="Visa Inc.">Visa</a>'s. <a href="/wiki/Visa_Inc." title="Visa Inc.">Visa</a>'s is a star-based system where all endpoints terminate at one of several main data centers, where all transactions are processed centrally. Mastercard's network is an edge-based, peer-to-peer network where transactions travel a meshed network directly to other endpoints, without the need to travel to a single point. This allows Mastercard's network to be much more resilient, in that a single failure cannot isolate a large number of endpoints.<sup id="cite_ref-81" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-81">[81]</a></sup></p>
