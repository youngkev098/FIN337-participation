<p><b>Kimberly-Clark Corporation</b> is an American <a href="/wiki/Multinational_corporation" title="Multinational corporation">multinational</a> <a href="/wiki/Personal_care" title="Personal care">personal care</a> corporation that produces mostly <a href="/wiki/Pulp_and_paper_industry" title="Pulp and paper industry">paper-based</a> consumer products. The company manufactures sanitary paper products and surgical &amp; medical instruments. It operates non-woven fabric mills and paper (except newsprint) mills.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Kimberly-Clark <a href="/wiki/Brand_name" class="mw-redirect" title="Brand name">brand name</a> products include <a href="/wiki/Kleenex" title="Kleenex">Kleenex</a> facial tissue, <a href="/wiki/Kotex" title="Kotex">Kotex</a> feminine hygiene products, <a href="/wiki/Cottonelle" title="Cottonelle">Cottonelle</a>, <a href="/wiki/Scott_Paper_Company" title="Scott Paper Company">Scott</a> and Andrex <a href="/wiki/Toilet_paper" title="Toilet paper">toilet paper</a>, Wypall utility wipes, <a href="/wiki/KimWipes" class="mw-redirect" title="KimWipes">KimWipes</a> scientific cleaning wipes and <a href="/wiki/Huggies" title="Huggies">Huggies</a> <a href="/wiki/Disposable" class="mw-redirect" title="Disposable">disposable</a> <a href="/wiki/Diaper" title="Diaper">diapers</a> and <a href="/wiki/Baby_wipes" class="mw-redirect" title="Baby wipes">baby wipes</a>.

</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(1)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kimberly-Clark&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History" data-section="1" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-1 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-1">
<table class="box-Original_research plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content ambox-Original_research" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">This section <b>possibly contains <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:No_original_research" title="Wikipedia:No original research">original research</a></b>.<span class="hide-when-compact"> Please <a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Kimberly-Clark&amp;action=edit">improve it</a> by <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability">verifying</a> the claims made and adding <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources#Inline_citations" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources">inline citations</a>. <p>Kimberly, Clark and Co. was founded in 1872 by <a href="/wiki/John_A._Kimberly" title="John A. Kimberly">John A. Kimberly</a>, <a href="/wiki/Havilah_Babcock" title="Havilah Babcock">Havilah Babcock</a>, <a href="/wiki/Charles_B._Clark" title="Charles B. Clark">Charles B. Clark</a> and Franklyn C. Shattuck in Neenah, Wisconsin, with $42,000 (equivalent to <a href="/wiki/United_States_dollar" title="United States dollar">US$</a>896,350 in 2019) of capital.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> The group's first business was operating <a href="/wiki/Paper_mill" title="Paper mill">paper mills</a>, which the collective expanded throughout the following decades. In 1888, the fledgling company faced a significant setback when its <a href="/wiki/Pulp_(paper)#Wood_pulp" title="Pulp (paper)">groundwood</a> "Atlas" paper mill burned in 1888, however through an extensive effort by labor and management, within five months the mill was rebuilt and in production at a greater capacity. In that same year the company began rapid expansion, purchasing land in a town then known as The Cedars for a new groundwood pulp plant designed by prominent paper mill architects <a href="/wiki/D._H._%26_A._B._Tower" title="D. H. &amp; A. B. Tower">D. H. &amp; A. B. Tower</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> In 1889, the town was renamed <a href="/wiki/Kimberly,_Wisconsin" title="Kimberly, Wisconsin">Kimberly</a> after John A. Kimberly. The company would also contract the firm to expand its vast <a href="/wiki/Sulfite_process" title="Sulfite process">sulphite pulp</a> complex in <a href="/wiki/Appleton,_Wisconsin" title="Appleton, Wisconsin">Appleton, Wisconsin</a>, which allowed it to become the first firm west of Pennsylvania to adopt this improved manufacturing process.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> The company developed cellu-cotton in 1914, a <a href="/wiki/Cotton" title="Cotton">cotton</a> substitute used by the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Army" title="United States Army">U.S. Army</a> as surgical cotton during <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a>. Army nurses used cellu-cotton pads as disposable <a href="/wiki/Sanitary_napkins" class="mw-redirect" title="Sanitary napkins">sanitary napkins</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> and six years later the company introduced <a href="/wiki/Kotex" title="Kotex">Kotex</a>, the first <a href="/wiki/Sanitary_napkin" title="Sanitary napkin">disposable feminine hygiene product</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Kleenex" title="Kleenex">Kleenex</a>, a disposable <a href="/wiki/Handkerchief" title="Handkerchief">handkerchief</a>, followed in 1924. Kimberly &amp; Clark joined with <a href="/wiki/New_York_Times" class="mw-redirect" title="New York Times">The New York Times Company</a> in 1926 to build a newsprint mill in <a href="/wiki/Kapuskasing" title="Kapuskasing">Kapuskasing</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ontario" title="Ontario">Ontario</a>, Canada. Two years later, the company went public as Kimberly-Clark.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p><p>The firm expanded internationally during the 1950s, opening plants in <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">Mexico</a>, <a href="/wiki/West_Germany" title="West Germany">West Germany</a> and the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>. It began operations in 17 more foreign locations in the 1960s.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The company formed <a href="/wiki/Midwest_Express_Airlines" class="mw-redirect" title="Midwest Express Airlines">Midwest Express Airlines</a> from its corporate flight department in 1984. Kimberly-Clark's headquarters moved from <a href="/wiki/Neenah,_Wisconsin" title="Neenah, Wisconsin">Neenah, Wisconsin</a> to Irving, Texas the following year,<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> although its products are still produced in Neenah. Under the leadership of <a href="/wiki/Darwin_Smith" title="Darwin Smith">Darwin Smith</a> as CEO from 1971 to 1991, the company was transformed from a business paper company to a consumer paper products company.
</p><p>In 1991, Kimberly-Clark and <a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times_Company" title="The New York Times Company">The New York Times Company</a> sold their jointly owned paper mill in <a href="/wiki/Kapuskasing" title="Kapuskasing">Kapuskasing</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ontario" title="Ontario">Ontario</a>. Kimberly-Clark entered a joint venture with <a href="/wiki/Buenos_Aires" title="Buenos Aires">Buenos Aires</a>-based Descartables Argentinos S.A. to produce <a href="/wiki/Personal_care_products" class="mw-redirect" title="Personal care products">personal care products</a> in <a href="/wiki/Argentina" title="Argentina">Argentina</a> in 1994<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> and also bought the feminine hygiene unit of VP-Schickedanz (Germany) for $123 million<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> and a 90% stake in Handan Comfort and Beauty Group (China).<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p><p>Kimberly-Clark bought <a href="/wiki/Scott_Paper" class="mw-redirect" title="Scott Paper">Scott Paper</a> in 1995 for $9.4 billion.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> In 1997, Kimberly-Clark sold its 50% stake in <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>'s Scott Paper to forest products company <a href="/wiki/Kruger_Inc." title="Kruger Inc.">Kruger Inc.</a><sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> and bought diaper operations in Spain and Portugal<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> and disposable <a href="/wiki/Surgical_mask" title="Surgical mask">surgical masks</a> maker Tecnol Medical Products.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> Augmenting its presence in Germany, Switzerland and Austria, in 1999 the company paid $365 million for the tissue business of Swiss-based Attisholz Holding.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> Expanding its offerings of medical products, the company bought Ballard Medical Products in 1999 for $774 million<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> and examination-glove maker Safeskin in 2000 for about $800 million.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p><p>Also in 2000, the company bought virtually all of <a href="/wiki/Taiwan" title="Taiwan">Taiwan</a>'s S-K Corporation; the move made Kimberly-Clark one of the largest manufacturers of packaged goods in Taiwan.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> The company later purchased Taiwan Scott Paper Corporation for about $40 million and merged the two companies, forming Kimberly-Clark Taiwan.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> In 2001, Kimberly-Clark bought Italian diaper maker Linostar and announced it was closing four Latin American manufacturing plants.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p><p>In 2002, Kimberly-Clark purchased paper-packaging rival <a href="/wiki/Amcor" title="Amcor">Amcor</a>'s stake in an Australian joint venture.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> In 2003, Kimberly-Clark added to its global consumer tissue business by acquiring the <a href="/wiki/Poland" title="Poland">Polish</a> tissue maker Klucze.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p><p>In early 2004, chairman and chief executive officer Thomas Falk began implementation of a global business plan that the company has detailed in July 2003. The firm combined its North American and European groups for personal care and consumer tissue under North Atlantic groups. In 2019, CEO Thomas Falk resigned his position but continued on as the company's <a href="/wiki/Chairman_of_the_board" class="mw-redirect" title="Chairman of the board">chairman of the board</a>. COO Michael D. Hsu became CEO following Falk's retirement.
</p>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(2)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Ownership">Ownership</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kimberly-Clark&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Ownership" data-section="2" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-2 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-2">
<p>Kimberly-Clark shares are mainly held by institutional investors (<a href="/wiki/Vanguard_group" class="mw-redirect" title="Vanguard group">Vanguard group</a>, <a href="/wiki/BlackRock" title="BlackRock">BlackRock</a>, <a href="/wiki/State_Street_Corporation" title="State Street Corporation">State Street Corporation</a> and others).<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(3)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Relationship_with_Midwest_Airlines">Relationship with Midwest Airlines</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kimberly-Clark&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Relationship with Midwest Airlines" data-section="3" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-3 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-3">
<p>The origin of <a href="/wiki/Midwest_Airlines" title="Midwest Airlines">Midwest Airlines</a> can be traced back to 1948, when Kimberly-Clark opened its corporate flight department and began providing air transportation for company executives and engineers between the company's headquarters in Neenah and its paper mills.
</p><p>In 1969, K-C Aviation was born from the company's air operations, and was dedicated to the maintenance of corporate aircraft. In 1982, K-C Aviation initiated shuttle flights for Kimberly-Clark employees between Appleton, Memphis and Atlanta. With this experience, and considering the <a href="/wiki/Airline_Deregulation_Act" title="Airline Deregulation Act">Airline Deregulation Act</a> of 1978, Kimberly-Clark and K-C Aviation formed a regularly scheduled passenger airline, <a href="/wiki/Midwest_Airlines" title="Midwest Airlines">Midwest Express Airlines</a>, which was started on June 11, 1984.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> The name of the airline was shortened to Midwest Airlines in 2003.
</p><p>K-C Aviation divested itself from the airline in 1996. Two years later, <a href="/wiki/Gulfstream_Aerospace" title="Gulfstream Aerospace">Gulfstream Aerospace</a> purchased K-C Aviation from Kimberly-Clark for $250 million, which included its operations at airports in Dallas, Appleton and <a href="/wiki/Westfield,_Massachusetts" title="Westfield, Massachusetts">Westfield, Massachusetts</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-kcavrnt_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kcavrnt-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(4)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Environmental_record">Environmental record</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kimberly-Clark&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Environmental record" data-section="4" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-4 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-4">
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Logging">Logging</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kimberly-Clark&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Logging" data-section="5" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Everett_-_Kimberly_Clark.jpg" class="image"><noscript><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/74/Everett_-_Kimberly_Clark.jpg/220px-Everett_-_Kimberly_Clark.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="3072" data-file-height="2304"></noscript><span class="lazy-image-placeholder" style="width: 220px;height: 165px;" data-src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/74/Everett_-_Kimberly_Clark.jpg/220px-Everett_-_Kimberly_Clark.jpg" data-alt="" data-width="220" data-height="165" data-class="thumbimage"> </span></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Everett_-_Kimberly_Clark.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The now razed Kimberly-Clark paper plant on the <a href="/wiki/Everett,_Washington" title="Everett, Washington">Everett, Washington</a>, waterfront.</div></div></div>
<p>In 2005, <a href="/wiki/Greenpeace" title="Greenpeace">Greenpeace</a> launched the <a href="/wiki/Kleercut" title="Kleercut">Kleercut</a> campaign against Kimberly-Clark because the company had been linked to the <a href="/wiki/Logging" title="Logging">logging</a> of <a href="/wiki/Old-growth_forest" title="Old-growth forest">ancient</a> <a href="/wiki/Boreal_forest" class="mw-redirect" title="Boreal forest">boreal forests</a>. The environmental organization charged that Kimberly-Clark was using more than three million tons of pulp a year from forests to produce <a href="/wiki/Tissue_paper" title="Tissue paper">tissue paper</a> products, such as the Kleenex brand. Greenpeace led a large grassroots student activist campaign targeting Kimberly-Clark for sourcing 22% of its <a href="/wiki/Paper_pulp" class="mw-redirect" title="Paper pulp">paper pulp</a> from Canadian boreal forests containing 200-year-old trees.<sup id="cite_ref-Grist.org_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Grist.org-33">[33]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Fast_Company_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fast_Company-34">[34]</a></sup></p><p>Greenpeace ended its campaign in August 2009 following the release of a new environmental policy by Kimberly-Clark.The corporation agreed to increase its use of recycled fiber, to use only Forest Stewardship Council certified wood and to stop purchasing pulp from the Kenogami and Ogoki forests.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> The two organizations announced that they were moving "away from conflict to a new collaborative relationship to further promote forest conservation, responsible <a href="/wiki/Forest_management" title="Forest management">forest management</a>, and the use of recycled fiber for the manufacture of tissue products."<sup id="cite_ref-Kleercut_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kleercut-36">[36]</a></sup></p><p>Kimberly-Clark has a target to purchase 100% of wood fiber from suppliers that gain independent sustainability certification, with a preference for <a href="/wiki/Forest_Stewardship_Council" title="Forest Stewardship Council">Forest Stewardship Council</a> (FSC)-certified fiber. Kimberly-Clark stated that by the end of 2010, it had achieved 98% of this target.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></p><p>As of 2015, most of Kimberly-Clarks tissue products are FSC-certified. <sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Chemical_pollution">Chemical pollution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kimberly-Clark&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Chemical pollution" data-section="6" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>By the 1980s, there was a <a href="/wiki/Dead_zone_(ecology)" title="Dead zone (ecology)">dead zone</a> for 12 kilometres downstream of the company's operations in <a href="/wiki/Terrace_Bay" title="Terrace Bay">Terrace Bay</a>, Canada resulting from a daily flow of 115 million litres of effluent.<sup id="cite_ref-TorStar_39-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TorStar-39">[39]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Jackfish,_Ontario" title="Jackfish, Ontario">Jackfish Bay</a> was also affected.<sup id="cite_ref-TorStar_39-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TorStar-39">[39]</a></sup></p><p>Initially, the company resisted calls to clean up its production methods, publicly hinting in 1986 that forcing the company to clean up would shut it down and force the <a href="/wiki/Layoff" title="Layoff">layoff</a> of 900 workers.<sup id="cite_ref-TorStar_39-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TorStar-39">[39]</a></sup></p><p>However, the company eventually yielded, and by 1989 it had spent $25 million <a href="/wiki/Canadian_dollar" title="Canadian dollar">CAD</a> on a "lagoon to remove suspended solids and oxygen-destroying chemicals from its waste."<sup id="cite_ref-TorStar_39-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TorStar-39">[39]</a></sup> At the same time, it also changed its bleaching process to stop producing dioxin and other poisons.<sup id="cite_ref-TorStar_39-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TorStar-39">[39]</a></sup></p>

<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="KimWipes">KimWipes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kimberly-Clark&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: KimWipes" data-section="23" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>KimWipes are a type of cleaning tissue commonly used in <a href="/wiki/Laboratories" class="mw-redirect" title="Laboratories">laboratories</a>. They are intended for applications in which leaving <a href="/wiki/Lint_(material)" title="Lint (material)">lint</a> or fibers on a surface would be undesirable, such as on <a href="/wiki/Microscopic_slide" class="mw-redirect" title="Microscopic slide">slides</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pipette" title="Pipette">pipettes</a>. They are sometimes used to clean <a href="/wiki/Lens_(optics)" class="mw-redirect" title="Lens (optics)">lenses</a>, but use on optical lenses with special water- and solvent-based coatings may cause light blemishes, and the manufacturer recommends using a wipe specifically designed for use with coated lenses. KimWipes are composed of virgin wood pulp from certified forests, with few chemical additives.
</p>
