<p>The <b>Otis Elevator Company</b> is an American company that develops, manufactures and markets elevators, escalators, moving walkways, and related equipment. Based in <a href="/wiki/Farmington,_Connecticut" title="Farmington, Connecticut">Farmington, Connecticut</a>, Otis is the world's largest manufacturer of vertical transportation systems, principally focusing on <a href="/wiki/Elevator" title="Elevator">elevators</a>, <a href="/wiki/Moving_walkways" class="mw-redirect" title="Moving walkways">moving walkways</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Escalator" title="Escalator">escalators</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> The company pioneered the development of the "safety elevator", invented by <a href="/wiki/Elisha_Otis" title="Elisha Otis">Elisha Otis</a> in 1852, which used a special mechanism to lock the elevator car in place should the hoisting ropes fail.

<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="The_booming_elevator_market">The booming elevator market</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Otis_Worldwide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: The booming elevator market" data-section="2" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3><p>
In 1852 <a href="/wiki/Elisha_Otis" title="Elisha Otis">Elisha Otis</a> invented the safety elevator, which automatically comes to a halt if the hoisting rope breaks. After a demonstration at the <a href="/wiki/Exhibition_of_the_Industry_of_All_Nations" title="Exhibition of the Industry of All Nations">1854 New York World's Fair</a> the elevator industry established credibility.<sup id="cite_ref-Invent.org_Article_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Invent.org_Article-4">[4]</a></sup></p><div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Otiselevator_1856.jpg" class="image"><noscript><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/27/Otiselevator_1856.jpg/220px-Otiselevator_1856.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="293" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="422" data-file-height="562"></noscript><span class="lazy-image-placeholder" style="width: 220px;height: 293px;" data-src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/27/Otiselevator_1856.jpg/220px-Otiselevator_1856.jpg" data-alt="" data-width="220" data-height="293" data-class="thumbimage"> </span></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Otiselevator_1856.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Otis elevator in Glasgow, Scotland, imported from the U.S. in 1856 for Gardner's Warehouse, the oldest cast-iron fronted building in the British Isles.</div></div></div><p>The Otis Elevator Company was founded in <a href="/wiki/Yonkers,_New_York" title="Yonkers, New York">Yonkers, New York</a> in 1853 by Elisha Otis. When Elisha died in 1861, his sons Charles and Norton formed a partnership and continued the business. During the <a href="/wiki/American_Civil_War" title="American Civil War">American Civil War</a>, their elevators were in high demand due to the shipment of war materials. Businesses throughout the United States purchased them. In 1864, with the partnership of J.M. Alvord, the company became known as Otis Brothers &amp; Co.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>  In 1867, Otis opened a factory in <a href="/wiki/Yonkers,_New_York" title="Yonkers, New York">Yonkers, New York</a>, the city where the company was founded.<sup id="cite_ref-:1_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-6">[6]</a></sup></p><p>In 1925, the world's first fully automatic elevator, Collective Control, was introduced. in 1931, the company installed the world's first double-deck elevator in New York City.<sup id="cite_ref-:3_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:3-7">[7]</a></sup></p><p>Otis opened a factory in <a href="/wiki/Bloomington,_Indiana" title="Bloomington, Indiana">Bloomington, Indiana</a> in 1965.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p><p><a href="/wiki/Fayette_S._Dunn" title="Fayette S. Dunn">Fayette S. Dunn</a> became president of the company in 1964, succeeding the late Percy Douglas.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="1980s_changes">1980s changes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Otis_Worldwide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: 1980s changes" data-section="3" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>Otis was acquired by <a href="/wiki/United_Technologies" title="United Technologies">United Technologies</a> in 1976 and became a wholly owned subsidiary. In 1981, the Yonkers factory closed,<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> and was later purchased by <a href="/wiki/Kawasaki_Heavy_Industries_Rolling_Stock_Company" title="Kawasaki Heavy Industries Rolling Stock Company">Kawasaki</a> for use as a rail car assembly plant.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Also in 1981, Otis signed a business agreement with an elevator company in China<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> and Francois Jaulin became president. In 1989 Karl J. Krapek became president of the company<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> and was replaced in 1991 by Jean-Pierre van Rooy.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p><p>During the 1980s, Otis developed the Remote Elevator Monitoring (REM) system that collected behavioral data from 300,000 elevators.<sup id="cite_ref-:2_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:2-16">[16]</a></sup></p><p>Otis has also dabbled in horizontal automated people-mover "shuttle" systems, such as the <a href="/wiki/Otis_Hovair" title="Otis Hovair">Otis Hovair</a>. In 1996, Otis formed a joint venture called "Poma-Otis Transportation Systems" with the French company <a href="/wiki/Pomagalski" class="mw-redirect" title="Pomagalski">Pomagalski</a> to promote these products. That partnership has since ended. 
</p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="1990s_acquisitions">1990s acquisitions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Otis_Worldwide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: 1990s acquisitions" data-section="4" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>Otis Elevator Company purchased Evans Lifts in the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">UK</a> when Evans Lifts Ltd went bankrupt in 1997 during its merge with Express Lift Company with the name ExpressEvans. It was the oldest and largest manufacturer of lift equipment in the UK, and was based in <a href="/wiki/Leicester" title="Leicester">Leicester</a>, <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a>. Otis' Customer Care Centre is still based in the old Evans Lifts building in Leicester. The building has since been extended by Otis. There are some installations of Evans Lifts in use today. Few lifts made by Otis are branded as Evans. Notably, an original Evans Lift is still in the Silver Arcade in Leicester. It formerly transported people to the upper floors. The upper floors are no longer occupied: the lift is no longer used. Also in 1997, Steve Page became president of Otis Elevator Co.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p><p>In 1999, Otis acquired CemcoLift, Inc, located in <a href="/wiki/Hatfield,_Pennsylvania" title="Hatfield, Pennsylvania">Hatfield, Pennsylvania</a>. The operation was later closed in October 2012, with the remaining business being sold to <a href="/w/index.php?title=Minnesota_Elevator_Inc&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Minnesota Elevator Inc (page does not exist)">Minnesota Elevator Inc</a>. In 2002, Ari Bousbib became president of Otis.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Recent_developments">Recent developments</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Otis_Worldwide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Recent developments" data-section="5" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Didier_Michaud-Daniel" title="Didier Michaud-Daniel">Didier Michaud-Daniel</a> became president of the company in 2008.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> In 2011, Otis acquired its competitor <a href="/wiki/Marshall_Elevator" title="Marshall Elevator">Marshall Elevator</a> for an undisclosed amount.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> Beginning in 2011, Otis cut its manufacturing operations in Nogales and supply-chain operations in <a href="/wiki/Tucson,_Arizona" title="Tucson, Arizona">Tucson, Arizona</a> as part of a consolidation of manufacturing operations in <a href="/wiki/Florence,_South_Carolina" title="Florence, South Carolina">Florence, South Carolina</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> where Otis purchased a former <a href="/wiki/Maytag" title="Maytag">Maytag</a> facility on 92 acres.<sup id="cite_ref-Lloyd_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lloyd-22">[22]</a></sup> As part of the consolidation, Otis shut down its Bloomington facility in 2012.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> In February 2012, Pedro Sainz de Baranda was named President of the company, replacing Michaud-Daniel.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p><p>In October 2013, Otis won its biggest ever contract; it will supply 670 elevators and escalators to the <a href="/wiki/Hyderabad_Metro" title="Hyderabad Metro">Hyderabad Metro</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-25">[25]</a></sup> Its second biggest contract was in 2012, to supply 349 elevators for the <a href="/wiki/Hangzhou_metro" class="mw-redirect" title="Hangzhou metro">Hangzhou metro</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_25-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-25">[25]</a></sup>  In 2015, Otis had to downplay its expansion in Asia after orders in the region started to drop.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> In January 2018, Otis inaugurated the installation of the world's longest double-deck elevator (30 elevators) in the 555-meter <a href="/wiki/Lotte_World_Tower" title="Lotte World Tower">Lotte World Tower</a> in Seoul. The "sky shuttle" can take 54 passengers from the 2nd floor to the 121st floor in 1 minute.<sup id="cite_ref-:3_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:3-7">[7]</a></sup></p><p>On October 9, 2017, Judy Marks was named President of Otis Elevator Company, replacing Philippe Delpech.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> In November 2018, United Technologies announced it would spin off Otis Elevator into an independent company, announced on November 26, 2018.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> Its headquarters are to remain in Connecticut.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> In 2019, Otis geared up its artificial intelligence technology by capitalizing on predictive technologies.<sup id="cite_ref-:2_16-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:2-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(2)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Otis_Worldwide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Description" data-section="6" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-2 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-2">
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Otis_Escalator_506_NCE_(Daniel_%C3%85hs).jpg" class="image"><noscript><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/be/Otis_Escalator_506_NCE_%28Daniel_%C3%85hs%29.jpg/220px-Otis_Escalator_506_NCE_%28Daniel_%C3%85hs%29.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="146" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="4288" data-file-height="2848"></noscript><span class="lazy-image-placeholder" style="width: 220px;height: 146px;" data-src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/be/Otis_Escalator_506_NCE_%28Daniel_%C3%85hs%29.jpg/220px-Otis_Escalator_506_NCE_%28Daniel_%C3%85hs%29.jpg" data-alt="" data-width="220" data-height="146" data-class="thumbimage"> </span></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Otis_Escalator_506_NCE_(Daniel_%C3%85hs).jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>An Otis escalator</div></div></div>
<p>The Otis Elevator Company develops, manufactures and markets elevators, escalators, moving walkways, and related equipment. The company is based in <a href="/wiki/Farmington,_Connecticut" title="Farmington, Connecticut">Farmington, Connecticut</a>. In 2014, Otis had more than 64,000 employees, with a revenue of US$13.0 billion. 
</p><p>Otis has installed elevators in some of the world's most famous structures, including the <a href="/wiki/Eiffel_Tower" title="Eiffel Tower">Eiffel Tower</a>, <a href="/wiki/Empire_State_Building" title="Empire State Building">Empire State Building</a> (modernization partnership signed in 2011<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup>), the original <a href="/wiki/World_Trade_Center_(1973%E2%80%932001)" title="World Trade Center (1973–2001)">World Trade Center</a>, <a href="/wiki/The_Twilight_Zone_Tower_of_Terror" title="The Twilight Zone Tower of Terror">The Twilight Zone Tower of Terror</a>, <a href="/wiki/Petronas_Twin_Towers" class="mw-redirect" title="Petronas Twin Towers">Petronas Twin Towers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Burj_Khalifa" title="Burj Khalifa">Burj Khalifa</a>, <a href="/wiki/CN_Tower" title="CN Tower">CN Tower</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Winchester_Mystery_House" title="Winchester Mystery House">Winchester Mystery House</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Hotel_del_Coronado" title="Hotel del Coronado">Hotel del Coronado</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Demarest_Building" title="Demarest Building">Demarest Building</a> (first electric elevator), the Singing Tower at <a href="/wiki/Bok_Tower_Gardens" title="Bok Tower Gardens">Bok Tower Gardens</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Skylon_Tower" title="Skylon Tower">Skylon Tower</a>.
</p>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(3)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Incidents">Incidents</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Otis_Worldwide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Incidents" data-section="7" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-3 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-3">
<p>On July 24, 2009, a group of 8 people were trapped for 8 hours in an Otis elevator in <a href="/wiki/Toronto" title="Toronto">Toronto</a>. A repair man who tried to fix the elevator fell 10 floors to his death.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> The following year on December 14, 2010, an Otis escalator installed in the <a href="/wiki/International_Trade" class="mw-redirect" title="International Trade">International Trade</a> station of <a href="/wiki/Shenzhen_Metro" title="Shenzhen Metro">Shenzhen Metro</a> Line 1 retrograded without notice, triggering a stampede that injured 25 passengers.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-33">[33]</a></sup> On July 5, 2011, an Otis 513MPE escalator installed at port A of <a href="/wiki/Beijing_Subway" title="Beijing Subway">Beijing Subway</a> <a href="/wiki/Line_4,_Beijing_Subway" class="mw-redirect" title="Line 4, Beijing Subway">Line 4</a> <a href="/wiki/Beijing_Zoo_Station" class="mw-redirect" title="Beijing Zoo Station">Zoo Station</a>  changed direction without notice, causing 30 people to fall. One boy was killed and 27 people injured, prompting China to halt the use of the escalator model. A Beijing official called the 513MPE escalator "defects in design, manufacturing and maintenance," and Otis had "unavoidable responsibility for the accident."<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> Shenzhen Metro authorities confirmed that the cause of the accident was also similar to the Shenzhen accident on December 14.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_33-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-33">[33]</a></sup></p><p>In March 2017, eighteen people suffered injuries at a Hong Kong's <a href="/wiki/Langham_Place_(Hong_Kong)" title="Langham Place (Hong Kong)">Langham Place</a> shopping mall when an escalator maintained by Otis reversed direction from up to down.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></p><p>On July 9, 2018 a one year old Otis escalator at <a href="/wiki/Stockholm_City_Station" title="Stockholm City Station">Stockholm City Station</a> changed direction from up to a rapid descent, in what local officials called a "free fall",<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> causing minor injuries. Inspection of the gear boxes of several Otis escalators revealed unexpected rust and heavy wear. This led to the newly-built train stations Stockholm City Station and Stockholm Odenplan being temporarily closed  on July 13, 2018, for security reasons until the problems were understood and resolved.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup></p>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(4)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Controversies">Controversies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Otis_Worldwide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Controversies" data-section="8" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-4 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-4">
<p>In February 2007, <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a> regulators fined Otis Elevator €225 million ($295.8 million) for being part of a price-fixing <a href="/wiki/Cartel" title="Cartel">cartel</a> in the Belgian, Dutch, Luxembourg, and German markets. Competitors <a href="/wiki/ThyssenKrupp" title="ThyssenKrupp">ThyssenKrupp</a>, <a href="/wiki/Schindler_Group" title="Schindler Group">Schindler Group</a>, <a href="/wiki/KONE" class="mw-redirect" title="KONE">KONE</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Mitsubishi_Elevator" class="mw-redirect" title="Mitsubishi Elevator">Mitsubishi Elevator</a> Europe were also fined for participating in the same cartel.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup></p>
