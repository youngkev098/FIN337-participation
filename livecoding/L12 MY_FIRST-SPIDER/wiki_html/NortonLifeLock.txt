<p><b>NortonLifeLock Inc.</b> (formerly known as <b>Symantec</b> <span class="rt-commentedText nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="'s' in 'sigh'">s</span><span title="/ɪ/: 'i' in 'kit'">ɪ</span><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'m' in 'my'">m</span><span title="/æ/: 'a' in 'bad'">æ</span><span title="'n' in 'nigh'">n</span><span title="/ˌ/: secondary stress follows">ˌ</span><span title="'t' in 'tie'">t</span><span title="/ɛ/: 'e' in 'dress'">ɛ</span><span title="'k' in 'kind'">k</span></span>/</a></span></span>) is an American <a href="/wiki/Software_company" title="Software company">software company</a> headquartered in <a href="/wiki/Tempe,_Arizona" title="Tempe, Arizona">Tempe, Arizona</a>, <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>. The company provides <a href="/wiki/Computer_security" title="Computer security">cybersecurity</a> <a href="/wiki/Software" title="Software">software</a> and services. NortonLifeLock is a <a href="/wiki/Fortune_500" title="Fortune 500">Fortune 500</a> company and a member of the <a href="/wiki/S%26P_500" class="mw-redirect" title="S&amp;P 500">S&amp;P 500</a> stock-market index. The company also has development centers in <a href="/wiki/Pune" title="Pune">Pune</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chennai" title="Chennai">Chennai</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bangalore" title="Bangalore">Bangalore</a>.

On January 29, 2016, Symantec sold its information-management subsidiary, named <a href="/wiki/Veritas_Technologies" title="Veritas Technologies">Veritas Technologies</a> (which Symantec had acquired in 2004)<sup id="cite_ref-p101_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-p101-4">[4]</a></sup> to <a href="/wiki/The_Carlyle_Group" title="The Carlyle Group">The Carlyle Group</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p><p>The name "<i>Symantec</i>" is a portmanteau of the words "<a href="/wiki/Syntax" title="Syntax">syntax</a>" and "<a href="/wiki/Semantics" title="Semantics">semantics</a>" with "technology".<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p><p>On August 9, 2019, <a href="/wiki/Broadcom_Inc." title="Broadcom Inc.">Broadcom Inc.</a> announced they would be acquiring the Enterprise Security software division of Symantec for $10.7 billion, after having attempted to purchase the whole company.  The sale closed November 4, 2019, and subsequently the company adopted the NortonLifeLock name.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>

</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(1)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span></h2><section class="mf-section-1 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-1">
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="1982_to_1989">1982 to 1989</span></h3>
<p>Founded in 1982 by <a href="/wiki/Gary_Hendrix" title="Gary Hendrix">Gary Hendrix</a> with a <a href="/wiki/National_Science_Foundation" title="National Science Foundation">National Science Foundation</a> grant, Symantec was originally focused on <a href="/wiki/Artificial_intelligence" title="Artificial intelligence">artificial intelligence</a>-related projects, including a database program.<sup id="cite_ref-Slaughter_2014_p._69_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Slaughter_2014_p._69-8">[8]</a></sup> Hendrix hired several <a href="/wiki/Stanford_University" title="Stanford University">Stanford University</a> <a href="/wiki/Natural_language_processing" title="Natural language processing">natural language processing</a> researchers as the company's first employees.<sup id="cite_ref-Slaughter_2014_p._69_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Slaughter_2014_p._69-8">[8]</a></sup></p><p>In 1984, it became clear that the advanced natural language and database system that Symantec had developed could not be ported from DEC minicomputers to the PC.<sup id="cite_ref-Springer_2015_p._193_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Springer_2015_p._193-9">[9]</a></sup> This left Symantec without a product, but with expertise in natural language database query systems and technology.<sup id="cite_ref-Jones_2014_p._198_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jones_2014_p._198-10">[10]</a></sup> As a result, later in 1984 Symantec was acquired by another, smaller software startup company, C&amp;E Software, founded by Denis Coleman and <a href="/wiki/Gordon_Eubanks" title="Gordon Eubanks">Gordon Eubanks</a> and headed by Eubanks.<sup id="cite_ref-Jones_2014_p._198_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jones_2014_p._198-10">[10]</a></sup> C&amp;E Software developed a combined file management and word processing program called <a href="/wiki/Q%26A_(software)" class="mw-redirect" title="Q&amp;A (software)">Q&amp;A</a><sup id="cite_ref-Jones_2014_p._198_10-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jones_2014_p._198-10">[10]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Barry_Greenstein" title="Barry Greenstein">Barry Greenstein</a>, now a professional poker player, was the principal developer of the word processor component within Q&amp;A.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2019)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p><p>The merged company retained the name Symantec.<sup id="cite_ref-Jones_2014_p._198_10-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jones_2014_p._198-10">[10]</a></sup> Eubanks became its chairman, Vern Raburn, the former president of the original Symantec, remained as president of the combined company.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> The new Symantec combined the file management and word processing functionality that C&amp;E had planned, and added an advanced Natural Language query system (designed by Gary Hendrix and engineered by Dan Gordon) that set new standards for ease of database query and report generation. The natural language system was named "The Intelligent Assistant". Turner chose the name of Q&amp;A for Symantec's flagship product, in large part because the name lent itself to use in a short, easily merchandised logo. Brett Walter designed the user interface of Q&amp;A (Brett Walter, director of product management). Q&amp;A was released in November 1985.
</p><p>During 1986, Vern Raburn and Gordon Eubanks swapped roles, and Eubanks became CEO and president of Symantec, while Raburn became its chairman.<sup id="cite_ref-AI_Trends_1985_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AI_Trends_1985-12">[12]</a></sup> Subsequent to this change, Raburn had little involvement with Symantec, and in a few years time, Eubanks added the chairmanship to his other roles.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> After a slow start for sales of Q&amp;A in the fall of 1985 and spring of 1986, Turner signed up a new advertising agency called Elliott/Dickens, embarked on an aggressive new advertising campaign, and came up with the "Six Pack Program" in which all Symantec employees, regardless of role, went on the road, training and selling dealer sales staff nationwide in the United States. Turner named it Six Pack because employees were to work six days a week, see six dealerships per day, train six sales representatives per store and stay with friends free or at <a href="/wiki/Motel_6" title="Motel 6">Motel 6</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Jones_2014_p._199_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jones_2014_p._199-13">[13]</a></sup> Simultaneously, a promotion was run jointly with SofSell (which was Symantec's exclusive wholesale distributor in the United States for the first year that Q&amp;A was on the market). This promotion was very successful in encouraging dealers to try Q&amp;A.
</p><p>During this time, Symantec was advised by its board members Jim Lally and <a href="/wiki/John_Doerr" title="John Doerr">John Doerr</a> that if it would cut its expenses and grow revenues enough to achieve cash flow break-even, then <a href="/wiki/Kleiner_Perkins_Caufield_%26_Byers" class="mw-redirect" title="Kleiner Perkins Caufield &amp; Byers">Kleiner Perkins Caufield &amp; Byers</a> would back the company in raising more <a href="/wiki/Venture_capital" title="Venture capital">venture capital</a>. To accomplish this, the management team worked out a salary reduction schedule where the chairman and the CEO would take zero pay, all vice presidents would take a 50% pay cut, and all other employees' pay was cut by 15%. Two employees were laid off.  Eubanks also negotiated a sizable rent reduction on the office space the company had leased in the days of the original Symantec.  These expense reductions, combined with strong international sales of Q&amp;A, enabled the company to attain break-even.
</p><p>The significantly increased traction for Q&amp;A from this re-launch grew Symantec's revenues substantially, along with early success for Q&amp;A in international markets (uniquely a German version was shipped three weeks after the United States version, and it was the first software in the world that supported German Natural Language) following Turner's having placed emphasis on establishing international sales distribution and multiple language versions of Q&amp;A from initial shipment.
</p><p>In 1985, Rod Turner negotiated the publishing agreement with David Whitney for Symantec's second product, which Turner named NoteIt (an annotation utility for <a href="/wiki/Lotus_1-2-3" title="Lotus 1-2-3">Lotus 1-2-3</a>). It was evident to Turner that NoteIt would confuse the dealer channel if it was launched under the Symantec name, because Symantec had built up interest by that stage in Q&amp;A (but not yet shipped it), and because the low price for the utility would not be initially attractive to the dealer channel until demand had been built up.  Turner felt that the product should be marketed under a unique brand name.
</p><p>Turner and <a href="/wiki/Gordon_Eubanks" title="Gordon Eubanks">Gordon E. Eubanks Jr.</a>, then chairman of Symantec Corporation, agreed to form a new division of Symantec, and Eubanks delegated the choice of name to Turner. Turner chose the name Turner Hall Publishing, to be a new division of Symantec devoted to publishing third-party software and hardware. The objective of the division was to diversify revenues and accelerate the growth of Symantec. Turner chose the name Turner Hall Publishing, using his last name and that of Dottie Hall (Director of Marketing Communications) in order to convey the sense of a stable, long established, company.<sup id="cite_ref-computerworld_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-computerworld-14">[14]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Turner Hall Publishing's first offering was Note-It, a notation utility add-in for Lotus 1-2-3, which was developed by David Whitney, and licensed to Symantec.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> Its second product was the Turner Hall Card, which was a 256k RAM, half slot memory card, initially made to inexpensively increase the available memory for Symantec's flagship product, Q&amp;A. The Turner Hall division also marketed the card as a standalone product. Turner Hall's third product, also a 1-2-3 add-in was <a href="/wiki/Synex_Systems_Corporation#SQZ!" title="Synex Systems Corporation">SQZ!</a> a Lotus 1-2-3 spreadsheet compression utility developed by Chris Graham <a href="/wiki/Synex_Systems_Corporation" title="Synex Systems Corporation">Synex Systems</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> In the summer of 1986 Eubanks and Turner recruited Tom Byers from <a href="/wiki/Digital_Research" title="Digital Research">Digital Research</a>, to expand the Turner Hall Publishing product family and lead the Turner Hall effort.
</p><p>By the winter of 1986–87, the Turner Hall Publishing division had achieved success with NoteIt, the Turner Hall Card and SQZ!.  The popularity of these products, while contributing a relatively small portion of revenues to Symantec, conveyed the impression that Symantec was already a diversified company, and indeed, many industry participants were under the impression that Symantec had acquired Turner Hall Publishing. In 1987, Byers recruited Ted Schlein into the Turner Hall Product Group to assist in building the product family and in marketing.
</p><p><span id="TimeLine"></span>Revenues from Q&amp;A, and from Symantec's early launch into the international marketplace, combined with Turner Hall Publishing, generated the market presence and scale that enabled Symantec to make its first merger/acquisition, in February 1987, that of <a href="/wiki/Breakthrough_Software" title="Breakthrough Software">Breakthrough Software</a>, maker of the TimeLine project management software for DOS. Because this was the first time that Symantec had acquired a business that had revenues, inventory and customers, Eubanks chose to change nothing at BreakThrough Software for six months, and the actual merger logistics started in the summer of 1987, with Turner being appointed by Eubanks as general manager of the TimeLine business unit, Turner was made responsible for the successful integration of the company into Symantec and ongoing growth of the business, with P&amp;L. There was a heavy emphasis placed on making the minimum disruption by Eubanks and Turner.
</p><p>Soon after the acquisition of TimeLine/Breakthrough Software, Eubanks reorganized Symantec, structuring the company around product-centric groups, each having its own development, quality assurance, technical support and product marketing functions, and a general manager with profit and loss responsibility. Sales, finance and operations were centralized functions that were shared. This structure lent itself well to Symantec's further growth through mergers and acquisitions. Eubanks made Turner general manager of the new TimeLine Product Group, and simultaneously of the Q&amp;A Product Group, and made Tom Byers general manager of the Turner Hall Product Group. Turner continued to build and lead the company's international business and marketing for the whole company.
</p><p>At the TimeLine Product Group, Turner drove strong marketing, promotion and sales programs in order to accelerate momentum. By 1989 this merger was very successful—product group morale was high, TimeLine development continued apace, and the increased sales and marketing efforts applied built the TimeLine into the clear market lead in PC project management software on <a href="/wiki/DOS" title="DOS">DOS</a>.  Both the Q&amp;A and TimeLine product groups were healthily profitable.  The profit stream and merger success set the stage for subsequent merger and acquisition activity by the company, and indeed funded the losses of some of the product groups that were subsequently acquired.<sup id="cite_ref-computerworld_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-computerworld-14">[14]</a></sup> In 1989, Eubanks hired John Laing as VP worldwide sales, and Turner transferred the international division to Laing.  Eubanks also recruited Bob Dykes to be executive vice president for operations and finance, in anticipation of the upcoming IPO. In July 1989 Symantec had its IPO.
</p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="1990_to_1999">1990 to 1999</span></h3>
<p>In May 1990, Symantec announced its intent to merge with and acquire <a href="/wiki/Peter_Norton_Computing" title="Peter Norton Computing">Peter Norton Computing</a>, a developer of various utilities for DOS.  Turner was appointed as product group manager for the Norton business, and made responsible for the merger, with P&amp;L responsibility.  Ted Schlein was made product group manager for the Q&amp;A business.
</p><p>The Peter Norton group merger logistical effort began immediately while the companies sought approval for the merger, and in August 1990, Symantec concluded the purchase—by this time the combination of the companies was already complete.  Symantec's consumer antivirus and data management utilities are still marketed under the <a href="/wiki/Norton_(Symantec)" class="mw-redirect" title="Norton (Symantec)">Norton</a> name. At the time of the merger, Symantec had built upon its Turner Hall Publishing presence in the utility market, by introducing Symantec Antivirus for the Macintosh (SAM), and Symantec Utilities for the Macintosh (SUM). These two products were already market leaders on the Mac, and this success made the Norton merger more strategic. Symantec had already begun development of a DOS-based antivirus program one year before the merger with Norton. The management team had decided to enter the antivirus market in part because it was felt that the antivirus market entailed a great deal of ongoing work to stay ahead of new viruses. The team felt that Microsoft would be unlikely to find this effort attractive, which would lengthen the viability of the market for Symantec.  Turner decided to use the Norton name for obvious reasons, on what became the <i>Norton Antivirus</i>, which Turner and the Norton team launched in 1991.  At the time of the merger, Norton revenues were approximately 20 to 25% of the combined entity.  By 1993, while being led by Turner, Norton product group revenues had grown to be approximately 82% of Symantec's total.
</p><p>At one time Symantec was also known for its development tools, particularly the THINK <a href="/wiki/Pascal_(programming_language)" title="Pascal (programming language)">Pascal</a>, <a href="/wiki/THINK_C" title="THINK C">THINK C</a>, Symantec <a href="/wiki/C%2B%2B" title="C++">C++</a>, Enterprise Developer and <a href="/wiki/Visual_Cafe" class="mw-redirect" title="Visual Cafe">Visual Cafe</a> packages that were popular on the <a href="/wiki/Apple_Macintosh" class="mw-redirect" title="Apple Macintosh">Macintosh</a> and <a href="/wiki/IBM_PC_compatible" title="IBM PC compatible">IBM PC compatible</a> platforms. These product lines resulted from acquisitions made by the company in the late 1980s and early 1990s.  These businesses and the Living Videotext acquisition were consistently unprofitable for Symantec, and these losses diverted expenditures away from both the Q&amp;A for Windows and the TimeLine for Windows development efforts during the critical period from 1988 through 1992. Symantec exited this business in the late-1990s as competitors such as <a href="/wiki/Metrowerks" title="Metrowerks">Metrowerks</a>, <a href="/wiki/Microsoft" title="Microsoft">Microsoft</a> and <a href="/wiki/Borland" title="Borland">Borland</a> gained significant market share.
</p><p>In 1996, Symantec Corporation was alleged of misleading financial statements in violation of <a href="/wiki/Generally_accepted_accounting_principles" class="mw-redirect" title="Generally accepted accounting principles">GAAP</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="2000_to_present">2000 to present</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:PLM12_24_Competition_Porsche.jpg" class="image"><noscript><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/db/PLM12_24_Competition_Porsche.jpg/220px-PLM12_24_Competition_Porsche.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="1600" data-file-height="1200"></noscript><span class="lazy-image-placeholder" style="width: 220px;height: 165px;" data-src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/db/PLM12_24_Competition_Porsche.jpg/220px-PLM12_24_Competition_Porsche.jpg" data-alt="" data-width="220" data-height="165" data-class="thumbimage"> </span></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:PLM12_24_Competition_Porsche.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Symantec sponsored <a href="/wiki/Porsche_911_GT3" title="Porsche 911 GT3">Porsche 997 GT3 Cup</a> competing at the <a href="/wiki/2012_Petit_Le_Mans" title="2012 Petit Le Mans">2012 Petit Le Mans</a></div></div></div>
<p>From 1999 to April 2009 Symantec was led by CEO <a href="/wiki/John_W._Thompson" title="John W. Thompson">John W. Thompson</a>, a former VP at IBM. At the time, Thompson was the only African-American leading a major US technology company. He was succeeded in April 2009 by the company's long-time Symantec executive <a href="/wiki/Enrique_Salem" title="Enrique Salem">Enrique Salem</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> Under Salem, Symantec completed the acquisition of <a href="/wiki/Verisign" title="Verisign">Verisign</a>'s Certificate Authority business, dramatically increasing their share of that market.
</p><p>Salem was abruptly fired in 2012 for disappointing earnings performance and replaced by Steve Bennett, a former CEO of Intuit and GE executive.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> In January 2013, Bennett announced a major corporate reorganization, with a goal of reducing costs and improving Symantec's product line. He said that sales and marketing "had been high costs but did not provide quality outcomes". He concluded that "Our system is just broken".<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p><p>Robert Enderle of CIO.com reviewed the reorganization and noted that Bennett was following the <a href="/wiki/General_Electric" title="General Electric">General Electric</a> model of being product-focused instead of customer-focused. He concluded "Eliminating middle management removes a large number of highly paid employees. This will tactically improve Symantec's bottom line but reduce skills needed to ensure high-quality products in the long term."<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p><p>In March 2014, Symantec fired Steve Bennett from his CEO position and named <a href="/wiki/Michael_Brown_(corporate_executive)" title="Michael Brown (corporate executive)">Michael Brown</a> as interim president and chief executive. With this acquisition, Thoma Bravo plans to merge the Symantec business unit with its own web certification company, <a href="/wiki/DigiCert" title="DigiCert">DigiCert</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p><p>On January 4, 2018, Symantec and <a href="/wiki/BT_Group" title="BT Group">BT</a> (formerly British Telecom) announced their partnership that provides new endpoint security protection.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p><p>In May 2018, Symantec initiated an internal audit to address <div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/List_of_mergers_and_acquisitions_by_Symantec" title="List of mergers and acquisitions by Symantec">List of mergers and acquisitions by Symantec</a></div>
<h3 class="in-block"><span id="ACT.21"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="ACT!">ACT!</span></h3>
<p>In 1993, Symantec acquired ACT! from Contact Software International. Symantec sold ACT! to SalesLogix in 1999. At the time it was the world's most popular CRM application for Windows and Macintosh.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Veritas">Veritas</span></h3>
<p>On December 16, 2004, <a href="/wiki/Veritas_Software" class="mw-redirect" title="Veritas Software">Veritas Software</a> and Symantec announced their plans for a merger. With Veritas valued at $13.5 billion, it was the largest software industry merger to date.<sup id="cite_ref-Lee_2014_46-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lee_2014-46">[46]</a></sup> Symantec's shareholders voted to approve the merger on June 24, 2005; the deal closed successfully on July 2, 2005.<sup id="cite_ref-Flynn_2005_47-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Flynn_2005-47">[47]</a></sup> July 5, 2005, was the first day of business for the U.S. offices of the new, combined software company. As a result of this merger, Symantec includes storage- and availability-related products in its portfolio, namely <a href="/wiki/Veritas_File_System" title="Veritas File System">Veritas File System</a> (VxFS), <a href="/wiki/Veritas_Volume_Manager" title="Veritas Volume Manager">Veritas Volume Manager</a> (VxVM), <a href="/wiki/Veritas_Volume_Replicator" class="mw-redirect" title="Veritas Volume Replicator">Veritas Volume Replicator</a> (VVR), <a href="/wiki/Veritas_Cluster_Server" title="Veritas Cluster Server">Veritas Cluster Server</a> (VCS), NetBackup (NBU), Backup Exec (BE) and Enterprise Vault (EV).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p><p>On January 29, 2016, Symantec sold <a href="/wiki/Veritas_Technologies" title="Veritas Technologies">Veritas Technologies</a> to <a href="/wiki/The_Carlyle_Group" title="The Carlyle Group">The Carlyle Group</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-p101_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-p101-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Sygate">Sygate</span></h3>
<p>On August 16, 2005, Symantec acquired Sygate,<sup id="cite_ref-Das_2016_48-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Das_2016-48">[48]</a></sup> a security software firm based in <a href="/wiki/Fremont,_California" title="Fremont, California">Fremont</a>, California, with about 200 staff.<sup id="cite_ref-Roberts_2005_49-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Roberts_2005-49">[49]</a></sup> As of November 30, 2005, all Sygate personal firewall products were discontinued.<sup id="cite_ref-Savvides_2005_50-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Savvides_2005-50">[50]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Altiris">Altiris</span></h3>
<p>On January 29, 2007, Symantec announced plans to acquire <a href="/wiki/Altiris" title="Altiris">Altiris</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-daouhdaosjnf_51-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-daouhdaosjnf-51">[51]</a></sup> and on April 6, 2007, the acquisition was completed.<sup id="cite_ref-PC_World_2007_52-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PC_World_2007-52">[52]</a></sup> Altiris specializes in service-oriented management software that allows organizations to manage <a href="/wiki/Information_Technology" class="mw-redirect" title="Information Technology">IT</a> assets.<sup id="cite_ref-daouhdaosjnf_51-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-daouhdaosjnf-51">[51]</a></sup> It also provides software for web services, security and systems management products. Established in 1998, Altiris is headquartered in <a href="/wiki/Lindon,_Utah" title="Lindon, Utah">Lindon, Utah</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Computerworld_p._69_53-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Computerworld_p._69-53">[53]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Vontu">Vontu</span></h3>
<p>On November 5, 2007, Symantec announced its acquisition of Vontu, a Data Loss Prevention (DLP) company, for $350 million.<sup id="cite_ref-Editorial_2007_54-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Editorial_2007-54">[54]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Application_Performance_Management_business">Application Performance Management business</span></h3>
<p>On January 17, 2008, Symantec announced<sup id="cite_ref-Precise_Spinoff_55-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Precise_Spinoff-55">[55]</a></sup> that it was spinning off its Application Performance Management (APM) business and the i3 product line to Vector Capital.<sup id="cite_ref-Dubie_2008_56-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dubie_2008-56">[56]</a></sup> Precise Software Solutions took over development, product management, marketing and sales for the APM business, launching as an independent company on September 17, 2008.<sup id="cite_ref-Precise_launch_57-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Precise_launch-57">[57]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="PC_Tools">PC Tools</span></h3>
<p>On August 18, 2008, Symantec announced the signing of an agreement to acquire <a href="/wiki/PC_Tools_(company)" title="PC Tools (company)">PC Tools</a>. Under the agreement, PC Tools would maintain separate operations. The financial terms of the acquisition were not disclosed. In May 2013, Symantec announced they were discontinuing the PC Tools line of internet security software.<sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup></p><p>In December 2013, Symantec announced they were discontinuing and retiring the entire PC Tools brand and offering a non expiring license to PC Tools Performance Toolkit, PC Tools Registry Mechanic, PC Tools File Recover and PC Tools Privacy Guardian users with an active subscription as of December 4, 2013.<sup id="cite_ref-59" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="AppStream">AppStream</span></h3>
<p>On April 18, 2008, Symantec completed the acquisition of AppStream, Inc. (“AppStream”), a nonpublic Palo Alto, California-based provider of endpoint virtualization software. <h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="MessageLabs">MessageLabs</span></h3>

Symantec purchased the online messaging and Web security provider for about $695 million in cash.<sup id="cite_ref-Stafford_2008_61-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Stafford_2008-61">[61]</a></sup> The acquisition closed on November 17, 2008.<sup id="cite_ref-Enterprise_Management_Associates_(EMA)_2008_62-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Enterprise_Management_Associates_(EMA)_2008-62">[62]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="PGP_and_GuardianEdge">PGP and GuardianEdge</span></h3>

In August 2012, Symantec completed its rebranding of the Verisign SSL Certificate Service by renaming the Verisign Trust Seal the Norton Secured Seal.<sup id="cite_ref-65" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Rulespace">Rulespace</span></h3>

<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Restatement">Restatement</span></h3>
<p>On August 9, 2004, the company announced that it discovered an error in its calculation of deferred revenue, which represented an accumulated adjustment of $20 million.<sup id="cite_ref-The_New_York_Times_2004_75-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-The_New_York_Times_2004-75">[75]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-McMillan_2004_76-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-McMillan_2004-76">[76]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Endpoint_bug">Endpoint bug</span></h3>
<p>The arrival of the year 2010 triggered a bug in Symantec Endpoint. Symantec reported that malware and intrusion protection updates with "a date greater than December 31, 2009 11:59pm [were] considered to be 'out of date.'" The company created and distributed a workaround for the issue.<sup id="cite_ref-Greene_2010_77-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Greene_2010-77">[77]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Scan_evasion_vulnerability">Scan evasion vulnerability</span></h3>
<p>In March 2010, it was reported that Symantec AntiVirus and Symantec Client Security were prone to a vulnerability that might allow an attacker to bypass on-demand virus scanning, and permit malicious files to escape detection.<sup id="cite_ref-78" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-78">[78]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-79" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-79">[79]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Denial-of-service_attack_vulnerabilities">Denial-of-service attack vulnerabilities</span></h3>
<p>In January 2011, multiple vulnerabilities in Symantec products that could be exploited by a <a href="/wiki/Denial-of-service_attack" title="Denial-of-service attack">denial-of-service attack</a>, and thereby compromise a system, were reported. The products involved were Symantec AntiVirus Corporate Edition Server and Symantec System Center.<sup id="cite_ref-80" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-80">[80]</a></sup></p><p>The November 12, 2012 Vulnerability Bulletin of the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Computer_Emergency_Readiness_Team" title="United States Computer Emergency Readiness Team">United States Computer Emergency Readiness Team</a> (US-CERT) reported the following vulnerability for older versions of Symantec's Antivirus system: "The decomposer engine in Symantec <a href="/wiki/Endpoint_Protection" class="mw-redirect" title="Endpoint Protection">Endpoint Protection</a> (SEP) 11.0, Symantec Endpoint Protection Small Business Edition 12.0, Symantec AntiVirus Corporate Edition (SAVCE) 10.x, and Symantec Scan Engine (SSE) before 5.2.8 does not properly perform bounds checks of the contents of CAB archives, which allows remote attackers to cause a denial of service (application crash) or possibly execute arbitrary code via a crafted file."<sup id="cite_ref-81" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-81">[81]</a></sup></p><p>The problem relates to older versions of the systems and a patch is available. US-CERT rated the seriousness of this vulnerability as a 9.7 on a 10-point scale. The "decomposer engine" is a component of the scanning system that opens <a href="/wiki/Digital_container_format" title="Digital container format">containers</a>, such as compressed files, so that the scanner can evaluate the files within.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Scareware_lawsuit">Scareware lawsuit</span></h3>
<p>In January 2012, James Gross filed a lawsuit against Symantec for distributing fake <a href="/wiki/Scareware" title="Scareware">scareware</a> scanners that purportedly alerted users of issues with their computers. Gross claimed that after the scan, only some of the errors and problems were corrected, and he was prompted by the scanner to purchase a Symantec app to remove the rest. Gross claimed that he bought the app, but it did not speed up his computer or remove the detected viruses. He hired a digital forensics expert to back up this claim. Symantec denied the allegations and said that it would contest the case.<sup id="cite_ref-82" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-82">[82]</a></sup> Symantec settled a $11 million fund (up to $9 to more than 1 million eligible customers representing the overpaid amount for the app) and the case was dismissed in court.<sup id="cite_ref-83" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-83">[83]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-84" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-84">[84]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Source_code_theft">Source code theft</span></h3>
<p>On January 17, 2012, Symantec disclosed that its network had been hacked. A hacker known as "Yama Tough" had obtained the <a href="/wiki/Source_code" title="Source code">source code</a> for some Symantec software by hacking an Indian government server.<sup id="cite_ref-daljfha_85-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-daljfha-85">[85]</a></sup> Yama Tough released parts of the code, and threatened to release more. According to Chris Paden, a Symantec spokesman, the source code that was taken was for Enterprise products that were between five and six years old.<sup id="cite_ref-daljfha_85-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-daljfha-85">[85]</a></sup></p><p>On September 25, 2012, an affiliate of the hacker group <a href="/wiki/Anonymous_(group)" title="Anonymous (group)">Anonymous</a> published source code from Norton Utilities.<sup id="cite_ref-Constantin_2012_86-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Constantin_2012-86">[86]</a></sup> Symantec confirmed that it was part of the code that had been stolen earlier, and that the leak included code for 2006 versions of Norton Utilities, pcAnywhere and Norton Antivirus.<sup id="cite_ref-Constantin_2012_86-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Constantin_2012-86">[86]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Verisign_data_breach">Verisign data breach</span></h3>
<p>In February 2012, it was reported that Verisign's network and data had been hacked repeatedly in 2010, but that the breaches had not been disclosed publicly until they were noted in an <a href="/wiki/U.S._Securities_and_Exchange_Commission" title="U.S. Securities and Exchange Commission">SEC</a> filing in October 2011.<sup id="cite_ref-Menn_2012_87-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Menn_2012-87">[87]</a></sup> Verisign did not provide information about whether the breach included its certificate authority business, which was acquired by Symantec in late 2010.<sup id="cite_ref-Menn_2012_87-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Menn_2012-87">[87]</a></sup> Oliver Lavery, director of security and research for nCircle, asked rhetorically, "Can we trust any site using Verisign SSL certificates? Without more clarity, the logical answer is no."<sup id="cite_ref-88" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-88">[88]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-89" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-89">[89]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="pcAnywhere_exploit">pcAnywhere exploit</span></h3>
<p>On February 17, 2012, details of an exploit of pcAnywhere were posted. The exploit would allow attackers to crash pcAnywhere on computers running <a href="/wiki/Microsoft_Windows" title="Microsoft Windows">Windows</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Keizer_2012_90-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Keizer_2012-90">[90]</a></sup> Symantec released a hotfix for the issue twelve days later.<sup id="cite_ref-91" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-91">[91]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Hacking_of_The_New_York_Times_network">Hacking of <i>The New York Times</i> network</span></h3>
<p>According to <a href="/wiki/Mandiant" title="Mandiant">Mandiant</a>, Symantec security products used by <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times" title="The New York Times">The New York Times</a></i> detected only one of 45 pieces of malware that were installed by Chinese hackers on the newspaper's network during a three-month period in late 2012.<sup id="cite_ref-92" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-92">[92]</a></sup> Symantec responded:
</p>
<blockquote><p>"Advanced attacks like the ones the <i>New York Times</i> described in the following article, &lt;<a rel="nofollow" class="external free" href="http://nyti.ms/TZtr5z">http://nyti.ms/TZtr5z</a>&gt;, underscore how important it is for companies, countries and consumers to make sure they are using the full capability of security solutions. The advanced capabilities in our [E]ndpoint offerings, including our unique reputation-based technology and behavior-based blocking, specifically target sophisticated attacks. Turning on only the signature-based anti-virus components of [E]ndpoint solutions alone [is] not enough in a world that is changing daily from attacks and threats. We encourage customers to be very aggressive in deploying solutions that offer a combined approach to security. Anti-virus software alone is not enough".<sup id="cite_ref-93" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-93">[93]</a></sup></p></blockquote>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Intellectual_Ventures_suit">Intellectual Ventures suit</span></h3>
<p>In February 2015, Symantec was found guilty of two counts of patent infringement in a suit by <a href="/wiki/Intellectual_Ventures" title="Intellectual Ventures">Intellectual Ventures</a> Inc and ordered to pay $17 million in compensation and damages,<sup id="cite_ref-94" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-94">[94]</a></sup>  In September 2016, this decision was reversed on appeal by the Federal Circuit.<sup id="cite_ref-95" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-95">[95]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-96" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-96">[96]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Sustaining_digital_certificate_security">Sustaining digital certificate security</span></h3>
<p>On September 18, 2015, Google notified Symantec that the latter issued 23 test certificates for five organizations, including Google and Opera, without the domain owners' knowledge.<sup id="cite_ref-Goodin_2015_97-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Goodin_2015-97">[97]</a></sup> Symantec performed another audit and announced that an additional 164 test certificates were mis-issued for 76 domains and 2,458 test certificates were mis-issued for domains that had never been registered. Google requested that Symantec update the public incident report with proven analysis explaining the details on each of the failures.<sup id="cite_ref-98" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-98">[98]</a></sup></p><p>The company was asked to report all the certificates issued to the Certificate Transparency log henceforth.<sup id="cite_ref-Constantin_2015_99-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Constantin_2015-99">[99]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-SearchSecurity_2017_100-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SearchSecurity_2017-100">[100]</a></sup> Symantec has since reported implementing Certificate Transparency for all its SSL Certificates.  Above all, Google has insisted that Symantec execute a security audit by a third party and to maintain tamper-proof security audit logs.<sup id="cite_ref-Constantin_2015_99-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Constantin_2015-99">[99]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Google_and_Symantec_clash_on_website_security_checks">Google and Symantec clash on website security checks</span></h3>
<p>On March 24, 2017, Google stated that it had lost confidence in Symantec, after the latest incident of improper certificate issuance.<sup id="cite_ref-101" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-101">[101]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-102" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-102">[102]</a></sup> Google says millions of existing Symantec certificates will become untrusted in Google Chrome over the next 12 months. According to Google, Symantec partners issued at least 30,000 certificates of questionable validity over several years, but Symantec disputes that number.<sup id="cite_ref-PC_World_103-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PC_World-103">[103]</a></sup> Google said Symantec failed to comply with industry standards and could not provide audits showing the necessary documentation.<sup id="cite_ref-104" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-104">[104]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-105" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-105">[105]</a></sup></p><p>Google's Ryan Sleevi said that Symantec partnered with other CAs (CrossCert (Korea Electronic Certificate Authority), Certisign Certificatadora Digital, Certsuperior S. de R. L. de C.V., and Certisur S.A.) who did not follow proper verification procedures leading to the misissuance of certificates.<sup id="cite_ref-106" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-106">[106]</a></sup></p><p>Following discussions in which Google had required that Symantec migrate Symantec-branded certificate issuance operations a non-Symantec-operated “Managed Partner Infrastructure”,<sup id="cite_ref-107" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-107">[107]</a></sup> a deal was announced whereby <a href="/wiki/DigiCert" title="DigiCert">DigiCert</a> acquired Symantec's website security business.<sup id="cite_ref-108" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-108">[108]</a></sup> In September 2017, Google announced that starting with Chrome 66, "Chrome will remove trust in Symantec-issued certificates issued prior to June 1, 2016".<sup id="cite_ref-GoogleSecurityBlog_109-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GoogleSecurityBlog-109">[109]</a></sup> Google further stated that "by December 1, 2017, Symantec will transition issuance and operation of publicly-trusted certificates to DigiCert infrastructure, and certificates issued from the old Symantec infrastructure after this date will not be trusted in Chrome."<sup id="cite_ref-GoogleSecurityBlog_109-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GoogleSecurityBlog-109">[109]</a></sup> Google predicted that toward the end of October, 2018, with the release of Chrome 70, the browser would omit all trust in Symantec's old infrastructure and all of the certificates it had issued, affecting most certificates chaining to Symantec roots.<sup id="cite_ref-GoogleSecurityBlog_109-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GoogleSecurityBlog-109">[109]</a></sup> Mozilla Firefox planned to distrust Symantec-issued certificates in Firefox 63 (released on October 23, 2018),<sup id="cite_ref-110" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-110">[110]</a></sup> but delivered the change in Firefox 64 (released on December 11, 2018).<sup id="cite_ref-111" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-111">[111]</a></sup> Apple has also planned to distrust Symantec root certificates.<sup id="cite_ref-112" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-112">[112]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-113" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-113">[113]</a></sup></p>
