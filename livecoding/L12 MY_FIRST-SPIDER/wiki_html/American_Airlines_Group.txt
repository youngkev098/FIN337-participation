<p><b>American Airlines Group Inc.</b> is an American publicly traded <a href="/wiki/Airline" title="Airline">airline</a> <a href="/wiki/Holding_company" title="Holding company">holding company</a> headquartered in <a href="/wiki/Fort_Worth,_Texas" title="Fort Worth, Texas">Fort Worth</a>, <a href="/wiki/Texas" title="Texas">Texas</a>. It was formed on December 9, 2013, in the merger of <a href="/wiki/AMR_Corporation" title="AMR Corporation">AMR Corporation</a>, the parent company of <a href="/wiki/American_Airlines" title="American Airlines">American Airlines</a>, and <a href="/wiki/US_Airways_Group" title="US Airways Group">US Airways Group</a>, the parent company of <a href="/wiki/US_Airways" title="US Airways">US Airways</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> The airline groups together form the <a href="/wiki/World%27s_largest_airlines" class="mw-redirect" title="World's largest airlines">largest airline in the world</a>, with more than 6,700 daily flights to 350 locations in 56 countries worldwide, about $40 billion in operating revenue, over 100,000 employees, and plans to take delivery of 607 new aircraft, including 517 narrowbody aircraft and 90 widebody international aircraft.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-7">[7]</a></sup> The integration of American Airlines and US Airways was completed when the <a href="/wiki/Federal_Aviation_Administration" title="Federal Aviation Administration">Federal Aviation Administration</a> granted a single operating certificate for both carriers on April 8, 2015.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> The company ranked No. 71 in the 2018 <a href="/wiki/Fortune_500" title="Fortune 500">Fortune 500</a> list of the largest United States corporations by total </section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(1)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=American_Airlines_Group&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History" data-section="1" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-1 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-1">
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Merger_between_AMR_Corporation_and_US_Airways_Group">Merger between AMR Corporation and US Airways Group</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=American_Airlines_Group&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Merger between AMR Corporation and US Airways Group" data-section="2" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>In January 2012, <a href="/wiki/US_Airways_Group" title="US Airways Group">US Airways Group</a>, the parent company of <a href="/wiki/US_Airways" title="US Airways">US Airways</a>, expressed interest in taking over <a href="/wiki/AMR_Corporation" title="AMR Corporation">AMR Corporation</a>, the parent company of <a href="/wiki/American_Airlines" title="American Airlines">American Airlines</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-7">[7]</a></sup> In March, AMR's CEO Tom Horton said that the company was open to a merger.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> US Airways (US) told some American Airlines (AA) creditors that merging the two carriers could yield more than $1.5 billion a year in added revenue and cost savings.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> On April 20, American Airlines' three unions said they supported a proposed merger between the two airlines.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> With AMR under <a href="/wiki/Chapter_11,_Title_11,_United_States_Code" title="Chapter 11, Title 11, United States Code">Chapter 11 bankruptcy protection</a>, American Airlines had been looking to merge with another airline. Earlier in July, a bankruptcy court filing stated that US Airways was an American Airlines creditor and "prospective merger partner"; on August 31, US Airways CEO Doug Parker announced that American Airlines and US Airways had signed a nondisclosure agreement, in which they would discuss the possibility of a merger.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p><p>In February 2013, American Airlines and US Airways announced plans to merge, creating the largest airline in the world by some measurements. In the deal, which was expected to close in the third quarter of 2013, stakeholders of AMR would own 72% of the company and US Airways shareholders would own the remaining 28%. <a href="/wiki/Rothschild_%26_Co" title="Rothschild &amp; Co">Rothschild &amp; Co</a> served as the investment bank for the transaction.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> The combined airline carries the American Airlines name and branding. The holding company was renamed American Airlines Group Inc.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> The US Airways' management team, including CEO <a href="/wiki/Doug_Parker" title="Doug Parker">Doug Parker</a>, retained most operational management positions. The combination was considered a "merger of equals" between US Airways and American Airlines, retaining the more well-established "American" name going forward.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> The headquarters for the new airline was also consolidated at American's current headquarters in <a href="/wiki/Fort_Worth,_Texas" title="Fort Worth, Texas">Fort Worth, Texas</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> US Airways exited <a href="/wiki/Star_Alliance" title="Star Alliance">Star Alliance</a> upon completion of the merger, and American retained its <a href="/wiki/Oneworld" title="Oneworld">Oneworld</a> alliance. Judge Sean Lane approved the merger on March 27, 2013, but declined to approve a proposed $20 million <a href="/wiki/Severance_package" title="Severance package">severance package</a> to AA executive <a href="/wiki/Thomas_W._Horton" title="Thomas W. Horton">Thomas W. Horton</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> On July 12, US Airways shareholders approved the proposed merger.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p><p>On August 13, 2013, the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Justice" title="United States Department of Justice">United States Department of Justice</a> along with attorneys general from the District of Columbia, Arizona (headquarters of US Airways), Florida, Pennsylvania, Tennessee, Texas (headquarters of American Airlines),<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> and Virginia filed a lawsuit seeking to block the merger, arguing that it would mean less competition and higher prices. American Airlines and US Airways both said that they would fight against the lawsuit and defend their merger.<sup id="cite_ref-justicedept_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-justicedept-23">[23]</a></sup> In early October 2013, the Attorney General of Texas quit the anti-trust lawsuit.
</p><p>The Department of Justice reached a settlement of its lawsuit on November 12, 2013. The settlement will require the merged airline to give up <a href="/wiki/Landing_slot" title="Landing slot">landing slots</a> or gates in 7 major airports.<sup id="cite_ref-Settlement_confirmation_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Settlement_confirmation-24">[24]</a></sup> Under the deal, the new American is required to sell 104 slots at <a href="/wiki/Ronald_Reagan_Washington_National_Airport" title="Ronald Reagan Washington National Airport">Ronald Reagan Washington National Airport</a> and 34 slots at <a href="/wiki/LaGuardia_Airport" title="LaGuardia Airport">LaGuardia Airport</a>. An additional requirement is that American sell two gates at <a href="/wiki/O%27Hare_International_Airport" title="O'Hare International Airport">O'Hare International Airport</a>, <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles_International_Airport" title="Los Angeles International Airport">Los Angeles International Airport</a>, <a href="/wiki/Logan_International_Airport" title="Logan International Airport">Logan International Airport</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dallas_Love_Field" title="Dallas Love Field">Dallas Love Field</a> and <a href="/wiki/Miami_International_Airport" title="Miami International Airport">Miami International Airport</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-mergerallowed_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mergerallowed-25">[25]</a></sup> Some of the slots will be sold to low-cost carriers such as <a href="/wiki/JetBlue" title="JetBlue">JetBlue</a> and <a href="/wiki/Southwest_Airlines" title="Southwest Airlines">Southwest Airlines</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p><p>A private antitrust suit, filed by a group of 40 passengers and travel agents, also sought to block the merger.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> American's bankruptcy court judge refused to enjoin the two airlines from merging, saying that the group did not demonstrate that the merger would irreparably harm them.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> The plaintiffs' lawyer appealed and was turned down at the U.S. District Court level and was further rebuffed at the Supreme Court after Justice Ruth Bader Ginsburg denied a stay request filed by him.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p><p>Following the Department of Justice approval, the merged company traded on the <a href="/wiki/NASDAQ" class="mw-redirect" title="NASDAQ">NASDAQ</a> stock exchange under the symbol AAL.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p><p>On July 13, 2015, American announced that it planned to discontinue the US Airways brand name on October 17, 2015. On October 16, US Airways flew its final flight, <a href="/wiki/US_Airways_Flight_1939" class="mw-redirect" title="US Airways Flight 1939">US Airways Flight 1939</a>, which was a flight from Philadelphia-Charlotte-Phoenix-San Francisco-Philadelphia.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
