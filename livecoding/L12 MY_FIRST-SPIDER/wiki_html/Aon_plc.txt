</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(1)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aon_(company)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History" data-section="1" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-1 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-1">
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Aon_Center_LA.jpg" class="image"><noscript><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6a/Aon_Center_LA.jpg/170px-Aon_Center_LA.jpg" decoding="async" width="170" height="227" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="2304" data-file-height="3072"></noscript><span class="lazy-image-placeholder" style="width: 170px;height: 227px;" data-src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6a/Aon_Center_LA.jpg/170px-Aon_Center_LA.jpg" data-alt="" data-width="170" data-height="227" data-class="thumbimage"> </span></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Aon_Center_LA.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Aon_Center_(Los_Angeles)" title="Aon Center (Los Angeles)">Aon Center</a>, Los Angeles</div></div></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/W._Clement_Stone" title="W. Clement Stone">W. Clement Stone</a>'s mother bought a small Detroit insurance agency, and in 1918 brought her son into the business. Mr. Stone sold low-cost, low-benefit accident insurance, underwriting and issuing policies on-site. The next year he founded his own agency, the <b>Combined Registry Co.</b>
</p><p>As the <a href="/wiki/Great_Depression" title="Great Depression">Great Depression</a> began, Stone reduced his workforce and improved training. Forced by his son's respiratory illness to winter in the South, Stone moved to Arkansas and Texas. In 1939 he bought American Casualty Insurance Co. of Dallas, Texas. It was consolidated with other purchases as the <a href="/wiki/Combined_Insurance" title="Combined Insurance">Combined Insurance Co. of America</a> in 1947. The company continued through the 1950s and 1960s, continuing to sell health and accident policies. In the 1970s, Combined expanded overseas despite being hit hard by the recession.
</p><p>In 1982, after 10 years of stagnation under Clement Stone Jr., the elder Stone, then 79, resumed control until the completion of a <a href="/wiki/Merger" class="mw-redirect" title="Merger">merger</a> with <b>Ryan Insurance Co.</b> allowed him to transfer control to <a href="/wiki/Pat_Ryan_(executive)" title="Pat Ryan (executive)">Patrick Ryan</a>. Ryan, the son of a Ford dealer in Wisconsin, had started his company as an auto credit insurer in 1964. In 1976, the company bought the insurance brokerage units of the <a href="/wiki/JBS_USA#Esmark" title="JBS USA">Esmark</a> conglomerate. Ryan focused on insurance brokering and added more upscale insurance products. He also trimmed staff and took other cost-cutting measures, and in 1987 he changed Combined's name to <b>Aon</b>. In 1992, he bought Dutch insurance broker <b>Hudig-Langeveldt</b>. In 1995, the company sold its remaining direct life insurance holdings to <a href="/wiki/Genworth_Financial" title="Genworth Financial">General Electric</a> to focus on consulting.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> The following year, it began offering hostile takeover insurance policies to small and mid-sized companies.
</p><p>Aon built a global presence through purchases. In 1997, it bought The Minet Group, as well as insurance brokerage Alexander &amp; Alexander Services, Inc. in a deal that made Aon (temporarily) the largest insurance broker worldwide. The firm made no US buys in 1998, but doubled its employee base with purchases including Spain's largest retail insurance broker, Gil y Carvajal, and the formation of Aon Korea, the first non-Korean firm of its kind<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (June 2010)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> to be licensed there.
</p><p>Responding to industry demands, Aon announced its new fee disclosure policy in 1999, and the company reorganised to focus on buying personal line insurance firms and to integrate its acquisitions. That year it bought Nikols Sedgwick Group, an Italian insurance firm, and formed RiskAttack (with <a href="/wiki/Zurich_Financial_Services" class="mw-redirect" title="Zurich Financial Services">Zurich US</a>), a risk analysis and financial management concern aimed at technology companies. The cost of integrating its numerous purchases, however, hammered profits in 1999.
</p><p>Despite its troubles, in 2000 Aon bought Reliance Group's accident and health insurance business, as well as Actuarial Sciences Associates, a compensation and employee benefits consulting company. Later in that year, however, the company decided to cut 6% of its workforce as part of a restructuring effort. In 2003, the company saw revenues increase primarily because of rate hikes in the insurance industry. Also that year, Endurance Specialty, a Bermuda-based underwriting operation that Aon helped to establish in November 2001 along with other investors, went public. The next year Aon sold most of its holdings in Endurance.
</p><p>In late 2007, Aon announced the divestiture of its underwriting business. With this move, the firm sold off its two major underwriting subsidiaries: Combined Insurance Company of America (acquired by ACE Limited for $2.4 billion) and Sterling Life Insurance Company (purchased by Munich Re Group for $352 million). The low margin and capital-intensive nature of the underwriting industry was the primary reason for the firm's decision to divest.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Upon completion of the move, Aon turned its attention to expanding its broking and consulting capabilities.
</p><p>This growth strategy manifested in November 2008 when Aon announced it had acquired reinsurance intermediary and capital advisor Benfield Group Limited for $1.75 billion. The acquisition amplified the firm's broking capabilities, positioning Aon one of the largest players in the reinsurance brokerage industry.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p><p>In 2010, Aon made its most significant acquisition to date with the purchase of Hewitt Associates for $4.9 billion.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Aside from drastically boosting Aon's human resources consulting capacity and entering the firm into the business process outsourcing industry, the move added 23,000 colleagues and more than $3 billion in revenue.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p><p>In January 2012, Aon announced that its headquarters would be moved from <a href="/wiki/Chicago" title="Chicago">Chicago</a> to London.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p><p>In 10 February 2017, Aon announced that it was selling its employee benefits outsourcing business to private equity firm <a href="/wiki/The_Blackstone_Group" title="The Blackstone Group">The Blackstone Group</a> for US$4.8 billion (£3.8 billion)<sup id="cite_ref-reuters.com_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-reuters.com-10">[10]</a></sup></p><p>In February 2020, Aon named Eric Andersen as president of Aon after co-president Michael O'Connor departed the company to pursue new opportunities. He will be reporting to Greg Case, the firm's CEO.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="11_September_attacks">11 September attacks</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aon_(company)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: 11 September attacks" data-section="2" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>Aon's New York offices were on the 92nd and 98th–105th floors of the <a href="/wiki/Two_World_Trade_Center_tenants" class="mw-redirect" title="Two World Trade Center tenants">South Tower</a> of the <a href="/wiki/World_Trade_Center_(1973-2001)" class="mw-redirect" title="World Trade Center (1973-2001)">World Trade Center</a> at the time of the <a href="/wiki/September_11,_2001_attacks" class="mw-redirect" title="September 11, 2001 attacks">11 September 2001 terrorist attack</a>. When the North Tower was struck at 8:46 a.m., many executives began evacuating their employees from the upper floors of the South Tower. The evacuation of Aon's offices, ordered by Eric Eisenberg, was carried out quickly as 924 of the estimated 1,100 Aon employees present at the time managed to evacuate the building before <a href="/wiki/United_Airlines_Flight_175" title="United Airlines Flight 175">United Airlines Flight 175</a> struck it twenty stories below them at 9:03 a.m.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p><p>However, many were influenced to stay by security guards and security announcements, or did not exit the building in time. As a result, 176 employees of Aon were killed in the attacks, including Eisenberg and <a href="/wiki/Kevin_Cosgrove" title="Kevin Cosgrove">Kevin Cosgrove</a>, a vice-president of the company, who was making a call to 911 when the tower collapsed at 9:59.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Spitzer_investigation">Spitzer investigation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aon_(company)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Spitzer investigation" data-section="3" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>In 2004–2005, Aon, along with other brokers including <a href="/wiki/Marsh_%26_McLennan" class="mw-redirect" title="Marsh &amp; McLennan">Marsh &amp; McLennan</a> and <a href="/wiki/Willis_Group_Holdings" class="mw-redirect" title="Willis Group Holdings">Willis</a>, fell under regulatory investigation under <a href="/wiki/New_York_Attorney_General" class="mw-redirect" title="New York Attorney General">New York Attorney General</a> <a href="/wiki/Eliot_Spitzer" title="Eliot Spitzer">Eliot Spitzer</a> and other <a href="/wiki/State_attorney_general" title="State attorney general">state attorneys general</a>. At issue was the practice of insurance companies' payments to brokers (known as contingent commissions). The payments were thought to bring a conflict of interest, swaying broker decisions on behalf of carriers, rather than customers. In the spring of 2005, without acknowledging any wrongdoing, Aon agreed to a $190 million <a href="/wiki/Settlement_(litigation)" title="Settlement (litigation)">settlement</a>, payable over 30 months.
</p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="UK_regulatory_breach">UK regulatory breach</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aon_(company)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: UK regulatory breach" data-section="4" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>In January 2009, Aon was fined £5.69 million in the UK by the Financial Services Authority, who stated that the fine related to the company's inadequate bribery and corruption controls, claiming that between 14 January 2005 and 30 September 2007 Aon had failed to properly assess the risks involved in its dealings with overseas firms and individuals. The Authority did not find that any money had actually made its way to illegal organisations. Aon qualified for a 30% discount on the fine as a result of its cooperation with the investigation. Aon said its conduct was not deliberate, adding it had since "significantly strengthened and enhanced its controls around the usage of third parties".<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="US_Foreign_Corrupt_Practices_Act_violations">US Foreign Corrupt Practices Act violations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aon_(company)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: US Foreign Corrupt Practices Act violations" data-section="5" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>In December 2011, Aon Corporation paid a $16.26 million penalty to the <a href="/wiki/U.S._Securities_and_Exchange_Commission" title="U.S. Securities and Exchange Commission">U.S. Securities and Exchange Commission</a> (SEC) and the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Justice" title="United States Department of Justice">U.S. Department of Justice</a> (DOJ) for violations of the US <a href="/wiki/Foreign_Corrupt_Practices_Act" title="Foreign Corrupt Practices Act">Foreign Corrupt Practices Act</a> (FCPA).<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>
According to the SEC, Aon's subsidiaries made improper payments of over $3.6 million to government officials and third-party facilitators in <a href="/wiki/Costa_Rica" title="Costa Rica">Costa Rica</a>, <a href="/wiki/Egypt" title="Egypt">Egypt</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vietnam" title="Vietnam">Vietnam</a>, <a href="/wiki/Indonesia" title="Indonesia">Indonesia</a>, the <a href="/wiki/United_Arab_Emirates" title="United Arab Emirates">United Arab Emirates</a>, <a href="/wiki/Myanmar" title="Myanmar">Myanmar</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bangladesh" title="Bangladesh">Bangladesh</a>, between 1983 and 2007, to obtain and retain insurance contracts.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Major_acquisitions">Major acquisitions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aon_(company)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Major acquisitions" data-section="6" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>On 5 January 2007, Aon announced that its Aon Affinity group had acquired the  WedSafe Wedding Insurance program.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p><p>On 22 August 2008, Aon announced that it had acquired London-based <a href="/wiki/Benfield_Group" title="Benfield Group">Benfield Group</a>. The acquiring price was US$1.75 billion or £935 million, with US$170 million of debt.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p><p>On 5 March 2010, <a href="/wiki/Hewitt_Associates" class="mw-redirect" title="Hewitt Associates">Hewitt Associates</a> announced that it acquired Senior Educators Ltd. The acquisition offers companies a new way to address retiree medical insurance commitments.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p><p>On 12 July 2010, Aon announced that it had agreed to buy <a href="/wiki/Lincolnshire,_Illinois" title="Lincolnshire, Illinois">Lincolnshire, Illinois</a>-based <a href="/wiki/Hewitt_Associates" class="mw-redirect" title="Hewitt Associates">Hewitt Associates</a> for $4.9 billion in cash and stock.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p><p>On 7 April 2011, Aon announced that it had acquired Johannesburg, South Africa-based Glenrand MIB. Financial terms were not disclosed.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p><p>On 19 July 2011, Aon announced that it bought Westfield Financial Corp., the owner of insurance-industry consulting firm Ward Financial Group, from Ohio Farmers Insurance Co. Financial terms were not disclosed.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p><p>On 22 October 2012, Aon announced that it agreed to buy OmniPoint, Inc, a <a href="/wiki/Workday,_Inc." title="Workday, Inc.">Workday</a> consulting firm. Financial terms were not disclosed.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p><p>On 16 June 2014, Aon announced that it agreed to buy National Flood Services, Inc., a leading processor of flood insurance, from Stoneriver Group, L.P.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p><p>On 31 October 2016, Aon's Aon Risk Solutions completed acquisition of Stroz Friedberg LLC, a specialised risk management firm focusing on cybersecurity.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p><p>On 14 November 2016, Aon acquired CoCubes an online Indian Assessment firm, facilitating hiring of entry level engineering graduates.
<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p><p>On 10 February 2017, Aon plc agreed to sell its human resources outsourcing platform for US$4.8 billion (£3.8 billion) <sup id="cite_ref-reuters.com_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-reuters.com-10">[10]</a></sup> to <a href="/wiki/Blackstone_Group_L.P." class="mw-redirect" title="Blackstone Group L.P.">Blackstone Group L.P.</a> (BX.N), creating a new company called Alight Solutions.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p><p>In September 2017, Aon announced its intent to purchase real estate investment management firm The Townsend Group from <a href="/wiki/Colony_NorthStar" class="mw-redirect" title="Colony NorthStar">Colony NorthStar</a> for $475 million, expanding Aon's property investment management portfolio.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p><p>On March 9th, 2020 Aon announced its acquisition of <a href="/wiki/Willis_Towers_Watson" title="Willis Towers Watson">Willis Towers Watson PLC</a> <sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> for nearly $30 billion in an all-stock deal that creates the world’s largest insurance broker<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>

<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Manchester_United">Manchester United</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aon_(company)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Manchester United" data-section="8" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>On 3 June 2009, it was reported that Aon had signed a four-year shirt sponsorship deal with English <a href="/wiki/Association_football" title="Association football">football</a> giant <a href="/wiki/Manchester_United_F.C." title="Manchester United F.C.">Manchester United</a>. On 1 June 2010, Aon replaced American insurance company <a href="/wiki/American_International_Group" title="American International Group">AIG</a> as the principal sponsor of the club. The Aon logo was prominently displayed on the front of the club's shirts until the 2014/2015 season when <a href="/wiki/Chevrolet" title="Chevrolet">Chevrolet</a> replaced them.<sup id="cite_ref-ManUtd_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ManUtd-32">[32]</a></sup> The deal was said to be worth £80 million over four years, replacing United's deal with AIG as the most lucrative shirt deal in history at the time.<sup id="cite_ref-ManUtd_32-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ManUtd-32">[32]</a></sup></p><p>In April 2013, Aon signed a new eight-year deal with Manchester United to rename their training ground as the <a href="/wiki/Trafford_Training_Centre" title="Trafford Training Centre">Aon Training Complex</a> and sponsor the club's training kits, reportedly worth £180 million to the club.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
