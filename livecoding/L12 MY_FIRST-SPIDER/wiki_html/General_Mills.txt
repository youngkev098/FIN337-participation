<p><b>General Mills, Inc.</b>, is an American <a href="/wiki/Multinational_corporation" title="Multinational corporation">multinational</a> manufacturer and marketer of branded consumer foods sold through retail stores. It is headquartered in <a href="/wiki/Golden_Valley,_Minnesota" title="Golden Valley, Minnesota">Golden Valley, Minnesota</a>, a suburb of <a href="/wiki/Minneapolis" title="Minneapolis">Minneapolis</a>.  The company markets many well-known North American brands, including Gold Medal flour, <a href="/wiki/Annie%27s_Homegrown" title="Annie's Homegrown">Annie's Homegrown</a>, <a href="/wiki/Betty_Crocker" title="Betty Crocker">Betty Crocker</a>, <a href="/wiki/Yoplait" title="Yoplait">Yoplait</a>, <a href="/wiki/Colombo_Yogurt" title="Colombo Yogurt">Colombo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Totino%27s" title="Totino's">Totino's</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pillsbury_Company" title="Pillsbury Company">Pillsbury</a>, <a href="/wiki/Old_El_Paso" title="Old El Paso">Old El Paso</a>, <a href="/wiki/H%C3%A4agen-Dazs" title="Häagen-Dazs">Häagen-Dazs</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cheerios" title="Cheerios">Cheerios</a>, <a href="/wiki/Trix_(cereal)" title="Trix (cereal)">Trix</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cocoa_Puffs" title="Cocoa Puffs">Cocoa Puffs</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Lucky_Charms" title="Lucky Charms">Lucky Charms</a>. Its brand portfolio includes more than 89 other leading U.S. <a href="/wiki/Brands" class="mw-redirect" title="Brands">brands</a> and numerous category leaders around the world.<sup id="cite_ref-2" </section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(1)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=General_Mills&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History" data-section="1" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-1 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-1">
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:The_Leading_Flour-A0327-01.jpeg" class="image"><noscript><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e6/The_Leading_Flour-A0327-01.jpeg/170px-The_Leading_Flour-A0327-01.jpeg" decoding="async" width="170" height="282" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="519" data-file-height="860"></noscript><span class="lazy-image-placeholder" style="width: 170px;height: 282px;" data-src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e6/The_Leading_Flour-A0327-01.jpeg/170px-The_Leading_Flour-A0327-01.jpeg" data-alt="" data-width="170" data-height="282" data-class="thumbimage"> </span></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:The_Leading_Flour-A0327-01.jpeg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Advertisement, late 1880s</div></div></div>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Washburn-Crosby_Company">Washburn-Crosby Company</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=General_Mills&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Washburn-Crosby Company" data-section="2" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>The company can trace its history to the <b>Minneapolis Milling Company</b>, incorporated in 1856.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>  The company was founded by Illinois Congressman <a href="/wiki/Robert_Smith_(Illinois_politician)" title="Robert Smith (Illinois politician)">Robert Smith</a>, who leased power rights to mills operating along the west side of <a href="/wiki/Saint_Anthony_Falls" title="Saint Anthony Falls">Saint Anthony Falls</a> on the <a href="/wiki/Mississippi_River" title="Mississippi River">Mississippi River</a> in <a href="/wiki/Minneapolis,_Minnesota" class="mw-redirect" title="Minneapolis, Minnesota">Minneapolis, Minnesota</a>.  <a href="/wiki/Cadwallader_C._Washburn" title="Cadwallader C. Washburn">Cadwallader C. Washburn</a> acquired the company shortly after its founding and hired his brother <a href="/wiki/William_D._Washburn" title="William D. Washburn">William D. Washburn</a> to assist in the company's development.  In 1866 the Washburns got into the business themselves, building the Washburn "B" Mill at the falls.  At the time, the building was considered to be so large and output so vast that it could not possibly sustain itself. However, the company succeeded, and in 1874 he built the even bigger <a href="/wiki/Washburn_%22A%22_Mill" class="mw-redirect" title='Washburn "A" Mill'>Washburn "A" Mill</a>.
</p><p>In 1877, the mill entered a partnership with <a href="/wiki/John_Crosby_(General_Mills)" title="John Crosby (General Mills)">John Crosby</a> to form the <b>Washburn-Crosby Company</b>, producing <a href="/wiki/Winter_Wheat" class="mw-redirect" title="Winter Wheat">Winter Wheat</a> Flour.  That same year Washburn sent <a href="/wiki/William_Hood_Dunwoody" title="William Hood Dunwoody">William Hood Dunwoody</a> to England to open the market for spring wheat.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>  Dunwoody was successful and became a silent partner.
</p><p>In 1878, the "A" mill was destroyed in a flour <a href="/wiki/Dust_explosion" title="Dust explosion">dust explosion</a> along with five nearby buildings. The ensuing fire led to the death of 18 workers.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>
Construction of a new mill began immediately.  Not only was the new mill safer but it also was able to produce a higher quality flour after the old grinding stones were replaced with automatic steel rollers, the first  ever used.
</p><p>In 1880, Washburn-Crosby flour brands won gold, silver and bronze medals at the Millers' International Exhibition in <a href="/wiki/Cincinnati" title="Cincinnati">Cincinnati</a>, causing them to launch the Gold Medal flour brand.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p><p>In 1924, the company acquired a failing <a href="/wiki/Minneapolis%E2%80%93Saint_Paul" title="Minneapolis–Saint Paul">Twin Cities</a> radio station, <a href="/wiki/WLAG" title="WLAG">WLAG</a>, renaming it <a href="/wiki/WCCO_(AM)" title="WCCO (AM)">WCCO</a> (from Washburn-Crosby Company).
</p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Founding">Founding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=General_Mills&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Founding" data-section="3" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p><b>General Mills</b> itself was created in June 1928<sup id="cite_ref-historysite_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-historysite-7">[7]</a></sup> when Washburn-Crosby President <a href="/wiki/James_Ford_Bell" title="James Ford Bell">James Ford Bell</a> merged Washburn-Crosby and 28 other mills.
</p><p>In 1928, General Mills acquired the Wichita Mill and Elevator Company of the industrialist <a href="/wiki/Frank_Kell" title="Frank Kell">Frank Kell</a> of <a href="/wiki/Wichita_Falls,_Texas" title="Wichita Falls, Texas">Wichita Falls</a>, <a href="/wiki/Texas" title="Texas">Texas</a>. With the sale, Kell acquired cash plus stock in the corporation.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Washburn_Crosby.jpg" class="image"><noscript><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a8/Washburn_Crosby.jpg/220px-Washburn_Crosby.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="139" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="448" data-file-height="284"></noscript><span class="lazy-image-placeholder" style="width: 220px;height: 139px;" data-src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a8/Washburn_Crosby.jpg/220px-Washburn_Crosby.jpg" data-alt="" data-width="220" data-height="139" data-class="thumbimage"> </span></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Washburn_Crosby.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Postcard image of the Gold Medal Flour factory in <a href="/wiki/Minneapolis,_Minnesota" class="mw-redirect" title="Minneapolis, Minnesota">Minneapolis</a> around 1900</div></div></div>
<p>The newly merged company paid a <a href="/wiki/Dividend" title="Dividend">dividend</a> in 1928, and has continued the dividend uninterrupted since then–one of only a few companies to pay a dividend every year since then.<sup id="cite_ref-historysite_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-historysite-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Merchandising_and_television_sponsorships">Merchandising and television sponsorships</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=General_Mills&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Merchandising and television sponsorships" data-section="4" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>Beginning in 1929, General Mills products contained box top coupons, known as <a href="/wiki/Betty_Crocker" title="Betty Crocker">Betty Crocker</a> coupons, with varying point values, which were redeemable for discounts on a variety of housewares products featured in the widely distributed Betty Crocker catalog. The coupons and the catalog were discontinued by the company in 2006.  A similar program, Box Tops for Education, in which coupon icons clipped off various General Mills products can be redeemed by schools for cash, started in 1996 and is still active.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p><p>General Mills became the sponsor of the popular radio show <i><a href="/wiki/The_Lone_Ranger" class="mw-redirect" title="The Lone Ranger">The Lone Ranger</a></i> in 1941. The show was then brought to television, and, after 20 years, their sponsorship came to an end in 1961.
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Mississippi-Minneapolis-20081005.jpg" class="image"><noscript><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/23/Mississippi-Minneapolis-20081005.jpg/170px-Mississippi-Minneapolis-20081005.jpg" decoding="async" width="170" height="227" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="1200" data-file-height="1600"></noscript><span class="lazy-image-placeholder" style="width: 170px;height: 227px;" data-src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/23/Mississippi-Minneapolis-20081005.jpg/170px-Mississippi-Minneapolis-20081005.jpg" data-alt="" data-width="170" data-height="227" data-class="thumbimage"> </span></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Mississippi-Minneapolis-20081005.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Former site of General Mills today on the <a href="/wiki/Mississippi_River" title="Mississippi River">Mississippi River</a> at Minneapolis</div></div></div>
<p>Beginning in 1959, General Mills sponsored the <i><a href="/wiki/The_Rocky_and_Bullwinkle_Show" class="mw-redirect" title="The Rocky and Bullwinkle Show">Rocky and His Friends</a></i> <a href="/wiki/Television_series" class="mw-redirect" title="Television series">television series</a>, later known as <i><a href="/wiki/The_Rocky_and_Bullwinkle_Show" class="mw-redirect" title="The Rocky and Bullwinkle Show">The Bullwinkle Show</a></i>. Until 1968, Rocky and Bullwinkle were featured in a variety of advertisements for General Mills. General Mills was also a sponsor of the Saturday-morning cartoons from the <a href="/wiki/Total_Television" title="Total Television">Total Television</a> productions studio, including <a href="/wiki/Tennessee_Tuxedo" class="mw-redirect" title="Tennessee Tuxedo">Tennessee Tuxedo</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> The company also was a sponsor of the <a href="/wiki/American_Broadcasting_Company" title="American Broadcasting Company">ABC</a> <a href="/wiki/Western_(genre)" title="Western (genre)">western</a> series <i><a href="/wiki/The_Life_and_Legend_of_Wyatt_Earp" title="The Life and Legend of Wyatt Earp">The Life and Legend of Wyatt Earp</a></i>, starring <a href="/wiki/Hugh_O%27Brian" title="Hugh O'Brian">Hugh O'Brian</a>.
</p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Aeronautical_Research_Division_and_Electronics_Division">Aeronautical Research Division and Electronics Division</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=General_Mills&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Aeronautical Research Division and Electronics Division" data-section="5" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>In 1946, General Mills established their Aeronautical Research Division with chief engineer <a href="/wiki/Otto_C._Winzen" title="Otto C. Winzen">Otto C. Winzen</a>. This division developed high altitude balloons in conjunction with the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Navy" title="United States Navy">United States Navy</a> <a href="/wiki/Office_of_Naval_Research" title="Office of Naval Research">Office of Naval Research</a> (ONR), such as the <a href="/wiki/Skyhook_balloon" title="Skyhook balloon">Skyhook balloon</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p><p>The Aeronautical work of General Mills done around the time of the second World War is continued by the company <a href="/wiki/Raven_Industries" title="Raven Industries">Raven Industries</a> in their <a href="/wiki/Raven_Aerostar" class="mw-redirect" title="Raven Aerostar">Raven Aerostar</a> department.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p><p>The General Mills Electronics division developed the <a href="/wiki/DSV_Alvin" title="DSV Alvin">DSV <i>Alvin</i></a> submersible, which is notable for being used in investigating the wreck of <i>Titanic</i> among other deep-sea exploration missions.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Diversification:_toys_and_restaurants">Diversification: toys and restaurants</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=General_Mills&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Diversification: toys and restaurants" data-section="6" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>The first venture General Mills took into the toy industry was in 1965. The company bought Rainbow Crafts, which was the manufacturer of <a href="/wiki/Play-Doh" title="Play-Doh">Play-Doh</a>. General Mills' purchase of the company was substantial because it brought production costs down and tripled the revenue.
</p><p>General Mills came out with their <a href="/wiki/General_Mills_monster-themed_breakfast_cereals" class="mw-redirect" title="General Mills monster-themed breakfast cereals">"Monster Cereals"</a> in the 1970s. The cereals are now produced and sold seasonally around <a href="/wiki/Halloween" title="Halloween">Halloween</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p><p>In 1970, General Mills acquired a five-unit restaurant company called <a href="/wiki/Red_Lobster_(restaurant)" class="mw-redirect" title="Red Lobster (restaurant)">Red Lobster</a> and expanded it nationwide. Soon, a division of General Mills titled General Mills Restaurants developed to take charge of the Red Lobster chain. In 1980, General Mills acquired the California-based <a href="/wiki/Good_Earth_(restaurant_chain)" title="Good Earth (restaurant chain)">Good Earth</a> health food restaurant chain.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> The company eventually converted the restaurants into other chain restaurants they were operating, such as Red Lobster.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> In 1982, General Mills Restaurants founded a new Italian-themed restaurant chain called <a href="/wiki/Olive_Garden" title="Olive Garden">Olive Garden</a>. Another themed restaurant, <a href="/wiki/China_Coast" title="China Coast">China Coast</a>, was added before the entire group was spun off to General Mills shareholders in 1995 as <a href="/wiki/Darden_Restaurants" title="Darden Restaurants">Darden Restaurants</a>.
</p><p>During the same decade, General Mills ventured further, starting the General Mills Specialty Retail Group.  They acquired two clothing and apparel companies, <a href="/wiki/Talbots" title="Talbots">Talbots</a> and <a href="/wiki/Eddie_Bauer" title="Eddie Bauer">Eddie Bauer</a>. The acquisition was short-lived. Talbots was purchased by a Japanese company, then known as <a href="/wiki/JUSCO" title="JUSCO">JUSCO</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Spiegel_(catalog)" title="Spiegel (catalog)">Spiegel</a> company purchased Bauer.  Spiegel later declared bankruptcy, yet Bauer still remains, albeit in a smaller presence in the United States today.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Gold_medal_flour_factory.jpg" class="image"><noscript><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/13/Gold_medal_flour_factory.jpg/220px-Gold_medal_flour_factory.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="2592" data-file-height="1944"></noscript><span class="lazy-image-placeholder" style="width: 220px;height: 165px;" data-src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/13/Gold_medal_flour_factory.jpg/220px-Gold_medal_flour_factory.jpg" data-alt="" data-width="220" data-height="165" data-class="thumbimage"> </span></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Gold_medal_flour_factory.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Washburn_%22A%22_Mill" class="mw-redirect" title='Washburn "A" Mill'>Washburn "A" Mill</a>, the producer of Gold Medal Flour, now the <a href="/wiki/Minnesota_Historical_Society" title="Minnesota Historical Society">Minnesota Historical Society</a> Mill City Museum</div></div></div>
<p>From 1976 to 1985, General Mills went to court as the parent company of <a href="/wiki/Parker_Brothers" title="Parker Brothers">Parker Brothers</a>, which held the rights on the brand name and gaming idea of the board game <i><a href="/wiki/Monopoly_(game)" title="Monopoly (game)">Monopoly</a></i>, claiming that the so-called <a href="/wiki/Anti-Monopoly" title="Anti-Monopoly">Anti-Monopoly</a> game of an economics professor infringed their trademark. The dispute extended up to the <a href="/wiki/U.S._Supreme_Court" class="mw-redirect" title="U.S. Supreme Court">U.S. Supreme Court</a>, which ruled against them, saying that while they have exclusive rights to the game <i>Monopoly</i>, they can not prevent others from using the word "<a href="/wiki/Monopoly" title="Monopoly">monopoly</a>" in the name of a game.
</p><p>In 1985, General Mills' toy division was separated from its parent as Kenner Parker Toys, Inc. There were many potential acquirers of the business but it was floated on the stock exchange with General Mills' shareholders getting equivalent shares in Kenner Parker. This was more tax efficient for General Mills.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Recent_history">Recent history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=General_Mills&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Recent history" data-section="7" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>In 1990, a joint venture with <a href="/wiki/Nestl%C3%A9" title="Nestlé">Nestlé S.A.</a> called <a href="/wiki/Cereal_Partners" class="mw-redirect" title="Cereal Partners">Cereal Partners</a> was formed<sup id="cite_ref-cerealpartners_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cerealpartners-19">[19]</a></sup> which markets cereals (including many existing General Mills cereal brands) outside the US and Canada under the Nestlé name.
</p><p>In 2001, the company purchased <a href="/wiki/Pillsbury_Company" title="Pillsbury Company">Pillsbury</a> (sans <a href="/wiki/Burger_King" title="Burger King">Burger King</a>) from <a href="/wiki/Diageo" title="Diageo">Diageo</a>, although it was officially described as a "merger".
</p><p>Since 2004, General Mills has been producing more products targeted to the growing ranks of health-conscious consumers. The company has chosen to switch its entire <a href="/wiki/Breakfast_cereal" title="Breakfast cereal">breakfast cereal</a> line to <a href="/wiki/Whole_grain" title="Whole grain">whole grain</a>. The company also started manufacturing their child-targeted cereals with less sugar.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> General Mills has reduced the level of sugar in all cereals advertised to children to 11 grams per serving.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> The company's recent marketing to children included the <a href="/wiki/Advergame" title="Advergame">advergame</a> <i>Millsberry</i>, a virtual city that included games featuring General Mills products.  The site launched in August 2004 and ran through December 2010. The site allowed you to create an avatar, build a house, and play various games to earn money. You could spend this money in the main town, and use it to purchase things like General Mills cereals or furniture for your home.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p><p>In April 2011, General Mills announced that it will switch all 1 million eggs it uses each year to cage-free.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p><p>General Mills was ranked #181 on the 2012 <a href="/wiki/Fortune_500" title="Fortune 500">Fortune 500</a> list of America's largest corporations,<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> 161 in 2015 and was the third-largest food consumer products company in the United States.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> During June 2012, the company's vice-president for diversity stated that General Mills opposes a Minnesota amendment banning gay marriage, stating that the company values "inclusion".<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> The company received positive feedback for its stand which might attract people to its global workforce.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p><p>The company announced in September 2014 that it would acquire organic food producer <a href="/wiki/Annie%27s_Homegrown" title="Annie's Homegrown">Annie's Inc.</a> for a fee of around $820 million, as part of its strategy to expand in the US natural foods market.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> In October 2014, General Mills announced plans to cut 700 to 800 jobs, mostly in U.S., in corporate restructuring planned to be completed by the end of 2015.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p><p>In 2015, citing climate change, General Mills promised to reduce its greenhouse gas emissions by 28 percent over 10 years.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> In December 2016, the company announced it would be restructuring, splitting into four business groups based on global region, and cutting as many as 600 jobs.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p><p>In February 2018, the company entered into the pet products industry, paying $8 billion to buy Blue Buffalo Pet Products, Inc.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> As of 2018, the company ranked 182nd on the <a href="/wiki/Fortune_500" title="Fortune 500">Fortune 500</a> list of the largest United States corporations by revenue.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></p><p>In February 2020, General Mills was recognized by <a href="/wiki/Carbon_Disclosure_Project" title="Carbon Disclosure Project">CDP</a> as a global leader in corporate sustainability and received a place on the CDP "A List" for both climate change and water security.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></p>

<li>1939: General Mills engineer Helmer Anderson creates the Anderson sealer. This new device allowed for bags of flour to be sealed with glue instead of just being tied with a string.</li>

<p>In April 2014, the company announced that it had changed its <a href="/wiki/Terms_of_service" title="Terms of service">legal terms</a> on its website to introduce an <a href="/wiki/Arbitration_clause" title="Arbitration clause">arbitration clause</a> requiring all disputes with General Mills to be resolved in <a href="/wiki/Small_claims_court" title="Small claims court">small claims court</a> or <a href="/wiki/Arbitration" title="Arbitration">arbitration</a> and not as a participant in a <a href="/wiki/Class_action" title="Class action">class action</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-strom_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-strom-37">[37]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-weiss_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-weiss-38">[38]</a></sup>  The change was made shortly after a judge's March 26, 2014, denial of a motion to dismiss a class action regarding the marketing of the company's Nature Valley brand products.<sup id="cite_ref-weiss_38-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-weiss-38">[38]</a></sup>  Users would be deemed to accept the terms by interacting with General Mills on its website in various ways, such as downloading coupons, subscribing to newsletters, or participating in <a href="/wiki/Internet_forum" title="Internet forum">Internet forums</a> hosted on the website.<sup id="cite_ref-sherman_39-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sherman-39">[39]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-strom2_40-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-strom2-40">[40]</a></sup>  <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times" title="The New York Times">The New York Times</a></i> stated that the agreement could be interpreted to additionally construe purchasing General Mills products at a grocery store or liking the company's Facebook page as assent to the terms;<sup id="cite_ref-strom_37-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-strom-37">[37]</a></sup> General Mills disclaimed that interpretation, calling it a "mischaracterization".<sup id="cite_ref-strom2_40-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-strom2-40">[40]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-gray_41-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gray-41">[41]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-foster_42-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-foster-42">[42]</a></sup>  The change in terms resulted in a massive backlash of protests via consumer groups and social media, and General Mills reverted the terms back to the original content after only a few days.<sup id="cite_ref-murphy_43-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-murphy-43">[43]</a></sup></p>

</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(5)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Box_Tops_for_Education"><i>Box Tops for Education</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=General_Mills&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Box Tops for Education" data-section="19" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-5 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-5">
<p><i><b>Box Tops for Education</b></i> is a program that was introduced by General Mills in 1996 to provide money for <a href="/wiki/K-12" class="mw-redirect" title="K-12">K-12</a> education.<sup id="cite_ref-76" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-76">[76]</a></sup> When the program started, the top right corner of the top label of the box contained a small coupon (known as a box top) that was able to be clipped off the box and sent to a child's school. Every box top that was sent would allow the school to pay for supplies, which would in turn help the school's students. In July 2019, <i>Box Tops for Education</i> introduced a mobile app where a store receipt would have to be scanned within two weeks of purchase in order for the money to be collected; the physical box tops themselves are expected to be phased out by December 2019.
</p>
