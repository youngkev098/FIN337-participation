numbers"><span title="The time period mentioned near this tag is ambiguous. (April 2020)">when?</span></a></i>]</sup> has several divisions, with products such as person-to-person money transfer, <a href="/wiki/Money_order" title="Money order">money orders</a>, business payments, and commercial services. The company offered<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Chronological_items" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers"><span title="The time period mentioned near this tag is ambiguous. (April 2020)">when?</span></a></i>]</sup> standard "Cablegrams", as well as Candygrams, Dollygrams, and Melodygrams.
</p><p>Western Union, as an industrialized <a href="/wiki/Monopoly" title="Monopoly">monopoly</a>, dominated the American <a href="/wiki/Telegraph" class="mw-redirect" title="Telegraph">telegraph</a> industry in both the 19th and 20th centuries.
</p>

</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(1)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Western_Union&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History" data-section="1" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-1 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-1">
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="19th_century">19th century</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Western_Union&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: 19th century" data-section="2" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>In 1851, the <b>New York and Mississippi Valley Printing Telegraph Company</b> was organized in <a href="/wiki/Rochester,_New_York" title="Rochester, New York">Rochester, New York</a>, by <a href="/wiki/Samuel_L._Selden" title="Samuel L. Selden">Samuel L. Selden</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hiram_Sibley" title="Hiram Sibley">Hiram Sibley</a>, and others, with the goal of creating one great <a href="/wiki/Telegraph" class="mw-redirect" title="Telegraph">telegraph</a> system with unified and efficient operations. Meanwhile, <a href="/wiki/Ezra_Cornell" title="Ezra Cornell">Ezra Cornell</a> had bought back one of his bankrupt companies and renamed it the New York &amp; Western Union Telegraph Company. Originally fierce competitors, by 1856<sup id="cite_ref-Western_Union_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Western_Union-1">[1]</a></sup> both groups were convinced that consolidation was their only alternative for progress. The merged company was named the Western Union Telegraph Company at Cornell's insistence, and Western Union was born.<sup id="cite_ref-history_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-history-5">[5]</a></sup></p><p>Western Union bought smaller companies rapidly, and by 1860 its lines reached from the <a href="/wiki/East_Coast_of_the_United_States" title="East Coast of the United States">East Coast</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Mississippi_River" title="Mississippi River">Mississippi River</a>, and from the <a href="/wiki/Great_Lakes" title="Great Lakes">Great Lakes</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Ohio_River" title="Ohio River">Ohio River</a>. In 1861 it opened the <a href="/wiki/First_transcontinental_telegraph" title="First transcontinental telegraph">first transcontinental telegraph</a>. In 1865 it formed the <a href="/wiki/Russian%E2%80%93American_Telegraph" title="Russian–American Telegraph">Russian–American Telegraph</a> in an attempt to link America to Europe, <a href="/wiki/Russian%E2%80%93American_Telegraph" title="Russian–American Telegraph">via Alaska</a>, into <a href="/wiki/Siberia" title="Siberia">Siberia</a>, to <a href="/wiki/Moscow" title="Moscow">Moscow</a> (This project was abandoned in 1867.) The company had phenomenal growth during the next few years. Under the leadership of presidents <a href="/wiki/Jeptha_Wade" title="Jeptha Wade">Jeptha Wade</a> and <a href="/wiki/William_Orton_(businessman)" title="William Orton (businessman)">William Orton</a> its capitalization rose from $385,700 in 1858 to $41 million in 1876. It was top-heavy with stock issues, and faced growing competition from several firms, especially the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_and_Pacific_Telegraph_Company" title="Atlantic and Pacific Telegraph Company">Atlantic and Pacific Telegraph Company</a> — itself taken over by financier <a href="/wiki/Jay_Gould" title="Jay Gould">Jay Gould</a> in 1875.<sup id="cite_ref-Klein_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Klein-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>196–201</span></sup> In 1881 Gould took control of Western Union.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-WU2010_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WU2010-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:1911-westernunion-childworker.jpg" class="image"><noscript><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/24/1911-westernunion-childworker.jpg/220px-1911-westernunion-childworker.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="156" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="640" data-file-height="455"></noscript><span class="lazy-image-placeholder" style="width: 220px;height: 156px;" data-src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/24/1911-westernunion-childworker.jpg/220px-1911-westernunion-childworker.jpg" data-alt="" data-width="220" data-height="156" data-class="thumbimage"> </span></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:1911-westernunion-childworker.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Wilbur Bold, a 12-year-old Western Union messenger boy, <a href="/wiki/Tampa,_Florida" title="Tampa, Florida">Tampa, Florida</a>, 1911</div></div></div>
<p>Western Union introduced the first <a href="/wiki/Ticker_tape" title="Ticker tape">stock ticker</a> in 1866, and a standardized time service in 1870. The next year, 1871, the company introduced its <a href="/wiki/Value_transfer_system" title="Value transfer system">money transfer</a> service, based on its extensive telegraph network. In 1879, Western Union left the <a href="/wiki/Telephone" title="Telephone">telephone</a> business, settling a <a href="/wiki/Elisha_Gray_and_Alexander_Bell_telephone_controversy" title="Elisha Gray and Alexander Bell telephone controversy">patent lawsuit with Bell Telephone Company</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> As the telephone replaced the telegraph, money transfer became its primary business.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:93px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Western_union_old_1969.png" class="image"><noscript><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/c/ca/Western_union_old_1969.png" decoding="async" width="91" height="40" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="91" data-file-height="40"></noscript><span class="lazy-image-placeholder" style="width: 91px;height: 40px;" data-src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/c/ca/Western_union_old_1969.png" data-alt="" data-width="91" data-height="40" data-class="thumbimage"> </span></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Western_union_old_1969.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Old logo of Western Union in 1969-1990</div></div></div>
<p>When the <a href="/wiki/Dow_Jones_Transportation_Average" title="Dow Jones Transportation Average">Dow Jones Transportation Average</a> stock market index for the <a href="/wiki/New_York_Stock_Exchange" title="New York Stock Exchange">New York Stock Exchange</a> (NYSE) was created in 1884, Western Union was one of the original eleven all-American companies tracked.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p><p>By 1900, Western Union operated a million miles of telegraph lines and two international <a href="/wiki/Submarine_communications_cable" title="Submarine communications cable">undersea cables</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="20th_century">20th century</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Western_Union&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: 20th century" data-section="3" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>In 1913 <a href="/wiki/AT%26T" title="AT&amp;T">AT&amp;T</a>, under indictment for violating the <a href="/wiki/Sherman_Antitrust_Act_of_1890" title="Sherman Antitrust Act of 1890">Sherman Antitrust Act of 1890</a>, sold its shares in Western Union.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p><p>The company continued to grow, acquiring more than 500 smaller competitors. Its monopoly power was almost complete in 1943! when it bought <a href="/wiki/Postal_Telegraph_Company" title="Postal Telegraph Company">Postal Telegraph, Inc.</a>, its chief rival.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2020)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Western_Union_building,_Manhattan.jpg" class="image"><noscript><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c9/Western_Union_building%2C_Manhattan.jpg/220px-Western_Union_building%2C_Manhattan.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="213" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="2176" data-file-height="2108"></noscript><span class="lazy-image-placeholder" style="width: 220px;height: 213px;" data-src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c9/Western_Union_building%2C_Manhattan.jpg/220px-Western_Union_building%2C_Manhattan.jpg" data-alt="" data-width="220" data-height="213" data-class="thumbimage"> </span></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Western_Union_building,_Manhattan.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Former headquarters of WU, located at 60 Hudson, New York, New York, United States, in the early and middle 20th century</div></div></div><p>In 1914, Western Union offered the first <a href="/wiki/Charge_card" title="Charge card">charge card</a> for consumers. In 1923 it introduced <a href="/wiki/Teletypewriter" class="mw-redirect" title="Teletypewriter">teletypewriters</a> to link its branches. <a href="/wiki/Singing_telegram" title="Singing telegram">Singing telegrams</a> followed in 1933, intercity <a href="/wiki/Fax" title="Fax">fax</a> in 1935, and commercial intercity <a href="/wiki/Microwave" title="Microwave">microwave</a> communications in 1943.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2020)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> In 1958, it began offering <a href="/wiki/Teleprinter#Telex" title="Teleprinter">Telex</a> service to customers in New York City.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> In honor of Valentine's Day 1959, Western Union introduced the Candygram, a box of chocolates accompanying a telegram that was featured in a commercial with the rotund <a href="/wiki/Don_Wilson_(announcer)" title="Don Wilson (announcer)">Don Wilson</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> On the 1970s version of <i><a href="/wiki/Let%27s_Make_a_Deal" title="Let's Make a Deal">Let's Make a Deal</a></i>, hosted by <a href="/wiki/Monty_Hall" title="Monty Hall">Monty Hall</a>, Western Union Candygrams (with a cash message inside) were offered to contestants as a prize during a deal. In 1964, Western Union initiated a transcontinental microwave system to replace land lines.
</p><p>During World War II, families with sons in military service dreaded the Western Union "boy on his bicycle" arriving at their home with a telegram from the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_War" title="United States Department of War">War Department</a> or the <a href="/wiki/Navy_Department" class="mw-redirect" title="Navy Department">Navy Department</a>. The message began: "The <a href="/wiki/Secretary_of_War" class="mw-redirect" title="Secretary of War">Secretary of War</a> [for soldiers and airmen] or <a href="/wiki/Secretary_of_Navy" class="mw-redirect" title="Secretary of Navy">Secretary of Navy</a> [for sailors and marines], regrets to inform you that [name, rank, and serial number of the man in the military service] was killed in action [or is missing in action)]."
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Western_Union_%22TOURATE%22_Telegram_ad_1939.jpg" class="image"><noscript><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b3/Western_Union_%22TOURATE%22_Telegram_ad_1939.jpg/220px-Western_Union_%22TOURATE%22_Telegram_ad_1939.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="330" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="2544" data-file-height="3816"></noscript><span class="lazy-image-placeholder" style="width: 220px;height: 330px;" data-src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b3/Western_Union_%22TOURATE%22_Telegram_ad_1939.jpg/220px-Western_Union_%22TOURATE%22_Telegram_ad_1939.jpg" data-alt="" data-width="220" data-height="330" data-class="thumbimage"> </span></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Western_Union_%22TOURATE%22_Telegram_ad_1939.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Advertisement for Western Union "Tourate" telegram service, 1939</div></div></div><p>Western Union became the first American <a href="/wiki/Telecommunications" class="mw-redirect" title="Telecommunications">telecommunications</a> <a href="/wiki/Corporation" title="Corporation">corporation</a> to maintain its own fleet of <a href="/wiki/Geosynchronous_orbit" title="Geosynchronous orbit">geosynchronous</a> <a href="/wiki/Communications_satellite" title="Communications satellite">communications satellites</a>, starting in 1974. The fleet of satellites, called <a href="/wiki/Westar" title="Westar">Westar</a>, carried communications within the Western Union company for telegram and <a href="/wiki/Mailgram" title="Mailgram">mailgram</a> message data to Western Union bureaus nationwide. It also handled traffic for its <a href="/wiki/Telex" title="Telex">Telex</a> and <a href="/wiki/Telex#Teletypewriter_eXchange" title="Telex">TWX</a> (<a href="/wiki/Telex#Telex_II" title="Telex">Telex II</a>) services. The Westar <a href="/wiki/Satellite" title="Satellite">satellites</a>' <a href="/wiki/Transponder" title="Transponder">transponders</a> were also leased by other companies for relaying <a href="/wiki/Video" title="Video">video</a>, <a href="/wiki/Voice_message" title="Voice message">voice</a>, <a href="/wiki/Data" title="Data">data</a>, and facsimile (fax) transmissions.
</p><p>In 1963, Western Union organized its international cable system properties and its right-of-way for connecting international telegraph lines into a separate company called Western Union International (WUI), which it divested that year to American Securities. In 1967 WUI issued an <a href="/wiki/IPO" class="mw-redirect" title="IPO">IPO</a>. In 1979 it was sold to <a href="/wiki/Xerox" title="Xerox">Xerox</a> for $207 million in stock. Xerox sold WUI in 1981 for $185 million in cash to <a href="/wiki/MCI_Communications" title="MCI Communications">MCI Communicationsm</a> which renamed it MCI International and moved its headquarters from <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a> to <a href="/wiki/Rye_Brook,_New_York" title="Rye Brook, New York">Rye Brook, New York</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2020)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p><p>In the 1970s, WUI installed and leased to the US Department of Defense (DOD) dedicated 50-<a href="/wiki/Kilobit_per_second" class="mw-redirect" title="Kilobit per second">Kbps</a> high-speed telecommunications facilities between the continental US and Hawaii, Germany and the UK to provide a test bed for the DOD's <a href="/wiki/DARPA" title="DARPA">Advanced Research Projects Agency</a> (ARPA). This test bed provided ARPA with a proof of concept for the technology of <a href="/wiki/Packet_switching" title="Packet switching">packet switching</a> which later became the Internet.
</p><p>In 1976, Western Union partnered with <a href="/wiki/E._F._Hutton_%26_Co." class="mw-redirect" title="E. F. Hutton &amp; Co.">E. F. Hutton &amp; Co.</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2020)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p><p>In 1981, Western Union purchased a 50% interest in <a href="/wiki/Airfone" title="Airfone">Airfone</a>. It sold Airfone to <a href="/wiki/GTE" title="GTE">GTE</a> in 1986 for $39 million in cash.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p><p>Because of declining profits and mounting debts, Western Union began to divest itself of telecommunications-based assets in the early 1980s. Because of deregulation at the time, Western Union began sending money outside the country, re-inventing itself as "The fastest way to send money worldwide" and expanding its agent locations internationally.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:1959_Western_Union_Telegram.jpg" class="image"><noscript><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/37/1959_Western_Union_Telegram.jpg/220px-1959_Western_Union_Telegram.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="154" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="4910" data-file-height="3436"></noscript><span class="lazy-image-placeholder" style="width: 220px;height: 154px;" data-src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/37/1959_Western_Union_Telegram.jpg/220px-1959_Western_Union_Telegram.jpg" data-alt="" data-width="220" data-height="154" data-class="thumbimage"> </span></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:1959_Western_Union_Telegram.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Example of a Western Union Telegram, 1959. Note that the message text is a continuous strip of paper which was cut and glued to the telegram form.</div></div></div>
<p>In 1987, Investor <a href="/wiki/Bennett_S._LeBow" title="Bennett S. LeBow">Bennett S. LeBow</a> acquired control of Western Union through an outside of chapter 11 process that was a complex leveraged recapitalization. The transaction was backed by a total of $900 million in <a href="/wiki/Junk_bonds" class="mw-redirect" title="Junk bonds">high-yield bonds and preferred stock</a> underwritten by <a href="/wiki/Michael_Milken" title="Michael Milken">Michael Milken</a>'s group at <a href="/wiki/Drexel_Burnham_Lambert" title="Drexel Burnham Lambert">Drexel Burnham Lambert</a> as part of an exchange offer. LeBow installed Robert J. Amman as President and CEO who led a complete strategic, operational and balance sheet restructuring of the company over the next six years.
</p><p>Amman executed a strategy of redirecting Western Union from being an asset-based provider of communications services, with a money transfer business as a large but less important part of the business, into being a provider of consumer-based <a href="/wiki/Money_transmitter" title="Money transmitter">money transfer financial services</a>. Thus, Amman ran the company as two separate companies. One business consisted of the money transfer business, which was funded and operated to take advantage of the significant growth opportunity. The second unit consisted of all the non-strategic communications assets such as the <a href="/wiki/Long-distance_calling" title="Long-distance calling">long-distance</a> analog voice network, satellite business and undersea cable assets. In the 3-year period through 1990 Amman was supported by <a href="/wiki/Robert_A._Schriesheim" class="mw-redirect" title="Robert A. Schriesheim">Robert A. Schriesheim</a>, also installed by LeBow, as a special advisor who oversaw the divestiture of the four non-strategic telecommunications assets for about $280 million.
</p><p>The official name of the corporation was changed to <a href="/wiki/New_Valley_Corporation" class="mw-redirect" title="New Valley Corporation">New Valley Corporation</a> in 1991, just in time for that entity to seek bankruptcy protection as part of Amman's strategy to eliminate the overleveraged balance sheet while continuing to grow the money transfer business. The name change was used to shield the Western Union name from being dragged through the proceedings (and the resulting bad PR).<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Under the day to day leadership of Amman and the backing of <a href="/wiki/Bennett_S._LeBow" title="Bennett S. LeBow">LeBow</a>, the company's value increased dramatically through its years operating under chapter 11.
</p><p>Following various restructurings that included negotiations with Carl Icahn who became a large bond holder, Amman engineered the sale of New Valley in a bankruptcy auction to First Financial Management Corporation in 1994 for $1.2 billion where he became vice chairman, and a year later merged with <a href="/wiki/First_Data_Corporation" class="mw-redirect" title="First Data Corporation">First Data Corporation</a> in a $6 billion transaction. On January 26, 2006, First Data Corporation announced plans to spin off Western Union as an independent publicly-traded company. Western Union's focus will remain money transfers. The next day Western Union announced that it would cease offering telegram transmission and delivery,<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> the product most associated with the company throughout its history. This was not the original Western Union telegram service, but a new service of First Data under the Western Union banner; the original telegram service was sold off after New Valley Corporation's bankruptcy and now operates as <a href="/wiki/ITelegram" title="ITelegram">iTelegram</a>.
</p><p>The spin off was completed September 29, 2006 and Western Union became an independent publicly traded company again.
</p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Involvement_in_early_computer_networking">Involvement in early computer networking</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Western_Union&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Involvement in early computer networking" data-section="4" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>Western Union telegrams were transmitted through a <a href="/wiki/Store_and_forward" title="Store and forward">store and forward</a> message switching system.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> Early versions were manual telegraph systems. Later systems using <a href="/wiki/Teleprinter" title="Teleprinter">teleprinters</a> were semi-automatic, using punched <a href="/wiki/Paper_tape" class="mw-redirect" title="Paper tape">paper tape</a> to receive, store, and retransmit messages. <a href="/wiki/Plan_55-A" title="Plan 55-A">Plan 55-A</a>, Western Union's last paper tape based switching system (1948–1976), was fully automatic, with automatic routing.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p><p>Western Union was a prime contractor in the <a href="/wiki/Automatic_Digital_Network" title="Automatic Digital Network">Automatic Digital Network</a> (AUTODIN) program. AUTODIN, a military application for communication, was first developed in the 1960s and became the precursor to the modern <a href="/wiki/Internet" title="Internet">Internet</a> in the 1990s. The <a href="/wiki/Defense_Message_System" title="Defense Message System">Defense Message System</a> (DMS) replaced AUTODIN in 2000.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p><p>AUTODIN, originally named "ComLogNet", was a reliable service that operated at 99.99% availability, using mechanical <a href="/wiki/Punched_card" title="Punched card">punched card</a> readers and tab machines to send and receive data over <a href="/wiki/Leased_line" title="Leased line">leased lines</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> During the peak operation of AUTODIN, the US portion of the network handled 20 million messages a month.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2020)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Western Union failed in its attempts to engineer a replacement (AUTODIN II), leading to the development of an acceptable <a href="/wiki/Packet-switched_network" class="mw-redirect" title="Packet-switched network">packet-switched network</a> by <a href="/wiki/BBN_Technologies" title="BBN Technologies">BBN</a> (the developer of the <a href="/wiki/ARPANET" title="ARPANET">ARPANET</a>) which became the foundation of today's Internet. AUTODIN service ceased in 2000, years after it had become obsolete.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p><p>A related innovation that came from AUTODIN was Western Union's computer based EasyLink service.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> This system allowed one of the first marketable <a href="/wiki/Email" title="Email">email</a> systems for non-government users. The system allowed the same message to be sent simultaneously to multiple recipients via email, fax, mailgram, or telex services as well as allowing messages to be sent from the integrated formats. With the service, users could also perform research utilizing its InfoLink application. EasyLink Services International Corporation is now a separate company.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="End_of_telegrams">End of telegrams</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Western_Union&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: End of telegrams" data-section="5" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>As of February 2006, the Western Union website showed this notice:
</p>
<blockquote><p>"Effective 2006-01-27, Western Union will discontinue all Telegram and Commercial Messaging services. We regret any inconvenience this may cause you, and we thank you for your loyal patronage. If you have any questions or concerns, please contact a customer service representative."<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p></blockquote>
<p>This ended the era of telegrams which began in 1851 with the founding of the New York and Mississippi Valley Printing Telegraph Company, and which spanned 155 years of continuous service. Western Union reported that telegrams sent had fallen to a total of 20,000 a year, because of competition from other communication services such as email. Employees were informed of the decision in mid-January.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p><p>Telegram service in the US continues to be available through <a href="/wiki/ITelegram" title="ITelegram">iTelegram</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Acquisitions">Acquisitions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Western_Union&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Acquisitions" data-section="6" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>In May 2009, Western Union announced its plan to acquire Canada-based <a href="/wiki/Custom_House_(global_payments)" title="Custom House (global payments)">Custom House</a> from Peter Gustavson.<sup id="cite_ref-Western-Union-Aug-2009-10-Q_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Western-Union-Aug-2009-10-Q-28">[28]</a></sup> The deal closed in September 2009 for a purchase price of <a href="/wiki/United_States_dollar" title="United States dollar">US$</a>371 million.<sup id="cite_ref-Western-Union-Oct-2009-10-Q_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Western-Union-Oct-2009-10-Q-29">[29]</a></sup> This acquisition led the company to be re-branded as Western Union Business Solutions.<sup id="cite_ref-Western-Union-Oct-2010-8-K_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Western-Union-Oct-2010-8-K-30">[30]</a></sup> Custom House and its subsidiary <a href="/wiki/XE.com" title="XE.com">XE.com</a> were sold to <a href="/wiki/Euronet_Worldwide" title="Euronet Worldwide">Euronet Worldwide</a> in 2015 and operate under its <a href="/wiki/HiFX" title="HiFX">HiFX</a> brand.
</p><p>In January 2011, Western Union acquired Angelo Costa, a group active in money transfer and services to immigrants. Angelo Costa has a network of 7,500 points of sale throughout Europe. The agreement was signed for US$200 million.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2020)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p><p>In July 2011, Western Union acquired <a href="/wiki/Travelex" title="Travelex">Travelex</a>'s Global Business Payments division for <a href="/wiki/Pound_sign" title="Pound sign">£</a>606 million.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p><p>In October 2011, Western Union completed the acquisition of Finint S.r.l., one of Western Union's leading money transfer network agents in Europe, with more than 10,000 subagent locations across Italy, Spain, and the United Kingdom.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p><p>In May 2015, rumors emerged over a proposed merger of Western Union and competitor <a href="/wiki/MoneyGram" title="MoneyGram">MoneyGram</a>, at a time when both companies’ revenue were declining.<sup id="cite_ref-:2_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:2-33">[33]</a></sup> Western Union denied this was the case.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> In January 2017, MoneyGram was instead acquired unsuccessfully by <a href="/wiki/Ant_Financial_Services_Group" class="mw-redirect" title="Ant Financial Services Group">Ant Financial</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alibaba_Group" title="Alibaba Group">Alibaba</a>’s financial technology firm, for $880 million.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></p>

<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Online">Online</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Western_Union&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Online" data-section="8" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>Western Union's website, <i>westernunion.com</i>, allows users to send and receive funds to others, pay bills, locate Western Union locations, track transfers, and to purchase Western Union <i>NetSpend</i> gift cards. The website also attempts to spread fraud awareness using videos, articles, and quizzes to do so.
</p><p>As of September 2017, the domain <i>westernunion.com</i> ranked globally as the 4,319th most visited website, according to <a href="/wiki/Alexa_Internet" title="Alexa Internet">Alexa Internet</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="BidPay">BidPay</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Western_Union&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: BidPay" data-section="9" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>As the Internet became an arena for commerce at the turn of the millennium, Western Union started its online services. BidPay was renamed "Western Union Auction Payments" in 2004 before being renamed back to BidPay. BidPay ceased operations on December 31, 2005, and was purchased for US$1.8 million in March 2006 by CyberSource Corp. who announced their intention to re-launch BidPay. BidPay was later discontinued by CyberSource effective December 31, 2007.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Western_Union_Mobile">Western Union Mobile</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Western_Union&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Western Union Mobile" data-section="10" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>In October 2007, Western Union announced plans to introduce a mobile money-transfer service with the <a href="/wiki/GSM_Association" class="mw-redirect" title="GSM Association">GSM Association</a>, a global trade association representing more than 700 mobile operators in 218 countries and covering 2.5 billion mobile subscribers.
</p><p>The proliferation of mobile phones in developed and developing economies provides a widely accessible consumer device capable of delivering mobile financial services ranging from text notifications associated with Western Union cash delivery services to phone-based remittance options. Western Union's mobile money transfer service offering will connect its core money-transfer platform to m-bank or m-wallet platforms provided by mobile operators and/or locally regulated financial institutions.
</p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Western_Union_Connect">Western Union Connect</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Western_Union&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Western Union Connect" data-section="11" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>The company launched the Western Union Connect service in October 2015, following partnership agreements with major instant messaging apps <a href="/wiki/WeChat" title="WeChat">WeChat</a> and <a href="/wiki/Viber" title="Viber">Viber</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-38">[38]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:1_39-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-39">[39]</a></sup> The partnership allows users of WeChat to send up to $100 to <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>, the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">US</a> and 200 other countries,<sup id="cite_ref-:1_39-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-39">[39]</a></sup> while Viber users can send up to $100 for $3.99 plus <a href="/wiki/Exchange_rate" title="Exchange rate">exchange rate fees</a>, with that fixed fee increasing the more money is sent up a limit of $499.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_38-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Sending_and_receiving_funds">Sending and receiving funds</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Western_Union&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Sending and receiving funds" data-section="12" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>Money can be sent online or in person at Western Union agent locations. Cash can be collected in person at any other Western Union agent location worldwide by providing the 10-digit MTCN (money Transfer Control Number) and identification. In some cases, a secret question and answer can be used instead of identification.
</p>

</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(4)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Scam_industry_and_money_laundering">Scam industry and money laundering</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Western_Union&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Scam industry and money laundering" data-section="15" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-4 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-4">
<p>Western Union advises its customers not to send money to someone that they have never met in person. Despite its efforts in increasing customers' awareness of the issue,<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> Western Union is used for <a href="/wiki/Internet_fraud" title="Internet fraud">internet fraud</a> by scammers.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup></p><p>Western Union has been required to maintain records of payout locations of the individuals who may be <a href="/wiki/Money_laundering" title="Money laundering">laundering</a> the money, though this information may be obtained only through the use of a <a href="/wiki/Subpoena" title="Subpoena">subpoena</a>. Hence <a href="/wiki/Advance-fee_fraud" class="mw-redirect" title="Advance-fee fraud">advance-fee fraud</a> and <a href="/wiki/Romance_scam" title="Romance scam">romance scammers</a> continue to receive funds via Western Union confident in the knowledge that money lost to overseas scammers is almost always unrecoverable.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> For this reason, it is banned as a medium of payment through <a href="/wiki/EBay" title="EBay">eBay</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> and discouraged on other online auction websites.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup></p><p>Western Union admitted to allowing wire fraud in January 2017 and agreed to pay $586m, for turning a blind eye as criminals used its service for advance fee fraud. Scammers engaging in various 419 advance fee scams including offering fake job offers and lottery prizes, were able to process transactions using Western Union money transfer, mainly by giving the agents a share of the earnings from their scams. Western Union failed to investigate hundreds of thousands of complaints that were filed by victims.<sup id="cite_ref-reuters.com_47-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-reuters.com-47">[47]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Money_laundering_and_terrorist_financing">Money laundering and terrorist financing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Western_Union&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Money laundering and terrorist financing" data-section="16" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Central_Bank_of_Ireland" title="Central Bank of Ireland">Central Bank of Ireland</a> reprimanded and fined Western Union €1.75m in May 2015 because of failures in anti money laundering practices which could have left the firm's payment services open to being used for money laundering and/or terrorist financing saying that they were concerned that Western Union "failed to have in place sufficiently robust systems and procedures to train agents, to monitor and identify suspicious activity in respect of smaller transactions, and to maintain appropriate records" and "the splitting of payments into many separate smaller payments is a common method used to launder money. Similarly, terrorist financing is often carried out by small payment transfers."<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup></p><p>Western Union agents also allowed Chinese immigrants to the US to use the service to send hundreds of millions of dollars, by sending the amounts in smaller increments to avoid transfer reporting requirements, to pay human smugglers.<sup id="cite_ref-reuters.com_47-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-reuters.com-47">[47]</a></sup></p>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(5)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Connection_to_military_intelligence">Connection to military intelligence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Western_Union&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Connection to military intelligence" data-section="17" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-5 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-5">
<p>The book "The One Percent Doctrine: Deep Inside America's Pursuit of Its Enemies Since 9/11" alleged that Western Union provided <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> <a href="/wiki/Military_intelligence" title="Military intelligence">military intelligence</a> with personal information.<sup id="cite_ref-Suskind2007_50-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Suskind2007-50">[50]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Blocked_transactions">Blocked transactions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Western_Union&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Blocked transactions" data-section="18" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>Western Union has begun blocking transactions based on suspicion of <a href="/wiki/Terrorism" title="Terrorism">terrorist</a> connections, as a part of the company's involvement with the <a href="/wiki/War_on_Terror" class="mw-redirect" title="War on Terror">War on Terror</a>.
</p>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(6)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Anti-competitive_behavior">Anti-competitive behavior </span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Western_Union&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Anti-competitive behavior " data-section="19" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-6 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-6">
<p>Western Union has been accused of engaging in anti-competitive behavior in Africa as a result of a lack of competition on the continent.<sup id="cite_ref-:2_33-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:2-33">[33]</a></sup> According to a report by the <a href="/wiki/Overseas_Development_Institute" title="Overseas Development Institute">Overseas Development Institute</a>, this allows Western Union to artificially inflate its fees for money transfers, charging what has been called an “Africa charge” of 8% consistently “applied across countries regardless of the size of the market, regulatory costs or market risk.”<sup id="cite_ref-:3_51-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:3-51">[51]</a></sup> However, the fees can be as high as 10% or more, depending on the region.<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup> Africa's remittance market remains the most expensive in the world,<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup> and the region is estimated to incur excess costs of $1.4 billion to $2.3 billion per year as a result of these high remittance fees.<sup id="cite_ref-:3_51-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:3-51">[51]</a></sup></p><p>The company has also been criticized for its use of exclusivity agreements with banks in countries that receive remittances, restricting competition and harming the consumer,<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup> by requiring the consumer to conduct transactions via nominated banks.<sup id="cite_ref-:3_51-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:3-51">[51]</a></sup> This also allows for the imposition of above-average transaction fees.<sup id="cite_ref-:3_51-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:3-51">[51]</a></sup></p><p>In February 2016, Western Union was questioned by the <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">EU</a> antitrust regulator over its network of agents to establish whether the company colluded with banks to push out smaller rivals from the money transfer market.<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (July 2017)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
