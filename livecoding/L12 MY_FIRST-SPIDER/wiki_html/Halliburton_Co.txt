Halliburton announced on April 5, 2007, that it had sold the division and severed its corporate relationship with <a href="/wiki/KBR_(company)" title="KBR (company)">KBR</a>, which had been its contracting, engineering and construction unit as a part of the company.<sup id="cite_ref-separation_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-separation-11">[11]</a></sup></p><p>The company has been involved in numerous controversies, including its involvement with <a href="/wiki/Dick_Cheney" title="Dick Cheney">Dick Cheney</a> – as <a href="/wiki/U.S._Secretary_of_Defense" class="mw-redirect" title="U.S. Secretary of Defense">U.S. Secretary of Defense</a>, then CEO of the company, then <a href="/wiki/Vice_President_of_the_United_States" title="Vice President of the United States">Vice President of the United States</a> – and the <a href="/wiki/Iraq_War" title="Iraq War">Iraq War</a>, and the <i><a href="/wiki/Deepwater_Horizon" title="Deepwater Horizon">Deepwater Horizon</a></i>, for which it agreed to settle outstanding legal claims against it by paying litigants $1.1 billion.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/KBR_(company)" title="KBR (company)">KBR</a>, one of Halliburton's subsidiaries at the time, paid bribes to high-ranking Nigerian officials between 1994 and 2004. Under a deal reached with the U.S. Justice Department, Halliburton has agreed to pay $382 million to settle the bribery case.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p><p><a href="/wiki/Jeff_Miller_(American_businessman)" title="Jeff Miller (American businessman)">Jeff Miller</a> was promoted to President of Halliburton on August 1, 2014, and CEO on June 1, 2017, replacing <a href="/wiki/David_J._Lesar" title="David J. Lesar">Dave Lesar</a>.
</p>

<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Divisions">Divisions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Halliburton&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Divisions" data-section="3" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>Energy services (the company's historical cornerstone), <a href="/wiki/Formation_evaluation" title="Formation evaluation">formation evaluation</a>, digital and consulting services, production volume optimization, and fluid systems are the major business segments. These businesses continue to be profitable, and the company is one of the world's largest players in these service industries; it is second after <a href="/wiki/Schlumberger_Limited" class="mw-redirect" title="Schlumberger Limited">Schlumberger</a>, and is followed by <a href="/wiki/Saipem" title="Saipem">Saipem</a>, <a href="/wiki/Weatherford_International" title="Weatherford International">Weatherford International</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Baker_Hughes" title="Baker Hughes">Baker Hughes</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p><p>With the acquisition of <a href="/wiki/Dresser_Industries" title="Dresser Industries">Dresser Industries</a> in 1998, the <a href="/wiki/KBR_(company)" title="KBR (company)">Kellogg-Brown &amp; Root</a> division (in 2002 renamed to KBR) was formed by <a href="/wiki/Merger" class="mw-redirect" title="Merger">merging</a> Halliburton's Brown &amp; Root (acquired 1962) subsidiary and the M.W. Kellogg division of Dresser (which Dresser had merged with in 1988). KBR is a major international construction company that works in an industry that tends to have an element of volatility and is subject to significant fluctuations in revenue and <a href="/wiki/Profit_(accounting)" title="Profit (accounting)">profit</a>. <a href="/wiki/Asbestos" title="Asbestos">Asbestos</a>-related litigation from Kellogg acquisition caused the company to book more than <a href="/wiki/United_States_dollar" title="United States dollar">US$</a>4.0 billion in losses from 2002 through 2004.
</p><p>As a result of the asbestos-related costs and staggering losses on the Barracuda Caratinga FPSO construction project based in Rio de Janeiro, Brazil, Halliburton lost approximately $900 million <a href="/wiki/U.S._dollar" class="mw-redirect" title="U.S. dollar">U.S.</a> a year from 2002 through 2004. A final non-appealable settlement in the asbestos case was reached in January 2005 which allowed Halliburton subsidiary KBR to exit Chapter 11 bankruptcy and returned the company to quarterly profitability. While Halliburton's revenues have increased because of its contracts in the Middle East, the overall impact on its bottom line has been mixed.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p><p>At a meeting for investors and analysts in August 2004, a plan was outlined to divest the KBR division through a possible <a href="/wiki/Sales" title="Sales">sale</a>, <a href="/wiki/Corporate_spin-off" title="Corporate spin-off">spin-off</a> or <a href="/wiki/Initial_public_offering" title="Initial public offering">initial public offering</a>. Analysts at <a href="/wiki/Deutsche_Bank" title="Deutsche Bank">Deutsche Bank</a> valued KBR at up to $2.15 billion, while others believed it could be worth closer to $3 billion by 2005. KBR became a separately listed company on April 5, 2007.<sup id="cite_ref-separation_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-separation-11">[11]</a></sup></p>

<h3 class="in-block"><span id="Early_history_.28as_HOWCO.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_history_(as_HOWCO)">Early history (as HOWCO)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Halliburton&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Early history (as HOWCO)" data-section="5" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>The company was started in 1919<sup id="cite_ref-Yahoo_Hal_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Yahoo_Hal-16">[16]</a></sup> by <a href="/wiki/Erle_P._Halliburton" title="Erle P. Halliburton">Erle P. Halliburton</a> as the New Method Oil Well Cementing Company.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2020)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:HAL_Duncan.jpg" class="image"><noscript><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/4/42/HAL_Duncan.jpg/220px-HAL_Duncan.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="140" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="450" data-file-height="286"></noscript><span class="lazy-image-placeholder" style="width: 220px;height: 140px;" data-src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/4/42/HAL_Duncan.jpg/220px-HAL_Duncan.jpg" data-alt="" data-width="220" data-height="140" data-class="thumbimage"> </span></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:HAL_Duncan.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Halliburton Research Center in <a href="/wiki/Duncan,_Oklahoma" title="Duncan, Oklahoma">Duncan, Oklahoma</a>, the city which was the original headquarters of Halliburton Company</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:HalliburtonBellaireBlvdOfficesHouston.JPG" class="image"><noscript><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/76/HalliburtonBellaireBlvdOfficesHouston.JPG/220px-HalliburtonBellaireBlvdOfficesHouston.JPG" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="2272" data-file-height="1704"></noscript><span class="lazy-image-placeholder" style="width: 220px;height: 165px;" data-src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/76/HalliburtonBellaireBlvdOfficesHouston.JPG/220px-HalliburtonBellaireBlvdOfficesHouston.JPG" data-alt="" data-width="220" data-height="165" data-class="thumbimage"> </span></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:HalliburtonBellaireBlvdOfficesHouston.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Halliburton (Oak Park) offices in <a href="/wiki/Westchase,_Houston" title="Westchase, Houston">Westchase</a> and in <a href="/wiki/Chinatown,_Houston" title="Chinatown, Houston">Chinatown</a> in <a href="/wiki/Houston" title="Houston">Houston</a></div></div></div>
<p>In 1920, he brought a wild gas well under control, using cement, for W.G. Skelly, near <a href="/wiki/Wilson,_Oklahoma" title="Wilson, Oklahoma">Wilson, Oklahoma</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-EPMag_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EPMag-17">[17]</a></sup> On March 1, 1921, the Halliburton "method and means of excluding water from oil wells" was assigned a patent from the <a href="/wiki/U.S._Patent_Office" class="mw-redirect" title="U.S. Patent Office">U.S. Patent Office</a>. Halliburton invented the revolutionary cement jet mixer, to eliminate hand-mixing of cement, and the measuring line, a tool used to guarantee cementing accuracy.<sup id="cite_ref-EPMag_17-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EPMag-17">[17]</a></sup> By 1922, the Halliburton Oil Well Cementing Company (HOWCO) was prospering from the <a href="/wiki/Mexia,_Texas" title="Mexia, Texas">Mexia, Texas</a> oil boom, having cemented its 500th well in late summer.<sup id="cite_ref-Hal_Legend_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hal_Legend-18">[18]</a></sup></p><p>In 1924, the company was incorporated in <a href="/wiki/Delaware" title="Delaware">Delaware</a>, with 56 people on its payroll. The stock of the corporation was owned by Erle and <a href="/w/index.php?title=Vida_Halliburton&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Vida Halliburton (page does not exist)">Vida Halliburton</a> and by seven major oil companies: Magnolia, Texas, Gulf, Humble, Sun, Pure and Atlantic.<sup id="cite_ref-TSHA_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TSHA-19">[19]</a></sup></p><p>In 1926, its first foreign venture began with sale of equipment to <a href="/wiki/Burma" class="mw-redirect" title="Burma">Burma</a> and India.<sup id="cite_ref-Egypt_Oil_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Egypt_Oil-20">[20]</a></sup></p><p>Throughout the 1930s and 1940s, Halliburton continued cementing across America.<sup id="cite_ref-Hal_Legend_18-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hal_Legend-18">[18]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-AAPG_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AAPG-21">[21]</a></sup> In 1938, Halliburton cemented its first offshore well using a truck on a barge off the Louisiana coast.<sup id="cite_ref-Egypt_Oil_20-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Egypt_Oil-20">[20]</a></sup> In 1940, Halliburton opened offices in Venezuela and introduced bulk handling of cementing to the industry.<sup id="cite_ref-TSHA_19-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TSHA-19">[19]</a></sup> In 1947, the Halliburton first marine cementing vessel went into service.<sup id="cite_ref-EPMag_17-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EPMag-17">[17]</a></sup></p><p>In 1951, Halliburton first appeared in Europe as Halliburton Italiana SpA, a wholly owned subsidiary in Italy. Over the next seven years, Halliburton launched Halliburton Company Germany GmbH, set up operations in Argentina and established a subsidiary in England. By 1951, HOWCO had service centers operating in Canada, Venezuela, Peru, Colombia, Saudi Arabia and Indonesia.<sup id="cite_ref-Hal_Legend_18-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hal_Legend-18">[18]</a></sup> Halliburton revenues topped $100 million for the first time in 1952.<sup id="cite_ref-Hal_Legend_18-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hal_Legend-18">[18]</a></sup></p><p>Erle P. Halliburton died in <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles" title="Los Angeles">Los Angeles</a> in 1957. HOWCO is at this time worth $190 million with camps all over the world. The same year, HOWCO purchased Welex, which pioneered jet perforation.<sup id="cite_ref-Hal_Legend_18-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hal_Legend-18">[18]</a></sup> Otis Engineering, an oil field service and equipment company specializing in manufacturing pressure control equipment for oil and gas producing wells, was acquired in 1959.<sup id="cite_ref-Hal_Legend_18-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hal_Legend-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="As_Halliburton">As Halliburton</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Halliburton&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: As Halliburton" data-section="6" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>On July 5, 1961, the company changed its name to the Halliburton Company. In 1963, Halliburton was the first company in Oklahoma to receive the Presidential "E" for Export flag in recognition of notable contributions to foreign trade.<sup id="cite_ref-Hal_Legend_18-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hal_Legend-18">[18]</a></sup></p><p>Halliburton opened a 500,000 sq ft (46,000 m<sup>2</sup>) manufacturing center in Duncan, Oklahoma, in 1964.<sup id="cite_ref-Hal_Legend_18-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hal_Legend-18">[18]</a></sup> The company began to experiment with new technologies to help their services – for example, beginning in 1965 a pilot operation of a computer network system – the first such installation in the oilfield services industry.<sup id="cite_ref-Hal_Legend_18-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hal_Legend-18">[18]</a></sup> In 1966, workers broke ground for a new wing at the Research Center in Duncan that tripled the available space for the Chemical Research and Design Department.<sup id="cite_ref-Hal_Legend_18-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hal_Legend-18">[18]</a></sup></p><p>In 1968, an automated mixing system for drilling mud was developed by Halliburton, primarily for use offshore.<sup id="cite_ref-Hal_Legend_18-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hal_Legend-18">[18]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Gearhart_Industries" title="Gearhart Industries">Gearhart Industries</a> (acquired by Halliburton Energy Services in 1989) introduced the first digital computer logging system in 1974.<sup id="cite_ref-Hal_Legend_18-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hal_Legend-18">[18]</a></sup></p><p>In 1969, Halliburton began construction of a base camp at <a href="/wiki/Prudhoe_Bay" class="mw-redirect" title="Prudhoe Bay">Prudhoe Bay</a> on <a href="/wiki/Alaska" title="Alaska">Alaska</a>'s North Slope.<sup id="cite_ref-Hal_Legend_18-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hal_Legend-18">[18]</a></sup></p><p>In 1975, it responded to environmental concerns by working with the nonprofit Clean Gulf Associates to contain and clean up oil spills.<sup id="cite_ref-Hal_Legend_18-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hal_Legend-18">[18]</a></sup> In 1976, Halliburton established the Halliburton Energy Institute in Duncan, Oklahoma, to provide an industry forum for disseminating technical information.<sup id="cite_ref-Hal_Legend_18-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hal_Legend-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Lincoln_Plaza.jpg" class="image"><noscript><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fb/Lincoln_Plaza.jpg/170px-Lincoln_Plaza.jpg" decoding="async" width="170" height="286" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="873" data-file-height="1470"></noscript><span class="lazy-image-placeholder" style="width: 170px;height: 286px;" data-src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fb/Lincoln_Plaza.jpg/170px-Lincoln_Plaza.jpg" data-alt="" data-width="170" data-height="286" data-class="thumbimage"> </span></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Lincoln_Plaza.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Lincoln_Plaza_(Dallas,_Texas)" class="mw-redirect" title="Lincoln Plaza (Dallas, Texas)">Lincoln Plaza</a> in <a href="/wiki/Downtown_Dallas" title="Downtown Dallas">Downtown Dallas</a>, which at one time housed the Halliburton headquarters</div></div></div>
<p>In 1980, Halliburton Research Center opened in Duncan, Oklahoma.<sup id="cite_ref-Hal_Legend_18-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hal_Legend-18">[18]</a></sup> The company's billionth sack of cement for customers was pumped in 1983.<sup id="cite_ref-Hal_Legend_18-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hal_Legend-18">[18]</a></sup> In 1989, Halliburton acquired logging and perforating specialist company <a href="/wiki/Gearhart_Industries" title="Gearhart Industries">Gearhart Industries</a> and combined it with its subsidiary Welex to form Halliburton Logging Services.
</p><p>Throughout the 1980s, Halliburton's subsidiaries continued their projects around the world (under management of former CEO Brian Darcy) even in countries once considered enemies. Equipment was provided for the first multiwell platform offshore <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>, and an Otis Engineering team controlled a gigantic Tengiz field blowout in the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Hal_Legend_18-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hal_Legend-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="1990s">1990s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Halliburton&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: 1990s" data-section="7" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>Following the end of <a href="/wiki/Operation_Desert_Storm" class="mw-redirect" title="Operation Desert Storm">Operation Desert Storm</a> in February 1991, <a href="/wiki/The_Pentagon" title="The Pentagon">the Pentagon</a>, led by then defense secretary <a href="/wiki/Richard_Cheney" class="mw-redirect" title="Richard Cheney">Dick Cheney</a>, paid Halliburton subsidiary Brown &amp; Root Services over $8.5 million to study the use of private military forces with American soldiers in combat zones.<sup id="cite_ref-Soldiers_of_Good_Fortune_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Soldiers_of_Good_Fortune-22">[22]</a></sup> Halliburton crews also helped bring 725 burning oil wells under control in <a href="/wiki/Kuwait" title="Kuwait">Kuwait</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p><p>In 1995, Cheney replaced <a href="/wiki/Thomas_H._Cruikshank" title="Thomas H. Cruikshank">Thomas H. Cruikshank</a>, as <a href="/wiki/Chairman" class="mw-redirect" title="Chairman">chairman</a> and <a href="/wiki/CEO" class="mw-redirect" title="CEO">CEO</a>. Cruikshank had served since 1989.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p><p>In the early 1990s, Halliburton was found to be in violation of federal trade barriers in Iraq and Libya, having sold these countries <a href="/wiki/Dual-use_technology" title="Dual-use technology">dual-use</a> oil drilling equipment and, through its former subsidiary, Halliburton Logging Services, sending six <a href="/wiki/Pulse_neutron_generator" class="mw-redirect" title="Pulse neutron generator">pulse neutron generators</a> to Libya. After having plead guilty, the company was fined $1.2 million, with another $2.61 million in penalties.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p><p>During the <a href="/wiki/Balkans" title="Balkans">Balkans</a> conflict in the 1990s, Kellogg Brown-Root (KBR) supported U.S. peacekeeping forces in <a href="/wiki/Bosnia_and_Herzegovina" title="Bosnia and Herzegovina">Bosnia and Herzegovina</a>, <a href="/wiki/Croatia" title="Croatia">Croatia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hungary" title="Hungary">Hungary</a> with food, laundry, transportation, and other life-cycle management services.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p><p>In 1998, Halliburton merged with Dresser Industries, which included Kellogg. <a href="/wiki/Prescott_Bush" title="Prescott Bush">Prescott Bush</a> was a director of Dresser Industries, which is now part of Halliburton; his son, former president <a href="/wiki/George_H._W._Bush" title="George H. W. Bush">George H. W. Bush</a>, worked for Dresser Industries in several positions from 1948 to 1951, before he founded <a href="/wiki/Zapata_Corporation" class="mw-redirect" title="Zapata Corporation">Zapata Corporation</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="2000s">2000s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Halliburton&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: 2000s" data-section="8" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:5HoustonCenter.JPG" class="image"><noscript><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d9/5HoustonCenter.JPG/220px-5HoustonCenter.JPG" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="2272" data-file-height="1704"></noscript><span class="lazy-image-placeholder" style="width: 220px;height: 165px;" data-src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d9/5HoustonCenter.JPG/220px-5HoustonCenter.JPG" data-alt="" data-width="220" data-height="165" data-class="thumbimage"> </span></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:5HoustonCenter.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/5_Houston_Center" class="mw-redirect" title="5 Houston Center">5 Houston Center</a> in <a href="/wiki/Downtown_Houston" title="Downtown Houston">Downtown Houston</a>, which at one time housed the headquarters of Halliburton</div></div></div>
<p><i><a href="/wiki/The_Wall_Street_Journal" title="The Wall Street Journal">The Wall Street Journal</a></i> reported in 2001 that a subsidiary of Halliburton Energy Services called Halliburton Products and Services Ltd. (HPS) opened an office in <a href="/wiki/Tehran" title="Tehran">Tehran</a>. The company, HPS, operated on the ninth floor of a new north Tehran tower block. Although HPS was incorporated in the <a href="/wiki/Cayman_Islands" title="Cayman Islands">Cayman Islands</a> in 1975 and is "non-American", it shares both the logo and name of Halliburton Energy Services and, according to <a href="/wiki/Dow_Jones_Newswires" class="mw-redirect" title="Dow Jones Newswires">Dow Jones Newswires</a>, offers services from Halliburton units worldwide through its Tehran office. Such behavior, undertaken while Cheney was CEO of Halliburton, may have violated the <a href="/wiki/Trading_with_the_Enemy_Act_of_1917" title="Trading with the Enemy Act of 1917">Trading with the Enemy Act</a>. A Halliburton spokesman, responding to inquiries from <a href="/wiki/Dow_Jones_%26_Company" title="Dow Jones &amp; Company">Dow Jones</a>, said "This is not breaking any laws. This is a foreign subsidiary and no U.S. person is involved in this. No U.S. person is facilitating any transaction. We are not performing directly in that country." Later David Lesar would book his own flights to the Tehran office through the UK arm of KBR. No legal action has been taken against the company or its officials.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p><p>In April 2002, KBR was awarded a $7 million contract to construct steel holding cells at <a href="/wiki/Camp_X-Ray" title="Camp X-Ray">Camp X-Ray</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p><p>In November 2002, KBR was tasked to plan oil well firefighting in Iraq, and in February 2003 was issued a contract to conduct the work. Critics contend that it was a <a href="/wiki/No-bid_contract" class="mw-redirect" title="No-bid contract">no-bid contract</a>, awarded due to Dick Cheney's position as vice president. Concern was also expressed that the contract could allow KBR to pump and distribute Iraqi oil.<sup id="cite_ref-bbc_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bbc-30">[30]</a></sup> Others contend, however, that this was not strictly a <i>no-bid contract,</i> and was invoked under a contract that KBR won "in a competitive bid process."<sup id="cite_ref-york_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-york-31">[31]</a></sup> The contract, referred to as LOGCAP, is a contingency-based contract that is invoked at the convenience of the Army. Because the contract is essentially a retainer, specific orders are not competitively bid (as the overall contract was).
</p><p>In May 2003, Halliburton revealed in <a href="/wiki/SEC_filings" class="mw-redirect" title="SEC filings">SEC filings</a> that its KBR subsidiary had paid a Nigerian official $2.4 million in <a href="/wiki/Bribery" title="Bribery">bribes</a> in order to receive favorable <a href="/wiki/Tax" title="Tax">tax</a> treatment.,<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/United_Arab_Emirates" title="United Arab Emirates">United Arab Emirates</a>
In October 2004, after emerging from the bankruptcy protection,<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> Halliburton opened a new 250,000-square-foot (23,000 m<sup>2</sup>) facility on 35 acres (140,000 m<sup>2</sup>), replacing an older facility that opened in 1948, in <a href="/wiki/Rock_Springs,_Wyoming" title="Rock Springs, Wyoming">Rock Springs, Wyoming</a>. With over approximately 500 employees, Halliburton is one of the largest private employers in <a href="/wiki/Sweetwater_County,_Wyoming" title="Sweetwater County, Wyoming">Sweetwater County</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></p><p>On January 24, 2006, Halliburton's subsidiary KBR (formerly Kellogg, Brown and Root) announced that it had been awarded a $385 million contingency contract by the <a href="/wiki/Department_of_Homeland_Security" class="mw-redirect" title="Department of Homeland Security">Department of Homeland Security</a> to build "temporary detention and processing facilities" or <a href="/wiki/Internment" title="Internment">internment</a> camps. According to <i><a href="/wiki/Business_Wire" title="Business Wire">Business Wire</a></i>, this contract will be executed in cooperation with the <a href="/wiki/U.S._Army_Corps_of_Engineers" class="mw-redirect" title="U.S. Army Corps of Engineers">U.S. Army Corps of Engineers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fort_Worth" class="mw-redirect" title="Fort Worth">Fort Worth</a> District. Critics point to the <a href="/wiki/Guantanamo_Bay_detention_camp" title="Guantanamo Bay detention camp">Guantanamo Bay detention camp</a> as a possible model. According to a press release posted on the Halliburton website, "The contract, which is effective immediately, provides for establishing temporary detention and processing capabilities to augment existing <a href="/wiki/Immigration_and_Customs_Enforcement" class="mw-redirect" title="Immigration and Customs Enforcement">Immigration and Customs Enforcement</a> (ICE) Detention and Removal Operations (DRO) Program facilities in the event of an emergency influx of immigrants into the U.S., or to support the rapid development of new programs. The contingency support contract provides for planning and, if required, initiation of specific engineering, construction and logistics support tasks to establish, operate and maintain one or more expansion facilities."<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></p><p>In February 2008, a hard disk and two computers containing classified information were stolen from Petrobras while in Halliburton's custody. Allegedly, the content inside the stolen material was data on the recently discovered <a href="/wiki/Tupi_oil_field" class="mw-redirect" title="Tupi oil field">Tupi oil field</a>. Initial police inquiries suggest that it could be a common container theft operation. The container was a ramshackle in complete disorder indicating that thieves were after "valuables and not only laptops," said an expert consulted by the daily newspaper Folha de S. Paulo.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></p><p>In 2008, Halliburton agreed to outsource its mission-critical information technology infrastructure to a <a href="/wiki/Dallas/Fort_Worth_Metroplex" class="mw-redirect" title="Dallas/Fort Worth Metroplex">Dallas/Fort Worth Metroplex</a> data center operated by CyrusOne Networks LLC.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></p><p>On May 14, 2010, President <a href="/wiki/Barack_Obama" title="Barack Obama">Barack Obama</a> said in an interview with <a href="/wiki/CNN" title="CNN">CNN</a> that "you had executives of BP and Transocean and Halliburton falling over each other to point the finger of blame at somebody else" when referring to the congressional hearings held during the <a href="/wiki/Deepwater_Horizon_oil_spill" title="Deepwater Horizon oil spill">Deepwater Horizon oil spill</a>. "The American people could not have been impressed with that display, and I certainly wasn't." According to Tim Probert, executive vice president of Halliburton, "Halliburton, as a service provider to the well owner, is contractually bound to comply with the well owner's instructions".<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup></p><p>It was anticipated that Halliburton's $2.5 billion "Restore Iraqi Oil" (RIO) contract<sup id="cite_ref-waxman_40-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-waxman-40">[40]</a></sup> would pay for itself as well as for reconstruction of the entire country. Plans called for more oil to be exported from Iraq's northern oil fields than actually occurred. Halliburton's work on the pipeline crossing the <a href="/wiki/Tigris" title="Tigris">Tigris</a> river at <a href="/wiki/Al_Fatah" class="mw-redirect" title="Al Fatah">Al Fatah</a> has been called a failure. Critics claim that the oil fields are barely usable and access to international markets is severely limited. As an example, against the advice of its own experts, Halliburton attempted to dig a tunnel through a geological <a href="/wiki/Fault_zone" class="mw-redirect" title="Fault zone">fault zone</a>. The underground terrain was a jumble of boulders, voids, cobblestones, and gravel and not appropriate for the kind of drilling Halliburton planned. "No driller in his right mind would have gone ahead," said Army geologist Robert Sanders when the military finally sent people to inspect the work.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Proposed_acquisition_of_Baker_Hughes">Proposed acquisition of Baker Hughes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Halliburton&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Proposed acquisition of Baker Hughes" data-section="9" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>On November 17, 2014, Halliburton and <a href="/wiki/Baker_Hughes" title="Baker Hughes">Baker Hughes</a> jointly announced a definitive agreement under which Halliburton will, subject to the conditions set forth in the agreement, acquire Baker Hughes in a stock and cash transaction valued at $34.6 billion. A press release made available on the former's website, as at December 11, 2014 detailed the restructuring in the integration to follow. The firm announced it would acquire <a href="/wiki/Baker_Hughes" title="Baker Hughes">Baker Hughes</a> for around $35 billion in cash and stock, creating an oilfield services company that aims to compete with <a href="/wiki/Schlumberger" title="Schlumberger">Schlumberger</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> Prior to the merger of Baker Hughes and Halliburton, Halliburton must divest over $5 billion of its assets according to the regulations created by US competition enforcement authorities.<sup id="cite_ref-Divestiture2015_43-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Divestiture2015-43">[43]</a></sup> The merger had a deadline of the end of April 2016 after which, if a decision had not been made, both companies could walk away from the deal if they chose. At the beginning of May 2016, the day after the deadline expired, Halliburton and Baker Hughes announced the termination of the merger agreement.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup></p>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(3)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Controversies">Controversies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Halliburton&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Controversies" data-section="10" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-3 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-3">
<p>Halliburton has become the object of several controversies involving the <a href="/wiki/Iraq_War" title="Iraq War">Iraq War</a> and the company's ties to former U.S. Vice President <a href="/wiki/Dick_Cheney" title="Dick Cheney">Dick Cheney</a>. Cheney retired from the company during the <a href="/wiki/2000_United_States_presidential_election" title="2000 United States presidential election">2000 U.S. presidential election</a> campaign with a severance package worth $36 million.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> As of 2004, he had received $398,548 in <a href="/wiki/Deferred_compensation" title="Deferred compensation">deferred compensation</a> from Halliburton while Vice President.<sup id="cite_ref-factcheck-cheney_47-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-factcheck-cheney-47">[47]</a></sup> Cheney was chairman and CEO of Halliburton Company from 1995 to 2000 and has received stock options from Halliburton.<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup></p><p>In the run-up to the Iraq War, Halliburton was awarded a $7 billion contract for which only Halliburton was allowed to bid.<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> Under U.S. law, the government uses <a href="/wiki/No-bid_contract" class="mw-redirect" title="No-bid contract">single-bid contracts</a> for a number of reasons, to include when in the view of the government, only one organization is capable of fulfilling the requirement.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:No_original_research" title="Wikipedia:No original research"><span title="The material near this tag possibly contains original research. (March 2020)">original research?</span></a></i>]</sup></p><p><a href="/wiki/Bunnatine_Greenhouse" class="mw-redirect" title="Bunnatine Greenhouse">Bunnatine Greenhouse</a>, a civil servant with 20 years of contracting experience, had complained to Army officials on numerous occasions that Halliburton had been unlawfully receiving special treatment for work in Iraq, Kuwait and the Balkans. Criminal investigations were opened by the U.S. Justice Department, the Federal Bureau of Investigation (<a href="/wiki/FBI" class="mw-redirect" title="FBI">FBI</a>) and the Pentagon's inspector general. These investigations found no wrongdoing within the contract award and execution process.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2020)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p><p>In one of Greenhouse's claims, she said that military auditors caught Halliburton overcharging the Pentagon for fuel deliveries into Iraq. She also complained that Defense Secretary <a href="/wiki/Donald_Rumsfeld" title="Donald Rumsfeld">Donald Rumsfeld</a>'s office took control of every aspect of Halliburton's $7 billion Iraqi oil/infrastructure contract. Greenhouse was later demoted for poor performance in her position.<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> Greenhouse's attorney, Michael Kohn portrayed her performance reviews as punishment for criticizing the administrations, he stated in <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times" title="The New York Times">The New York Times</a></i> that "she is being demoted because of her strict adherence to procurement requirements and the Army's preference to sidestep them when it suits their needs."<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Deepwater_Horizon_explosion">Deepwater Horizon explosion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Halliburton&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Deepwater Horizon explosion" data-section="11" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>An internal report released in 2010 by <a href="/wiki/BP" title="BP">BP</a> into the <a href="/wiki/Deepwater_Horizon_explosion" title="Deepwater Horizon explosion">Deepwater Horizon explosion</a> claimed that poor practices of Halliburton staff had contributed to the disaster. Investigations carried out by the National Commission on the BP Deepwater Horizon Oil Spill and Offshore Drilling found that Halliburton was jointly at fault along with BP and Transocean for the spill. The concrete that Halliburton used was an unstable mixture, and eventually caused hydrocarbons to leak into the well, causing the explosion that started the crisis.<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup></p><p>Halliburton pleaded guilty to destroying evidence after the April 2010 Deepwater Horizon disaster; the company destroyed computer simulations it performed in the months after the accident, simulations that contradicted Halliburton's claim that it was BP who had not followed Halliburton's advice. BP had employed Halliburton to oversee the process by which cement is used to seal casing in oil and gas wells, thereby preventing leaks. Government investigators had ordered companies involved in drilling the well to preserve all relevant evidence.<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Allegations_of_corruption_in_Nigeria">Allegations of corruption in Nigeria</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Halliburton&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Allegations of corruption in Nigeria" data-section="12" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>In early December 2010, the <a href="/wiki/Nigerian_government" class="mw-redirect" title="Nigerian government">Nigerian government</a> filed <a href="/wiki/Political_corruption" title="Political corruption">corruption</a> charges against Cheney in connection with his role as the chief executive of Halliburton.<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup> The case relates to an alleged $182 million contract involving a four-company joint venture to build a liquefied natural gas plant on <a href="/wiki/Bonny_Island" title="Bonny Island">Bonny Island</a> in southern Nigeria.<sup id="cite_ref-USAToday_56-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-USAToday-56">[56]</a></sup> Earlier in 2009, KBR, a former subsidiary of Halliburton, agreed to pay $402 million after admitting that it bribed Nigerian officials, and Halliburton paid $177 million to settle allegations by the <a href="/wiki/U.S._Securities_and_Exchange_Commission" title="U.S. Securities and Exchange Commission">U.S. Securities and Exchange Commission</a> without admitting any wrongdoing.<sup id="cite_ref-Bloomberg_57-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bloomberg-57">[57]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup> In mid-December 2010, the case was <a href="/wiki/Settlement_(litigation)" title="Settlement (litigation)">settled</a> when Nigeria agreed to drop the corruption charges against Cheney and Halliburton in exchange for a $250 million settlement.<sup id="cite_ref-Bloomberg_57-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bloomberg-57">[57]</a></sup> According to Femi Babafemi, the spokesperson for the Economic and Financial Crimes Commission, the $250 million would include approximately $130 million frozen in a <a href="/wiki/Swiss_bank_account" class="mw-redirect" title="Swiss bank account">Swiss bank</a>, and the rest would be paid as fines.<sup id="cite_ref-USAToday_56-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-USAToday-56">[56]</a></sup></p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Project_on_Government_Oversight" class="mw-redirect" title="Project on Government Oversight">Federal Contractor Misconduct Database</a> details 10 instances of misconduct since 1995 under which Halliburton has agreed to pay settlements of $791 million.<sup id="cite_ref-59" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup> A further 22 instances of misconduct relate to the company's former subsidiary KBR.<sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Environmental_issues">Environmental issues</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Halliburton&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Environmental issues" data-section="13" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>In 2002, Toxics Release Inventory (TRI) reports were completed to measure the amount of chemicals emitted from Halliburton's <a href="/wiki/Harris_County,_Texas" title="Harris County, Texas">Harris County, Texas</a> facility. The TRI is a publicly available EPA database that contains information on toxic chemical releases and waste management activities reported annually by certain industries as well as federal facilities. The facility had 230 TRI air releases in 2001 and 245 in 2002.<sup id="cite_ref-61" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup></p><p>On June 7, 2006, Halliburton's <a href="/wiki/Farmington,_New_Mexico" title="Farmington, New Mexico">Farmington, New Mexico</a> facility created a toxic cloud that forced people to evacuate their homes.<sup id="cite_ref-62" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup></p><p>Halliburton may also be implicated<sup id="cite_ref-63" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup> in the oil spills in the <a href="/wiki/Montara_oil_spill" title="Montara oil spill">Timor Sea</a> off Australia in August 2009 and in the Gulf of Mexico in April 2010 for improper cementing. Halliburton staff were employed on the Transocean operated <i><a href="/wiki/Deepwater_Horizon" title="Deepwater Horizon">Deepwater Horizon</a></i> oil rig in the Mexican Gulf. Halliburton staff completed cementation of the final production well 20 hours prior to the Deepwater Horizon drilling rig explosion, but had not yet set the final.<sup id="cite_ref-64" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup></p><p>In July 2013, Halliburton Co agreed to plead guilty to charges that it destroyed evidence relating to the 2010 Deepwater Horizon oil spill. This incurred a $200,000 fine; the firm also agreed to three years of probation and to continue cooperating with the criminal probe into the spill.<sup id="cite_ref-65" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup> In September 2014, the company agreed to pay $1.1 billion in damages to settle the majority of claims against it relating to the explosion, removing the uncertainty which had hung over the company for the previous four years
</p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Jamie_Leigh_Jones_incident">Jamie Leigh Jones incident</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Halliburton&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Jamie Leigh Jones incident" data-section="14" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Jamie_Leigh_Jones" title="Jamie Leigh Jones">Jamie Leigh Jones</a> testified at a Congressional hearing that she had been gang-raped by as many as seven co-workers in Iraq in 2005 when she was an employee of KBR, and then falsely imprisoned in a shipping container for 24 hours without food or drink.<sup id="cite_ref-66" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-think_67-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-think-67">[67]</a></sup> KBR was a subsidiary of Halliburton at the time. Jones and her lawyers said that 38 women have contacted her reporting similar experiences while working as contractors in Iraq, Kuwait, and other countries. On September 15, 2009, the 5th Circuit Court of Appeals ruled in favor of Halliburton, in a 2 to 1 ruling, and found that her alleged injuries were not, in fact, in any way related to her employment and thus, not covered by the contract. This decision effectively meant that the mandatory arbitration clause in her contract did not apply.<sup id="cite_ref-think_67-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-think-67">[67]</a></sup></p><p>These incidents have tainted the public perception of Halliburton, with a consumer study rating it as the 5th least reputable company in America.<sup id="cite_ref-68" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Sale_of_KBR">Sale of KBR</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Halliburton&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Sale of KBR" data-section="15" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>On April 15, 2006, Halliburton filed a registration statement with the <a href="/wiki/Securities_and_Exchange_Commission" class="mw-redirect" title="Securities and Exchange Commission">Securities and Exchange Commission</a> to sell up to 20 percent of its KBR stock on the <a href="/wiki/NYSE" class="mw-redirect" title="NYSE">NYSE</a> under the ticker symbol "KBR", as part of an eventual plan for KBR to be a separate company from Halliburton.<sup id="cite_ref-69" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup></p><p>In November 2006, Halliburton began selling its stake in KBR, its major subsidiary, and by February 2007 had completely sold off the subsidiary. In June 2007, several days after Stewart Bowen, the Special Inspector General, released a new report, the Army announced that KBR would share another $150 billion contract with two other contractors, <a href="/wiki/Fluor_Corporation" title="Fluor Corporation">Fluor</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dyncorp" class="mw-redirect" title="Dyncorp">Dyncorp</a>, over the next 10 years.<sup id="cite_ref-70" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Baghdad_incident">Baghdad incident</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Halliburton&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Baghdad incident" data-section="16" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>In accordance with the <a href="/wiki/Law_of_armed_conflict" class="mw-redirect" title="Law of armed conflict">law of armed conflict</a> and to maintain <a href="/wiki/Non-combatant" title="Non-combatant">non-combatant</a> status, Halliburton does not arm its truck drivers. Trucks are often the target of <a href="/wiki/Iraqi_insurgency_(Iraq_War)" class="mw-redirect" title="Iraqi insurgency (Iraq War)">insurgent</a> attacks. On September 20, 2005, a convoy of four Halliburton trucks was ambushed north of Baghdad. All four trucks were struck by <a href="/wiki/Improvised_explosive_device" title="Improvised explosive device">improvised explosive devices</a> and were disabled. Their US <a href="/wiki/National_Guard_of_the_United_States" class="mw-redirect" title="National Guard of the United States">National Guard</a> escort was thought to have abandoned the disabled vehicles, leaving the drivers defenseless. Three of the four truck drivers were killed by the insurgents while the surviving driver caught the event on video. Although the trucks had military camouflage paint, the drivers were civilian. The US military returned to the scene 45 minutes later.<sup id="cite_ref-71" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup> However, in a statement by senior military officials in Iraq, an investigation revealed that troops did not abandon the civilians and they were all exiting the "kill zone" during the ambush.<sup id="cite_ref-72" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-72">[72]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-73" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-73">[73]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Restatements">Restatements</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Halliburton&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Restatements" data-section="17" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>On March 31, 2003, Management at Halliburton restated earnings downward by $14 million for the fourth quarter of 2002. In the restatement, an additional $3 million expense (net of tax) to continuing operations and an $11 million expense, net of tax, to discontinued operations were recorded.<sup id="cite_ref-74" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-74">[74]</a></sup> In March 2, 2005, Halliburton restated its 2004 fourth-quarter earnings to add $2 million US in after-tax losses to reflect the collection of a $10 million receivable that had been reserved and a correction in lease accounting.
</p>

<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:352px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Halliburton_North_Belt_Sign_04_-_West_Side_(Red_Sign_Removed).jpg" class="image"><noscript><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cd/Halliburton_North_Belt_Sign_04_-_West_Side_%28Red_Sign_Removed%29.jpg/350px-Halliburton_North_Belt_Sign_04_-_West_Side_%28Red_Sign_Removed%29.jpg" decoding="async" width="350" height="198" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="5184" data-file-height="2940"></noscript><span class="lazy-image-placeholder" style="width: 350px;height: 198px;" data-src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cd/Halliburton_North_Belt_Sign_04_-_West_Side_%28Red_Sign_Removed%29.jpg/350px-Halliburton_North_Belt_Sign_04_-_West_Side_%28Red_Sign_Removed%29.jpg" data-alt="" data-width="350" data-height="198" data-class="thumbimage"> </span></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Halliburton_North_Belt_Sign_04_-_West_Side_(Red_Sign_Removed).jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Halliburton headquarters (North Belt Campus) in north Houston</div></div></div>
<p>Halliburton's headquarters (North Belt Campus) are located in northern Houston, Texas, near <a href="/wiki/George_Bush_Intercontinental_Airport" title="George Bush Intercontinental Airport">George H.W. Bush Intercontinental Airport</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-HQNorthBeltCampus_76-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HQNorthBeltCampus-76">[76]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Haltoconsol_77-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Haltoconsol-77">[77]</a></sup></p><p>Halliburton was headquartered in <a href="/wiki/Dallas,_Texas" class="mw-redirect" title="Dallas, Texas">Dallas, Texas</a>, from 1961 to 2003.<sup id="cite_ref-Haltoconsol_77-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Haltoconsol-77">[77]</a></sup> The company moved its headquarters from the Southland Life Building in Dallas to 50,648 square feet (4,705.4 m<sup>2</sup>) of space in Lincoln Plaza in <a href="/wiki/Downtown_Dallas" title="Downtown Dallas">Downtown Dallas</a> in 1985.<sup id="cite_ref-78" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-78">[78]</a></sup> 20 employees worked in Halliburton's headquarters in Dallas.<sup id="cite_ref-79" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-79">[79]</a></sup></p><p>Halliburton planned to move its headquarters to Houston in 2002.<sup id="cite_ref-80" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-80">[80]</a></sup> Halliburton, which signed its lease to occupy a portion of <a href="/wiki/5_Houston_Center" class="mw-redirect" title="5 Houston Center">5 Houston Center</a> in <a href="/wiki/Downtown_Houston" title="Downtown Houston">Downtown Houston</a> in 2002,<sup id="cite_ref-HoustonCenterFive_81-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HoustonCenterFive-81">[81]</a></sup> moved its headquarters there by July 2003.<sup id="cite_ref-Bivins3towers_82-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bivins3towers-82">[82]</a></sup> Halliburton occupied 26,000 square feet (2,400 m<sup>2</sup>) of space on the 24th floor in 5 Houston Center.<sup id="cite_ref-Haltoconsol_77-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Haltoconsol-77">[77]</a></sup></p>

<p>In 2009 Halliburton announced that it planned to move its headquarters to the North Belt Campus in Houston. In addition it planned to consolidate operations at its Westchase and North Belt Campus.<sup id="cite_ref-ClantonHalliburtLeaveDowntown_83-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ClantonHalliburtLeaveDowntown-83">[83]</a></sup> The move occurred in 2009.<sup id="cite_ref-HQNorthBeltCampus_76-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HQNorthBeltCampus-76">[76]</a></sup> The 90 acres (36 ha) North Belt complex was tohouse 2,500 employees. Halliburton planned to add a research and development facility with laboratories, a new cafeteria, a childcare center, two additional parking garages, and fitness and wellness centers for employees.<sup id="cite_ref-Haltoconsol_77-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Haltoconsol-77">[77]</a></sup> The plans for the North Belt Campus had been delayed by one year, and Halliburton expects<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Chronological_items" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers"><span title="The time period mentioned near this tag is ambiguous. The construction of the North Belt administration building is scheduled<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Chronological_items" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers"><span title="The time period mentioned near this tag is ambiguous. Subsequently, Halliburton retained the North Belt office.<sup id="cite_ref-85" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-85">[85]</a></sup></p>
