Its subsidiaries and affiliates are involved in the distribution, transmission, and generation of electricity, as well as energy management and other energy-related services. Its ten electric utility operating companies comprise one of the United States' largest <a href="/wiki/Investor-owned_utility" title="Investor-owned utility">investor-owned utilities</a>, based on serving 6 million customers within a 65,000-square-mile (170,000 km<sup>2</sup>) area of <a href="/wiki/Ohio" title="Ohio">Ohio</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pennsylvania" title="Pennsylvania">Pennsylvania</a>, <a href="/wiki/West_Virginia" title="West Virginia">West Virginia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Virginia" title="Virginia">Virginia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Maryland" title="Maryland">Maryland</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_Jersey" title="New Jersey">New Jersey</a> and <a href="/wiki/New_York_(state)" title="New York (state)">New York</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Its generation subsidiaries control more than 16,000 megawatts of capacity, and its distribution lines span over 194,000 miles. In 2018, FirstEnergy ranked 219 on the <a href="/wiki/Fortune_500" title="Fortune 500">Fortune 500</a> list of the largest public corporations in the United States by revenue.<sup id="cite_ref-5" </section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(1)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=FirstEnergy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History" data-section="1" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-1 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-1">
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Formation">Formation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=FirstEnergy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Formation" data-section="2" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>FirstEnergy was formed on November 7, 1997, when Ohio Edison acquired Centerior Energy and its subsidiaries for $1.6 billion in stock.<sup id="cite_ref-one_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-one-6">[6]</a></sup> The company was acquired with plans for a restructuring and layoffs to cut costs.<sup id="cite_ref-one_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-one-6">[6]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> That same month the <a href="/wiki/Public_Utilities_Commission_of_Ohio" title="Public Utilities Commission of Ohio">Public Utilities Commission of Ohio</a> (PUCO) initiated an investigation into the reliability of FirstEnergy's energy transmission in the context of possible plant shutdowns and prior problems with Centerior.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Subsequent_Mergers">Subsequent Mergers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=FirstEnergy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Subsequent Mergers" data-section="3" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>In 2001, FirstEnergy merged with GPU, Inc., the owner of Jersey Central Power &amp; Light Company, Pennsylvania Electric Company (Penelec), and Metropolitan Edison Company (Met-Ed).<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p><p>FirstEnergy would later merge with Greensburg, Pennsylvania-based <a href="/wiki/Allegheny_Energy" title="Allegheny Energy">Allegheny Energy</a> in 2011.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Bankruptcy_of_FirstEnergy_Solutions">Bankruptcy of FirstEnergy Solutions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=FirstEnergy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Bankruptcy of FirstEnergy Solutions" data-section="4" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>On March 31, 2018, FirstEnergy Solutions filed for bankruptcy. FirstEnergy Solutions was a generation subsidiary, and FirstEnergy itself remains solvent.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> The case is being closely watched as it could have significant implications for the U.S. power sector, as the U.S. Bankruptcy Court for the Northern District of Ohio has asserted its primacy over the <a href="/wiki/Federal_Energy_Regulatory_Commission" title="Federal Energy Regulatory Commission">Federal Energy Regulatory Commission</a> (FERC) relating to certain FirstEnergy Solutions FERC-regulated power purchase agreements.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p><p>The Company filed its eighth amended bankruptcy plan on October 14, 2019.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup>  Judge Alan M. Koschik issued an order confirming the plan on October 16, 2019.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Proposed_Nuclear_and_Coal_Plant_Closings">Proposed Nuclear and Coal Plant Closings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=FirstEnergy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Proposed Nuclear and Coal Plant Closings" data-section="5" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>In March, 2018, FirstEnergy announced it is closing the <a href="/wiki/Perry_Nuclear_Generating_Station" title="Perry Nuclear Generating Station">Perry Nuclear Generating Station</a> and <a href="/wiki/Davis%E2%80%93Besse_Nuclear_Power_Station" title="Davis–Besse Nuclear Power Station">Davis–Besse Nuclear Power Station</a>, both in Ohio, and <a href="/wiki/Beaver_Valley_Nuclear_Power_Station" title="Beaver Valley Nuclear Power Station">Beaver Valley Nuclear Power Station</a> in Pennsylvania.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> This was followed in August 2018 with the announcement of the closure of two coal-fired plants, the <a href="/wiki/W.H._Sammis_Power_Plant" class="mw-redirect" title="W.H. Sammis Power Plant">W.H. Sammis Power Plant</a> in Stratton, Ohio and the <a href="/wiki/Bruce_Mansfield_Power_Plant" title="Bruce Mansfield Power Plant">Bruce Mansfield Power Plant</a> in Shippingport, Pennsylvania by June 2022.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p><p>However, the closure of the Perry, Davis–Besse and Sammis plants were rescinded in July 2019 when the State of Ohio passed and signed into law a subsidy to support the Perry and Davis–Besse nuclear plants.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Electric_vehicles">Electric vehicles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=FirstEnergy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Electric vehicles" data-section="6" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>FirstEnergy has worked to put more electric vehicles on the road. To accomplish this, the company partnered with Nissan and BMW to offer customers rebates who purchase electric vehicles from those two companies. The 2018 Nissan LEAF and BMW i3 models are eligible. LEAF buyers get a $3,000 rebate while i3 buyers get $10,000.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Reaction_to_coronavirus_pandemic">Reaction to coronavirus pandemic</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=FirstEnergy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Reaction to coronavirus pandemic" data-section="7" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>During the <a href="/wiki/Coronavirus_pandemic" class="mw-redirect" title="Coronavirus pandemic">coronavirus pandemic</a> in March 2020, the company stopped power shutoffs and restored connections for those whose power had been terminated because of non-payment. They also requested that customers who were facing hardship paying their utility bills contact the company to set up alternate payment programs, energy assistance programs or other energy arrangements, based on the customer’s ability to pay. This included customers of all ten First Energy utility companies in six states.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p><p>As of 4/1/2020 FirstEnergy Corp or any of its subsidiaries has not offered to waive late fees or penalties for any of their customers.
</p>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(2)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Former_Subsidiaries">Former Subsidiaries</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=FirstEnergy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Former Subsidiaries" data-section="8" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-2 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-2">
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Ohio_Edison">Ohio Edison</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=FirstEnergy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Ohio Edison" data-section="9" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>Ohio Edison Company (formerly OEC on the NYSE)<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> was a publicly traded holding company that began in 1930, from the consolidation of 200 electric companies.  By 1950, it ended up with two utility operating companies, Pennsylvania Power and Ohio Edison.  It continued in existence until 1997, when its merger with Centerior formed FirstEnergy:
</p>
<ul><li>In 1944, the <b>Pennsylvania Power Company</b> became a subsidiary of Ohio Edison, and is now one of the ten operating utilities.</li>
<li>In 1950, the <b>Ohio Edison Company</b> merged with the <b>Ohio Public Service Company</b>, which continued to operate under its new Ohio Edison name. It is now one of the ten FirstEnergy operating companies, and is the main power provider for northeastern Ohio outside of Cleveland itself.</li></ul><h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Centerior">Centerior</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=FirstEnergy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Centerior" data-section="10" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>Centerior Energy Corporation (formerly CX on the NYSE) was formed in 1986 from the merger of two old operating companies. Centerior was based in <a href="/wiki/Independence,_Ohio" title="Independence, Ohio">Independence, Ohio</a>, and existed as a publicly traded holding company for ten years, until its merger with Ohio Edison formed FirstEnergy in 1997:
</p>
<ul><li><b>Cleveland Electric Illuminating Company</b>, commonly known as <b>The Illuminating Company</b>, was a publicly traded operating company through 1986, until it merged with Toledo Edison to come under the control of Centerior. Having been acquired by 1929, by 1940 it had become one of ten major direct subsidiaries of <a href="/wiki/North_American_Company" title="North American Company">North American Company</a>, which in turn had been one of the original 12 stocks listed in the <a href="/wiki/Dow_Jones_Industrial_Average" title="Dow Jones Industrial Average">Dow Jones Industrial Average</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-supreme_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-supreme-22">[22]</a></sup> It is one of two power companies serving Greater Cleveland, the other being city-owned <a href="/wiki/Cleveland_Public_Power" title="Cleveland Public Power">Cleveland Public Power</a>.</li>
<li><b>Toledo Edison Company</b> (formerly TED on the NYSE) was a publicly traded utility operating company, until it merged to form Centerior in 1986. It is the main power provider for northwestern Ohio.</li></ul><h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="GPU">GPU</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=FirstEnergy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: GPU" data-section="11" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>General Public Utilities (formerly GPU on the NYSE) was a publicly traded utility holding company in <a href="/wiki/Morristown,_New_Jersey" title="Morristown, New Jersey">Morristown, New Jersey</a>. In 1996, the company was reorganized and renamed GPU, Inc. In 1996, it formed a new division as well, GPU Energy, which became the holding company for its three utility operating companies:
</p>
<ul><li><b>Jersey Central Power and Light</b> (JCP&amp;L, serving most of central and northwestern New Jersey)</li>
<li><b>Pennsylvania Electric Company</b> (Penelec, serving northern and central Pennsylvania)</li>
<li><b>Metropolitan Edison</b> (Met-Ed, serving eastern and south-central Pennsylvania)</li></ul><p>In 2001, FirstEnergy Corporation, with its four utility operating companies, merged with GPU, Inc., bringing GPU's three additional operating companies into FirstEnergy as well.
</p><p>Through the 2001 acquisition of GPU, FirstEnergy also acquired:
</p>
<ul><li><b>MYR Group</b> (formerly MYR on the NYSE), a subsidiary that GPU had created as a publicly traded company in the 1996 reorganization, to  install and maintain utility <a href="/wiki/Electric_power_transmission" title="Electric power transmission">power lines</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cellular_telephone" class="mw-redirect" title="Cellular telephone">cellular telephone</a> communications towers.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></li></ul><p>GPU is best known as the former owner of the <a href="/wiki/Three_Mile_Island_nuclear_plant" class="mw-redirect" title="Three Mile Island nuclear plant">Three Mile Island nuclear plant</a>.  In 1989, Standley H. Hoch, a former executive with <a href="/wiki/General_Dynamics_Corporation" class="mw-redirect" title="General Dynamics Corporation">General Dynamics</a>, became the CEO of GPU.  Hoch had two main goals—cut costs and fight to repeal the <a href="/wiki/Public_Utility_Holding_Company_Act_of_1935" title="Public Utility Holding Company Act of 1935">Public Utility Holding Company Act of 1935</a>, which made it difficult for utilities to operate across state lines.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Allegheny_Energy">Allegheny Energy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=FirstEnergy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Allegheny Energy" data-section="12" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Allegheny_Energy" title="Allegheny Energy">Allegheny Energy</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Allegheny_Energy" title="Allegheny Energy">Allegheny Energy</a> was an electric utility serving customers in Pennsylvania, West Virginia, Virginia, and Maryland. Its regulated subsidiaries are <b>West Penn Power</b> (serving Southwestern and Central Pennsylvania), <b>Monongahela Power</b> (a.k.a. "Mon Power", serving Northern and Southern West Virginia), and <b>The Potomac Edison Company</b> (western Maryland, parts of eastern West Virginia, and northern Virginia). The electric generating plants are operated by subsidiary Allegheny Energy Supply Company and Monongahela Power.
</p><p>Before the formation of Allegheny Energy, the holding company was known as Allegheny Power System which had the three utility operating units. The brand name Allegheny Power was used on customer bills, trucks and company equipment starting in 1996. In 1997, the company attempted to merge with Pittsburgh-based <a href="/wiki/DQE" class="mw-redirect" title="DQE">Duquesne Light Company</a>. The merger was withdrawn by both parties and both companies did not merge. In 1999, Allegheny Power purchased the West Virginia operations of <a href="/wiki/Aquila,_Inc." title="Aquila, Inc.">UtiliCorp United</a>'s West Virginia Power. UtiliCorp purchased Virginia Electric and Power Company's (present day <a href="/wiki/Dominion_Resources" class="mw-redirect" title="Dominion Resources">Dominion Resources</a>) West Virginia service area in 1986 and renamed the acquired service area West Virginia Power.
</p><p>In February 2010, Allegheny Energy announced plans to merge with FirstEnergy. The merger was approved by stockholders of both companies, the <a href="/wiki/Federal_Energy_Regulatory_Commission" title="Federal Energy Regulatory Commission">Federal Energy Regulatory Commission</a>, and regulatory commissions in Virginia, West Virginia, Maryland, and Pennsylvania.  The merger was finalized when the Pennsylvania Public Utilities Commission approved the merger on February 24, 2011; the merger closed on February 25, 2011.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> The merger does not include Allegheny's service area in Virginia, which was purchased in 2010 by the Shenandoah Valley Electric Cooperative and the Rappahannock Electric Cooperative.
</p><p>After the merger with Allegheny Energy, FirstEnergy was the largest investor-owned electric utility in the country for a short period of time based on customers served before the <a href="/wiki/Exelon" title="Exelon">Exelon</a>/<a href="/wiki/Constellation_(An_Exelon_Company)" class="mw-redirect" title="Constellation (An Exelon Company)">Constellation</a> and <a href="/wiki/Duke_Energy" title="Duke Energy">Duke Energy</a>/<a href="/wiki/Progress_Energy_Inc" title="Progress Energy Inc">Progress Energy</a> mergers.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(3)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Intent_to_exit_non-regulated_business">Intent to exit non-regulated business</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=FirstEnergy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Intent to exit non-regulated business" data-section="13" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-3 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-3">
<p>FirstEnergy announced its intent in November 2016 to exit the competitive businesses while staying in the regulated businesses and become a fully <a href="/wiki/Regulated_market" title="Regulated market">regulated company</a> during the next 18 months.<sup id="cite_ref-powermag-20161111_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-powermag-20161111-26">[26]</a></sup> FirstEnergy Solutions, the company's competitive subsidiary, manages 13,000 MW of generating capacity and is a leading energy supplier serving residential, commercial and industrial customers in the Northeast, Midwest and Mid-Atlantic regions. It was anticipated that some generating units would be sold, and some would be shut down.<sup id="cite_ref-powermag-20161111_26-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-powermag-20161111-26">[26]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Robert_E._Murray" title="Robert E. Murray">Robert E. Murray</a>, CEO of Murray Energy, warned in August 2017 that FirstEnergy Solutions was in danger of bankruptcy if an emergency order to open coal-fired plants was not issued by the White House.<sup id="cite_ref-cnn170822_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cnn170822-27">[27]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-cd170822_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cd170822-28">[28]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Federal_Energy_Regulatory_Commission" title="Federal Energy Regulatory Commission">Federal Energy Regulatory Commission</a> (FERC) unanimously rejected a <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Energy" title="United States Department of Energy">United States Department of Energy</a> (DOE) <a href="/wiki/Notice_of_proposed_rulemaking" title="Notice of proposed rulemaking">Notice of Proposed Rulemaking</a> (NOPR) to subsidize coal and nuclear plants in January 2018.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> FirstEnergy Solutions filed for <a href="/wiki/Chapter_11,_Title_11,_United_States_Code" title="Chapter 11, Title 11, United States Code">Chapter 11 bankruptcy</a> on March 31, 2018.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> As a result of the  bankruptcy, FirstEnergy Solutions sought federal intervention of invoking Section 202(c) of the <a href="/wiki/Federal_Power_Act" title="Federal Power Act">Federal Power Act</a> to keep their power plants operating.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p><p>In February 2018, FirstEnergy announced plans to deactivate or sell <a href="/wiki/Pleasants_Power_Station" title="Pleasants Power Station">Pleasants Power Station</a> in West Virginia.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup>  In March 2018, FirstEnergy announced plans to deactivate or sell the <a href="/wiki/Beaver_Valley_Nuclear_Generating_Station" class="mw-redirect" title="Beaver Valley Nuclear Generating Station">Beaver Valley</a>, <a href="/wiki/Davis-Besse" class="mw-redirect" title="Davis-Besse">Davis-Besse</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Perry_Nuclear_Generating_Station" title="Perry Nuclear Generating Station">Perry</a> <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_power_plants" class="mw-redirect" title="Nuclear power plants">nuclear power plants</a>, which are in the Ohio and Pennsylvania deregulated electricity market, during the next three years.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p><p>FirstEnergy's electric generation is primarily from coal and nuclear power plants, but also includes natural gas, oil, and hydroelectric power plants.
</p>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(4)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Environmental_record">Environmental record</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=FirstEnergy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Environmental record" data-section="14" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-4 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-4">
<p>A 2017 report conducted by the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Massachusetts_Amherst" title="University of Massachusetts Amherst">University of Massachusetts Amherst</a> placed FirstEnergy 9 out of the top 100 of the country's largest greenhouse polluters.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></p><p>FirstEnergy was required to pay $1.5 billion by 2011 as part of a settlement to end a lawsuit filed by the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Environmental_Protection_Agency" title="United States Environmental Protection Agency">United States Environmental Protection Agency</a>. This lawsuit alleged that the company failed to install pollution control equipment when upgrading its coal burning plants.  Also as part of the settlement, major pollution control equipment is now being installed at the FirstEnergy Sammis site and others. This lawsuit was one of the <a href="/wiki/New_Source_Review" title="New Source Review">New Source Review</a> lawsuits filed in the 1990s.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></p><p>To provide cleaner energy to its customers, FirstEnergy took several important steps in 2009.  First, the company announced plans in April to repower Units 4 and 5 at its <a href="/wiki/R.E._Burger_Power_Station" title="R.E. Burger Power Station">R.E. Burger Power Station</a> in <a href="/wiki/Shadyside,_Ohio" title="Shadyside, Ohio">Shadyside, Ohio</a>, to generate electricity principally with <a href="/wiki/Biomass" title="Biomass">biomass</a>, the only <a href="/wiki/Base_load" title="Base load">base load</a> renewable source that can displace coal emissions.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup>  Furthermore, FirstEnergy hosted a 1 MW pilot plant test of carbon capture retrofit equipment for <a href="/wiki/Carbon_sequestration" title="Carbon sequestration">carbon sequestration</a> on one of the remaining coal units at the R.E. Burger.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup>  In September, FirstEnergy decided to complete construction on the Fremont Energy Center, a 707-MW natural-gas-fired peaking plant by the end of 2010.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> And finally in November, FirstEnergy purchased the rights to develop a compressed-air electric generating plant in <a href="/wiki/Norton,_Ohio" title="Norton, Ohio">Norton, Ohio</a>, which Ohio Governor <a href="/wiki/Ted_Strickland" title="Ted Strickland">Ted Strickland</a> praised as "an example of how we can leverage technology and our natural resources to grow our economy and ensure our energy future." The Norton project, part of the company's overall climate change strategy, has the potential to be expanded to up to 2,700 MW of capacity—the largest in the world by far. According to the <a href="/wiki/Electric_Power_Research_Institute" title="Electric Power Research Institute">Electric Power Research Institute</a>, "a <a href="/wiki/Compressed-air_energy_storage" title="Compressed-air energy storage">compressed-air energy storage</a> project of this size...could be a key component in integrating large-scale intermittent renewables (such as wind) onto the nation's grid system."<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> Together, these projects, when completed, will further reduce the utility's emissions of <span class="nowrap"><span class="chemf nowrap">CO<span style="display:inline-block; margin-bottom:-0.3em; vertical-align:-0.4em; line-height:1.2em; font-size:70%; text-align:left;"><br>2</span></span></span>, which already is about one-third below the regional average.
</p><p>FirstEnergy Solutions Corp. has also given renewable energy certificates to help balance out the amount of electricity used in <a href="/wiki/Earth_Day" title="Earth Day">Earth Day</a> events that were held at nine post-secondary education locations in Maryland, New Jersey, Ohio, and Pennsylvania. Each of the schools received five SmartWind REC's, enough energy to light a large building for the entire day.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup></p><p>Energy efficiency became a state mandate in 2008 when lawmakers agreed with former Gov. Ted Strickland after months of debate to pass a law requiring electric utilities to help customers use less electricity every year—22 percent less by 2025 than they did in 2009. Under the 2008 law, FirstEnergy slaps companies that do not invest in energy efficiency with higher rates. But industries that use their <a href="/wiki/Waste_heat" title="Waste heat">waste heat</a> to make power could escape that rate increase.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup></p><p>As of November 29, 2012 FirstEnergy Corp. has abandoned its behind-the-scene lobbying campaign to persuade lawmakers to gut a four-year-old law requiring utilities to help customers use less electricity by switching to energy efficient equipment and lighting.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Little_Blue_Run">Little Blue Run</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=FirstEnergy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Little Blue Run" data-section="15" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>Several cases have been brought against FirstEnergy for its dumping of coal waste into <a href="/wiki/Little_Blue_Run_Lake" title="Little Blue Run Lake">Little Blue Run Lake</a> in West Virginia and Pennsylvania.<sup id="cite_ref-Vice_news_43-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Vice_news-43">[43]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Triblive_44-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Triblive-44">[44]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Pittsburgh_Post-Gazette_45-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pittsburgh_Post-Gazette-45">[45]</a></sup> FirstEnergy has dumped more than 20 billion gallons of coal ash and smokestack scrubber waste into the body of water which has contaminated local water supplies with <a href="/wiki/Arsenic" title="Arsenic">arsenic</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sulfates" class="mw-redirect" title="Sulfates">sulfates</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sodium" title="Sodium">sodium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Calcium" title="Calcium">calcium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Magnesium" title="Magnesium">magnesium</a> and <a href="/wiki/Chloride" title="Chloride">chloride</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Pittsburgh_Post-Gazette_45-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pittsburgh_Post-Gazette-45">[45]</a></sup></p><p>A July 2012 consent decree from the <a href="/wiki/Pennsylvania_Department_of_Environmental_Protection" title="Pennsylvania Department of Environmental Protection">Pennsylvania Department of Environmental Protection</a> is forcing FirstEnergy to close the Little Blue Run Lake, an unlined waste impoundment in <a href="/wiki/Beaver_County,_Pennsylvania" title="Beaver County, Pennsylvania">Beaver County, Pennsylvania</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hancock_County,_West_Virginia" title="Hancock County, West Virginia">Hancock County, West Virginia</a>. <a href="/wiki/Coal_ash" class="mw-redirect" title="Coal ash">Coal ash</a> waste slurry has been piped there from FirstEnergy's <a href="/wiki/Bruce_Mansfield_Power_Plant" title="Bruce Mansfield Power Plant">Bruce Mansfield Power Plant</a> since 1974. The reservoir at Little Blue Run is the country's largest <a href="/wiki/Coal_slurry" title="Coal slurry">coal ash impoundment</a>. Pollutants including <a href="/wiki/Sulfates" class="mw-redirect" title="Sulfates">sulfates</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chloride" title="Chloride">chlorides</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Arsenic" title="Arsenic">arsenic</a> have been found in groundwater nearby. FirstEnergy must stop dumping coal ash at the site by 2016, pay a penalty of $800,000, provide clean water to local residents, and do environmental monitoring of seeps for toxic pollutants including <a href="/wiki/Selenium" title="Selenium">selenium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Boron" title="Boron">boron</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Arsenic" title="Arsenic">arsenic</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-PG20120727_46-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PG20120727-46">[46]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-DEP-LBR-consent_47-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DEP-LBR-consent-47">[47]</a></sup></p>

</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(6)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Notable_accidents_and_incidents">Notable accidents and incidents</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=FirstEnergy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Notable accidents and incidents" data-section="17" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-6 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-6">
<ul><li>The <a href="/wiki/2003_North_American_blackout" class="mw-redirect" title="2003 North American blackout">2003 North American blackout</a> was attributed partly to FirstEnergy's failure to trim the trees around its high voltage lines in a certain sector of Ohio; heat and extreme power needs caused the lines to sag, coming into contact with the trees and causing <a href="/wiki/Electric_arc" title="Electric arc">flashover</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup></li>
<li>On Friday, January 20, 2006, FirstEnergy acknowledged a cover-up of serious safety violations by former workers at the <a href="/wiki/Davis-Besse_Nuclear_Power_Station" class="mw-redirect" title="Davis-Besse Nuclear Power Station">Davis-Besse Nuclear Power Station</a>, and accepted a <a href="/wiki/Plea_bargain" title="Plea bargain">plea bargain</a> with the <a href="/wiki/U.S._Department_of_Justice" class="mw-redirect" title="U.S. Department of Justice">U.S. Department of Justice</a> in lieu of possible federal criminal prosecution.  The plea bargain relates to the March 2002 discovery of severe <a href="/wiki/Corrosion" title="Corrosion">corrosion</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Pressure_vessel" title="Pressure vessel">pressure vessel</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_reactor" title="Nuclear reactor">nuclear reactor</a>, contained within the plant's <a href="/wiki/Containment_building" title="Containment building">containment building</a>.  In the agreement, the company agreed to pay fines of $23 million, with an additional $5 million to be contributed toward research on alternative energy sources and to <a href="/wiki/Habitat_for_Humanity" title="Habitat for Humanity">Habitat for Humanity</a> as well as to pay for costs related to the Federal investigation.  In addition, two former employees and one former contractor were indicted for purposely deceiving <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_Regulatory_Commission" title="Nuclear Regulatory Commission">Nuclear Regulatory Commission</a> (NRC) inspectors in multiple documents (including one videotape) over several years, hiding evidence that the reactor pressure vessel was being seriously corroded by <a href="/wiki/Boric_acid" title="Boric acid">boric acid</a>.  The maximum penalty for the three is 25 years in prison.  The indictment also cites other employees as providing false information to inspectors, but does not name them.</li>
<li>In 2005, the NRC identified two earlier incidents at <a href="/wiki/Davis-Besse_Nuclear_Power_Station" class="mw-redirect" title="Davis-Besse Nuclear Power Station">Davis-Besse</a> as being among the top five events (excluding the actual disaster at Three Mile Island) most likely to have resulted in a nuclear disaster in the event of a subsequent failure.<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup></li></ul></section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(7)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=FirstEnergy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: See also" data-section="18" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-7 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-7">
