Morgan &amp; Co.">J.P. Morgan &amp; Co.</a> partners <a href="/wiki/Henry_Sturgis_Morgan" title="Henry Sturgis Morgan">Henry Sturgis Morgan</a> (grandson of <a href="/wiki/J.P._Morgan" class="mw-redirect" title="J.P. Morgan">J.P. Morgan</a>), <a href="/wiki/Harold_Stanley" title="Harold Stanley">Harold Stanley</a>, and others, came into existence on September 16, 1935, in response to the <a href="/wiki/Glass%E2%80%93Steagall_Act" class="mw-redirect" title="Glass–Steagall Act">Glass–Steagall Act</a> that required the splitting of commercial and investment banking businesses.<sup id="cite_ref-:3_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:3-5">[5]</a></sup> In its first year, the company operated with a 24% market share (US$1.1 billion) in <a href="/wiki/Public_offering" title="Public offering">public offerings</a> and <a href="/wiki/Private_placement" title="Private placement">private placements</a>.
</p><p>The current Morgan Stanley is the result of merger of the original Morgan Stanley with <a href="/wiki/Dean_Witter_Reynolds" title="Dean Witter Reynolds">Dean Witter Discover &amp; Co.</a> in 1997.<sup id="cite_ref-:3_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:3-5">[5]</a></sup> Dean Witter's Chairman and CEO, <a href="/wiki/Philip_J._Purcell" title="Philip J. Purcell">Philip J. Purcell</a>, became the Chairman and CEO of the newly merged "Morgan Stanley Dean Witter Discover &amp; Co." <sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> The new firm changed its name back to "Morgan Stanley" in 2001.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-8">[8]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:1_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-9">[9]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:2_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:2-10">[10]</a></sup> The main areas of business for the firm today are <a href="/wiki/Institutional_investor" title="Institutional investor">institutional securities</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wealth_management" title="Wealth management">wealth management</a> and <a href="/wiki/Investment_management" title="Investment management">investment management</a>.
</p>

</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(1)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Overview">Overview</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Morgan_Stanley&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Overview" data-section="1" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-1 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-1">
<p>Morgan Stanley is a <a href="/wiki/Financial_services" title="Financial services">financial services</a> corporation that, through its affiliates and subsidiaries, advises, and originates, trades, manages, and distributes capital for institutions,  governments, and individuals. The company operates in three business segments: Institutional Securities, Wealth Management, and Investment Management.<sup id="cite_ref-10K_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10K-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Morgan_Stanley_Headquarters_(48105951892).jpg" class="image"><noscript><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/99/Morgan_Stanley_Headquarters_%2848105951892%29.jpg/220px-Morgan_Stanley_Headquarters_%2848105951892%29.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="199" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="3601" data-file-height="3260"></noscript><span class="lazy-image-placeholder" style="width: 220px;height: 199px;" data-src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/99/Morgan_Stanley_Headquarters_%2848105951892%29.jpg/220px-Morgan_Stanley_Headquarters_%2848105951892%29.jpg" data-alt="" data-width="220" data-height="199" data-class="thumbimage"> </span></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Morgan_Stanley_Headquarters_(48105951892).jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Morgan Stanley's office on <a href="/wiki/Times_Square" title="Times Square">Times Square</a></div></div></div>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(2)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Morgan_Stanley&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History" data-section="2" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-2 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-2">
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/JPMorgan_Chase" title="JPMorgan Chase">JPMorgan Chase</a> and <a href="/wiki/J.P._Morgan_%26_Co." title="J.P. Morgan &amp; Co.">J.P. Morgan &amp; Co.</a></div>
<h3 class="in-block"><span id="The_original_Morgan_Stanley_.281935.E2.80.931997.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="The_original_Morgan_Stanley_(1935–1997)">The original Morgan Stanley (1935–1997)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Morgan_Stanley&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: The original Morgan Stanley (1935–1997)" data-section="3" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>Morgan Stanley traces its roots in the history of <a href="/wiki/J.P._Morgan_%26_Co." title="J.P. Morgan &amp; Co.">J.P. Morgan &amp; Co.</a> Following the <a href="/wiki/Glass%E2%80%93Steagall_Act" class="mw-redirect" title="Glass–Steagall Act">Glass–Steagall Act</a>, it was no longer possible for a corporation to have investment banking and <a href="/wiki/Commercial_banking" class="mw-redirect" title="Commercial banking">commercial banking</a> businesses under a single holding entity. J.P. Morgan &amp; Co. chose the commercial banking business over the investment banking business. As a result, some of the employees of J.P. Morgan &amp; Co., most notably <a href="/wiki/Henry_S._Morgan" class="mw-redirect" title="Henry S. Morgan">Henry S. Morgan</a> and <a href="/wiki/Harold_Stanley" title="Harold Stanley">Harold Stanley</a>, left J.P. Morgan &amp; Co. and joined some others from the Drexel partners to form Morgan Stanley. The firm formally opened the doors for business on September 16, 1935, at Floor 19, 2 <a href="/wiki/Wall_Street" title="Wall Street">Wall Street</a>, New York City. Within its first year, it achieved 24% market share (US$1.1 billion) among public offerings. The firm was involved with the distribution of 1938 US$100 million of <a href="/wiki/Debenture" title="Debenture">debentures</a> for the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Steel_Corporation" class="mw-redirect" title="United States Steel Corporation">United States Steel Corporation</a> as the lead <a href="/wiki/Underwriter" class="mw-redirect" title="Underwriter">underwriter</a>. The firm also obtained the distinction of being the lead syndicate in the 1939 U.S. rail financing. The firm went through a major reorganization in 1941 to allow for more activity in its securities business.<sup id="cite_ref-mshistory_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mshistory-11">[11]</a></sup></p><p>The firm was led by Perry Hall, the last founder to lead Morgan Stanley, from 1951 until 1961. During this period the firm co-managed the World Bank's US$50 million triple-A-rated bonds offering of 1952, as well as coming up with <a href="/wiki/General_Motors" title="General Motors">General Motors</a>' US$300 million debt issue, US$231 million <a href="/wiki/IBM" title="IBM">IBM</a> stock offering, and the US$250 million <a href="/wiki/AT%26T_Corporation" title="AT&amp;T Corporation">AT&amp;T's</a> debt offering.<sup id="cite_ref-mshistory_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mshistory-11">[11]</a></sup></p><p>Morgan Stanley credits itself with having created the first viable computer model for financial analysis in 1962,<sup id="cite_ref-mshistory_11-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mshistory-11">[11]</a></sup> thereby starting a new trend in the field of financial analysis. Future president and chairman Dick Fisher contributed to the computer model as a young employee, learning the FORTRAN and COBOL programming languages at IBM.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> In 1967 it established the Morgan &amp; Cie, International in Paris in an attempt to enter the European securities market. It acquired Brooks, Harvey &amp; Co., Inc. in 1967 and established a presence in the real estate business. By 1971 the firm had established its Mergers &amp; Acquisitions business along with Sales &amp; Trading. The sales and trading business is believed to be the brainchild of Bob Baldwin.<sup id="cite_ref-mshistory_11-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mshistory-11">[11]</a></sup></p><p>In 1996 Morgan Stanley acquired <a href="/wiki/Van_Kampen_Investments" title="Van Kampen Investments">Van Kampen</a> American Capital.
</p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span id="Morgan_Stanley_after_the_Merger_.281997.E2.80.93present.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Morgan_Stanley_after_the_Merger_(1997–present)">Morgan Stanley after the Merger  (1997–present)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Morgan_Stanley&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Morgan Stanley after the Merger (1997–present)" data-section="4" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Morgan_Stanley_Logo_1.svg" class="image"><noscript><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/34/Morgan_Stanley_Logo_1.svg/200px-Morgan_Stanley_Logo_1.svg.png" decoding="async" width="200" height="50" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="360" data-file-height="90"></noscript><span class="lazy-image-placeholder" style="width: 200px;height: 50px;" data-src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/34/Morgan_Stanley_Logo_1.svg/200px-Morgan_Stanley_Logo_1.svg.png" data-alt="" data-width="200" data-height="50" data-srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/34/Morgan_Stanley_Logo_1.svg/300px-Morgan_Stanley_Logo_1.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/34/Morgan_Stanley_Logo_1.svg/400px-Morgan_Stanley_Logo_1.svg.png 2x" data-class="thumbimage"> </span></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Morgan_Stanley_Logo_1.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Current Morgan Stanley Logo 2013</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Morgan_Stanley_Historical_Logo.png" class="image"><noscript><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/b/bf/Morgan_Stanley_Historical_Logo.png/200px-Morgan_Stanley_Historical_Logo.png" decoding="async" width="200" height="40" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="375" data-file-height="75"></noscript><span class="lazy-image-placeholder" style="width: 200px;height: 40px;" data-src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/b/bf/Morgan_Stanley_Historical_Logo.png/200px-Morgan_Stanley_Historical_Logo.png" data-alt="" data-width="200" data-height="40" data-class="thumbimage"> </span></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Morgan_Stanley_Historical_Logo.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Historical logo used by Morgan Stanley in the early 2000s</div></div></div>
<p>On February 5, 1997 the company merged with <a href="/wiki/Dean_Witter_Reynolds" title="Dean Witter Reynolds">Dean Witter Discover &amp; Co.</a>, the spun-off financial services business of <a href="/wiki/Sears_Roebuck" class="mw-redirect" title="Sears Roebuck">Sears Roebuck</a>. Dean Witter's Chairman and CEO, <a href="/wiki/Philip_J._Purcell" title="Philip J. Purcell">Philip J. Purcell</a>, continued to hold the same roles in the newly merged "Morgan Stanley Dean Witter Discover &amp; Co.".  Originally, the name of this new firm was chosen to be the combination of the names of the two predecessor companies in order to avoid tension between executives from the two firms.<sup id="cite_ref-:1_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-9">[9]</a></sup> In 1998, the name of the firm was changed to "Morgan Stanley Dean Witter &amp; Co.".<sup id="cite_ref-:0_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-8">[8]</a></sup> Eventually in 2001 "Dean Witter" was further dropped and the name became "Morgan Stanley" for unrevealed reasons, even though in the 1990s the new company had made efforts to keep alive the image of its founder <a href="/wiki/Dean_G._Witter" title="Dean G. Witter">Dean G. Witter</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_8-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-8">[8]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:1_9-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-9">[9]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:2_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:2-10">[10]</a></sup></p><p>Morgan Stanley had offices located on 24 floors across buildings 2 and 5 of the <a href="/wiki/World_Trade_Center_(1973%E2%80%932001)" title="World Trade Center (1973–2001)">World Trade Center</a> in New York City. These offices had been inherited from Dean Witter which had occupied the space since the mid-1980s. The firm lost thirteen employees during the <a href="/wiki/September_11_attacks" title="September 11 attacks">September 11 attacks</a> in 2001 (Thomas F. Swift, Wesley Mercer, Jennifer de Jesus, Joseph DiPilato, Nolbert Salomon, Godwin Forde, Steve R. Strauss, Lindsay C. Herkness, Albert Joseph, Jorge Velazquez, Titus Davidson, Charles Laurencin and Security Director <a href="/wiki/Rick_Rescorla" title="Rick Rescorla">Rick Rescorla</a>) in the towers, while 2,687 were successfully evacuated by <a href="/wiki/Rick_Rescorla" title="Rick Rescorla">Rick Rescorla</a>. The surviving employees moved to temporary headquarters in the vicinity. In 2005 Morgan Stanley moved 2,300 of its employees back to <a href="/wiki/Lower_Manhattan" title="Lower Manhattan">lower Manhattan</a>, at that time the largest such move.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p><p>In 2003 <a href="/wiki/NewYork%E2%80%93Presbyterian_Hospital" title="NewYork–Presbyterian Hospital">NewYork–Presbyterian Hospital</a> named the <a href="/wiki/Morgan_Stanley_Children%27s_Hospital" title="Morgan Stanley Children's Hospital">Morgan Stanley Children's Hospital</a> in recognition of the firm's sponsorship of the hospital, which largely funded its construction through philanthropy.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> started under CEO <a href="/wiki/Philip_J._Purcell" title="Philip J. Purcell">Philip J. Purcell</a> and completed under <a href="/wiki/John_J._Mack" title="John J. Mack">John Mack</a>. Employees at the firm have been involved with the hospital since the 1990s and personally donated to the construction of the current child-friendly building, which opened in November 2003.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p><p>The company found itself in the midst of a management crisis starting in March 2005<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> that resulted in a loss of a number of the firm's staff.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> Purcell resigned as CEO of Morgan Stanley in June 2005 when a highly public campaign against him by former Morgan Stanley partners<sup id="cite_ref-PJP-B-05_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PJP-B-05-19">[19]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-PJP-B-08_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PJP-B-08-20">[20]</a></sup> threatened to disrupt and damage the firm and challenged his refusal to aggressively increase leverage, increase risk, enter the sub-prime mortgage business and make expensive acquisitions, the same strategies that forced Morgan Stanley into massive write-downs, related to the <a href="/wiki/Subprime_mortgage_crisis" title="Subprime mortgage crisis">subprime mortgage crisis</a>, by 2007.<sup id="cite_ref-PJP-B-03_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PJP-B-03-21">[21]</a></sup></p><p>On December 19, 2006, after reporting 4th quarter earnings, Morgan Stanley announced the spin-off of its <a href="/wiki/Discover_Card" title="Discover Card">Discover Card</a> unit. The bank completed the spinoff of Discover Financial on June 30, 2007.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p><p>In order to cope with the write-downs during the subprime mortgage crisis, Morgan Stanley announced on December 19, 2007 that it would receive a US$5 billion capital infusion from the <a href="/wiki/China_Investment_Corporation" title="China Investment Corporation">China Investment Corporation</a> in exchange for securities that would be convertible to 9.9% of its shares in 2010.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p><p>The bank's Process Driven Trading unit was amongst several on Wall Street caught in a <a href="/wiki/Short_squeeze" title="Short squeeze">short squeeze</a>, reportedly losing nearly $300 million in one day. One of the stocks involved in this squeeze, <a href="/wiki/Beazer_Homes_USA" title="Beazer Homes USA">Beazer Homes USA</a>, was a component of the then-bulging <a href="/wiki/Real_estate_bubble" title="Real estate bubble">real estate bubble</a>. The bubble's subsequent collapse was considered to be a central feature of the <a href="/wiki/Financial_crisis_of_2007%E2%80%932010" class="mw-redirect" title="Financial crisis of 2007–2010">financial crisis of 2007–2010</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p><p>The bank was contracted by the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Treasury" class="mw-redirect" title="United States Treasury">United States Treasury</a> in August 2008 to advise the government on potential rescue strategies for <a href="/wiki/Fannie_Mae" title="Fannie Mae">Fannie Mae</a> and <a href="/wiki/Freddie_Mac" title="Freddie Mac">Freddie Mac</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> Within days, Morgan Stanley itself was at risk of failure, with rapidly changing prospects, regulatory model and ownership stakes over the course of four weeks from mid-September to mid-October 2008.
</p><p>To set the context: Morgan Stanley is said to have lost over 80% of its market value between 2007 and 2008 during the financial crisis.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> On September 17, 2008, the British evening-news analysis program <i><a href="/wiki/Newsnight" title="Newsnight">Newsnight</a></i> reported that Morgan Stanley was facing difficulties after a 42% slide in its share price in two days. CEO <a href="/wiki/John_J._Mack" title="John J. Mack">John J. Mack</a> wrote in a memo to staff "we're in the midst of a market controlled by fear and rumours and <a href="/wiki/Short_(finance)" title="Short (finance)">short-sellers</a> are driving our stock down." By September 19, 2008, the share price had slid 57% in four days, and the company was said to have explored merger possibilities with <a href="/wiki/CITIC" class="mw-redirect" title="CITIC">CITIC</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wachovia" title="Wachovia">Wachovia</a>, <a href="/wiki/HSBC" title="HSBC">HSBC</a>, <a href="/wiki/Standard_Chartered" title="Standard Chartered">Standard Chartered</a>, <a href="/wiki/Banco_Santander" title="Banco Santander">Banco Santander</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nomura_Securities" title="Nomura Securities">Nomura</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> At one point, <a href="/wiki/Hank_Paulson" class="mw-redirect" title="Hank Paulson">Hank Paulson</a> offered Morgan Stanley to <a href="/wiki/JPMorgan_Chase" title="JPMorgan Chase">JPMorgan Chase</a> at no cost, but JPMorgan's <a href="/wiki/Jamie_Dimon" title="Jamie Dimon">Jamie Dimon</a> refused the offer.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p><p>Morgan Stanley and <a href="/wiki/Goldman_Sachs" title="Goldman Sachs">Goldman Sachs</a>, the last two major investment banks in the US, both announced on September 22, 2008 that they would become traditional <a href="/wiki/Bank_holding_company" title="Bank holding company">bank holding companies</a> regulated by the <a href="/wiki/Federal_Reserve" title="Federal Reserve">Federal Reserve</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> The Federal Reserve's approval of their bid to become banks ended the ascendancy of securities firms, 75 years after Congress separated them from deposit-taking lenders, and capped weeks of chaos that sent <a href="/wiki/Lehman_Brothers" title="Lehman Brothers">Lehman Brothers Holdings Inc.</a> into bankruptcy and led to the rushed sale of <a href="/wiki/Merrill_Lynch" class="mw-redirect" title="Merrill Lynch">Merrill Lynch &amp; Co.</a> to <a href="/wiki/Bank_of_America" title="Bank of America">Bank of America Corp.</a><sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p><p><a href="/wiki/Mitsubishi_UFJ_Financial_Group" title="Mitsubishi UFJ Financial Group">Mitsubishi UFJ Financial Group</a>, Japan's largest bank, invested $9 billion in a direct purchase of a 21% ownership stake in Morgan Stanley on September 29, 2008.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> This may have been the largest check ever written.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> Concerns over the completion of the Mitsubishi deal during the October 2008 stock market volatility caused a dramatic fall in Morgan Stanley's stock price to levels last seen in 1994. It recovered once Mitsubishi UFJ's 21% stake in Morgan Stanley was completed on October 14, 2008.<sup id="cite_ref-MS-TMSF-01_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MS-TMSF-01-33">[33]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-MS-TMSF-02_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MS-TMSF-02-34">[34]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-MS-TMSF-03_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MS-TMSF-03-35">[35]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-MS-TMSF-04_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MS-TMSF-04-36">[36]</a></sup></p><p>Morgan Stanley borrowed $107.3 billion from the Fed during the 2008 crisis, the most of any bank, according to data compiled by Bloomberg News Service and published August 22, 2011.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></p><p>In 2009, Morgan Stanley purchased <a href="/wiki/Smith_Barney" class="mw-redirect" title="Smith Barney">Smith Barney</a> from <a href="/wiki/Citigroup" title="Citigroup">Citigroup</a> and the new broker-dealer operates under the name <a href="/wiki/Morgan_Stanley_Smith_Barney" class="mw-redirect" title="Morgan Stanley Smith Barney">Morgan Stanley Smith Barney</a>, the largest wealth management business in the world.
</p><p>In November 2013, Morgan Stanley announced that it would invest $1 billion to help improve affordable housing as part of a wider push to encourage investment in efforts that aid economic, social and environmental sustainability.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></p><p>In July 2014, Morgan Stanley's Asian private equity arm announced it had raised around $1.7 billion for its fourth fund in the area.<sup id="cite_ref-The_Wall_Street_Journal_39-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-The_Wall_Street_Journal-39">[39]</a></sup></p><p>In December 2015, it was reported that Morgan Stanley would be cutting around 25 percent of its fixed income jobs before month end.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> In January 2016, the company reported that it had offices in more than 43 countries.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup></p><p>On February 20th, 2020, Morgan Stanley announced that it would purchase <a href="/wiki/E-Trade" title="E-Trade">E-Trade</a> for about $13 billion. <sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup></p>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(3)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Organization">Organization</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Morgan_Stanley&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Organization" data-section="5" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-3 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-3">
<p>Morgan Stanley splits its businesses into three business units. As listed below:
</p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Institutional_Securities_Group">Institutional Securities Group</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Morgan_Stanley&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Institutional Securities Group" data-section="6" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>Morgan Stanley's Institutional Securities has been the most profitable business segment<sup id="cite_ref-morganstanley.com_43-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-morganstanley.com-43">[43]</a></sup> for Morgan Stanley in recent times. This business segment provides institutions with services such as capital raising and financial advisory services including <a href="/wiki/Mergers_and_acquisitions" title="Mergers and acquisitions">mergers and acquisitions</a> advisory, restructurings, real estate and project finance, and corporate lending. The segment also encompasses the Equities and the Fixed Income divisions of the firm; trading is anticipated to maintain its position as the "engine room" of the company.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> Among the major U.S. banks, Morgan Stanley sources the highest portion of revenues from fixed income underwriting which was reported at 6.0% of total revenue in FY12.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Wealth_Management">Wealth Management</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Morgan_Stanley&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Wealth Management" data-section="7" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>The Global Wealth Management Group provides <a href="/wiki/Stockbroker" title="Stockbroker">stockbrokerage</a> and investment advisory services. As of 2014 Q2 this segment had reported an annual increase of 21 percent in pre-tax income.<sup id="cite_ref-morganstanley.com_43-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-morganstanley.com-43">[43]</a></sup> This segment provides financial and wealth planning services to its clients, who are primarily high-net-worth individuals.
</p><p>On January 13, 2009, the Global Wealth Management Group was merged with Citi's Smith Barney to form the joint venture Morgan Stanley Smith Barney. Morgan Stanley holds 51% of the entity, and Citi holds 49%.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> As of May 31, 2012, Morgan Stanley planned to purchase an additional 14% of the joint venture from Citi.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> In June 2013, Morgan Stanley stated it had secured all regulatory approvals to buy Citigroup's remaining 35% stake in Smith Barney and would proceed to finalize the deal.<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Investment_Management">Investment Management</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Morgan_Stanley&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Investment Management" data-section="8" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Investment_Management" class="mw-redirect" title="Investment Management">Investment Management</a> provides asset management products and services in equity, fixed income, alternative investments, real estate investment, and <a href="/wiki/Private_equity" title="Private equity">private equity</a> to institutional and retail clients through third-party retail distribution channels, intermediaries and Morgan Stanley's institutional distribution channel. Morgan Stanley's asset management activities were principally conducted under the Morgan Stanley and Van Kampen brands until 2009.
</p><p>On October 19, 2009, Morgan Stanley announced that it would sell Van Kampen to <a href="/wiki/Invesco" title="Invesco">Invesco</a> for $1.5 billion, but would retain the Morgan Stanley brand.<sup id="cite_ref-Van_Kampen_Sale_49-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Van_Kampen_Sale-49">[49]</a></sup> It provides asset management products and services to institutional investors worldwide, including pension plans, corporations, private funds, non-profit organizations, foundations, endowments, governmental agencies, insurance companies and banks.
</p><p>On September 29, 2013, Morgan Stanley announced a partnership with Longchamp Asset Management, a French-based asset manager that specialises in the distribution of UCITS hedge funds, and La Française AM, a multi-specialist asset manager with a 10-year track record in alternative investments.<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup></p>

<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="2003">2003</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Morgan_Stanley&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: 2003" data-section="11" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>In 2003, Morgan Stanley agreed to pay $125 million to settle its portion of a $1.4 billion settlement of a suit brought by <a href="/wiki/Eliot_Spitzer" title="Eliot Spitzer">Eliot Spitzer</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Attorney_General_of_New_York" title="Attorney General of New York">Attorney General of New York</a>, the National Association of Securities Dealers (now the <a href="/wiki/Financial_Industry_Regulatory_Authority" title="Financial Industry Regulatory Authority">Financial Industry Regulatory Authority</a> (FINRA), the United States <a href="/wiki/Securities_and_Exchange_Commission" class="mw-redirect" title="Securities and Exchange Commission">Securities and Exchange Commission</a> (SEC), and a number of state securities regulators, relating to intentionally misleading research motivated by a desire to win investment banking business with the companies covered.<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="2004">2004</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Morgan_Stanley&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: 2004" data-section="12" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>In June 2004, the <a href="/wiki/New_York_Stock_Exchange" title="New York Stock Exchange">New York Stock Exchange</a> (NYSE) imposed a penalty of a censure and $140,000 fine for incorrectly using customers' margined securities as collateral for cash management loans.<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup></p><p>In 2004, Morgan Stanley settled a sex discrimination suit brought by the <a href="/wiki/Equal_Employment_Opportunity_Commission" title="Equal Employment Opportunity Commission">Equal Employment Opportunity Commission</a> for $54 million.<sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup>  In 2007, the firm agreed to pay $46 million to settle a class action lawsuit brought by eight female brokers.<sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup></p><p>In July 2004, the firm paid NASD a $2.2 million fine for more than 1,800 late disclosures of reportable information about its brokers.<sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup></p><p>In September 2004, the firm paid a $19 million fine imposed by NYSE for failure to deliver prospectuses to customers in registered offerings, inaccurate reporting of certain program trading information, short sale violations, failures to fingerprint new employees and failure to timely file exchange forms.<sup id="cite_ref-59" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup></p><p>In December 2004, the firm paid a $100,000 fine to NASD and $211,510 in restitution to customers for failure to make proper disclosures to municipal bond investors. In the course of NASD's investigation, Morgan Stanley's failure to make a timely response to requests for information resulted in censure and an additional $25,000 fine.<sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="2005">2005</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Morgan_Stanley&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: 2005" data-section="13" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>The New York Stock Exchange imposed a $19 million fine on January 12, 2005 for alleged regulatory and supervisory lapses.  At the time, it was the largest fine ever imposed by the NYSE.<sup id="cite_ref-61" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup></p><p>On May 16, 2005, a Florida jury found that Morgan Stanley failed to give adequate information to <a href="/wiki/Ronald_Perelman" title="Ronald Perelman">Ronald Perelman</a> about <a href="/wiki/Sunbeam_Products" title="Sunbeam Products">Sunbeam</a> thereby defrauding him and causing damages to him of $604 million. In addition, punitive damages were added for total <a href="/wiki/Damages" title="Damages">damages</a> of $1.450 billion. This verdict was directed by the judge as a sanction against Morgan Stanley after the firm's attorneys infuriated the court by failing and refusing to produce documents, and falsely telling the court that certain documents did not exist.<sup id="cite_ref-62" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup> The ruling was overturned on March 21, 2007 and Morgan Stanley was no longer required to pay the $1.57 billion verdict.<sup id="cite_ref-63" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="2006">2006</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Morgan_Stanley&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: 2006" data-section="14" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>Morgan Stanley settled a class action lawsuit on March 2, 2006. It had been filed in California by both current and former Morgan Stanley employees for unfair labor practices instituted to those in the financial advisor training program. Employees of the program had claimed the firm expected trainees to clock overtime hours without additional pay and handle various administrative expenses as a result of their expected duties. A $42.5 million settlement was reached and Morgan Stanley admitted no fault.<sup id="cite_ref-64" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup></p><p>In May the firm agreed to pay a $15 million fine. The Securities and Exchange Commission accused the firm of deleting emails and failing to cooperate with SEC investigators.<sup id="cite_ref-65" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup></p><p>On September 25, 2009, Citigroup Inc. filed a federal lawsuit against Morgan Stanley, claiming its rival failed to pay $245 million due under a credit default swap agreement. The breach-of-contract lawsuit was filed in Manhattan federal court and seeks unspecified damages.<sup id="cite_ref-66" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="2007">2007</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Morgan_Stanley&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: 2007" data-section="15" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Financial_Industry_Regulatory_Authority" title="Financial Industry Regulatory Authority">Financial Industry Regulatory Authority</a> (FINRA) announced a $12.5 million settlement with Morgan Stanley on September 27, 2007. This resolved charges that the firm's former affiliate, Morgan Stanley DW, Inc. (MSDW), failed on numerous occasions to provide emails to claimants in arbitration proceedings as well as to regulators. The company had claimed that the destruction of the firm's email servers in the September 11, 2001 terrorist attacks on New York's World Trade Center resulted in the loss of all email before that date. In fact, the firm had millions of earlier emails that had been retrieved from <a href="/wiki/Off-site_data_protection" title="Off-site data protection">backup copies stored in another location</a> that was not destroyed in the attacks.<sup id="cite_ref-67" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup> Customers who had lost their arbitration cases against Morgan Stanley DW Inc. because of their inability to obtain these emails to demonstrate Morgan Stanley's misconduct received a token amount of money as a result of the settlement.
</p><p>In July 2007, Morgan Stanley agreed to pay $4.4 million to settle a class-action lawsuit.  The firm was accused of incorrectly charging clients for storage of precious metals.<sup id="cite_ref-68" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup></p><p>In August 2007, Morgan Stanley was fined $1.5 million and paid $4.6 million in restitution to customers related to excessive mark-ups in 2,800 transactions. An employee was charged $40,000 and suspended for 15 days.<sup id="cite_ref-69" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="2008">2008</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Morgan_Stanley&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: 2008" data-section="16" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>Under a settlement with New York Attorney General Andrew M. Cuomo, the firm agreed to repurchase approximately $4.5 billion worth of <a href="/wiki/Auction_rate_security" title="Auction rate security">auction rate securities</a>.  The firm was accused of misrepresenting auction rate securities in their sales and marketing.<sup id="cite_ref-70" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="2009">2009</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Morgan_Stanley&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: 2009" data-section="17" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>In March 2009, FINRA announced Morgan Stanley was to pay more than $7 million for misconduct in the handling of the accounts of 90 Rochester, New York-area retirees.  
<sup id="cite_ref-71" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup></p><p>In May 2009, a trader at the firm was suspended by the FSA for a series of unauthorized commodities trades entered after becoming intoxicated during a three and half hour lunch.<sup id="cite_ref-72" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-72">[72]</a></sup> A week later another trader at the firm was banned for deliberately disadvantaging clients by 'pre-hedging' trades without their consent.<sup id="cite_ref-73" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-73">[73]</a></sup></p><p>The Financial Services Authority fined the firm £1.4m for failing to use controls properly relating to the actions of a rogue trader on one of its trading desks.  Morgan Stanley admitted on June 18, 2008, this resulted in a $120m loss for the firm.<sup id="cite_ref-74" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-74">[74]</a></sup></p><p>Morgan Stanley managing director Du Jun was convicted of <a href="/wiki/Insider_trading" title="Insider trading">insider trading</a> after a criminal trial in Hong Kong.  Mr. Du was accused of buying 26.7 million shares of Citic Resource Holdings while in possession of confidential information about the company.  He gained this information as part of a Morgan Stanley team working with the company on a bond issuance and the purchase of an oil field in Kazakhstan. Morgan Stanley's compliance department was criticized for failing to detect Mr. Du's illegal trades.<sup id="cite_ref-75" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-75">[75]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="2010">2010</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Morgan_Stanley&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: 2010" data-section="18" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>In April, the Commodity Futures Trading Commission announced the firm agreed to pay $14 million related to an attempt to hide prohibited trading activity in oil futures.<sup id="cite_ref-76" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-76">[76]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="2011">2011</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Morgan_Stanley&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: 2011" data-section="19" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>A Morgan Stanley trader was barred from the brokerage industry and fined for entering fake trades to fool firm risk management systems, causing millions in losses.<sup id="cite_ref-77" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-77">[77]</a></sup></p><p>The Department of Justice sought a $4.8 million fine from Morgan Stanley for its part in an electricity price-fixing scandal.  Con Edison estimated that the crime cost New York State consumers about $300 million.  Morgan Stanley earned revenues of $21.6 million from the fraud.<sup id="cite_ref-78" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-78">[78]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="2012">2012</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Morgan_Stanley&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: 2012" data-section="20" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>On April 3, the <a href="/wiki/Federal_Reserve" title="Federal Reserve">Federal Reserve</a> announced a consent order against the firm for "a pattern of misconduct and negligence in residential mortgage loan servicing and foreclosure processing." The consent order required the firm to review foreclosure proceedings conducted by the firm. The firm was also responsible for monetary sanctions.
<sup id="cite_ref-79" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-79">[79]</a></sup></p><p>Garth R. Peterson, one of Morgan Stanley's highest-ranking real estate executives in China, pleaded guilty on April 25 to violating U.S. federal anticorruption laws. He was charged with secretly acquiring millions of dollars' worth of property investments for himself and a Chinese government official.  The official steered business to Morgan Stanley.<sup id="cite_ref-80" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-80">[80]</a></sup></p><p>Morgan Stanley was fined $55,000 by <a href="/wiki/Nasdaq_OMX" class="mw-redirect" title="Nasdaq OMX">Nasdaq OMX</a> for three violations of exchange rules. A Morgan Stanley client algorithm started buying and selling enormous volumes by mistake.  Furthermore, after the exchange detected the error, they were unable to contact the employee responsible.<sup id="cite_ref-81" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-81">[81]</a></sup></p><p>Morgan Stanley settled a claim from FINRA and paid restitution totaling almost $2.4 million. Morgan Stanley was accused of improperly supervising and training financial advisors in the use of non-traditional ETF products. This resulted in inappropriate recommendations to several retail brokerage customers.<sup id="cite_ref-82" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-82">[82]</a></sup></p><p>Morgan Stanley is facing lawsuits and government investigation surrounding the <a href="/wiki/Facebook" title="Facebook">Facebook</a> IPO.  It was claimed that Morgan Stanley downgraded their earnings forecasts for the company while conducting the IPO roadshow.  Allegedly, they passed this information to only a handful of <a href="/wiki/Institutional_investor" title="Institutional investor">institutional investors</a>. "The allegations, if true, are a matter of regulatory concern" to FINRA and the SEC according to FINRA Chairman <a href="/wiki/Richard_Ketchum" title="Richard Ketchum">Richard Ketchum</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-83" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-83">[83]</a></sup></p><p>Morgan Stanley agreed to pay a $5 million fine to the <a href="/wiki/Commodity_Futures_Trading_Commission" title="Commodity Futures Trading Commission">Commodity Futures Trading Commission</a> and an additional $1.75 million to <a href="/wiki/Chicago_Mercantile_Exchange" title="Chicago Mercantile Exchange">CME</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Chicago_Board_of_Trade" title="Chicago Board of Trade">Chicago Board of Trade</a>. Morgan Stanley employees improperly executed fictitious sales in Eurodollar and Treasury Note futures contracts.<sup id="cite_ref-84" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-84">[84]</a></sup></p><p>On August 7, 2012, it was announced that Morgan Stanley would pay $4.8 million in fines to settle a price fixing scandal, which had been estimated to have cost New Yorkers $300 million to date. Morgan Stanley made no admission of any wrongdoing; however, the Justice Department commented that they hoped this would "send a message to the banking industry".<sup id="cite_ref-85" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-85">[85]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="2013">2013</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Morgan_Stanley&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: 2013" data-section="21" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>In <i><a href="/wiki/Chip_Skowron" title="Chip Skowron">Morgan Stanley v. Skowron</a></i>, 989 F. Supp. 2d 356 (S.D.N.Y. 2013), applying New York's <a href="/wiki/Faithless_servant" title="Faithless servant">faithless servant</a> doctrine to a case involving Morgan Stanley's hedge fund subsidiary, <a href="/wiki/United_States_federal_judge" title="United States federal judge">United States District Judge</a> <a href="/wiki/Shira_Scheindlin" title="Shira Scheindlin">Shira Scheindlin</a> held that a <a href="/wiki/Hedge_fund" title="Hedge fund">hedge fund</a>'s employee engaging in <a href="/wiki/Insider_trading" title="Insider trading">insider trading</a> in violation of his company's <a href="/wiki/Company_code_of_conduct" title="Company code of conduct">code of conduct</a>, which also required him to report his misconduct, must repay his employer the full $31 million his employer paid him as compensation during his period of faithlessness.<sup id="cite_ref-auto6_86-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-auto6-86">[86]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-auto4_87-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-auto4-87">[87]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-88" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-88">[88]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-89" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-89">[89]</a></sup> Judge Scheindlin called the insider trading the "ultimate abuse of a <a href="/wiki/Portfolio_manager" title="Portfolio manager">portfolio manager</a>'s position."<sup id="cite_ref-auto4_87-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-auto4-87">[87]</a></sup> The judge also wrote: ""In addition to exposing Morgan Stanley to government investigations and direct financial losses, Skowron's behavior damaged the firm's reputation, a valuable corporate asset."<sup id="cite_ref-auto4_87-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-auto4-87">[87]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="2014">2014</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Morgan_Stanley&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: 2014" data-section="22" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>In February, Morgan Stanley agreed to pay $1.25 billion to the US government, as a penalty for concealing the full risk associated with mortgage securities with the <a href="/wiki/Federal_Housing_Finance_Agency" title="Federal Housing Finance Agency">Federal Housing Finance Agency</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-90" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-90">[90]</a></sup></p><p>In September 2014, Morgan Stanley agreed to pay $95 million to resolve a lawsuit by the Public Employees' Retirement System of Mississippi (MissPERS) and the West Virginia Investment Management Board.  Morgan Stanley was accused of misleading investors in mortgage-backed securities.<sup id="cite_ref-91" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-91">[91]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="2015">2015</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Morgan_Stanley&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: 2015" data-section="23" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>In May 2015, Morgan Stanley was fined $2 million for short-interest reporting and rule violations for more than six years, by the Financial Industry Regulatory Authority.
<sup id="cite_ref-92" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-92">[92]</a></sup></p><p>In June, the Financial Industry Regulatory Authority announced that it fined Morgan Stanley Smith Barney, LLC (Morgan Stanley) $650,000 for failing to implement reasonable supervisory systems to monitor the transmittal of customer funds to third-party accounts.
<sup id="cite_ref-93" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-93">[93]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="2016">2016</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Morgan_Stanley&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: 2016" data-section="24" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>February 2016, Morgan Stanley will pay $3.2 billion to settle with state and federal authorities over Morgan Stanley's creation of mortgage-backed bonds before the financial crisis.
<sup id="cite_ref-94" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-94">[94]</a></sup></p><p>August 2016, Morgan Stanley Hong Kong Securities Ltd. was fined HK$18.5 million ($2.4 million) by Hong Kong's securities regulator, Securities and Futures Commission, for violations of Hong Kong's Code of Conduct. Included was Morgan Stanley's failure to avoid a conflict of interest between principal and agency trading.<sup id="cite_ref-95" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-95">[95]</a></sup></p><p>December 2016, another unit of Morgan Stanley paid $7.5 million to settle customer protection rule violations.<sup id="cite_ref-reuters1_96-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-reuters1-96">[96]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="2017">2017</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Morgan_Stanley&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: 2017" data-section="25" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>In January 2017, the corporation was fined $13 million due to overbilling and violating investor asset safeguarding custody rules. Morgan Stanley agreed to pay the fine without commenting on the charges.<sup id="cite_ref-reuters1_96-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-reuters1-96">[96]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="2018">2018</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Morgan_Stanley&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: 2018" data-section="26" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>Douglas E. Greenberg, a broker, was fired in 2018 after it was reported that four women from <a href="/wiki/Lake_Oswego,_Oregon" title="Lake Oswego, Oregon">Lake Oswego, Oregon</a>, had sought police protection against him over a 15-year period on allegations of harassment, threats, and assault.<sup id="cite_ref-NYT_97-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NYT-97">[97]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Post_98-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Post-98">[98]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Live_99-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Live-99">[99]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-100" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-100">[100]</a></sup> According to the report, Morgan Stanley executives were aware of the allegations, and knew of at least two arrests and a federal <a href="/wiki/Subpoena" title="Subpoena">subpoena</a> against him, but did not take any action.<sup id="cite_ref-NYT_97-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NYT-97">[97]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-101" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-101">[101]</a></sup> The story was called a <a href="/wiki/Me_Too_movement" title="Me Too movement">#MeToo</a> moment for Portland's financial service industry.<sup id="cite_ref-Live_99-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Live-99">[99]</a></sup> He managed tens of millions of dollars, and had made the 2018 <i>Forbes</i> list for top wealth advisors in Oregon.<sup id="cite_ref-NYT_97-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NYT-97">[97]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-102" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-102">[102]</a></sup></p><p>In December 2018, FINRA announced a $10 million fine against Morgan Stanley for failures in its anti-money laundering compliance. Morgan Stanley violated the Bank Secrecy Act over a period of five years.  <sup id="cite_ref-103" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-103">[103]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="2019">2019</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Morgan_Stanley&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: 2019" data-section="27" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>In April 2019, Morgan Stanley agreed to pay $150 million to settle charges that it had mislead two large California public pension funds about the risks of mortgage-backed securities.<sup id="cite_ref-104" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-104">[104]</a></sup> California Attorney General Xavier Becerra commented "Morgan Stanley lied about the risk of its products and put profits over teachers and public employees who relied on its advice." Morgan Stanley denied wrongdoing. <sup id="cite_ref-105" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-105">[105]</a></sup></p><p>Morgan Stanley also paid $382,500 for failure to supervise a broker's trading activity over a four-year period. <sup id="cite_ref-106" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-106">[106]</a></sup> The firm paid $225,000 for transactions where customers sold one municipal bond while purchasing another municipal bond that was nearly identical to the bond sold or provided no apparent economic benefit to the customer. These transactions generated $340,000 in commissions and fees for Morgan Stanley.<sup id="cite_ref-107" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-107">[107]</a></sup></p><p>Morgan Stanley paid a $1.5 million fine to settle SEC claims that it put client money into more expensive mutual fund share classes when cheaper options were available despite representations to clients that it used tools to find the least costly option.<sup id="cite_ref-108" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-108">[108]</a></sup></p>
