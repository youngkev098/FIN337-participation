			<div id="bodyContent" class="content"><div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><script>function mfTempOpenSection(id){var block=document.getElementById("mf-section-"+id);block.className+=" open-block";block.previousSibling.className+=" open-block";}</script><div class="mw-parser-output"><section class="mf-section-0" id="mf-section-0"><p><b>Hess Corporation</b> (formerly <b>Amerada Hess Corporation</b>) is an American global independent <a href="/wiki/Energy_company" class="mw-redirect" title="Energy company">energy company</a> engaged in the <a href="/wiki/Oil_exploration" class="mw-redirect" title="Oil exploration">exploration</a> and production of crude oil and <a href="/wiki/Natural_gas" title="Natural gas">natural gas</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> It was formed by the merger of <a href="/wiki/Hess_Oil_and_Chemical" title="Hess Oil and Chemical">Hess Oil and Chemical</a> and Amerada Petroleum in 1968 led by <a href="/wiki/Leon_Hess" title="Leon Hess">Leon Hess</a>. In 1995, his son <a href="/wiki/John_B._Hess" title="John B. Hess">John B Hess</a> succeeded him as chairman and CEO. Hess, headquartered in New York City, placed #394 in the 2016 list of Fortune 500 corporations.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> In 2014, Hess completed a multi-year transformation to an exploration and production company by exiting all downstream operations, generating approximately $13 billion from assets sales beginning in 2013.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Hess sold its gas station network to <a href="/wiki/Marathon_Petroleum" title="Marathon Petroleum">Marathon Petroleum</a> (which operates under the retail brand <a href="/wiki/Speedway_LLC" title="Speedway LLC">Speedway</a>); sold its wholesale and retail oil, natural gas and electricity marketing business to Direct Energy; closed its refineries in Port Reading NJ and St. Croix USVI (Hovensa JV with PDVSA); sold its bulk storage and terminalling business mostly to Buckeye Partners; and sold its 50% interests in two New Jersey power plants to their respective JV partners (Bayonne Energy Center: ArcLight Capital and Newark Energy Center: Ares EIF). Hess also sold its 50% interest in its JV commodities trading arm HETCO (Hess Energy Trading Company) to Oaktree Capital. </section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(1)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hess_Corporation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History" data-section="1" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-1 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-1">
<p>In 1919, British oil entrepreneur <a href="/wiki/Weetman_Pearson,_1st_Viscount_Cowdray" title="Weetman Pearson, 1st Viscount Cowdray">Lord Cowdray</a> formed <b>Amerada Corporation</b> to explore for oil in North America.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> The firm was incorporated February 7, 1920, in Delaware as a holding company for its principal subsidiary, the <b>Amerada Petroleum Corporation</b>. The oil producer experienced growth during most of the 1920s, hitting a peak in 1926 with a net income of US$4.9 million.  However, in the years leading to the <a href="/wiki/Great_Depression" title="Great Depression">Great Depression</a>, weakness in the oil markets contributed to sluggish profits.  The aftermath of the market crash aggravated the unsteady oil industry.  In the first quarter of 1930, the company experienced a minor loss.  The early years of the Depression were a struggle against wavering demand and overproduction in some regions.  Later into the 1930s, the financial forecast became more sanguine for Amerada.
</p><p>In December 1941, the company reorganized by merging the holding company with the principal operating subsidiary, Amerada Petroleum Corporation, into a simplified operating company.  The new entity also adopted the former subsidiary's name.
</p><p>Robust postwar growth rocketed the company past US$100 million in sales in 1955.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Hess_Oil_and_Chemical" title="Hess Oil and Chemical">Hess Oil and Chemical</a>, an oil refiner and marketer founded by <a href="/wiki/Leon_Hess" title="Leon Hess">Leon Hess</a>, acquired 10% of the company for US$100 million in 1966 after the British government sold a stake it had amassed during <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>. Albert Levinson became the senior vice president and designed the Hess logo. Hess and Amerada would announce plans for a merger in December 1968.  Some Amerada stockholders led by Morton Adler criticized the arrangement as being too favorable for Hess. Adler argued Amerada's <a href="/wiki/Oil_reserves" title="Oil reserves">oil reserves</a> would contribute the lion's share of assets for the proposed company, so Amerada stockholders should retain more control of the new company.  Before the stockholder vote on the matter, <a href="/wiki/ConocoPhillips" title="ConocoPhillips">Phillips Petroleum</a>, an integrated oil firm, approached Amerada with its own merger proposal, but the offer was declined in March.  Still interested, Phillips nonetheless stated it would not carry out a <a href="/wiki/Proxy_fight" title="Proxy fight">proxy fight</a> against the proposed Hess deal.  Hess, fearing such a strategy, made a cash <a href="/wiki/Tender_offer" title="Tender offer">tender offer</a> of US$140 million for an additional 1.1 million shares of Amerada, which would double its holding in the company.  The new shares would be employed in a May stockholder vote deciding the merger's fate.  The vote took place amidst shareholder rancor that in addition to echoing Adler's arguments, objected to Amerada's financing of the recently completed tender offer.  Hess planned to cancel the shares and the cost of the acquisition would be absorbed by the newly formed company.  One shareholder at the meeting quipped, "It looks to me as if Hess is buying Amerada with Amerada's money."  Proponents of the deal won, and the US$2.4 billion merger combining a purely production company with a refinery and marketer operation was completed.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> However controversy was not yet extinguished by the stockholder confirmation.  A <a href="/wiki/Class_action" title="Class action">class action</a> federal lawsuit in 1972 claiming the proxy vote information was misleading.  In 1976, a court agreed that the company falsely claimed to have considered each company's assets as a reason for the merger.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:277px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Hess_Station,_Rensselaer_County,_NY.jpg" class="image"><noscript><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/da/Hess_Station%2C_Rensselaer_County%2C_NY.jpg/275px-Hess_Station%2C_Rensselaer_County%2C_NY.jpg" decoding="async" width="275" height="178" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="1900" data-file-height="1228"></noscript><span class="lazy-image-placeholder" style="width: 275px;height: 178px;" data-src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/da/Hess_Station%2C_Rensselaer_County%2C_NY.jpg/275px-Hess_Station%2C_Rensselaer_County%2C_NY.jpg" data-alt="" data-width="275" data-height="178" data-class="thumbimage"> </span></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Hess_Station,_Rensselaer_County,_NY.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A former Hess Station in <a href="/wiki/Rensselaer_County,_New_York" title="Rensselaer County, New York">Rensselaer County, New York</a></div></div></div>
<p>In February 2000, Hess acquired the 51% shares of the <a href="/wiki/Meadville_Corporation" title="Meadville Corporation">Meadville Corporation</a> it didn't already own, and rebranded all 178 Merit gas stations as Hess.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> The Merit gas station chain were primarily in the Boston, New York, and Philadelphia markets.
</p><p>In 2001, Amerada Hess purchased <a href="/wiki/Triton_Energy_Limited" class="mw-redirect" title="Triton Energy Limited">Triton Energy Limited</a> in a cash tender deal valued at approximately US$3.2 billion.  Triton, one of the largest independent oil and natural gas exploration and production companies in the U.S., had earned a reputation as a maverick oil company due to its highly successful yet potentially risky overseas exploration.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>  According to Amerada Hess press releases at the time, Triton's major oil and gas assets in West Africa, Latin America, and Southeast Asia would strengthen its exploration and production business and give it access to long life international reserves.  Hess also stated that the purchase was expected to immediately increase the company's per-day barrel output by more than 25 percent.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p><p>Also in 2001, Amerada Hess entered into a <a href="/wiki/Joint_venture" title="Joint venture">joint venture</a> with <a href="/wiki/A.T._Williams_Oil_Co." title="A.T. Williams Oil Co.">A.T. Williams Oil Co.</a> of <a href="/wiki/Winston-Salem,_North_Carolina" title="Winston-Salem, North Carolina">Winston-Salem, North Carolina</a>. The company and its gas stations were called <a href="/wiki/WilcoHess" class="mw-redirect" title="WilcoHess">WilcoHess</a>. Eventually, there were 1200 WilcoHess stations.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p><p>Following on the heels of the Triton purchase, energy prices fell and global economies weakened.  Amerada Hess struggled through the following years, posting a US$218 million loss in 2002 due primarily to a US$530 million charge relating to its write-down of the Ceiba oil field, but then posting steadily increasing profits from 2003 through 2006, when the company posted US$1.920 billion in net income.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p><p>In May 2006, Amerada Hess Corp. changed its name to Hess Corp.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p><p>On January 18, 2012 the company announced that it would close the <a href="/wiki/Hovensa" title="Hovensa">Hovensa</a> refinery in <a href="/wiki/Saint_Croix,_U.S._Virgin_Islands" class="mw-redirect" title="Saint Croix, U.S. Virgin Islands">St. Croix</a>, <a href="/wiki/United_States_Virgin_Islands" title="United States Virgin Islands">United States Virgin Islands</a> by mid-February 2012. The refinery will then serve as a storage terminal
</p><p>Hess will permanently close its <a href="/wiki/Port_Reading,_New_Jersey" title="Port Reading, New Jersey">Port Reading, New Jersey</a> petroleum refinery by the end of February 2013: Gas prices rose to their highest levels since October and Hess said it will lay off 170 of 217 employees, exit the refinery business and seek a buyer for its 19 storage terminals. It will focus on exploration and production.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>   A Hess press release<sup id="cite_ref-phx.corporate-ir.net_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-phx.corporate-ir.net-17">[17]</a></sup> announces the company's plans for "Fully exiting the Company's downstream businesses, including retail, energy marketing, and energy trading."<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> there is no link between the rise in gas prices after the announcement of the closing of the Woodbridge (Port Reading) NJ facility.  The output of that facility was more geared to the aviation and specialty fuels markets and not automotive grade products
</p><p>On March 4, 2013 Hess announced that it would sell its domestic refineries and retail operations. The <i>New York Times</i> also reported that Hess retail and refinery operations contributed about 4 percent of the company's revenue. It also noted that Hess will sell its holdings in Indonesia and Thailand.<sup id="cite_ref-Merced_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Merced-19">[19]</a></sup> The company will focus exclusively on oil production, following a recent trend in the oil industry for companies to spin off their <a href="/wiki/Downstream_(petroleum_industry)" title="Downstream (petroleum industry)">downstream</a> assets and focus on their more profitable <a href="/wiki/Upstream_(petroleum_industry)" title="Upstream (petroleum industry)">upstream</a> business; <a href="/wiki/ConocoPhillips" title="ConocoPhillips">ConocoPhillips</a> and <a href="/wiki/Marathon_Oil" title="Marathon Oil">Marathon Oil</a> have also made similar spinoffs in recent years with <a href="/wiki/Phillips_66" title="Phillips 66">Phillips 66</a> and <a href="/wiki/Marathon_Petroleum" title="Marathon Petroleum">Marathon Petroleum</a>, respectively.
</p><p>In April 2013, Hess Corp announced it would be selling its Russian unit to <a href="/wiki/Lukoil" title="Lukoil">Lukoil</a> for $2.05 billion.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> In July 2013, Hess Corp said it would sell its energy marketing unit to UK firm <a href="/wiki/Centrica" title="Centrica">Centrica</a> for around $1.03 billion.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p><p>Hess Corp announced in October 2013 that it was planning on selling its East Coast and St.Lucia storage terminal network to Buckeye Partners LP for $850 million.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p><p>Hess Corp announced in December 2013 that it is selling its Indonesian assets to an Indonesian petroleum consortium.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p><p>On January 8, 2014, Hess filed for a tax-free spin-off of its gas station network. The newly formed company was to be known as Hess Retail and will include over 1,200 stores throughout the Eastern United States.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> Before completing the spin-off, Marathon Petroleum subsidiary <a href="/wiki/Speedway_LLC" title="Speedway LLC">Speedway LLC</a> announced on May 22, 2014 that it would acquire the retail unit of Hess Corp for $2.87 billion. Following the closure of the acquisition in late 2014, all Hess gas stations will be rebranded as Speedway gas stations by the end of 2017.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> The transaction completed the transformation of Hess into an energy company focused solely on exploration and production, effectively reversing the Amerada merger almost 50 years prior.
</p>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(2)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Environmental_record">Environmental record</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hess_Corporation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Environmental record" data-section="2" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-2 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-2">
<p><i><a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times" title="The New York Times">The New York Times</a></i> reported on October 28, 1990, that a barge with a load of 31,000 barrels (4,900 m<sup>3</sup>) of <a href="/wiki/Kerosene" title="Kerosene">kerosene</a> struck a reef in the <a href="/wiki/Hudson_River" title="Hudson River">Hudson River</a>, spilling 163,000 US gallons (620 m<sup>3</sup>) of fuel. Immediately, Hess assumed responsibility for the cleanup; the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Coast_Guard" title="United States Coast Guard">Coast Guard</a> worked alongside the Red Star company to clean and to contain the spill to one area. Coast Guard official Mr. Holmes said "The weather and wind conditions are almost as close to perfect as they could get," and this contributed to a quicker and surer cleanup than could otherwise be. According to <i>The New York Times</i>, Mr. Holmes also said that 70 percent of the spill would be gone in three days due to the natural evaporation rate of kerosene. Even though most kerosene evaporates, toxic chemicals such as <a href="/wiki/Benzene" title="Benzene">benzene</a> stay in the water and harm certain fish. Hess claims that their corporate policy has "long stressed" their "fundamental commitment to comply with applicable <a href="/wiki/Environment,_health_and_safety" title="Environment, health and safety">environment, health and safety</a> laws and regulations," and they claim to clean every spill made.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p><p>In accordance with a <a href="/wiki/New_York_State_Department_of_Environmental_Conservation" title="New York State Department of Environmental Conservation">New York State Department of Environmental Conservation</a> (DEC) agreement the Hess Corporation will pay $1.1 million in fines and also "bring 65 gasoline stations and oil storage facilities into compliance with state requirements." The agreement addresses more than 100 violations at 65 gas stations and Hess's Brooklyn major oil storage facility. The agreement is aimed at resolving Hess's violations in the DEC's New York City and lower Hudson Valley regions.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p><p>In a recent water contamination case against several major US oil companies, the Hess Corporation will pay part of a $422 million settlement. The case was filed by 153 public water providers in 17 states against the oil companies "over drinking water contamination caused by the gasoline additive <a href="/wiki/Methyl_tert-butyl_ether" title="Methyl tert-butyl ether">Methyl Tertiary Butyl Ether</a> (MTBE)." The settlement also stipulates that the settling parties pay their share of treatment costs of the plaintiff's wells that may become contaminated or require treatment for the next 30 years.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p><p>In regard to greenhouse gas emissions, Hess outlined in their 2006 Corporate Sustainability Report a "four element" strategy to reduce and control emissions. The strategy's steps include monitoring, measuring, managing, and mitigating. Through reporting results, energy efficiency and recovery, and carbon capture and trading the company intends to improve its environmental impact.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(3)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Locations">Locations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hess_Corporation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Locations" data-section="3" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-3 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-3">
<p>Prior to the March 4, 2013 announcement of its withdrawal from refining and retail sales of petroleum products, Hess operated gas stations in <a href="/wiki/Alabama" title="Alabama">Alabama</a>, <a href="/wiki/Arkansas" title="Arkansas">Arkansas</a>, <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a>, <a href="/wiki/Connecticut" title="Connecticut">Connecticut</a>, <a href="/wiki/Delaware" title="Delaware">Delaware</a>, <a href="/wiki/Washington,_D.C." title="Washington, D.C.">District of Columbia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Florida" title="Florida">Florida</a>, <a href="/wiki/Georgia_(U.S._state)" title="Georgia (U.S. state)">Georgia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Indonesia" title="Indonesia">Indonesia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Maryland" title="Maryland">Maryland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts" title="Massachusetts">Massachusetts</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_Hampshire" title="New Hampshire">New Hampshire</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_Jersey" title="New Jersey">New Jersey</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_York_(state)" title="New York (state)">New York</a>, <a href="/wiki/North_Carolina" title="North Carolina">North Carolina</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pennsylvania" title="Pennsylvania">Pennsylvania</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rhode_Island" title="Rhode Island">Rhode Island</a>, <a href="/wiki/South_Carolina" title="South Carolina">South Carolina</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tennessee" title="Tennessee">Tennessee</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Virginia" title="Virginia">Virginia</a>.
</p><p>In May 2014, <a href="/wiki/Speedway_LLC" title="Speedway LLC">Speedway LLC</a>, a subsidiary of <a href="/wiki/Marathon_Petroleum_Company" class="mw-redirect" title="Marathon Petroleum Company">Marathon Petroleum Company</a>, announced they would purchase Hess Corporation's retail business for $2.6 billion. Hess had 1,342 locations along the Eastern United States. The conversion from Hess branding to Speedway branding took place over the course of 2015. Hess locations still remain in select states as of 2018, including Connecticut.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
