</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(1)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Overview">Overview</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Duke_Energy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Overview" data-section="1" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-1 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-1">
<p>Based in Charlotte, North Carolina, Duke Energy owns 58,200 <a href="/wiki/Watt" title="Watt">megawatts</a> of base-load and peak generation in the United States, which it distributes to its 7.2 million customers. The company has approximately 29,000 employees.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Duke Energy's service territory covers 104,000 square miles (270,000 km<sup>2</sup>) with 250,200 miles (402,700 km) of distribution lines.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Almost all of Duke Energy's Midwest generation comes from coal, <a href="/wiki/Natural_gas" title="Natural gas">natural gas</a>, or oil, while half of its Carolinas generation comes from its <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_power" title="Nuclear power">nuclear power plants</a>. During 2006, Duke Energy generated 148,798,332 megawatt-hours of electrical energy.
</p><p>Duke Energy Renewable Services (DERS), a subsidiary of Duke Energy, specializes in the development, ownership, and operation of various generation facilities throughout the United States. This segment of the company operates 1,700 megawatts of generation. 240 megawatts of <a href="/wiki/Wind_power" title="Wind power">wind generation</a> were under construction and 1,500 additional megawatts of wind generation were in planning stages.<sup id="cite_ref-DEGS_overview_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DEGS_overview-4">[4]</a></sup> On September 9, 2008, DERS updated its projections for future <a href="/wiki/Wind_power" title="Wind power">wind power</a> capacity. By the end of 2008, it would have over 500 MW of <a href="/wiki/Nameplate_capacity" title="Nameplate capacity">nameplate capacity</a> of wind power online, and an additional 5,000 MW in development.<sup id="cite_ref-DEGS_wind_2008_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DEGS_wind_2008-5">[5]</a></sup></p><p>Duke Energy has a controversial environmental record.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-6">[6]</a></sup></p>

<li>Duke Energy International</li></ul></section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(2)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Duke_Energy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: History" data-section="3" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-2 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-2">
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:142px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Duke_Energy_Center_cropped.jpg" class="image"><noscript><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/54/Duke_Energy_Center_cropped.jpg/140px-Duke_Energy_Center_cropped.jpg" decoding="async" width="140" height="328" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="630" data-file-height="1477"></noscript><span class="lazy-image-placeholder" style="width: 140px;height: 328px;" data-src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/54/Duke_Energy_Center_cropped.jpg/140px-Duke_Energy_Center_cropped.jpg" data-alt="" data-width="140" data-height="328" data-class="thumbimage"> </span></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Duke_Energy_Center_cropped.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Duke_Energy_Center" title="Duke Energy Center">Duke Energy Center</a> in <a href="/wiki/Charlotte,_North_Carolina" title="Charlotte, North Carolina">Charlotte</a>, 2010</div></div></div>
<p>The company began in 1900 as the Catawba Power Company when Dr. Walker Gill Wylie and his brother financed the building of a hydroelectric power station at India Hook Shoals along the Catawba River near <a href="/wiki/India_Hook,_South_Carolina" title="India Hook, South Carolina">India Hook, South Carolina</a>. In need of additional funding to further his ambitious plan for construction of a series of hydroelectric power plants, Wylie convinced <a href="/wiki/James_B._Duke" class="mw-redirect" title="James B. Duke">James Buchanan Duke</a> to invest in the Southern Power Company, founded in 1905.
</p><p>In 1917 the Wateree Power Company was formed as a holding company for several utilities that had been founded and/or owned by Duke, his family, or his associates, and in 1924 the name was changed to Duke Power. In 1927, most of the subsidiary companies, including Southern Power Company, Catawba Power Company, Great Falls Power Company, and Western Carolina Power Company were merged into Duke Power, although Southern Public Utilities, 100% owned by Duke Power, maintained a legally separate existence for the retail marketing of Duke-generated power to residential and commercial customers.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p><p>Before the passage of the Civil Rights Act of 1964, Duke Power had an overt policy of openly discriminating on the basis of race in hiring and assigning employees at its <a href="/wiki/Dan_River_Steam_Station" title="Dan River Steam Station">Dan River plant</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>  In the years following the passage of the Civil Rights Act, Duke Power continued to racially discriminate by implementing education requirements for work placement that did not directly relate to the work being done.  The <a href="/wiki/Supreme_Court_of_the_United_States" title="Supreme Court of the United States">Supreme Court</a> found in <i><a href="/wiki/Griggs_v._Duke_Power_Co." title="Griggs v. Duke Power Co.">Griggs v. Duke Power Co.</a></i> that this policy violated the law and was discriminatory. 
</p><p>A 1973 labor dispute between mine workers and Duke Power was the subject of the documentary <i><a href="/wiki/Harlan_County,_USA" title="Harlan County, USA">Harlan County, USA</a></i>. The film documents the use of "gun thugs" to intimidate striking workers.
</p><p>In 1988, Nantahala Power &amp; Light Co., which served southwestern North Carolina, was purchased by Duke and is now operated under the Duke Power Nantahala Area brand. Duke Power merged with PanEnergy, a natural gas company, in 1997 to form Duke Energy.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> The Duke Power name continued as the electric utility business of Duke Energy until the Cinergy merger.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Duke_Energy_Field_Services_(East_Texas_Plant).jpg" class="image"><noscript><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/a/a7/Duke_Energy_Field_Services_%28East_Texas_Plant%29.jpg/220px-Duke_Energy_Field_Services_%28East_Texas_Plant%29.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="1600" data-file-height="1200"></noscript><span class="lazy-image-placeholder" style="width: 220px;height: 165px;" data-src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/a/a7/Duke_Energy_Field_Services_%28East_Texas_Plant%29.jpg/220px-Duke_Energy_Field_Services_%28East_Texas_Plant%29.jpg" data-alt="" data-width="220" data-height="165" data-class="thumbimage"> </span></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Duke_Energy_Field_Services_(East_Texas_Plant).jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Duke Energy Field Services near <a href="/wiki/Palestine,_Texas" title="Palestine, Texas">Palestine, Texas</a>.  The facilities include refineries and oil wells throughout the region.</div></div></div>
<p>With the purchase of <a href="/wiki/Cinergy" title="Cinergy">Cinergy Corporation</a> announced in 2005 and completed on April 3, 2006, Duke Energy Corporation's customer base grew to include the <a href="/wiki/Midwestern_United_States" title="Midwestern United States">Midwestern United States</a> as well. The company operates <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_power" title="Nuclear power">nuclear power</a> plants, coal-fired plants, conventional <a href="/wiki/Hydroelectricity" title="Hydroelectricity">hydroelectric</a> plants, natural-gas turbines to handle peak demand, and pumped hydro storage. During 2006, Duke Energy also acquired <a href="/wiki/Chatham,_Ontario" class="mw-redirect" title="Chatham, Ontario">Chatham, Ontario</a>-based <a href="/wiki/Union_Gas" title="Union Gas">Union Gas</a>, which is regulated under the Ontario Energy Board Act (1998).
</p><p>On January 3, 2007, Duke Energy spun off its gas business to form <a href="/wiki/Spectra_Energy" title="Spectra Energy">Spectra Energy</a>. Duke Energy shareholders received 1 share of Spectra Energy for each 2 shares of Duke Energy. After the spin-off, Duke Energy now receives the majority of its revenue from its electric operations in portions of North Carolina, South Carolina, Kentucky, Ohio, and Indiana. The spinoff to Spectra also included Union Gas, which Duke Energy acquired the previous year.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p><p>In 2011, Duke Energy worked with Charlotte’s business leader community to help build Charlotte into a <a href="/wiki/Smart_city" title="Smart city">smart city</a>. The group called the initiative "Envision Charlotte". At the time, the group decided on a goal to reduce energy use in the "urban core of the city by 20 percent". To do so, the group focused on making energy consumption changes to commercial buildings larger than 10,000 square feet.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p><p>On July 3, 2012, Duke Energy merged with <a href="/wiki/Progress_Energy_Inc" title="Progress Energy Inc">Progress Energy Inc</a> with the Duke Energy name being retained along with the <a href="/wiki/Charlotte,_North_Carolina" title="Charlotte, North Carolina">Charlotte, North Carolina</a>, headquarters.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p><p>Duke announced on June 18, 2013, that CEO Jim Rogers was retiring and Lynn Good would become the new CEO. Rogers has been CEO and Chairman since 2006, while Good was Chief Financial Officer of Duke since 2009, having joined Duke in the 2006 Cinergy merger. Rogers' retirement was part of an agreement to end an investigation into Duke's Progress Energy acquisition in 2012.<sup id="cite_ref-NEXT_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NEXT-15">[15]</a></sup></p><p>In 2016, Duke Energy purchased Piedmont Natural Gas for $4.9 billion to become its wholly owned subsidiary.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>  Duke Energy completed selling its remaining power operations in Central and South America for $1.2 billion months afterwards.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup>  At one point Duke Energy had more than 4,300 megawatts of electric generation in Latin America.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> It operated eight hydroelectric power plants in Brazil with an installed capacity of 2,307 megawatts.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p><p>The company expects to spend $13 billion upgrading the North Carolina grid from 2017.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="New_nuclear_power_plant">New nuclear power plant</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Duke_Energy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: New nuclear power plant" data-section="4" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/William_States_Lee_III_Nuclear_Generating_Station" title="William States Lee III Nuclear Generating Station">William States Lee III Nuclear Generating Station</a></div>
<p>On March 16, 2006, Duke Power announced that a <a href="/wiki/Cherokee_County,_South_Carolina" title="Cherokee County, South Carolina">Cherokee County, South Carolina</a> site had been selected for a potential new <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_power_plant" title="Nuclear power plant">nuclear power plant</a>. The site is jointly owned by Duke Power and <a href="/wiki/Southern_Company" title="Southern Company">Southern Company</a>. Duke plans to develop the site for two <a href="/wiki/Westinghouse_Electric_Company" title="Westinghouse Electric Company">Westinghouse Electric Company</a> <a href="/wiki/AP1000" title="AP1000">AP1000</a> (advanced passive) <a href="/wiki/Pressurized_water_reactor" title="Pressurized water reactor">pressurized water reactors</a>. Each reactor is capable of producing approximately 1,117 megawatts. (See <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_Power_2010_Program" title="Nuclear Power 2010 Program">Nuclear Power 2010 Program</a>.)
</p><p>On December 14, 2007, Duke Power submitted a <a href="/wiki/Combined_Construction_and_Operating_License" title="Combined Construction and Operating License">Combined Construction and Operating License</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_Regulatory_Commission" title="Nuclear Regulatory Commission">Nuclear Regulatory Commission</a>, with an announcement that it will spend $160 million in 2008 on the plant with a total cost of $5 billion to $6 billion.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> The plant was approved in 2016.<sup id="cite_ref-nrc-Lee_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nrc-Lee-22">[22]</a></sup></p><p>In August 2017, Duke decided to seek permission from the North Carolina Utility Commission to cancel the project due to the bankruptcy of Westinghouse and "other market activity", although they will retain the option of restarting the project at some point in the future if circumstances change.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p><p>This site would have been adjacent to the old site, which was never completed and abandoned in the early 1980s, and used by <a href="/wiki/James_Cameron" title="James Cameron">James Cameron</a> as a film set for the 1989 movie <i><a href="/wiki/The_Abyss" title="The Abyss">The Abyss</a></i>.
</p><p>In 2018, Duke Energy decided not to include new nuclear power in its long-range plans.<sup id="cite_ref-bizjournals-20180905_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bizjournals-20180905-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Headquarters_buildings">Headquarters buildings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Duke_Energy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Headquarters buildings" data-section="5" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/J.A._Jones" class="mw-redirect" title="J.A. Jones">J.A. Jones</a> designed the first headquarters building, known as the Power Building, which was completed in 1928 at <a href="/wiki/440_South_Church" title="440 South Church">440 South Church</a>. The Electric Center at 526 South Church Street opened in 1975 with an addition in 1988.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p><p><a href="/wiki/Duke_Energy_Center" title="Duke Energy Center">Duke Energy Center</a> at 550 South Tryon Street across the street was announced as the company's headquarters in 2009<sup id="cite_ref-WBTV_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WBTV-26">[26]</a></sup> and will continue with that role, even after the completion of a tower across the street from the headquarters, which is scheduled to open in 2022. <a href="/w/index.php?title=Childress_Klein&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Childress Klein (page does not exist)">Childress Klein</a> is developing the new building, which will allow Duke to sell its Church Street and College Street buildings, and end its lease at <a href="/wiki/400_South_Tryon" title="400 South Tryon">400 South Tryon</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-WFAE_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WFAE-27">[27]</a></sup> Named <a href="/wiki/Charlotte_Metro_Tower" title="Charlotte Metro Tower">Charlotte Metro Tower</a>, the 40-story building is being purchased for up to $675 million by Childress Klein and CGA Capital, in the largest real estate deal in the city's history, announced in December 2019.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(3)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Finances">Finances</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Duke_Energy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Finances" data-section="6" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-3 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-3">

<p>In 1999, the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Environmental_Protection_Agency" title="United States Environmental Protection Agency">United States Environmental Protection Agency</a> commenced an enforcement action against Duke Energy for making modifications to very old and deteriorating coal-burning power plants without getting permits under the <a href="/wiki/Clean_Air_Act_(United_States)" class="mw-redirect" title="Clean Air Act (United States)">Clean Air Act</a>. Duke asserted that a "modification" under the Clean Air Act did not require a permit. Environmental groups asserted that Duke was using loopholes in the law to increase emissions. Initially, Duke prevailed at the trial court level, but in 2006 the case was argued before the <a href="/wiki/Supreme_Court_of_the_United_States" title="Supreme Court of the United States">Supreme Court</a> (<i><a href="/wiki/Environmental_Defense" class="mw-redirect" title="Environmental Defense">Environmental Defense</a> v. Duke Energy Corp.</i> (05-848)). The Court unanimously ruled on April 2, 2007 that the modifications allowed the power plants to operate for more hours, increasing emissions, so Clean Air Act permits were needed.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p><p>In 2002, researchers at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Massachusetts_Amherst" title="University of Massachusetts Amherst">University of Massachusetts Amherst</a> identified Duke Energy as the 46th-largest corporate producer of <a href="/wiki/Air_pollution_in_the_United_States" title="Air pollution in the United States">air pollution in the United States</a>, with roughly 36 million
pounds of toxic chemicals released annually into the air.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> Major pollutants included <a href="/wiki/Sulfuric_acid" title="Sulfuric acid">sulfuric</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hydrochloric_acid" title="Hydrochloric acid">hydrochloric acid</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chromium" title="Chromium">chromium</a> compounds, and <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_fluoride" title="Hydrogen fluoride">hydrogen fluoride</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Political_Economy_Research_Institute" title="Political Economy Research Institute">Political Economy Research Institute</a> ranks Duke Energy 13th among corporations emitting airborne pollutants in the United States. The ranking is based on the quantity (80 million pounds in 2005) and toxicity of the emissions.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> This change reflects the purchase of fossil fuel-heavy Cinergy, which occurred in 2005.

</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(7)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Criticism">Criticism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Duke_Energy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Criticism" data-section="20" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-7 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-7">
<p>Following a February 2, <a href="/wiki/2014_Dan_River_coal_ash_spill" title="2014 Dan River coal ash spill">2014 coal ash spill</a> which was the third-largest of its kind in US history, the US Attorney's Office opened a grand jury investigation into Duke Energy and North Carolina regulators in the administration of Governor Pat McCrory. McCrory had been an employee of Duke Energy for 28 years and critics have said his administration intervened on Duke's behalf to settle lawsuits over environmental violations.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup>  The US Attorney subpoenaed over 20 officials of the McCrory administration and sought records of "investments, cash or other items of value" from Duke to regulators.<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup></p><p>On February 14, 2011, <a href="/wiki/Greenpeace" title="Greenpeace">Greenpeace</a> launched a campaign in which <a href="/wiki/Phil_Radford" title="Phil Radford">Phil Radford</a> called on Duke Energy to "not renew a single new contract for <a href="/wiki/Mountaintop_removal_mining" title="Mountaintop removal mining">mountaintop removal</a> coal, deliver at least a third of Duke's energy from renewable sources by 2020, and quit coal altogether by 2030."<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> In May 2013, university student activists launched a <a href="/wiki/Disinvestment" title="Disinvestment">divestment campaign</a> against Duke Energy and other coal plant operators.<sup id="cite_ref-Shogren01_50-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Shogren01-50">[50]</a></sup></p><p>In July 2012, Duke Energy was criticized for paying former Progress Energy CEO Bill Johnson $44 million in compensation, including $10 million severance, for essentially 20 minutes on the job as Duke CEO.<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup> On July 10, new CEO Jim Rogers spoke before the N.C. Utilities Commission, explaining the reason for Johnson's dismissal as "loss of confidence". He also mentioned an "autocratic style", though some former Progress directors disagreed.<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup></p><p>In December 2011, the non-partisan organization <a href="/wiki/Public_Campaign#Reports" class="mw-redirect" title="Public Campaign">Public Campaign</a> criticized Duke Energy for spending $17.47 million on <a href="/wiki/Lobbying" title="Lobbying">lobbying</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tax_avoidance" title="Tax avoidance">not paying any taxes</a> during 2008 through 2010 and receiving $216 million in tax rebates, despite making a profit of $5.4 billion and increasing executive pay by 145% to $17.2 million in 2010 for its top 5 executives.<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup> The company became the object of protest for its close relationship to the <a href="/wiki/Democratic_Party_(United_States)" title="Democratic Party (United States)">Democratic Party</a> and its <a href="/wiki/2012_Democratic_National_Convention#Funding" title="2012 Democratic National Convention">funding</a> for the <a href="/wiki/2012_Democratic_National_Convention" title="2012 Democratic National Convention">2012 Democratic National Convention</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-McMillan16Aug2012_55-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-McMillan16Aug2012-55">[55]</a></sup></p><p>In September 2016, the $800 billion <a href="/wiki/Government_Pension_Fund_of_Norway" title="Government Pension Fund of Norway">Government Pension Fund of Norway</a> excluded Duke and 3 of its subsidiaries, citing "Risk of severe environmental damage".<sup id="cite_ref-:0_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxes">Taxes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Duke_Energy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Taxes" data-section="21" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Duke_Energy_Office.JPG" class="image"><noscript><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e0/Duke_Energy_Office.JPG/220px-Duke_Energy_Office.JPG" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="4608" data-file-height="3456"></noscript><span class="lazy-image-placeholder" style="width: 220px;height: 165px;" data-src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e0/Duke_Energy_Office.JPG/220px-Duke_Energy_Office.JPG" data-alt="" data-width="220" data-height="165" data-class="thumbimage"> </span></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Duke_Energy_Office.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Duke Energy Office in Raleigh, NC</div></div></div>
<p>Duke Energy owns the <a href="/wiki/Crystal_River_Energy_Complex" title="Crystal River Energy Complex">Crystal River Energy Complex</a> in rural <a href="/wiki/Citrus_County,_Florida" title="Citrus County, Florida">Citrus County, Florida</a> which includes the now-closed <a href="/wiki/Crystal_River_3_Nuclear_Power_Plant" class="mw-redirect" title="Crystal River 3 Nuclear Power Plant">Crystal River 3 Nuclear Power Plant</a> and four <a href="/wiki/Fossil-fuel_power_station" class="mw-redirect" title="Fossil-fuel power station">fossil-fuel power stations</a> using coal. In late 2012, Duke paid $19 million of their 2012 property tax bill of $36 million, and filed suit against the county, claiming the assessment was too high. Duke Power owns 4,700 acres (1,900 hectares) of land and improvements assessed at over $2 billion, constituting more than a quarter of the county tax base.<sup id="cite_ref-TAX_56-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TAX-56">[56]</a></sup> Citrus County Property Appraiser Geoff Greene commented, "Given the complexity, history and dispute over the last year's assessment of Duke Energy's property, an <i>independent</i> appraisal team was needed and tasked to arrive at a fair and just value."<sup id="cite_ref-OWES_57-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OWES-57">[57]</a></sup> With the financial assistance from the Board of County Commissioners and the School Board,<sup id="cite_ref-OWES_57-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OWES-57">[57]</a></sup> Greene hired two independent appraisal firms at a cost of $330,000.<sup id="cite_ref-TAX_56-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TAX-56">[56]</a></sup> After five months of inspection, documentation and crunching numbers,<sup id="cite_ref-OWES_57-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OWES-57">[57]</a></sup> the outside appraisal concluded that the county assessment was incorrect at $2.32 billion; it should have been $3.47 billion. Based on that figure, Duke owed the remaining $17 million for 2012 and 2013 taxes should be $54.6 million. Greene stated, "There were a lot of unreported and underreported items."<sup id="cite_ref-TAX_56-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TAX-56">[56]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-OWES_57-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OWES-57">[57]</a></sup></p>
