</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(1)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Celanese&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History" data-section="1" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-1 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-1">
<p>In 1918, the American Cellulose &amp; Chemical Manufacturing Company was founded in <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a> by Swiss chemist <a href="/wiki/Camille_Dreyfus_(chemist)" title="Camille Dreyfus (chemist)">Camille Dreyfus</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p><p>The American Cellulose and Chemical Manufacturing Co. Ltd plant in Cumberland, Maryland was set up during <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a> to produce cheaper fabric for airplane manufacturing. The plant location was chosen inland to protect against <a href="/wiki/Zeppelin" title="Zeppelin">Zeppelin</a> attacks. It was also situated in proximity to a ready source of water at the <a href="/wiki/Potomac_River" title="Potomac River">Potomac River</a> and easy access to coal supplies and railroad lines. After a series of delays, actual production began in 1924 with a series of <a href="/wiki/Cellulose_acetate" title="Cellulose acetate">cellulose acetate</a> commercial fabrics and yarns intended as alternatives to <a href="/wiki/Silk" title="Silk">silk</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> The plant was closed in 1983, and was later torn down to provide a space for a new state prison.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p><p>In 1927, the American Cellulose &amp; Chemical Manufacturing Company changed its name to Celanese Corporation of America.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:1211_Avenue_of_the_Americas.jpg" class="image"><noscript><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/66/1211_Avenue_of_the_Americas.jpg/220px-1211_Avenue_of_the_Americas.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="301" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="1474" data-file-height="2019"></noscript><span class="lazy-image-placeholder" style="width: 220px;height: 301px;" data-src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/66/1211_Avenue_of_the_Americas.jpg/220px-1211_Avenue_of_the_Americas.jpg" data-alt="" data-width="220" data-height="301" data-class="thumbimage"> </span></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:1211_Avenue_of_the_Americas.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/1211_Avenue_of_the_Americas" title="1211 Avenue of the Americas">1211 Avenue of the Americas</a>, formerly the Celanese Building, completed in 1973</div></div></div>
<p>Celanese bought operations of <a href="/wiki/Imperial_Chemical_Industries" title="Imperial Chemical Industries">Imperial Chemicals Incorporated</a> in 1982. This included the Fiber Industries Incorporated plant in <a href="/wiki/Salisbury,_North_Carolina" title="Salisbury, North Carolina">Salisbury, North Carolina</a>, a part of <a href="/wiki/Invista" title="Invista">Invista</a> since 2004.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p><p>In 1986, its pharmaceutical business was spun off as <a href="/wiki/Celgene" title="Celgene">Celgene</a>, and, in 1987, Celanese Corporation was acquired by <a href="/wiki/Hoechst_AG" title="Hoechst AG">Hoechst</a> and merged with its American subsidiary, American Hoechst, to form Hoechst Celanese Corporation.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p><p>In 1998, in a $2.7 billion deal, Hoescht Celanese sold its Trevira division to a consortium between <a href="/wiki/Houston" title="Houston">Houston</a>-based KoSa, a joint venture of <a href="/wiki/Koch_Industries" title="Koch Industries">Koch Industries</a> and IMASAB S.A. of Mexico, and <a href="/w/index.php?title=Grupo_Xtra&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Grupo Xtra (page does not exist)">Grupo Xtra</a> of Mexico.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Canada_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Canada-13">[13]</a></sup></p><p>Also in 1998, Hoechst combined most of its industrial chemical operations in a new company, Celanese AG, and, in 1999, Hoechst spun off Celanese AG as a publicly traded, German corporation, <a href="/wiki/Cross_listing" title="Cross listing">traded on both the Frankfurt and New York stock exchanges</a>.
</p><p>On 16 December 2003, the U.S. private equity firm <a href="/wiki/Blackstone_Group" class="mw-redirect" title="Blackstone Group">Blackstone Group</a> announced a <a href="/wiki/Takeover_offer" class="mw-redirect" title="Takeover offer">takeover offer</a> for Celanese, after two years of wooing management.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Shareholders formally approved the offer from Blackstone on 16 June 2004, and Blackstone completed the acquisition of Celanese AG. The company was delisted from the <a href="/wiki/New_York_Stock_Exchange" title="New York Stock Exchange">New York Stock Exchange</a>, and Blackstone changed the entity's name to Celanese Corporation. Under Blackstone, a number of streamlining initiatives were undertaken, and several acquisitions were made.
</p><p>On 21 January 2005, Celanese Corporation conducted an <a href="/wiki/Initial_public_offering" title="Initial public offering">initial public offering</a> and became a publicly traded corporation traded on the New York Stock Exchange under the symbol "CE".<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> When Blackstone sold the last of its shares in 2007, it had made five times what it had invested and it, and its co-investors collected a $2.9 billion profit.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p><p>In June 2009, the company sold its polyvinyl alcohol (PVOH) business to Sekisui Chemical Co., Ltd.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p><p>Celanese has a <a href="/wiki/TCX_Technology" title="TCX Technology">process</a> to make <a href="/wiki/Ethanol" title="Ethanol">ethanol</a> from <a href="/wiki/Natural_gas" title="Natural gas">natural gas</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Class_action_lawsuits">Class action lawsuits</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Celanese&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Class action lawsuits" data-section="2" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>In 1995 Hoechst Celanese was named along with <a href="/wiki/Shell_Oil" class="mw-redirect" title="Shell Oil">Shell Oil</a> as a defendant in a class action lawsuit for $7 billion in both past and potential future damages for which they were accused of being liable because of leaks in their polybutylene plumbing systems.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p><p>In January 2014 a class action lawsuit was filed on behalf of the residents of the community of Cannon's Campground and other residents of the area surrounding a former Hoechst Celanese industrial site in <a href="/wiki/South_Carolina" title="South Carolina">South Carolina</a>. The lawsuit alleged the dumping of a number of toxic chemicals into local waters, which has diminished property values and caused a number of illnesses. Hoechst Celanese asked the courts to dismiss these charges as spurious, claiming that its discharges have not caused substantial harm to anyone or to the environment, and further asserting that a 3-year limit on <a href="/wiki/Tort" title="Tort">tort</a> claims has expired, relieving the company of any responsibility for damages which might be eventually discovered.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>

<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Acetyl_intermediates">Acetyl intermediates</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Celanese&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Acetyl intermediates" data-section="4" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>Acetyl intermediates is Celanese's largest segment, with a product range consisting of basic chemicals such as <a href="/wiki/Acetic_acid" title="Acetic acid">acetic acid</a>, <a href="/wiki/Acetic_anhydride" title="Acetic anhydride">acetic anhydride</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Vinyl_acetate" title="Vinyl acetate">vinyl acetate</a>. Customers of Acetyl Intermediates and Industrial Specialties are in the chemical, paint and coatings, construction, and adhesive industries for <a href="/wiki/Polymerization" title="Polymerization">polymerization</a>.
</p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Advanced_engineered_materials">Advanced engineered materials</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Celanese&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Advanced engineered materials" data-section="5" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>Advanced engineered materials offers <a href="/wiki/Plastic" title="Plastic">plastic</a> polymers to customers in the automotive, electronics, telecommunications, and medical industries. Major products include: engineered plastics for fuel system components (provided by <a href="/wiki/Ticona" class="mw-redirect" title="Ticona">Ticona</a>, the engineering polymer business of Celanese), conveyor belts, electronics, safety systems, emissions filtration, and fluid handling. Polymer production is improved by the acquisition of SO.F.TER Group, <a href="/wiki/Forl%C3%AC" title="Forlì">Forlì</a>, in <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a> (2016).<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Consumer_specialties">Consumer specialties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Celanese&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Consumer specialties" data-section="6" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>The food ingredients business <a href="/wiki/Nutrinova" title="Nutrinova">Nutrinova</a> produces the high intensity sweetener Sunett (<a href="/wiki/Acesulfame_potassium" title="Acesulfame potassium">acesulfame K</a>), the <a href="/wiki/Preservative" title="Preservative">preservatives</a> Nutrinova <a href="/wiki/Potassium_sorbate" title="Potassium sorbate">potassium sorbate</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sorbic_acid" title="Sorbic acid">sorbic acid</a>, and other food ingredients. Major end-use markets include beverages, confections, baked goods, and dairy products.
</p><p>Celanese is one of the world's largest producers of <a href="/wiki/Cellulose_acetate" title="Cellulose acetate">cellulose acetate</a>. Acetate products are primarily used in <a href="/wiki/Cigarette_filters" class="mw-redirect" title="Cigarette filters">cigarette filters</a>, as well as in the production of fashion apparel and <a href="/wiki/Lining_(sewing)" title="Lining (sewing)">linings</a>. Celanese also manufactures Clarifoil cellulose acetate film that is wood pulp based and certified biodegradable and compostable in home and industrial composting conditions.
</p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Industrial_specialties">Industrial specialties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Celanese&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Industrial specialties" data-section="7" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>Industrial specialties using the feedstock from acetyl intermediates manufactures polymer and emulsions, such as <a href="/wiki/Polyvinyl_acetate" title="Polyvinyl acetate">polyvinyl acetate</a> emulsions, and specialty chemicals as <a href="/wiki/Ethylene_vinyl_acetate" class="mw-redirect" title="Ethylene vinyl acetate">ethylene vinyl acetate</a>. Major end-use markets include <a href="/wiki/Polyvinyl_alcohol" title="Polyvinyl alcohol">polyvinyl alcohol</a> producers, paper, mortar and gypsum, textiles, paints, coatings, adhesives manufacturers.
</p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Advanced_fuel_technology">Advanced fuel technology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Celanese&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Advanced fuel technology" data-section="8" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/TCX_Technology" title="TCX Technology">TCX Technology</a> is a hydrocarbon-based ethanol production process developed and marketed by Celanese and Launched in November 2010. Celanese researchers developed the TCX Technology to create a fuel that  helps countries reduce their need to import oil and gas.  Celanese plans to invest $700 million to build one-to-two plants in China and one in Texas that will produce TCX-based ethanol.<sup id="cite_ref-uk.reuters.com_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-uk.reuters.com-24">[24]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
