</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(1)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Loews_Corporation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History" data-section="1" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-1 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-1">
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Loews_Corp_logo.svg" class="image"><noscript><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e0/Loews_Corp_logo.svg/150px-Loews_Corp_logo.svg.png" decoding="async" width="150" height="150" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="18" data-file-height="18"></noscript><span class="lazy-image-placeholder" style="width: 150px;height: 150px;" data-src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e0/Loews_Corp_logo.svg/150px-Loews_Corp_logo.svg.png" data-alt="" data-width="150" data-height="150" data-srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e0/Loews_Corp_logo.svg/225px-Loews_Corp_logo.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e0/Loews_Corp_logo.svg/300px-Loews_Corp_logo.svg.png 2x" data-class="thumbimage"> </span></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Loews_Corp_logo.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Old Loews logo</div></div></div>
<p>Loews Corporation traces its roots to 1946, when <a href="/wiki/Laurence_Tisch" title="Laurence Tisch">Laurence Tisch</a> persuaded his parents to invest $125,000 to buy a resort hotel in Lakewood, N.J.  Laurence's brother <a href="/wiki/Robert_Tisch" class="mw-redirect" title="Robert Tisch">Robert</a> joined the business shortly thereafter. The Tisch brothers began to invest their profits in an expanding hotel business. By 1956, the brothers were in a position to build their first hotel, the Americana in Bal Harbour, Fla., for $17 million in cash.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p><p>In 1959, the brothers purchased a controlling interest in <a href="/wiki/Loews_Cineplex_Entertainment" title="Loews Cineplex Entertainment">Loew's Theatres</a>, Inc., a nationwide chain of 102 movie theaters, which formed the foundation of modern-day Loews (Loews sold the theater business in 1985). The two founders developed a conservative and flexible value-oriented philosophy which continues to be a cornerstone of Loews today.
</p><p>The Tisch brothers soon diversified the Loews business, successfully venturing into a variety of areas as the 1960s and 1970s progressed. Loews acquired <a href="/wiki/Lorillard_Tobacco_Company" title="Lorillard Tobacco Company">Lorillard</a>, a tobacco company, in 1968; <a href="/wiki/CNA_Financial" title="CNA Financial">CNA Financial</a> in 1974, and the <a href="/wiki/Bulova_Watch_Company" class="mw-redirect" title="Bulova Watch Company">Bulova Watch Company</a> in 1979. Through acquisitions, Loews' revenues grew from $100 million in 1970 to more than $3 billion by a decade later.
</p><p>Laurence Tisch briefly owned, through Loews, a significant portion of U.S. television and radio broadcaster <a href="/wiki/CBS" title="CBS">CBS</a> in the 1980s. As chairman of CBS, he also approved the deal that brought late night host <a href="/wiki/David_Letterman" title="David Letterman">David Letterman</a> to the network from <a href="/wiki/NBC" title="NBC">NBC</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>  Loews involvement in CBS ended in 1995, with the sale of CBS to <a href="/wiki/Westinghouse_Electric_Corporation" title="Westinghouse Electric Corporation">Westinghouse Electric Corporation</a> for $5.4 billion.
</p><p>In the past two decades, Loews further diversified into the energy business. In 1989, the company acquired Diamond M Drilling.  The subsidiary acquired ODECO in 1992, adding 39 rigs.  A year later, the combined businesses were renamed Diamond Offshore. The operating subsidiary, which went public in 1995, provides contract drilling services to the energy industry globally.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p><p>In 2003, Loews purchased Texas Gas Transmission, LLC and, a year later, bought Gulf South Pipeline Company. These two companies were consolidated into a new entity, Boardwalk Pipeline Partners, LP, which went public in 2005. Structured as a midstream master limited partnership (“MLP”), Boardwalk Pipeline provides transportation, storage, gathering and processing of natural gas and liquids for its customers.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p><p>On May 10, 2006, Loews Corporation announced that it would offer 15 million shares of <a href="/wiki/Carolina_Group" class="mw-redirect" title="Carolina Group">Carolina Group</a> via a public offering, with the proceeds to be used for general corporate purposes.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> The sale's value was $740 million.
</p><p>Loews Corporation was the parent company of <a href="/wiki/Bulova_Watches" class="mw-redirect" title="Bulova Watches">Bulova Watches</a> until 2007, when it sold the company to Tokyo-based Citizen Holdings Co.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p><p>On June 4, 2007, Loews Corporation announced it would acquire a portion of the operations of <a href="/wiki/Dominion_Resources" class="mw-redirect" title="Dominion Resources">Dominion Resources</a> for 4.025 billion dollars.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Loews rebranded the assets HighMount Exploration &amp; Production LLC. This wholly owned subsidiary is engaged in the exploration and production of natural gas and natural gas liquids.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> On May 23, 2014, Loews announced that HighMount is pursuing strategic alternatives, including a potential sale of business. HighMont was sold later that year to the private equity firm EnerVest.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p><p>On December 17, 2007, Loews Corporation announced a plan to spin off its entire ownership interest in its subsidiary, Lorillard, Inc., to holders of Carolina Group stock and Loews common stock.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p><p>In 2009, the corporation marked the 50th anniversary of its listing on the New York Stock Exchange.
</p>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(2)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Management">Management</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Loews_Corporation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Management" data-section="2" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-2 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-2">
<p>Founders Laurence and Robert Tisch served as co-CEOs until 1998, when they retired as part of a generational shift in corporate leadership. That year, James Tisch was named president and CEO, and the Office of the president was created with three members: James Tisch, Andrew Tisch and Jonathan Tisch. In 2006, Jonathan Tisch and Andrew Tisch were elected co-chairmen of the board. The company has issued only a single class of common stock and all shareholders have equal voting rights.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>
Other senior officers include David B. Edelson, chief financial officer and senior vice president, Richard W. Scott chief investment officer and Sr. VP, Marc A. Alpert, secretary and general counsel and Sr. VP, and Kenneth I. Siegel, senior vice president.
</p>

Fribourg, Walter L. Harris, Philip A. Laskawy, Ken Miller, Anthony Welters, Andrew H. Tisch, James S. Tisch and Jonathan M. Tisch.

</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(4)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Restatement">Restatement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Loews_Corporation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Restatement" data-section="4" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-4 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-4">
<p>On February 13, 2003, the company restated its financial statements as of and for the years ended December 31, 2001 to 2000 as well as its interim financial statements for the first three quarters of 2002 to reflect an adjustment to the company's historical accounting for CNA's investment in life settlement contracts and the related revenue recognition.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup>  On March 19, 2003, the company announced a revision to its previously reported fourth quarter and year-end 2002 results to reflect an additional $28.9 million of impairment losses on equity securities at the company's CNA Financial Corporation subsidiary.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup>  On February 16, 2006, the company restated results from 2001 through September 2005 to correct accounting for discontinued operations acquired in CNA's merger with the Continental Corp. in 1995.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup>  On March 8, 2006, the company said that financial statements for periods from 2001 to 2004 and the first three quarters of 2005 shouldn't be relied on because of accounting classification errors at its majority-owned subsidiary CNA Financial Corp, and it would restate financial statements for 2003 and 2004 and for the first three quarters of 2005 to correct the classification errors.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
