<p><b>DaVita Inc.</b> provides kidney <a href="/wiki/Dialysis" title="Dialysis">dialysis</a> services through a network of 2,753 outpatient dialysis centers in the United States, serving 206,900 patients, and 259 outpatient dialysis centers in 10 other countries serving 28,700 patients. The company primarily treats <a href="/wiki/End-stage_renal_disease" class="mw-redirect" title="End-stage renal disease">end-stage renal disease</a> (ESRD), which requires patients to undergo dialysis 3 times per week for the rest of their lives. The company has a 37% <a href="/wiki/Market_share" title="Market share">market share</a> in the U.S. dialysis market. </section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(1)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=DaVita_Inc.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History" data-section="1" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-1 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-1">
<p>The company was founded in 1979 as Medical Ambulatory Care, Inc., a subsidiary of National Medical Enterprises, Inc. (now <a href="/wiki/Tenet_Healthcare" title="Tenet Healthcare">Tenet Healthcare</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-199810K_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-199810K-5">[5]</a></sup></p><p>In August 1994, 70% of the company was acquired by <a href="/wiki/DLJ_Merchant_Banking_Partners" class="mw-redirect" title="DLJ Merchant Banking Partners">DLJ Merchant Banking Partners</a> in a <a href="/wiki/Leveraged_buyout" title="Leveraged buyout">leveraged buyout</a> for $75.5 million,<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>  including a $10.5 million investment by DLJ.<sup id="cite_ref-lbo_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-lbo-7">[7]</a></sup> The company then changed its name to Total Renal Care Holdings, Inc.
</p><p>In October 1995, the company became a <a href="/wiki/Public_company" title="Public company">public company</a> via an <a href="/wiki/Initial_public_offering" title="Initial public offering">initial public offering</a>, raising $107 million.<sup id="cite_ref-199810K_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-199810K-5">[5]</a></sup></p><p>By December 1996, DLJ had made a 386% return on its $10.5 million investment.<sup id="cite_ref-lbo_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-lbo-7">[7]</a></sup></p><p>On February 27, 1998, the company acquired Renal Treatment Centers for $1.3 billion in stock.<sup id="cite_ref-199810K_5-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-199810K-5">[5]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p><p>The integration went poorly and in July 1999, the CEO and CFO resigned. After tripling in value between 1995 and 1998, by July 1999, the stock price was down 71% year-to-date.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p><p>In October 1999, Kent J. Thiry, then 43 years old, was named CEO.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p><p>In 2000, the company sold its non-U.S. operations.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p><p>In October 2000, the company was renamed DaVita Inc.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p><p>In October 2005, the company acquired Gambro Healthcare.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p><p>In October 2014, the company agreed to pay $350 million to settle claims that it provided illegal <a href="/wiki/Kickback_(bribery)" title="Kickback (bribery)">kickbacks</a> to doctors.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p><p>In June 2015, the company agreed to pay $450 million to settle allegations that it unnecessarily disposed of drugs and then billed the U.S. federal government for this waste.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p><p>In June 2018, a jury awarded the families of 3 of the company's patients $383 million in <a href="/wiki/Wrongful_death_claim" title="Wrongful death claim">wrongful death claims</a> after the patients died from <a href="/wiki/Cardiac_arrest" title="Cardiac arrest">cardiac arrest</a> after undergoing treatment at DaVita centers.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Healthcare_Partners">Healthcare Partners</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=DaVita_Inc.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Healthcare Partners" data-section="2" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>In 2012, DaVita acquired Healthcare Partners for $4.42 billion.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> In 2014, it acquired Colorado Springs Health Partners, with 600 employees and 110,000 patients.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> In March 2016, it acquired The Everett Clinic Medical Group, a 20-site physicians practice with 315,000 patients in the Seattle area, for $385 million.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> In September 2016, Healthcare Partners was renamed DaVita Medical Group. In May 2017, it acquired WellHealth Quality Care.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> In October 2018, it agreed to pay $270 million to settle allegations that it violated the <a href="/wiki/False_Claims_Act" title="False Claims Act">False Claims Act</a> by providing inaccurate information that caused <a href="/wiki/Medicare_Advantage" title="Medicare Advantage">Medicare Advantage</a> Plans to receive inflated payments. James Swoben, a <a href="/wiki/Whistleblower" title="Whistleblower">whistleblower</a>, received $10 million.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> In June 2019, the division was sold to <a href="/wiki/UnitedHealth_Group" title="UnitedHealth Group">UnitedHealth Group</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Optum" title="Optum">Optum</a> division for $4.3 billion.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
