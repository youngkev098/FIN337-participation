<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">This article is about the company. For the person, see <a href="/wiki/John_Deere_(inventor)" title="John Deere (inventor)">John Deere (inventor)</a>. <p><b>John Deere</b> is the brand name of <b>Deere &amp; Company</b>, an American <a href="/wiki/Corporation" title="Corporation">corporation</a> that manufactures <a href="/wiki/Agricultural" class="mw-redirect" title="Agricultural">agricultural</a>, <a href="/wiki/Construction" title="Construction">construction</a>, and forestry <a href="/wiki/Machinery" class="mw-redirect" title="Machinery">machinery</a>, <a href="/wiki/Diesel_engine" title="Diesel engine">diesel engines</a>, <a href="/wiki/Drivetrain" title="Drivetrain">drivetrains</a> (axles, transmissions, gearboxes) used in heavy equipment, and lawn care equipment. In 2019, it was listed as 87th in the <a href="/wiki/Fortune_500" title="Fortune 500"><i>Fortune</i> 500</a> America's ranking<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> and was ranked 329th in the <a href="/wiki/Fortune_Global_500" title="Fortune Global 500">global</a> ranking.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> The company also provides financial services and other related activities.

</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(1)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="19th_century">19th century</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=John_Deere&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: 19th century" data-section="1" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-1 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-1">
<table class="box-More_citations_needed_section plainlinks metadata ambox mbox-small-left ambox-content ambox-Refimprove" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">This section <b>needs additional citations for <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability">verification</a></b>.  <small class="date-container"><i>(<span class="date">April 2017</span>)</i></small><small class="hide-when-compact"><i> (<a href="/wiki/Help:Maintenance_template_removal" title="Help:Maintenance template removal">Learn how and when to remove this template message</a>)</i></small></div></td></tr></tbody></table><p>Deere &amp; Company began when <a href="/wiki/John_Deere_(inventor)" title="John Deere (inventor)">John Deere</a>, born in <a href="/wiki/Rutland_(town),_Vermont" title="Rutland (town), Vermont">Rutland, Vermont</a>, United States on February 7, 1804, moved to <a href="/wiki/Grand_Detour,_Illinois" title="Grand Detour, Illinois">Grand Detour, Illinois</a> in 1836 to escape <a href="/wiki/Bankruptcy_in_the_United_States" title="Bankruptcy in the United States">bankruptcy</a> in <a href="/wiki/Vermont" title="Vermont">Vermont</a>. Already an established <a href="/wiki/Blacksmith" title="Blacksmith">blacksmith</a>, Deere opened a 1,378-square-foot (128 m<sup>2</sup>) shop in Grand Detour in 1837, which allowed him to serve as a general repairman in the village, as well as a manufacturer of tools such as pitchforks and shovels.
Tools were just a start; the item that set him apart was the self-scouring steel plow, which was pioneered in 1837 when John Deere fashioned a Scottish steel saw blade into a plow. Prior to Deere's steel plow, most farmers used iron or wooden plows to which the rich Midwestern soil stuck, so they had to be cleaned frequently. The smooth-sided steel plow solved this problem, and greatly aided migration into the American Great Plains in the 19th and early 20th centuries.
</p><p>The traditional way of doing business was to make the product as and when it was ordered. This style was very slow, As Deere realized that this was not going to be a viable business model, he increased the rate of production by manufacturing plows before putting them up for sale; this allowed customers to not only see what they were buying beforehand, but also allowed his customers to purchase his products straight away. Word of his products began to spread quickly.
</p><p>In 1842, Deere entered a business partnership with Leonard Andrus and purchased land for the construction of a new, two-story factory along the <a href="/wiki/Rock_River_(Mississippi_River)" class="mw-redirect" title="Rock River (Mississippi River)">Rock River</a> in Illinois. This factory, named the "L. Andrus Plough Manufacturer", produced about 100 plows in 1842 and around 400 plows during the next year. Deere's partnership with Andrus ended in 1848, and Deere relocated to <a href="/wiki/Moline,_Illinois" title="Moline, Illinois">Moline, Illinois</a>, to have access to the railroad and the <a href="/wiki/Mississippi_River" title="Mississippi River">Mississippi River</a>. There, Deere formed a partnership with Robert Tate and John Gould and built a 1,440-square-foot (134 m<sup>2</sup>) factory the same year. Production rose quickly, and by 1849, the Deere, Tate &amp; Gould Company was producing over 200 plows a month. A two-story addition to the plant was built, allowing further production.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a href="/wiki/File:John_Deere_logo_1876-1912.jpg" class="image"><noscript><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a6/John_Deere_logo_1876-1912.jpg/200px-John_Deere_logo_1876-1912.jpg" decoding="async" width="200" height="95" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="631" data-file-height="301"></noscript><span class="lazy-image-placeholder" style="width: 200px;height: 95px;" data-src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a6/John_Deere_logo_1876-1912.jpg/200px-John_Deere_logo_1876-1912.jpg" data-alt="" data-width="200" data-height="95" data-class="thumbimage"> </span></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:John_Deere_logo_1876-1912.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Company logo used between 1876 and 1912</div></div></div>
<p>Deere bought out Tate and Gould's interests in the company in 1853, and was joined in the business by his son <a href="/wiki/Charles_Deere" class="mw-redirect" title="Charles Deere">Charles Deere</a>. At that time, the company was manufacturing a variety of farm equipment products in addition to plows, including wagons, corn planters, and cultivators. In 1857, the company's production totals reached almost 1,120 implements per month. In 1858, a nationwide financial recession took a toll on the company. To prevent bankruptcy, the company was reorganized and Deere sold his interests in the business to his son-in-law, Christopher Webber, and his son, Charles Deere, who would take on most of his father's managerial roles. John Deere served as president of the company until 1886. The company was reorganized again in 1868, when it was incorporated as Deere &amp; Company. While the company's original stockholders were Charles Deere, Stephen Velie, George Vinton, and John Deere, Charles effectively ran the company. In 1869, Charles began to introduce marketing centers and independent retail dealers to advance the company's sales nationwide. This same year, Deere &amp; Company won "Best and Greatest Display of Plows in Variety" at the 17th Annual <a href="/wiki/Illinois_State_Fair" title="Illinois State Fair">Illinois State Fair</a>, for which it won $10 and a Silver Medal.<sup id="cite_ref-Reynolds_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Reynolds-6">[6]</a></sup></p><p>The core focus remained on the agricultural implements, but John Deere also made a few bicycles in the 1890s.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(2)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="20th_century">20th century</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=John_Deere&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: 20th century" data-section="2" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-2 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-2">
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:NOLA1903JohnDeerePlowHouse.jpg" class="image"><noscript><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/09/NOLA1903JohnDeerePlowHouse.jpg/170px-NOLA1903JohnDeerePlowHouse.jpg" decoding="async" width="170" height="194" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="526" data-file-height="600"></noscript><span class="lazy-image-placeholder" style="width: 170px;height: 194px;" data-src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/09/NOLA1903JohnDeerePlowHouse.jpg/170px-NOLA1903JohnDeerePlowHouse.jpg" data-alt="" data-width="170" data-height="194" data-class="thumbimage"> </span></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:NOLA1903JohnDeerePlowHouse.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>John Deere Plow &amp; Cultivators Co.'s <a href="/wiki/New_Orleans" title="New Orleans">New Orleans</a> House, 1903</div></div></div>
<p>Increased competition during the early <a href="/wiki/1900s_(decade)" title="1900s (decade)">1900s</a> from the new <a href="/wiki/International_Harvester_Company" class="mw-redirect" title="International Harvester Company">International Harvester Company</a> led the company to expand its offerings in the implement business, but the production of gasoline tractors came to define Deere &amp; Company's operations during the 20th century.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a href="/wiki/File:John_Deere_logo_1912-1936.jpg" class="image"><noscript><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b9/John_Deere_logo_1912-1936.jpg/200px-John_Deere_logo_1912-1936.jpg" decoding="async" width="200" height="125" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="736" data-file-height="459"></noscript><span class="lazy-image-placeholder" style="width: 200px;height: 125px;" data-src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b9/John_Deere_logo_1912-1936.jpg/200px-John_Deere_logo_1912-1936.jpg" data-alt="" data-width="200" data-height="125" data-class="thumbimage"> </span></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:John_Deere_logo_1912-1936.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Company logo used between 1912 and 1936</div></div></div> 
<p>In 1912, Deere &amp; Company president <a href="/wiki/William_Butterworth_(businessman)" title="William Butterworth (businessman)">William Butterworth</a> (Charles' son-in-law), who had replaced Charles Deere after his death in 1907, began the company's expansion into the tractor business. Deere &amp; Company briefly experimented with its own tractor models, the most successful of which was the Dain All-Wheel-Drive, but in the end decided to continue its foray into the tractor business by purchasing the <a href="/wiki/Waterloo_Gasoline_Engine_Company" title="Waterloo Gasoline Engine Company">Waterloo Gasoline Engine Company</a> in 1918, which manufactured the popular <a href="/wiki/Waterloo_Boy" class="mw-redirect" title="Waterloo Boy">Waterloo Boy</a> tractor at its facilities in <a href="/wiki/Waterloo,_Iowa" title="Waterloo, Iowa">Waterloo, Iowa</a>. Deere &amp; Company continued to sell tractors under the Waterloo Boy name until 1923, when the <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.tractordata.com/farm-tractors/000/0/2/23-john-deere-d.html">John Deere Model D</a> was introduced. The company continues to manufacture a large percentage of its tractors in Waterloo, Iowa, namely the 7R, 8R, and 9R series.
</p><p>The company produced its first combine harvester, the John Deere No. 2, in 1927. A year later, this innovation was followed up by the introduction of John Deere No. 1, a smaller machine that was more popular with customers. By 1929, the No. 1 and No. 2 were replaced by newer, lighter-weight harvesters. In the 1930s, John Deere and other farm equipment manufacturers began developing hillside harvesting technology. Harvesters now had the ability to effectively use their combines to harvest grain on hillsides with up to a 50% slope gradient.<sup id="cite_ref-blog.machinefinder.com_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-blog.machinefinder.com-8">[8]</a></sup></p><p>On an episode of the <a href="/wiki/Travel_Channel" title="Travel Channel">Travel Channel</a> series <i>Made in America</i> that profiled Deere &amp; Company, host <a href="/wiki/John_Ratzenberger" title="John Ratzenberger">John Ratzenberger</a> stated that the company never <a href="/wiki/Repossession" title="Repossession">repossessed</a> any equipment from American farmers during the <a href="/wiki/Great_Depression" title="Great Depression">Great Depression</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p><p>During World War II, the great-grandson of John Deere, Charles Deere Wiman, was president of the company, but he accepted a commission as a <a href="/wiki/Colonel" title="Colonel">colonel</a> in the U.S. Army. A replacement was hired and before returning to work at the company in late 1944, Wiman directed the farm machinery and equipment division of the <a href="/wiki/War_Production_Board" title="War Production Board">War Production Board</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-machinefinder_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-machinefinder-10">[10]</a></sup> In addition to farm machinery, John Deere manufactured military tractors, and transmissions for the <a href="/wiki/M3_Grant" class="mw-redirect" title="M3 Grant">M3 tank</a>. They also made aircraft parts, ammunition, and mobile laundry units to support the war effort.<sup id="cite_ref-livinghistoryfarm_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-livinghistoryfarm-11">[11]</a></sup></p><p>In 1947, John Deere introduced its first self-propelled combine, the model 55. It was soon followed by the smaller models 40 and 45, the larger model 95, and an even larger model 105 was introduced in the 1960s. In the mid-1950s, Deere introduced attachable corn heads, allowing crop producers to cut, shell, and clean corn in one smooth operation.<sup id="cite_ref-blog.machinefinder.com_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-blog.machinefinder.com-8">[8]</a></sup></p><p>In 1956, Deere &amp; Company bought-out the German tractor manufacturer, <a href="/wiki/Heinrich_Lanz_AG" title="Heinrich Lanz AG">Heinrich Lanz AG</a> (see <a href="/wiki/Lanz_Bulldog" title="Lanz Bulldog">Lanz Bulldog</a>).
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:John_Deere-Lanz_700.jpg" class="image"><noscript><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9d/John_Deere-Lanz_700.jpg/220px-John_Deere-Lanz_700.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="2272" data-file-height="1704"></noscript><span class="lazy-image-placeholder" style="width: 220px;height: 165px;" data-src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9d/John_Deere-Lanz_700.jpg/220px-John_Deere-Lanz_700.jpg" data-alt="" data-width="220" data-height="165" data-class="thumbimage"> </span></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:John_Deere-Lanz_700.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A John Deere-Lanz <i>700</i> tractor</div></div></div>
<p>In the last months of 1958, John Deere constructed a factory in northern Rosario, Argentina.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> In Argentina, the make was managed by Agar Cross &amp; Co.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>
John Deere made in Argentina the following models of tractors: 445,<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> 730;<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> the models of the serie 20 like 1420,<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> 2420,<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> 3420, 4420;<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> the models of the serie 30 like 2330,<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> 2530, 2730,<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> 3330,<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> 3530,<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> 4530;<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> the models of the serie 40 like 2140,<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> 3140 / 3140 DT, 3440,<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> 3540<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> and the last made in Baigorria of the serie 50 like 2850, 3350, 3550<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> until 1994.
Seventeen years later, (in 2011) the argentinian plant returns the assembly of tractors with the following models: 5036C, 5045D (45 HP) Serie 5D, 5045E (45 HP) Serie 5E, 5065E (65 HP) Serie 5E, 5075E (75 HP) Serie 5E, 5425N (77 HP) Serie 5000, 5725 (92 HP) Serie 5025, 5725HC (92 HP) Serie 5025, 5090E, 5090EH, 5076EF, 6110J, 6130J, 6145J and 6165J.
</p><p>Plus, in 2012, added in SKD/CKD format, the assembly of combine harvesters 9570 STS Serie 70, 9470 STS, 9670 STS and 9770 STS.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p><p>Also with the green line, the argentinian facility made some backhoe loaders and motor graders like 570 A/B,<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> 544 A/B,<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> 507, 308, 200<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> and the 627, 727 model tractors.
</p><p>On August 30, 1960, John Deere dealers from around the world converged on Dallas, Texas, for an unprecedented product showcase. Deere Day in Dallas, as the event was called, introduced the world to the "New Generation of Power", the company's first modern four-cylinder and six-cylinder tractors, during a day packed with high-tech presentations, live demonstrations, and a parking lot full of brand-new green and yellow machines. The line of tractors introduced that day was five years in the making, and the event itself took months to plan. Deere chose Dallas to host the event partly because it was home to facilities large enough to accommodate the 6,000 guests and the equipment they were all there to see. The <a href="/wiki/Dallas_Memorial_Auditorium" class="mw-redirect" title="Dallas Memorial Auditorium">Dallas Memorial Auditorium</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Texas_State_Fairgrounds" class="mw-redirect" title="Texas State Fairgrounds">Texas State Fairgrounds</a> Coliseum, the <a href="/wiki/Cotton_Bowl_(stadium)" title="Cotton Bowl (stadium)">Cotton Bowl</a>, and the Cotton Bowl parking lot were each the site of part of the event. During the event, a new John Deere tractor with a diamond-covered nameplate was displayed for all to see inside <a href="/wiki/Neiman-Marcus" class="mw-redirect" title="Neiman-Marcus">Neiman-Marcus</a>, a popular Dallas-based department store.
</p><p>According to information released by the company at the time of the event, John Deere dealers and key employees came to Dallas via the "largest commercial airlift of its type ever attempted." During the 24 hours leading up to the event, 16 airlines brought Deere employees and sales people from all over the United States and Canada to <a href="/wiki/Dallas_Love_Field" title="Dallas Love Field">Love Field</a> in Dallas. Bill Hewitt, then chairman and CEO of Deere &amp; Company, welcomed the dealers and introduced the new tractors. Hewitt told the guests they were about to see "a line
of entirely new tractors – completely modern in every respect – with outstanding features not duplicated in any other make of tractor."
</p><p>Since entering the tractor business in 1918, John Deere had focused on two-cylinder machines. The New Generation of Power introduced at Deere Day in Dallas was very different from anything Deere had built before. The new line of four- and six-cylinder tractors, the models 1010, 2010, 3010, and 4010, were more far more powerful than Deere's two-cylinder models, and also easier and more comfortable to operate, with conveniently located controls, better visibility, and improved seat suspension. These new tractors were also easier to service.<sup id="cite_ref-deere_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-deere-32">[32]</a></sup></p><p>The <a href="/wiki/John_Deere_4010" title="John Deere 4010">4010</a> was rated at 80 horsepower in 1960, but tested at 84 horsepower during testing trials, making it one of the most powerful two-wheel-drive farm tractors at that time. The 4010 was the predecessor to the <a href="/wiki/John_Deere_4020" class="mw-redirect" title="John Deere 4020">4020</a>, which is widely regarded as the most popular tractor ever produced by John Deere, and perhaps any tractor manufacturer in the United States. Although the 4020, which was available with Deere's optional <a href="/wiki/John_Deere_Powershift_transmission" title="John Deere Powershift transmission">Power Shift</a>, enjoyed greater popularity, the 4010 moved John Deere into the modern era of farm tractor technology and design following its successful history as a tractor manufacturer that was by the late 1950s experiencing waning market share due to its outdated technology.
</p><p>In addition to the advanced engine technology, the "10" series tractors offered many other upgrades from the older two-cylinder models they replaced, including significantly higher horsepower-to-weight ratio, advanced hydraulics, more convenient and comfortable operator stations, and many other improvements. Of the "10" series John Deere tractors introduced in 1960, the 4010 was by far the most popular, with more than 58,000 units sold from 1960 to 1963. The success of the "10" series John Deere tractors, led by the 4010, helped propel John Deere from a 23% market share in 1959 to 34% by 1964 when the 4020 was introduced, making it the top manufacturer of farm equipment in the United States.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></p><p>In 1973, Deere introduced its new 'Sound Idea' tractors, the 4030, 4230, 4430, and 4630. While these tractors were mechanically similar to the New Generation tractors they replaced, and the 4230, 4430, and 4630 used a 404-cubic-inch displacement engine like the 4020, they featured redesigned sheet metal and most importantly they were available with an optional completely integrated operator's cab that John Deere called the Sound Gard body.  This insulated cab that included a roll-over protective structure had a distinctive rounded windshield and came equipped with heat and air conditioning, as well as speakers for an optional radio. An <a href="/wiki/8-track_tape" title="8-track tape">8-track tape</a> player was also available as an option.  The 5020 was replaced by the very similar 6030 and continued in production with New Generation styling until 1977 when the 30 Series tractors were replaced by Deere's 'Iron Horses' series that included the 90-hp 4040, 110-hp 4240, 130-hp 4440, 150-HP 4640, and 180-hp 4840.  The 4240, 4440, 4640, and 4840 featured a new 466-cubic-inch displacement engine, and improvements to the cab including an optional hydraulic seat for a smoother ride. The Sound Gard body and Power Shift transmission were standard equipment on the 4840.
</p><p>In 1983, Deere introduced the 4050, 4250, 4450, 4650, and 4850.  These tractors were essentially the same machines as the Iron Horses they replaced, but with significant upgrades. They offered a new 15-speed Power Shift transmission, and were available with optional mechanical front-wheel drive featuring caster action for better traction and a tighter turning radius.  They also featured cosmetic upgrades, including a new light brown cab interior, instead of the black interior on previous models. These tractors were followed by the mechanically similar 55 and 60 series tractors before they were replaced by the Deere's completely redesigned 7000 and 8000 series tractors in the early 1990s.
</p><p>In the 1962 Illinois Manufacturers Directory (50th anniversary edition), John Deere, listed as Deere and Company, claimed a total work force of 35,000, of which 9,000 were in Illinois. The corporate headquarters were located at 1325 Third Ave. in Moline, Illinois, with six manufacturing plants located around that city and a seventh plant in Hoopston, Illinois. The six plants in Moline were listed as:
</p>
<ul><li>John Deere Harvester Works at 1100 - 13th Ave., East Moline, where 3,000 employees made agricultural implements</li>
<li>John Deere Industrial Equipment Works at 301 Third Ave., Moline, where 500 employees made earth-moving equipment</li>
<li>John Deere Malleable Works at 1335-13th Street, East Moline, where 600 employees made malleable and nodular iron castings</li>
<li>John Deere Planter Works at 501 Third Ave., Moline, where 1,000 employees made agricultural implements *John Deere Plow Works at 1225 Third Ave., Moline, where 1,100 employees made agricultural implements</li>
<li>John Deere Spreader Works at 1209-13th Ave., Moline where 800 employees made agricultural implements</li></ul><p>The John Deere Vermilion Works was located at North Sixth Ave., Hoopston, Illinois, where 140 employees were listed as making iron work and implement parts. Moline, with 42,705 residents in 1962, had the local 7,000 employees of John Deere represent 16% of the city's entire population.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></p><p>In 1969, John Deere followed its New Generation tractors of the 1960s with a New Generation of combines. These included the 3300, 4400, 6600, and 7700. These models were also the first to come with Quik-Tatch header mounting capabilities as standard equipment. In the 1980s, these combines were followed by the 4420, 6620, 7720, and 8820 that were essentially updated and improved versions of the previous models with larger capacity, a nicer cab, and easier maintenance and service. The 4420 was discontinued in 1984 and replaced by the 4425 combine imported from Germany, and the 6620, 7720, and 8820 received the Titan II updates.
</p><p>In 1989, Deere replaced the 6620, 7720, and 8820 with a new line of completely redesigned 'Maximizer' combines that included the 9400, 9500, and 9600 walker combines. These combines featured a center-mounted cab, rear-mounted engine, and more comforts in the cab. Also in 1989, Deere was inducted into the <a href="/wiki/National_Inventors_Hall_of_Fame" title="National Inventors Hall of Fame">National Inventors Hall of Fame</a>. In 1997, Deere celebrated 50 years of self-propelled combine production, and the 1997 models featured a 50th-anniversary decal. In 1998, the 9410, 9510, and 9610 were introduced. These were essentially the same machines, but with minor upgrades. Deere dealers offered '10 series' upgrades to owners of older 9000 series Maximizer combines. In 1999, Deere introduced the 50 series Maximizer combines. These machines featured significant cosmetic upgrades including a more streamlined appearance, improved ergonomics in the cab, <a href="/wiki/Power_take-off" title="Power take-off">PTO</a> shaft-style header hook-up, and the larger models were available as rotary machines which were a complete departure from the combines that Deere had built in the past.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></p><p>In the late 1970s, <a href="/wiki/International_Harvester" title="International Harvester">International Harvester</a> had pioneered rotary combines with their <a href="/wiki/Axial_flow" class="mw-redirect" title="Axial flow">Axial flow</a> machines, and were soon followed by other manufacturers, but Deere continued to build only conventional walker combines through the 1980s and 1990s. In 1999, John Deere introduced the Single-Tine Separation (STS) system on its 9550, 9650, and 9750 combines, representing a step forward in rotary combine technology. The STS system uses less horsepower and improves material handling.<sup id="cite_ref-blog.machinefinder.com_8-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-blog.machinefinder.com-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(3)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="21st_century">21st century</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=John_Deere&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: 21st century" data-section="3" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-3 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-3">
<p>As of 2018<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=John_Deere&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, Deere &amp; Company employed about 67,000 people worldwide, of which half are in the United States and Canada, and is the largest agriculture machinery company in the world. In August 2014, the company announced it was indefinitely laying off 600 of its workers at plants in Illinois, Iowa, and Kansas due to less demand for its products.<sup id="cite_ref-DeereJobs_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DeereJobs-38">[38]</a></sup> Inside the United States, the company's primary locations are its administrative center in <a href="/wiki/Moline,_Illinois" title="Moline, Illinois">Moline, Illinois</a>, and manufacturing factories in central and southeastern United States.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> As of 2016<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=John_Deere&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, the company experiments with <a href="/wiki/Electric_vehicle#On-_and_off-road_EVs" title="Electric vehicle">an electric farm tractor</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup></p><p>The logo of the leaping deer has been used by this company for over 155 years. Over the years, the logo has had minor changes and pieces removed. Some of the older style logos have the deer leaping over a log.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> The company uses different logo colors for agricultural vs. construction products. The company's agricultural products are identifiable by a distinctive shade of green paint, with the inside border being yellow. While the construction products are identifiable by a shade of black with the deer being yellow, and the inside border also being yellow.
</p><p>In September 2017, Deere &amp; Company signed a definitive agreement to acquire Blue River Technology, which is based in Sunnyvale, California and is a leader in applying <a href="/wiki/Machine_learning" title="Machine learning">machine learning</a> to agriculture. Blue River has designed and integrated computer vision and machine learning technology that will enable growers to reduce the use of herbicides by spraying only where weeds are present, optimizing the use of inputs in farming.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup></p><p>On August 29, 2019,<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> it was announced that <a href="/wiki/Samuel_R._Allen" title="Samuel R. Allen">Samuel R. Allen</a> will step down as CEO and president of John Deere. John May, president of the Worldwide Agriculture and Turf and Integrated Solutions divisions will replace him in November 2019.
</p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Use_of_the_Digital_Millennium_Copyright_Act_to_prevent_user_repairs">Use of the Digital Millennium Copyright Act to prevent user repairs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=John_Deere&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Use of the Digital Millennium Copyright Act to prevent user repairs" data-section="4" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>John Deere's license covering the internal software on tractor control computers does not allow users to modify the software. This prevents <a href="/wiki/Repairs" class="mw-redirect" title="Repairs">repairs</a> by farmers and creates a monopoly for John Deere dealerships.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> John Deere claims user repair is forbidden by the <a href="/wiki/Digital_Millennium_Copyright_Act" title="Digital Millennium Copyright Act">Digital Millennium Copyright Act</a>, through bypassing of <a href="/wiki/Digital_rights_management" title="Digital rights management">digital rights management</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Wired_2015-04-21_46-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wired_2015-04-21-46">[46]</a></sup> Groups including the <a href="/wiki/Electronic_Frontier_Foundation" title="Electronic Frontier Foundation">Electronic Frontier Foundation</a> have criticised this activity.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup>  Some farmers use Ukrainian versions of John Deere software to circumvent restrictions on repair.<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup></p>

<p>Construction equipment includes:

<p>The company manufactures lawn mowers and also is a manufacturer of consumer and commercial equipment, and <a href="/wiki/Snow_blower" title="Snow blower">snow throwers</a>, as well as a supplier of <a href="/wiki/Diesel_engine" title="Diesel engine">diesel engines</a> and <a href="/wiki/Powertrain" title="Powertrain">powertrains</a> (<a href="/wiki/Axle" title="Axle">axles</a>, <a href="/wiki/Transmission_(mechanics)" title="Transmission (mechanics)">transmissions</a>, etc.) used especially in heavy equipment.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p><p>Other products were/are <a href="/wiki/John_Deere_snowmobiles" title="John Deere snowmobiles">snowmobiles</a>, <a href="/wiki/All-terrain_vehicle" title="All-terrain vehicle">all-terrain vehicles</a>, and <a href="/wiki/StarFire_(navigation_system)" title="StarFire (navigation system)">StarFire</a> (a wide-area differential GPS).
</p><p>John Deere leasing has expanded to non-equipment loans. As of 2017, this is the leading division of John Deere. With a loan portfolio of $2 billion, it accounts for a third of John Deere's income.<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup></p>

<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Joe_Diffie" title="Joe Diffie">Joe Diffie</a> released a song in 1993 called "<a href="/wiki/John_Deere_Green" title="John Deere Green">John Deere Green</a>", a top-five hit.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Kenny_Chesney" title="Kenny Chesney">Kenny Chesney</a> released a song in 1999 called "<a href="/wiki/She_Thinks_My_Tractor%27s_Sexy" title="She Thinks My Tractor's Sexy">She Thinks My Tractor's Sexy</a>" referencing John Deere Tractors: "She ain't into cars or pick up trucks,/But if it runs like a Deere, man, her eyes light up."</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/George_Jones" title="George Jones">George Jones</a> famously drove a John Deere <a href="/wiki/Lawn_tractor" class="mw-redirect" title="Lawn tractor">lawn tractor</a> to a liquor store after his wife had taken away his car keys to keep him from driving drunk. The incident was later memorialized as part of <a href="/wiki/Country_music" title="Country music">country music</a> lore in numerous songs and videos, including Jones’ own “Honky Tonk Song” in 1996. Twelve years earlier, the video for <a href="/wiki/Hank_Williams_Jr" class="mw-redirect" title="Hank Williams Jr">Hank Williams Jr</a>.’s “All My Rowdy Friends Are Coming Over Tonight” featured George Jones riding a lawnmower. <a href="/wiki/Vince_Gill" title="Vince Gill">Vince Gill</a>’s 1993 hit “One More Last Chance” includes the line, “She might have took my car keys, but she forgot about my old John Deere.” The video ends with Gill on a John Deere tractor passing Jones on a John Deere lawnmower. The video for <a href="/wiki/John_Rich" title="John Rich">John Rich</a>’s “Country Done Come to Town” also features Jones on a riding lawnmower.<sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Alvin_Straight" title="Alvin Straight">Alvin Straight</a> rode a John Deere 240 miles in 1994, inspiring <a href="/wiki/David_Lynch" title="David Lynch">David Lynch</a>'s 1999 film <i><a href="/wiki/The_Straight_Story" title="The Straight Story">The Straight Story</a></i>.</li></ul></section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(10)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Sponsorships">Sponsorships</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=John_Deere&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Sponsorships" data-section="17" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-10 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-10">
<ul><li>The <a href="/wiki/John_Deere_Classic" title="John Deere Classic">John Deere Classic</a> is an American professional golf tournament sponsored by the company.</li>
<li>John Deere sponsored the #23 and #97 cars for <a href="/wiki/NASCAR" title="NASCAR">NASCAR</a> driver <a href="/wiki/Chad_Little" title="Chad Little">Chad Little</a> in the late 1990s.<sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup></li>
<li>John Deere sponsored the #17 car for <a href="/wiki/NASCAR" title="NASCAR">NASCAR</a> driver <a href="/wiki/Ricky_Stenhouse_Jr" class="mw-redirect" title="Ricky Stenhouse Jr">Ricky Stenhouse Jr</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Monster_Energy_NASCAR_Cup_Series" class="mw-redirect" title="Monster Energy NASCAR Cup Series">Monster Energy NASCAR Cup Series</a> in the late 2010s.</li></ul></section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(11)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Green_Magazine"><i>Green Magazine</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=John_Deere&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Green Magazine" data-section="18" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-11 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-11">
<p><i>Green Magazine</i> is a publication devoted to John Deere enthusiasts.<sup id="cite_ref-59" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup> It was begun in November 1984 by Richard and Carol Hain of <a href="/wiki/Bee,_Nebraska" title="Bee, Nebraska">Bee, Nebraska</a>.
</p><p>The first issue was mailed in early November 1984 to 135 paid subscribers, and had 10 black-and-white pages with features on tractors, letters from readers, and advertisements.<sup id="cite_ref-61" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup> At the time, the magazine was published bimonthly. It was written in <a href="/wiki/Lincoln,_Nebraska" title="Lincoln, Nebraska">Lincoln, Nebraska</a>, and it was mailed from the Bee post office.
</p><p>The magazine grew rapidly, and in 1990, bowing to public demand, it became a monthly. Circulation continued to increase, and currently hovers around 30,000. The magazine now generally contains 88 full-color pages and is perfect bound. It is now printed in Michigan and mailed from several post offices throughout the country.
</p><p>Current content usually includes a "Tip of the Month" article covering New Generation restoration written by Dan Brotzman, a "Youngtimer" article written by Tyler Buchheit, "Shop Talk" by Ron and JoAnn O'Neill, "Saw it On eBay" by Adam Smith and Benjamin Hain, "Scale Models" by Bill Proft, "What's New and Old" by Greg Stephen, "Feature Model" by Benjamin Hain, "Do You Have One of These" by Richard Hain, and "Mr. Thinker", which is said to be written by "a variety of experts".
</p>
