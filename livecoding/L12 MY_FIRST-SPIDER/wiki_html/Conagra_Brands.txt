</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(1)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Conagra_Brands&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History" data-section="1" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-1 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-1">
<p>Conagra was founded in 1919<sup id="cite_ref-Baghai_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Baghai-1">[1]</a></sup> by Frank Little and Alva Kinney, who brought together four grain mills as <b>Nebraska Consolidated Mills</b> (NCM) with its  head <a href="/wiki/Grand_Island,_Nebraska" title="Grand Island, Nebraska">Grand Island, Nebraska</a>. The headquarters were moved to Omaha in 1922. The company ran at a profit until 1936, when Kinney retired. In 1940, the company began producing <a href="/wiki/Flour" title="Flour">flour</a> at its own mill, and in 1942 ventured into the livestock feed business. That year president R. S. Dickinson opened the company's first out-of-state facility in <a href="/wiki/Alabama" title="Alabama">Alabama</a> with a flour mill and animal feed plant.
</p><p>After researching new uses for its flour, NCM funded the establishment of the <a href="/wiki/Duncan_Hines" title="Duncan Hines">Duncan Hines</a> brand of cake mixes in 1951 as a way to market more flour. This venture was very successful, leading the company to its current place as the third largest flour miller in the U.S. However, this did not lead NCM to consider other food ventures, and instead it sold its assets in Duncan Hines to <a href="/wiki/Procter_%26_Gamble" title="Procter &amp; Gamble">Procter &amp; Gamble</a> in 1956. As American households purchased more and more prepared and instant foods in the 1950s and 1960s, NCM chose not to expand into the businesses that used its flour, instead turning in the opposite direction and focusing more on raw foods like poultry and expanding its livestock feed business.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;"><a href="/wiki/File:ConAgra_Foods_Logo.png" class="image"><noscript><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7e/ConAgra_Foods_Logo.png/150px-ConAgra_Foods_Logo.png" decoding="async" width="150" height="82" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="219" data-file-height="120"></noscript><span class="lazy-image-placeholder" style="width: 150px;height: 82px;" data-src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7e/ConAgra_Foods_Logo.png/150px-ConAgra_Foods_Logo.png" data-alt="" data-width="150" data-height="82" data-class="thumbimage"> </span></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:ConAgra_Foods_Logo.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The previous Conagra Brands logo, which was used until June 2009.</div></div></div>
<p>In 1971, Nebraska Consolidated Mills changed its name to "ConAgra", a <a href="/wiki/Portmanteau" title="Portmanteau">portmanteau</a> of <i>con</i> for <i>consolidated</i> and <i>agra</i>, similarly short in English for <a href="/wiki/Agriculture" title="Agriculture">agriculture</a>. The 1970s brought the company to the brink of ruin as it lost money expanding into the fertilizer, catfish, and pet product industries and as commodity speculation wiped out ConAgra's margins on raw foods.  In 1974, <a href="/wiki/C._Michael_Harper" title="C. Michael Harper">C. Michael "Mike" Harper</a>, an experienced food industry executive, took over the firm and brought it back from the brink of bankruptcy. Nonetheless, ConAgra's business model left it vulnerable to volatile commodity prices. In response, the company set off on a two-decade-long buying spree, purchasing over one hundred prepared food brands, starting with its 1980 purchase of <a href="/wiki/Banquet_Foods" title="Banquet Foods">Banquet Foods</a>. It moved heavily into the frozen food business and the packaged meat industry, and then picked up a selection of other brands from firms like <a href="/wiki/RJR_Nabisco" title="RJR Nabisco">RJR Nabisco</a> and <a href="/wiki/Beatrice_Foods" title="Beatrice Foods">Beatrice Foods</a> (including <a href="/wiki/Hunt-Wesson" class="mw-redirect" title="Hunt-Wesson">Hunt-Wesson</a> and <a href="/wiki/Swift-Eckrich" class="mw-redirect" title="Swift-Eckrich">Swift-Eckrich</a>) among others, as the <a href="/wiki/Leveraged_buyout" title="Leveraged buyout">leveraged buyouts</a> of the 1980s resulted in the divestiture or breakup of many major American consumer product firms. In 1993 alone it purchased $500 million in smaller firms, and in 1998 it purchased another $480 million in brands from <a href="/wiki/Nabisco" title="Nabisco">Nabisco</a>.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a href="/wiki/File:ConagraHQ.jpg" class="image"><noscript><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e6/ConagraHQ.jpg/200px-ConagraHQ.jpg" decoding="async" width="200" height="133" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="3240" data-file-height="2160"></noscript><span class="lazy-image-placeholder" style="width: 200px;height: 133px;" data-src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e6/ConagraHQ.jpg/200px-ConagraHQ.jpg" data-alt="" data-width="200" data-height="133" data-class="thumbimage"> </span></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:ConagraHQ.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Conagra Brands' former headquarters in <a href="/wiki/Omaha,_Nebraska" title="Omaha, Nebraska">Omaha, Nebraska</a>.</div></div></div>
<p>On May 23, 2001, ConAgra Foods Inc (the predecessor to Conagra Brands) said it would restate its earnings for 1998, 1999 and 2000, due to accounting and conduct matters at its United Agri Products Cos unit. For fiscal 1998, revenues was cut from $24.27 billion to $24.19 billion.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p><p>In 2002, ConAgra Foods sold its fresh meat operations under the name <a href="/wiki/Swift_%26_Company" class="mw-redirect" title="Swift &amp; Company">Swift &amp; Company</a> to <a href="/wiki/Hicks,_Muse,_Tate_%26_Furst" class="mw-redirect" title="Hicks, Muse, Tate &amp; Furst">Hicks, Muse, Tate &amp; Furst</a> Inc. and Booth Creek Management.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p><p>On March 24, 2005, ConAgra Foods Inc (the predecessor to Conagra Brands) said the results for fiscal 2003 and 2004 would be restated to reflect a reduction in after-tax profits of $150 million to $200 million in total.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p><p>In 2006, ConAgra Foods sold its Chicago-area-based refrigerated meats business (<a href="/wiki/Butterball" title="Butterball">Butterball</a>, <a href="/wiki/Eckrich" title="Eckrich">Eckrich</a>, <a href="/wiki/Armour_%26_Co." class="mw-redirect" title="Armour &amp; Co.">Armour</a>) to <a href="/wiki/Smithfield_Foods" title="Smithfield Foods">Smithfield Foods</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> ConAgra maintained a presence in Chicago by moving its <a href="/wiki/Hunt-Wesson" class="mw-redirect" title="Hunt-Wesson">Hunt-Wesson</a> business there from California.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p><p>On November 27, 2012, Conagra officials announced the company was purchasing <a href="/wiki/Ralcorp" title="Ralcorp">Ralcorp</a>, pending Ralcorp shareholder approval, for approximately $4.95 billion. Stockholders of Ralcorp Holdings Inc. would receive $90 per share. The deal completed in January 2013 and made ConAgra the largest private-label packaged food business in the United States.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p><p>On October 1, 2015, Conagra announced that it would cut about 1,500 jobs and relocate its headquarters to <a href="/wiki/Chicago" title="Chicago">Chicago</a> as part of a restructuring plan.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> The move of headquarters from Omaha to Chicago was completed in late June 2016 with the opening of their new HQ at the <a href="/wiki/Merchandise_Mart" title="Merchandise Mart">Merchandise Mart</a> building. It is the first time since 1922 that ConAgra has not been headquartered in Omaha and the first time in the company's history, dating back to its 1919 founding, that the headquarters will not be in the state of Nebraska.
</p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Lamb_Weston_spin_off">Lamb Weston spin off</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Conagra_Brands&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Lamb Weston spin off" data-section="2" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>On July 14, 2014, Conagra announced that it had acquired TaiMei Potato Industry Limited, a potato processor in Shangdu, Inner Mongolia. This acquisition expands ConAgra Foods' Lamb Weston operations in a market that has growing demand for frozen potato products.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p><p>On November 18, 2015, Conagra announced it was spinning off its Lamb Weston division into a separate company that would be based in <a href="/wiki/Kennewick,_Washington" title="Kennewick, Washington">Kennewick, Washington</a>. They also announced that ConAgra Foods would be renamed Conagra Brands when they moved HQ to Chicago in 2016. Thomas Werner (unrelated to the <a href="/wiki/Tom_Werner" title="Tom Werner">television producer</a> and <a href="/wiki/Boston_Red_Sox" title="Boston Red Sox">Boston Red Sox</a> owner) and Timothy McLevish were named executive and executive chairman.<sup id="cite_ref-WSJ_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WSJ-12">[12]</a></sup></p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Lamb_Weston" title="Lamb Weston">Lamb Weston</a> (<a href="/wiki/New_York_Stock_Exchange" title="New York Stock Exchange">NYSE</a>: <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://www.nyse.com/quote/XNYS:LW">LW</a>) spinoff was completed on November 9, 2016, with the new company headquartered in the suburbs of <a href="/wiki/Boise,_Idaho" title="Boise, Idaho">Boise, Idaho</a>, with major manufacturing facilities in <a href="/wiki/Oregon" title="Oregon">Oregon</a> and <a href="/wiki/Washington_(state)" title="Washington (state)">Washington</a>. At the same time, ConAgra Foods changed their name to ConAgra Brands, complete with a brand new logo.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span id="Angie.27s_acquisition"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Angie's_acquisition">Angie's acquisition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Conagra_Brands&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Angie's acquisition" data-section="3" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>On September 22, 2017, Conagra announced that it was acquiring <a href="/wiki/Angie%27s_Kettle_Corn" title="Angie's Kettle Corn">Angie's Artisan Treats</a>, maker of Angie's Boomchickapop popcorn.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> The acquisition was completed on October 23, 2017.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Pinnacle_Foods_Acquisition">Pinnacle Foods Acquisition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Conagra_Brands&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Pinnacle Foods Acquisition" data-section="4" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>On June 27, 2018, Conagra Brands announced the acquisition of <a href="/wiki/Pinnacle_Foods" title="Pinnacle Foods">Pinnacle Foods</a> for $8.1 billion.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> The acquisition closed on October 26, 2018.
</p>

<p>The <a href="/wiki/Nonprofit" class="mw-redirect" title="Nonprofit">nonprofit</a> ConAgra Foods Foundation has pledged a five-year commitment of $10 million to <a href="/wiki/Feeding_America" title="Feeding America">Feeding America</a> to fight child hunger. Additionally, ConAgra Foods founded the <i>Child Hunger Ends Here</i> campaign in 2013 that would donate up to 3 million meals equivalent to Feeding America by donating one meal for each code submitted to the campaign's website by consumers.<sup id="cite_ref-lts-childhungerendshere_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-lts-childhungerendshere-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(5)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Criticism">Criticism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Conagra_Brands&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Criticism" data-section="8" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-5 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-5">
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Environmental_issues">Environmental issues</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Conagra_Brands&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Environmental issues" data-section="9" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>Conagra has been criticized for its lack of response to global <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" class="mw-redirect" title="Climate change">climate change</a>. A 2006 report by <a href="/wiki/Ceres_(organization)" title="Ceres (organization)">Ceres</a>, a non-profit organization that works to address global climate change and other <a href="/wiki/Sustainability" title="Sustainability">sustainability</a> issues, entitled "Corporate Governance and Climate Change: Making the Connection", measures how 100 leading global companies are responding to global warming. Companies in the report were evaluated on a 0 to 100 scale. ConAgra scored a total of 4 points, the lowest of any of the food companies rated.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> In a 2009 ranking by <i><a href="/wiki/Newsweek" title="Newsweek">Newsweek</a></i>, ConAgra ranked 342nd out of U.S. 500 largest corporations in terms of overall environmental score.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p><p>In 2003–2004, Conagra participated in a <a href="/wiki/Minnesota_Pollution_Control_Agency" title="Minnesota Pollution Control Agency">Minnesota Pollution Control Agency</a> voluntary investigation and clean-up program. Through the program, the company cleaned up a property previously used for <a href="/wiki/Lithium" title="Lithium">lithium</a> ore processing and constructed a new 80,000-square-foot (7,000 m<sup>2</sup>) office/warehouse building.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> This voluntary program offers "future liability protection".<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p><p>
On January 7, 2014, a California Superior Court found that Conagra and its co-defendants were liable in creating a public nuisance due to lead-based paint the companies sold.  Ten local governments in California filed the suit and the court ordered Conagra, <a href="/wiki/NL_Industries" title="NL Industries">NL Industries</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sherwin-Williams" title="Sherwin-Williams">Sherwin-Williams</a> to pay $1.15 billion to remove or abate the lead in homes located in those cities and counties.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup>  Although ConAgra never produced paint, it assumed the liabilities of W. P. Fuller &amp; Co., a San Francisco-based paint producer, through a series of acquisitions.<sup id="cite_ref-Bloomberg_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bloomberg-24">[24]</a></sup> The disposition of <i>The People v. ConAgra Grocery Products Company et al.</i> in the California 6th Appellate District Court on November 14, 2017 is that <style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r886047036">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style></p><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>... the judgment is reversed, and the matter is remanded to the trial court with directions to (1) recalculate the amount of the abatement fund to limit it to the amount necessary to cover the cost of remediating pre-1951 homes, and (2) hold an evidentiary hearing regarding the appointment of a suitable receiver. The Plaintiff shall recover its costs on appeal.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p></blockquote>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Labor_issues">Labor issues</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Conagra_Brands&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Labor issues" data-section="10" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>In May 2003, Conagra and its subsidiary Gilroy Foods agreed to pay $1.5 million to settle charges of hiring discrimination brought by the <a href="/wiki/Equal_Employment_Opportunity_Commission" title="Equal Employment Opportunity Commission">Equal Employment Opportunity Commission</a> (EEOC). The charges involved a July 1999 <a href="/wiki/Teamsters" class="mw-redirect" title="Teamsters">Teamsters</a> strike at a plant in <a href="/wiki/King_City,_California" title="King City, California">King City, California</a>, then owned by Basic Vegetable Products LP but later purchased by ConAgra. In August 2001, the company negotiated with the union an end to the two-year strike with a new contract that would recall workers based on seniority. However, the recall process excluded workers who were on leave at the time of the purchase including those out due to work injury or pregnancy. Others were denied jobs due to a history of previous injury or illness, despite their having no restrictions on returning to work. According to the EEOC, most of the 39 workers who were excluded from the recall process had been working at the plant for 10 to 30 years and were primarily <a href="/wiki/Hispanic" title="Hispanic">Hispanic</a> and female.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p><p>The company's <a href="/wiki/Greeley,_Colorado" title="Greeley, Colorado">Greeley, Colorado</a>, plant had been cited almost 10 times from 1999 to 2002 for violating worker safety.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p><p>In July 2004, six people were killed in a shooting inside the ConAgra Foods plant in Kansas City, Kansas.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Fraud_and_bribery">Fraud and bribery</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Conagra_Brands&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Fraud and bribery" data-section="11" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>In 1997, Conagra pleaded guilty to federal criminal charges that its Peavey Grain unit illegally sprayed water on stored <a href="/wiki/Grain" title="Grain">grain</a> to increase its weight and value and also bribed federal inspectors. The company agreed to pay $8.3 million to resolve the charges, which included a $4.4 million criminal fine, $3.45 million as compensation for illegal profits and $450,000 to reimburse the <a href="/wiki/U.S._Department_of_Agriculture" class="mw-redirect" title="U.S. Department of Agriculture">U.S. Department of Agriculture</a> for storage and investigation expenses. Conagra had also paid $2 million to settle a related civil case filed by a group of Indiana farmers.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p><p><a href="/wiki/Multinational_Monitor" title="Multinational Monitor">Multinational Monitor</a>, a corporate watchdog organization, named Conagra one of the 'Top 100 Corporate Criminals of the 1990s'.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Demolition_of_historic_site">Demolition of historic site</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Conagra_Brands&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Demolition of historic site" data-section="12" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>In 1988, Conagra threatened to relocate from Omaha to Denver, Chicago or Minneapolis if the city didn't help find a new location for its headquarters. Charles Harper, the chief executive of Conagra at the time, requested that the city of <a href="/wiki/Omaha" class="mw-redirect" title="Omaha">Omaha</a> demolish a historic site, one of the largest on the <a href="/wiki/National_Register_of_Historic_Places" title="National Register of Historic Places">National Register of Historic Places</a>.  Omaha approved the demolition of over 20 historic structures in <i><a href="/wiki/Jobbers_Canyon_Historic_District" title="Jobbers Canyon Historic District">Jobbers Canyon Historic District</a></i>, a 19th-century <a href="/wiki/Warehouse_district" class="mw-redirect" title="Warehouse district">warehouse district</a> along the banks of the <a href="/wiki/Missouri_River" title="Missouri River">Missouri River</a> in <a href="/wiki/Downtown_Omaha" title="Downtown Omaha">Downtown Omaha</a>, Nebraska.  The demolition made room for a sprawling new corporate campus and headquarters, and prompted protests and lawsuits from historic preservationists. Harper had described the structures as "some big, ugly red brick buildings."<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup>  The <a href="/wiki/National_Trust_for_Historic_Preservation" title="National Trust for Historic Preservation">National Trust for Historic Preservation</a> asked that the historic legacy of a city and region not be held hostage to the narrow corporate preferences of a single commercial enterprise, but Conagra refused to reconsider. The Jobbers Canyon district was adjacent to another historic district, the <a href="/wiki/Old_Market_(Omaha,_Nebraska)" title="Old Market (Omaha, Nebraska)">Old Market</a>, which has proved to be an important center of cultural, tourist, and residential development in Omaha. Omaha's then-planning director, Marty Shukert, said it was more important to keep the city's downtown core healthy than to keep the historic district. Others noted that the district was in disrepair and ConAgra's new headquarters have been an aesthetic and economic improvement for the city. In 2015, Conagra announced the relocation of its headquarters to the 100 year old historic Merchandise Market, in Chicago, in spite of Omaha's 1980's  demolition of Jobbers Canyon.
</p>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(6)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Genetically_modified_food">Genetically modified food</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Conagra_Brands&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Genetically modified food" data-section="13" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-6 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-6">
<p>In 2002, Conagra and other major food and beverage companies including <a href="/wiki/PepsiCo" title="PepsiCo">PepsiCo</a>, <a href="/wiki/General_Mills" title="General Mills">General Mills</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kellogg_Company" class="mw-redirect" title="Kellogg Company">Kelloggs</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sara_Lee_Corporation" title="Sara Lee Corporation">Sara Lee</a>, and <a href="/wiki/H._J._Heinz_Company" class="mw-redirect" title="H. J. Heinz Company">H. J. Heinz Co.</a> spent millions to defeat <a href="/wiki/Oregon_Ballot_Measure_27_(2002)" class="mw-redirect" title="Oregon Ballot Measure 27 (2002)">Oregon Ballot Measure 27</a>, which would have required food companies to label products that contain <a href="/wiki/Genetically_modified_food" title="Genetically modified food">genetically modified ingredients</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> According to the <a href="/wiki/Oregon_Secretary_of_State" title="Oregon Secretary of State">Oregon Secretary of State</a>, ConAgra contributed $71,000 to the campaign to defeat the <a href="/wiki/Initiative" title="Initiative">state ballot initiative</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p><p>Throughout 2012, ConAgra contributed $1,176,700 to a $46 million political campaign known as the Coalition Against the Costly Food Labeling Proposition, sponsored by food producers.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> The organization's goal was to oppose the California citizen's initiative, known as Proposition 37, demanding mandatory labeling of foods containing genetically modified ingredients.
</p><p>Although the proposition was defeated, there was strong consumer backlash against the coalition's opposition. Consumer advocates encouraged nationwide boycotts of coalition members, and movements were started in several other states to enact similar labeling requirements. As a result, ConAgra and others in the Coalition met with <a href="/wiki/Walmart" title="Walmart">Walmart</a> (the largest food retailer in the U.S.) to seek a nationwide labeling system for genetically modified foods, instead of trying to defeat the measures in every state.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(7)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Product_incidents">Product incidents</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Conagra_Brands&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Product incidents" data-section="14" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-7 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-7">

<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="2002_E._coli_outbreak">2002 E. coli outbreak</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Conagra_Brands&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: 2002 E. coli outbreak" data-section="15" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>Conagra recalled 19 million pounds of <a href="/wiki/Ground_beef" title="Ground beef">ground beef</a> in July 2002 with <i><a href="/wiki/Escherichia_coli_O157:H7" title="Escherichia coli O157:H7">E. coli</a></i> bacterial contamination. It was the third-largest recall up to that time. That meat was linked to the illnesses of 19 people in six Western and Midwestern states.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span id="2006.E2.80.932007_Salmonella_outbreak"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="2006–2007_Salmonella_outbreak">2006–2007 <i>Salmonella</i> outbreak</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Conagra_Brands&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: 2006–2007 Salmonella outbreak" data-section="16" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>In February 2007, Conagra recalled jars of <a href="/wiki/Peter_Pan_(peanut_butter)" title="Peter Pan (peanut butter)">Peter Pan</a> and <a href="/wiki/Great_Value" class="mw-redirect" title="Great Value">Great Value</a> brand <a href="/wiki/Peanut_butter" title="Peanut butter">peanut butter</a> with the product code "2111" on the lid, because they were linked to a <i><a href="/wiki/Salmonella" title="Salmonella">Salmonella</a></i> outbreak. Ultimately, the <a href="/wiki/Centers_for_Disease_Control" class="mw-redirect" title="Centers for Disease Control">Centers for Disease Control</a> (CDC) documented more than 628 individuals who were stricken with <i>Salmonella</i> poisoning in 47 states that could be traced back to Peter Pan and Great Value peanut butter. Of those, 20% were hospitalized, according to the CDC, which reported no deaths associated with the outbreak.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-37">[37]</a></sup>  Since Peter Pan (but not Great Value) is only made at one plant, the recall included all Peter Pan jars sold in the U.S. between May 2006 and February 2007. In May 2015, the company agreed to plead guilty releasing products tainted with <i>Salmonella</i> into interstate commerce. Sentencing was delayed by U.S. District Court Judge W. Louis Sands, who ordered nationwide newspaper advertisements searching for victims of the outbreak so the government could supply Victim Impact Statements for inclusion in the pre-sentence report.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Diacetyl">Diacetyl</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Conagra_Brands&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Diacetyl" data-section="17" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>On September 4, 2007, the <a href="/wiki/Flavor_and_Extract_Manufacturers_Association" title="Flavor and Extract Manufacturers Association">Flavor and Extract Manufacturers Association</a> recommended reduction of <a href="/wiki/Diacetyl" title="Diacetyl">diacetyl</a> in butter-like flavorings, such as those used in popcorn, due to cases of the potentially fatal disease <a href="/wiki/Bronchiolitis_obliterans" class="mw-redirect" title="Bronchiolitis obliterans">bronchiolitis obliterans</a> or "Popcorn Workers's Lung" appearing among plant workers exposed to diacetyl fumes, as well as in one case that involved a popcorn consumer. The next day ConAgra Foods announced that it would soon remove diacetyl from its <a href="/wiki/Jiffy_Pop" title="Jiffy Pop">Jiffy Pop</a> and <a href="/wiki/Orville_Redenbacher%27s" title="Orville Redenbacher's">Orville Redenbacher's</a> popcorn products.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="2007_Salmonella_outbreak">2007 <i>Salmonella</i> outbreak</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Conagra_Brands&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: 2007 Salmonella outbreak" data-section="18" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>On October 11, 2007, Conagra asked stores to pull the <a href="/wiki/Banquet_Foods" title="Banquet Foods">Banquet</a> and generic brand chicken and turkey pot pies due to 152 cases of <i>Salmonella</i> poisoning in 31 states being linked to the consumption of Conagra pot pies, with 20 people hospitalized. At that time, both the USDA and Conagra decided in favor of a consumer advisory and against a recall. ConAgra said the issue stemmed from pies not being cooked thoroughly in older microwaves, and that the package's heating instructions would be changed to reflect different microwaves.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> However, the plant in <a href="/wiki/Marshall,_Missouri" title="Marshall, Missouri">Marshall</a>, <a href="/wiki/Missouri" title="Missouri">Missouri</a>, where the pot pies were manufactured closed on October 11 as well.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> By October 12, a full recall was announced, affecting all varieties of frozen pot pies sold under the brands Banquet, <a href="/wiki/Albertsons_(supermarket)" class="mw-redirect" title="Albertsons (supermarket)">Albertson's</a>, <a href="/wiki/Food_Lion" title="Food Lion">Food Lion</a>, Great Value, <a href="/wiki/H-E-B#Operations" title="H-E-B">Hill Country Fare</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kirkland_Signature" class="mw-redirect" title="Kirkland Signature">Kirkwood</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kroger" title="Kroger">Kroger</a>, <a href="/wiki/Meijer" title="Meijer">Meijer</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Western_Family" class="mw-redirect" title="Western Family">Western Family</a>. The recalled pot pies included all varieties in 7-oz. single-serving packages bearing the number P-9 or "Est. 1059" printed on the side of the package.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> By October 14, 174 cases of <i>Salmonella</i> poisoning in 32 states were linked to consumption of the contaminated ConAgra pot pies, with 33 people hospitalized. Public interest groups criticized Conagra for the delay in issuing the recall, a decision which Conagra defended by saying the recall was a precaution. At the time of the recall, the USDA had still not identified the source of the <i>Salmonella</i> contamination.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> On October 17, the Colorado Department of Public Health reported that "An investigation by the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention and state public health departments involved a large cluster of illnesses caused by <i>Salmonella</i> that identified these products" and stated that, "Nationally, at least 211 individuals from 35 states have become ill."  From January 1 through December 31, 2007, the CDC identified a total of 401 cases in 41 states.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup></p>

<table role="presentation" class="mbox-small plainlinks sistersitebox" style="background-color:#f9f9f9;border:1px solid #aaa;color:#000"><tbody><tr>
<td class="mbox-text plainlist">Wikinews has related news:
<i><b><a href="https://en.wikinews.org/wiki/Explosion_at_ConAgra_plant_in_North_Carolina,_multiple_injuries" class="extiw" title="wikinews:Explosion at ConAgra plant in North Carolina, multiple injuries"> Explosion at ConAgra plant in North Carolina, multiple injuries</a></b></i></td></tr></tbody></table><p>On June 9, 2009 at 11:27 am ET, an explosion rocked the <a href="/wiki/Slim_Jim_(snack_food)" title="Slim Jim (snack food)">Slim Jim</a> manufacturing plant in <a href="/wiki/Garner,_North_Carolina" title="Garner, North Carolina">Garner</a>, <a href="/wiki/North_Carolina" title="North Carolina">North Carolina</a> resulting in the collapse of a section of the facility's roof and wall. Four workers were killed while 67 others – including three firefighters – were hospitalized for burns and exposure to <a href="/wiki/Ammonia" title="Ammonia">ammonia</a> gases. The explosion happened when natural gas was purged into the interior of the building during commissioning of a new, gas-fired water heater.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> This explosion was directly responsible for an amendment to the National Fuel Gas Code prohibiting fuel gas piping systems in large buildings from being purged indoors.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup></p><p>On March 3, 2010, ConAgra announced that the Garner plant would close in approximately 18 months, and Slim Jim production would be moved to a plant located in <a href="/wiki/Troy,_Ohio" title="Troy, Ohio">Troy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ohio" title="Ohio">Ohio</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup></p>
