</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(1)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Company_history">Company history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Universal_Health_Services&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Company history" data-section="1" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-1 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-1">
<p><a href="/wiki/Alan_B._Miller" title="Alan B. Miller">Alan B. Miller</a>, who currently serves as the company’s Chairman and Chief Executive Officer, founded Universal Health Services, Inc. in 1979 after he engineered a financial turnaround of American Medicorp, only to lose the control of the company in a hostile takeover by <a href="/wiki/Humana" title="Humana">Humana</a>. The next day, Miller founded UHS with $3.2 million from venture capitalists and $750,000 put up by himself and several former American Medicorp employees who decided to follow Miller into his new venture. Within 18 months of its founding, UHS would own four hospitals and have management contracts with two additional hospitals.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p><p>In 1979, UHS entered <a href="/wiki/Las_Vegas" title="Las Vegas">Las Vegas</a> with the purchase of <a href="/wiki/Valley_Hospital_Medical_Center" title="Valley Hospital Medical Center">Valley Hospital</a>, which has been part of UHS longer than any other hospital in the system. The acquisition gave UHS its entry into the growing southeast Nevada marketplace.<sup id="cite_ref-timeline_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-timeline-3">[3]</a></sup></p><p>In 1980, the company chose its first Board of Directors. Members included Alan B. Miller, Sidney Miller, George H. Strong, Edwin C. Cohen, Leonard Shaykin and Thomas L. Kempner.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2020)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> In 1981, UHS held its Initial Public Offering.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Annual gross revenue at the time was $119.4 million.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2020)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> In 1982, UHS purchased five hospitals from the Stewards Foundation, marking the first time a for-profit corporation purchased hospitals from a nonprofit religious organization.<sup id="cite_ref-timeline_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-timeline-3">[3]</a></sup> In 1983, UHS purchased Qualicare, Inc. for more than $116 million. The purchase included 11 acute care hospitals and four behavioral health hospitals.<sup id="cite_ref-timeline_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-timeline-3">[3]</a></sup> In 1986, UHS created Universal Health Realty Income Trust,<sup id="cite_ref-timeline_3-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-timeline-3">[3]</a></sup> the first REIT in the healthcare industry.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2020)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p><p>1991, UHS stock trading moved from NASDAQ to NYSE.<sup id="cite_ref-timeline_3-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-timeline-3">[3]</a></sup> In 1997, UHS acquired an 80% stake in <a href="/wiki/George_Washington_University_Hospital" title="George Washington University Hospital">George Washington University Hospital</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> In 1999, Debra K. Osteen was appointed vice president of UHS and president of the Behavioral Health Division.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p><p>In 2003, Steve Filton was named UHS's Chief Operating Officer.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> In 2007, UHS formed UHS Development Company, Inc. to build hospitals. Richard C. Wright was named President of the new venture.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2020)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p><p>In November 2010, UHS reached an agreement in May to acquire <a href="/wiki/Psychiatric_Solutions,_Inc." class="mw-redirect" title="Psychiatric Solutions, Inc.">Psychiatric Solutions, Inc.</a> for $3,1 billion.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> In June 2012, UHS announced its plans to acquire <i>Ascend Health Corporation</i> for $517 million.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> In February 2014, UHS bought <i>Palo Verde Mental Health</i> for an undisclosed amount, renaming the facility to Palo Verde Behavioral Health.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> In April of that year, UHS announced the acquisition of the <a href="/wiki/Psychiatric_Institute_of_Washington" title="Psychiatric Institute of Washington">Psychiatric Institute of Washington</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> In September of that year, UHS's stock joined the <a href="/wiki/S%26P_500_Index" title="S&amp;P 500 Index">S&amp;P 500 Index</a> and acquired <i>Cygnet Health Care Limited</i> for approximately $335 million.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> <h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Hospital_licenses">Hospital licenses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Universal_Health_Services&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Hospital licenses" data-section="3" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Centers_for_Medicare_and_Medicaid_Services" class="mw-redirect" title="Centers for Medicare and Medicaid Services">Centers for Medicare and Medicaid Services</a> (CMS) threatened the Rancho Springs Medical Center (<a href="/wiki/Murrieta,_California#Health" title="Murrieta, California">Murrieta</a>) and Inland Valley Regional Medical Center (<a href="/wiki/Wildomar,_California#Hospitals" title="Wildomar, California">Wildomar</a>) in California with decertification in June 2010 while the <a href="/wiki/State_of_California,_United_States" class="mw-redirect" title="State of California, United States">State of California</a> warned of a possible hospital license revocation.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup>  Universal Health Services implemented a program to address all concerns and in November 2011, the two hospitals passed a CMS Certification Survey.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> As a result, CMS rescinded its termination notice and the <a href="/wiki/California_Department_of_Public_Health" title="California Department of Public Health">California Department of Public Health</a> withdrew its license revocation notice.
</p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Allegations_of_noncompliance_with_same-sex_visitation_law">Allegations of noncompliance with same-sex visitation law</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Universal_Health_Services&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Allegations of noncompliance with same-sex visitation law" data-section="4" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>According to a petition started on change.org<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> by Terri-Ann Simonelli of <a href="/wiki/Henderson,_Nevada" title="Henderson, Nevada">Henderson, Nevada</a>, <a href="/wiki/Spring_Valley_Hospital" title="Spring Valley Hospital">Spring Valley Hospital</a> (owned and operated by UHS) claimed that their policy required power of attorney for a same-sex partner to make medical decisions on behalf of their partner. If true, this would seemingly violate new <a href="/wiki/Department_of_Health_and_Human_Services" class="mw-redirect" title="Department of Health and Human Services">Department of Health and Human Services</a> rules enabling same-sex partners to make said decisions, with or without power of attorney.
</p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Fraudulent_Medicaid_claims">Fraudulent Medicaid claims</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Universal_Health_Services&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Fraudulent Medicaid claims" data-section="5" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>In September 2012, UHS and its subsidiaries, Keystone Education and Youth Services LLC and Keystone Marion LLC d/b/a Keystone Marion Youth Center agreed to pay over $6.9 million to resolve allegations that they submitted false and fraudulent claims to Medicaid. Between October 2004 and March 2010, the entities allegedly provided substandard psychiatric counseling and treatment to adolescents in violation of the Medicaid requirements. The United States alleged that UHS falsely represented Keystone Marion Youth Center as a residential treatment facility providing inpatient psychiatric services to Medicaid enrolled children, when in fact it was a juvenile detention facility. The United States further alleged that neither a medical director nor licensed psychiatrist provided the required direction for psychiatric services or for the development of initial or continuing treatment plans. The settlement further resolved allegations that the entities filed false records or statements to Medicaid when they filed treatment plans that falsely represented the level of services that would be provided to the patients.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Buzzfeed_investigation">Buzzfeed investigation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Universal_Health_Services&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Buzzfeed investigation" data-section="6" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>On December 7, 2016, <a href="/wiki/Buzzfeed" class="mw-redirect" title="Buzzfeed">Buzzfeed</a> published a report detailing questionable practices within UHS psychiatric facilities.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup>  The report includes allegations of holding nonthreatening patients against their will, manipulative misinterpretation of patient testimonies to fit guidelines to involuntary confinement, aggressive staff layoffs and understaffing in hospitals, needless patient deaths due to understaffing and misprescription of medication, "violating a patient’s right to be discharged or holding a patient without the proper documentation", and unnecessary extension of stay times to the maximum <a href="/wiki/Medicare_(United_States)" title="Medicare (United States)">Medicare</a> payout. UHS denied the conclusions of the report;<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> its stock fell approximately 12% after publication.
</p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Cygnet_Healthcare">Cygnet Healthcare</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Universal_Health_Services&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Cygnet Healthcare" data-section="7" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>A UK subsidiary, <a href="/wiki/Cygnet_Health_Care" title="Cygnet Health Care">Cygnet Health Care</a>, was the subject of a BBC investigation that found that staff had been taunting, provoking and scaring vulnerable people.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup>   It runs 140 mental health services across the UK.  85% of its services are “rated good or outstanding by our regulators”. <sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup>  New admissions were banned at Cygnet Acer clinic after the <a href="/wiki/Care_Quality_Commission" title="Care Quality Commission">Care Quality Commission</a> found it unsafe to use.  A patient hanged herself, others self harmed, ligature points were found where patients could hang themselves and too many of the staff were untrained to deal with the highly vulnerable patients at the clinic.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p><p>The company bought four inpatient units which were previously operated by the Danshell Group in 2018. All four were condemned by the <a href="/wiki/Care_Quality_Commission" title="Care Quality Commission">Care Quality Commission</a> which raised concerns about patients’ “unexplained injuries” and high levels of restraint in 2019.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
