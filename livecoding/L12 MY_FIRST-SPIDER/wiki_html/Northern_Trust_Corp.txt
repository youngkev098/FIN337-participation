<p><b>Northern Trust Corporation</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Financial_services" title="Financial services">financial services</a> company headquartered in <a href="/wiki/Chicago,_Illinois" class="mw-redirect" title="Chicago, Illinois">Chicago, Illinois</a> that caters to corporations, institutional investors, and ultra high net worth individuals. It is one of the <a href="/wiki/List_of_largest_banks_in_the_United_States" title="List of largest banks in the United States">largest banks in the United States</a> and one of the <a href="/wiki/List_of_oldest_banks_in_continuous_operation" title="List of oldest banks in continuous operation">oldest banks in continuous operation</a>. <h3 class="in-block"><span id="Corporate_and_Institutional_Services_.28C.26IS.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Corporate_and_Institutional_Services_(C&amp;IS)">Corporate and Institutional Services (C&amp;IS)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Northern_Trust&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Corporate and Institutional Services (C&amp;IS)" data-section="2" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>C&amp;IS is a global provider of <a href="/wiki/Custodian_bank" title="Custodian bank">custodian bank</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fund_administration" title="Fund administration">fund administration</a>, investment operations outsourcing, <a href="/wiki/Investment_management" title="Investment management">investment management</a>, investment risk and analytical services, <a href="/wiki/Employee_benefits" title="Employee benefits">employee benefits</a> services, <a href="/wiki/Securities_lending" title="Securities lending">securities lending</a>, <a href="/wiki/Foreign_exchange_market" title="Foreign exchange market">foreign exchange market</a>, <a href="/wiki/Treasury_management" title="Treasury management">treasury management</a>, <a href="/wiki/Brokerage_firm" class="mw-redirect" title="Brokerage firm">brokerage firm</a> services, <a href="/wiki/Transition_management" title="Transition management">transition management</a> services, banking, and <a href="/wiki/Cash_management" title="Cash management">cash management</a> services to corporate and public <a href="/wiki/Pension_fund" title="Pension fund">pension funds</a>, foundations, <a href="/wiki/Financial_endowment" title="Financial endowment">financial endowments</a>, fund managers, <a href="/wiki/Insurance" title="Insurance">insurance</a> companies, <a href="/wiki/Sovereign_wealth_fund" title="Sovereign wealth fund">sovereign wealth funds</a>, and other <a href="/wiki/Institutional_investor" title="Institutional investor">institutional investors</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-10K_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10K-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Wealth_Management">Wealth Management</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Northern_Trust&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Wealth Management" data-section="3" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>Over 20% of the wealthiest families in the United States are clients of the company's wealth management division. It provides personal trust, investment management, custodian bank, and philanthropic services, financial consulting, guardianship and estate administration, qualified retirement plans and private and business banking. Wealth Management focuses on <a href="/wiki/High-net-worth_individual" title="High-net-worth individual">high-net-worth individuals</a> and families, business owners, executives, professionals, retirees, and established privately held businesses in its target markets with assets typically exceeding $75 million. Wealth Management services are delivered through a network of 85 offices in 18 U.S. states as well as offices in London, Guernsey, and Abu Dhabi.<sup id="cite_ref-10K_1-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10K-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span id="Northern_Trust_Asset_Management_.28NTAM.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Northern_Trust_Asset_Management_(NTAM)">Northern Trust Asset Management (NTAM)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Northern_Trust&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Northern Trust Asset Management (NTAM)" data-section="4" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>Northern Trust Asset Management provides <a href="/wiki/Investment_management" title="Investment management">investment management</a> services. It offers both <a href="/wiki/Active_management" title="Active management">active management</a> and <a href="/wiki/Passive_management" title="Passive management">passive management</a> strategies for equity and fixed income investing, as well as alternative asset classes such as <a href="/wiki/Private_equity" title="Private equity">private equity</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hedge_fund" title="Hedge fund">hedge funds</a> and multi-manager products and services.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2020)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(2)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Northern_Trust&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: History" data-section="5" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-2 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-2">
<p>Northern Trust was founded in 1889 by Byron Laflin Smith in a one-room office in the <a href="/wiki/Rookery_Building" title="Rookery Building">Rookery Building</a> in <a href="/wiki/The_Loop,_Chicago" class="mw-redirect" title="The Loop, Chicago">The Loop, Chicago</a>, with a focus on providing trust and banking services for the city's prosperous citizens. It opened on Aug.12, 1889 with 7 accounts and $137,981.25 in deposits.<sup id="cite_ref-celebrates_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-celebrates-4">[4]</a></sup> Smith provided 40% of the bank's original capitalization of $1 million, and the original 27 shareholders included such businessmen and civic leaders as <a href="/wiki/Marshall_Field" title="Marshall Field">Marshall Field</a>, <a href="/wiki/Martin_A._Ryerson" title="Martin A. Ryerson">Martin A. Ryerson</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Philip_D._Armour" class="mw-redirect" title="Philip D. Armour">Philip D. Armour</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2020)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p><p>The company's headquarters building at 50 South LaSalle Street in the Financial District of Chicago dates from 1905 and was designed by <a href="/wiki/Frost_and_Granger" class="mw-redirect" title="Frost and Granger">Frost and Granger</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>  The structure opened with five stories, but is designed to accommodate additional floors.  However, when the bank needed additional space in 1965, it opted to construct a new 14-story building in the international style to the west and selected <a href="/wiki/Cesar_Pelli" class="mw-redirect" title="Cesar Pelli">Cesar Pelli</a> as its architect.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p><p>In March 1914, Byron Laflin Smith died and his son, Solomon Albert Smith, took over the bank.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p><p>The company's conservative policies served it well during the 1920s and the company's assets actually grew during the <a href="/wiki/Great_Depression" title="Great Depression">Great Depression</a>, while many other banks suffered from <a href="/wiki/Bank_failure" title="Bank failure">bank failure</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-celebrates_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-celebrates-4">[4]</a></sup></p><p>By 1941, nearly half of all the bank's commercial accounts were drawn from outside the Chicago metropolitan area. During <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, the company took part in the government's <a href="/wiki/War_bond" title="War bond">war bond</a> drives and  provided loans for manufacturing war materials under special government programs. The war created more opportunities for the bank; all sectors of its business expanded.<sup id="cite_ref-celebrates_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-celebrates-4">[4]</a></sup></p><p>During the 1950s, Northern Trust spent heavily to develop automated banking services, including the first fully automated financial statements for trust clients.<sup id="cite_ref-celebrates_4-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-celebrates-4">[4]</a></sup></p><p>In October 1963, Solomon A. Smith died. Solomon Byron Smith became chairman of the executive committee and his brother, Edward Byron Smith, became chairman.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p><p>In 1986, the bank acquired First Lake Forest Corporation for $61 million in cash.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p><p>Edward Byron Smith retired as chief executive officer in 1979.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> He was succeeded by E. Norman "Bud" Staub.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> A few years later, Philip W. K. Sweet took over but he resigned in 1984.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p><p>In 1984, Weston Christopherson, former CEO of <a href="/wiki/Jewel_(supermarket)" title="Jewel (supermarket)">Jewel</a>, took the helm. He is credited with guiding the bank through a difficult period when sour loans to Latin American countries were hurting profits. When oil prices dropped suddenly in the early 1980s, many South American nations realized they could not repay their enormous bank loans. The bank suffered uncharacteristically high losses. Aggressive management, loan reserves, and write-offs enabled the bank to restore its asset quality. During Christopherson's six years at Northern Trust, profits rose from $34 million to $113 million.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> At the time of his retirement in 1990, the company was the 11th most profitable of the 100 largest banks in the United States.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p><p>In 1990, company veteran David W. Fox became the seventh CEO of the company.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p><p><a href="/wiki/William_A._Osborn" title="William A. Osborn">William A. Osborn</a> was named president and chief operating officer in 1993 and became chairman and chief executive officer, in addition to president, in 1995.<sup id="cite_ref-retire_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-retire-17">[17]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-end_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-end-18">[18]</a></sup></p><p>In July 2005, Northern Trust and <a href="/wiki/Enron" title="Enron">Enron</a> reached a $365 million settlement<sup id="cite_ref-WSJ2005-07-12_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WSJ2005-07-12-19">[19]</a></sup> with 20,000 employees of <a href="/wiki/Enron" title="Enron">Enron</a> after the energy company's collapse (see <a href="/wiki/Enron_scandal" title="Enron scandal">Enron scandal</a>). Norther Trust had managed the <a href="/wiki/401k" class="mw-redirect" title="401k">401k</a> plan for Enron's employeees, who alleged mismanagement and breach of <a href="/wiki/Fiduciary" title="Fiduciary">fiduciary</a> duty. The initial settlement was reduced a year later to $32.5 million in an agreement approved by Federal judge <a href="/wiki/Melinda_Harmon" title="Melinda Harmon">Melinda Harmon</a>, with Northern Trust neither admitting nor denying wrongdoing.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p><p>Osborn stepped down as president in 2006 and as CEO on January 1, 2008.<sup id="cite_ref-retire_17-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-retire-17">[17]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-end_18-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-end-18">[18]</a></sup> Frederick H. "Rick" Waddell then became chairman and CEO.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p><p>During his tenure, Waddell was able to guide the bank through the financial crisis. In 2009 Northern Trust was one of 3 banks able to gain large profits. Other than <a href="/wiki/M%26T_Bank" title="M&amp;T Bank">M&amp;T Bank</a>, Northern Trust was the only bank in the <a href="/wiki/S%26P_500_Index" title="S&amp;P 500 Index">S&amp;P 500 Index</a> not to lower its dividend during the <a href="/wiki/Financial_crisis_of_2007%E2%80%932008" class="mw-redirect" title="Financial crisis of 2007–2008">financial crisis of 2007–2008</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p><p>In November 2008, the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_the_Treasury" title="United States Department of the Treasury">United States Department of the Treasury</a> invested $1.5 billion in the company as part of the <a href="/wiki/Troubled_Asset_Relief_Program" title="Troubled Asset Relief Program">Troubled Asset Relief Program</a> and in June 2009, the company repurchased the investment from the Treasury.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> The company had been criticized for heavy spending on parties during a 2009 golf tournament that it sponsored, after receiving government funds.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p><p>The company strives to give approximately 1.5% of its pre-tax profits to charities every year. In the 10 years preceding 2014, the company gave over $120 million in support of non-profit organizations.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p><p>The company currently sponsors <a href="/wiki/The_Northern_Trust" title="The Northern Trust">The Northern Trust</a>, a <a href="/wiki/PGA_Tour" title="PGA Tour">PGA TOUR</a> event that is the first event in the <a href="/wiki/FedEx_Cup" title="FedEx Cup">FedEx Cup</a> playoffs. From 2008 through 2017, Northern Trust sponsored the Northern Trust Open in Los Angeles, also a PGA TOUR event.
</p><p>On October 2, 2017, Northern Trust acquired UBS Asset Management’s fund administration servicing units in Switzerland and Luxembourg.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p><p>Effective January 1, 2018, Michael O'Grady became chief executive officer of the company.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> He also assumed the chairmanship on January 23, 2019.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
