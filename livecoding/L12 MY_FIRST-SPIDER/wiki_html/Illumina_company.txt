Incorporated in April 1998, Illumina develops, manufactures, and markets integrated systems for the analysis of genetic variation and biological function. The company provides a line of products and services that serves the <a href="/wiki/Sequencing" title="Sequencing">sequencing</a>, <a href="/wiki/Genotyping" title="Genotyping">genotyping</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gene_expression" title="Gene expression">gene expression</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Proteomics" title="Proteomics">proteomics</a> markets. Its headquarters are located in <a href="/wiki/San_Diego" title="San Diego">San Diego</a>, <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a>.

<p>Illumina was founded in April 1998 by David Walt, <a href="/wiki/Larry_Bock" title="Larry Bock">Larry Bock</a>, John Stuelpnagel, <a href="/wiki/Anthony_Czarnik" title="Anthony Czarnik">Anthony Czarnik</a>, and Mark Chee. While working with CW Group, a venture-capital firm, Bock and Stuelpnagel uncovered what would become Illumina's BeadArray technology at <a href="/wiki/Tufts_University" title="Tufts University">Tufts University</a> and negotiated an exclusive license to that technology. In 1999, Illumina acquired Spyder Instruments (founded by Michal Lebl, Richard Houghten, and Jutta Eichler) for their technology of high-throughput synthesis. Illumina completed its initial public offering in July 2000.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p><p>Illumina began offering <a href="/wiki/Single_nucleotide_polymorphism" class="mw-redirect" title="Single nucleotide polymorphism">single nucleotide polymorphism</a> (SNP) <a href="/wiki/Genotyping" title="Genotyping">genotyping</a> services in 2001 and launched its first system, the Illumina BeadLab, in 2002, using GoldenGate Genotyping technology. Illumina currently offers microarray-based products and services for an expanding range of genetic analysis sequencing, including SNP genotyping, gene expression, and <a href="/wiki/Protein_analysis" class="mw-redirect" title="Protein analysis">protein analysis</a>. Illumina's technologies are used by a broad range of academic, government, pharmaceutical, biotechnology, and other leading institutions around the globe.
</p><p>On January 26, 2007, the company completed the acquisition of Solexa, Inc., which was founded in June 1998 by <a href="/wiki/Shankar_Balasubramanian" title="Shankar Balasubramanian">Shankar Balasubramanian</a> and <a href="/wiki/David_Klenerman" title="David Klenerman">David Klenerman</a> to develop and commercialize genome-sequencing technology invented by the founders at the University of Cambridge. Solexa INC was formed in 2005 when Solexa Ltd reversed into Lynx Therapeutics of <a href="/wiki/Hayward,_California" title="Hayward, California">Hayward</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
Illumina also uses the DNA colony sequencing technology, invented in 1997 by Pascal Mayer and Laurent Farinelli <sup id="cite_ref-DNA_colony_patents_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DNA_colony_patents-5">[5]</a></sup> and which was acquired by Solexa in 2004 from the company <a href="/wiki/Manteia_Predictive_Medicine" title="Manteia Predictive Medicine">Manteia Predictive Medicine</a>. It is being used to perform a range of analyses, including whole genome resequencing, gene-expression analysis, and small ribonucleic acid (sRNA) analysis.
</p><p>In June 2009, Illumina announced the launch of their own <a href="/wiki/Personal_genomics" title="Personal genomics">Personal</a> <a href="/wiki/Full_Genome_Sequencing" class="mw-redirect" title="Full Genome Sequencing">Full Genome Sequencing</a> Service at a depth of 30X for $48,000 per genome,<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> and a year later dropped the price to $19,500.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> As of May 2011<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Illumina,_Inc.&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, Illumina reduced the price to $4,000.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-9">[9]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated2_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated2-10">[10]</a></sup></p><p>Until 2010, Illumina sold only instruments that were labeled "for research use only"; in early 2010, Illumina obtained FDA approval for its BeadXpress system to be used in clinical tests.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> This was part of the company's strategy at the time to open its own CLIA lab and begin offering clinical genetic testing itself.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p><p>Illumina acquired Epicentre Biotechnologies, based in <a href="/wiki/Madison,_Wisconsin" title="Madison, Wisconsin">Madison, Wisconsin</a>, on January 11, 2011.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> On January 25, 2012, <a href="/wiki/Hoffmann-La_Roche" title="Hoffmann-La Roche">Hoffmann-La Roche</a> made an unsolicited bid to buy Illumina for $44.50 per share or about $5.7 billion.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Roche tried other tactics, including raising its offer (to $51.00, for about $6.8 billion).<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> Illumina rejected the offer,<sup id="cite_ref-:0_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-2">[2]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> and Roche abandoned the offer in April.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p><p>In 2014, the company announced a multimillion-dollar product, HiSeq X Ten, that it forecast would provide large-scale whole-genome sequencing for $1,000/genome.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> The company claimed that 40 such machines would be able to sequence more genomes in one year than had been produced by all other sequencers to date.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> In January 2014, Illumina already held 70% of the market for genome-sequencing machines.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> Illumina machines accounted for more than 90 percent of all DNA data produced.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p><p>On July 5, 2016, Jay Flatley, who had been CEO since 1999, assumed the role of executive chairman of the board of directors. <a href="/wiki/Francis_deSouza" title="Francis deSouza">Francis deSouza</a> took on the role of president and chief executive officer, and continues to serve on the Illumina board of directors.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p><p>In late 2015, Illumina spun off the company Grail, focused on <a href="/wiki/Blood_test" title="Blood test">blood testing</a> for <a href="/wiki/Tumor_marker" title="Tumor marker">cancer tumors</a> in the bloodstream. The company has plans in 2017 to raise $1 billion in its second round of financing, and has received funding from <a href="/wiki/Bill_Gates" title="Bill Gates">Bill Gates</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jeff_Bezos" title="Jeff Bezos">Jeff Bezos</a> investing $100 million in <a href="/wiki/Series_A_round" title="Series A round">series A funding</a>, and with Illumina maintaining a 20% holding share in Grail.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> The company is working with a blood test trial with over 120,000 women during scheduled <a href="/wiki/Mammography" title="Mammography">mammogram</a> visits in the states of <a href="/wiki/Minnesota" title="Minnesota">Minnesota</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wisconsin" title="Wisconsin">Wisconsin</a>, as well as a partnership with the <a href="/wiki/Mayo_Clinic" title="Mayo Clinic">Mayo Clinic</a>. The company Grail uses Illumina sequencing technology for tests.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> The company plans to roll out the tests by 2019, with a cost of $500 per individual.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p><p>In November 2018, the company proposed the acquisition of <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Biosciences" title="Pacific Biosciences">Pacific Biosciences</a> for $8.00 per share or around $1.2 billion in total.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> The proposed deal was abandoned on January 2, 2020, with Illumina paying Pacific a $98 million termination fee.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p>

<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="DNA_sequencing">DNA sequencing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Illumina,_Inc.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: DNA sequencing" data-section="3" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/DNA_sequencing#Illumina_(Solexa)_sequencing" title="DNA sequencing">Illumina (Solexa) sequencing</a> and <a href="/wiki/Illumina_dye_sequencing" title="Illumina dye sequencing">Illumina dye sequencing</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Illumina_MiSeq_sequencer.jpg" class="image"><noscript><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7b/Illumina_MiSeq_sequencer.jpg/220px-Illumina_MiSeq_sequencer.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="3264" data-file-height="2448"></noscript><span class="lazy-image-placeholder" style="width: 220px;height: 165px;" data-src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7b/Illumina_MiSeq_sequencer.jpg/220px-Illumina_MiSeq_sequencer.jpg" data-alt="" data-width="220" data-height="165" data-class="thumbimage"> </span></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Illumina_MiSeq_sequencer.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Illumina MiSeq sequencer</div></div></div>
<p>Illumina sells a number of high-throughput DNA sequencing systems, also known as DNA sequencers, based on technology developed by Solexa. The technology features bridge amplification to generate clusters and reversible terminators for sequence determination.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> The technology behind these sequencing systems involves ligation of fragmented DNA to a chip, followed by primer addition and sequential fluorescent <a href="/wiki/DNTP" class="mw-redirect" title="DNTP">dNTP</a> incorporation and detection.
</p>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(3)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Litigation">Litigation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Illumina,_Inc.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Litigation" data-section="4" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-3 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-3">
<table class="box-Update plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content ambox-Update" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">This section needs to be <b>updated</b>.<span class="hide-when-compact"> Please update this article to reflect recent events or newly available information.</span>  <small class="date-container"><i>(<span class="date">July 2018</span>)</i></small></div></td></tr></tbody></table><p>In 2005, co-founder and former <a href="/wiki/Chief_Scientific_Officer" class="mw-redirect" title="Chief Scientific Officer">Chief Scientific Officer</a> <a href="/wiki/Anthony_Czarnik" title="Anthony Czarnik">Anthony Czarnik</a> sued Illumina; see <a href="/wiki/Czarnik_v._Illumina_Inc." title="Czarnik v. Illumina Inc.">Czarnik v. Illumina Inc.</a>
</p><p>In 2010, <a href="/wiki/Cornell_University" title="Cornell University">Cornell University</a> and <a href="/wiki/Life_Technologies_(Thermo_Fisher_Scientific)" title="Life Technologies (Thermo Fisher Scientific)">Life Technologies</a> filed a lawsuit against Illumina, alleging that its microarray products infringed on eight patents held by the university and exclusively licensed to the start-up. The case was settled in April 2017 without any finding of fault. In September 2017 both parties asked to have the settlement reviewed, with Cornell accusing both Illumina and Life Technologies of misrepresentation and fraud.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></p><p>In February 2016, Illumina filed a lawsuit against <a href="/wiki/Oxford_Nanopore_Technologies" title="Oxford Nanopore Technologies">Oxford Nanopore Technologies</a>, which was the first company to commercialize <a href="/wiki/Nanopore_sequencing" title="Nanopore sequencing">nanopore sequencing</a>. Illumina claimed that Oxford Nanopore infringed on its patents.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></p><p>In February 2020, Illumina filed a patent infringement suit against BGI relating to its "CoolMPS" sequencing products.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
