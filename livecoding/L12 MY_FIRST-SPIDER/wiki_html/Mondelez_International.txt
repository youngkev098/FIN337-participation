<p><b>Mondelez International, Inc.</b> (<span class="rt-commentedText nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˌ/: secondary stress follows">ˌ</span><span title="'m' in 'my'">m</span><span title="/oʊ/: 'o' in 'code'">oʊ</span><span title="'n' in 'nigh'">n</span><span title="'d' in 'dye'">d</span><span title="/ə/: 'a' in 'about'">ə</span><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'l' in 'lie'">l</span><span title="/iː/: 'ee' in 'fleece'">iː</span><span title="'z' in 'zoom'">z</span></span>/</a></span></span>),<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> often stylized as <b>Mondelēz</b>, is an American <a href="/wiki/Multinational_corporation" title="Multinational corporation">multinational</a> confectionery, <a href="/wiki/Food_industry" title="Food industry">food</a>, holding and <a href="/wiki/Drink_industry" title="Drink industry">beverage</a> company based in <a href="/wiki/Chicago" title="Chicago">Chicago, Illinois</a> which employs approximately 83,000 individuals around the world. It consists of the global <a href="/wiki/Convenience_food" title="Convenience food">snack</a> and food brands of <a href="/wiki/Kraft_Foods_Inc." title="Kraft Foods Inc.">Kraft Foods Inc.</a> after the October 2012 spin-off of its North American <a href="/wiki/Grocery" class="mw-redirect" title="Grocery">grocery</a>-foods products. The Mondelez name is a coined word adopted in 2012 that was suggested by Kraft Foods employees, and is derived from the <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> word <i>mundus</i> ("world") and href="https://www.mondelezinternational.com">www<wbr></wbr>.mondelezinternational<wbr></wbr>.com</a></span></td></tr></tbody></table><p>The company, headquartered near <a href="/wiki/Chicago" title="Chicago">Chicago</a>, manufactures chocolate, cookies, biscuits, gum, confectionery, and powdered beverages. Mondelez International's portfolio includes several billion-dollar components and includes cookie and cracker brands <a href="/wiki/Belvita" title="Belvita">Belvita</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chips_Ahoy!" title="Chips Ahoy!">Chips Ahoy!</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nabisco" title="Nabisco">Nabisco</a>, <a href="/wiki/Oreo" title="Oreo">Oreo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ritz_Crackers" title="Ritz Crackers">Ritz</a>, <a href="/wiki/TUC_(cracker)" title="TUC (cracker)">TUC</a>, <a href="/wiki/Triscuit" title="Triscuit">Triscuit</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lef%C3%A8vre-Utile" title="Lefèvre-Utile">LU</a>, Club Social, <a href="/wiki/Barny_Cakes" title="Barny Cakes">Barny</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Peek_Freans" title="Peek Freans">Peek Freans</a>; chocolate brands <a href="/wiki/Milka" title="Milka">Milka</a>, <a href="/wiki/C%C3%B4te_d%27Or_(brand)" class="mw-redirect" title="Côte d'Or (brand)">Côte d'Or</a>, <a href="/wiki/Toblerone" title="Toblerone">Toblerone</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cadbury" title="Cadbury">Cadbury</a>, <a href="/wiki/Green_%26_Black%27s" title="Green &amp; Black's">Green &amp; Black's</a>, <a href="/wiki/Freia_(chocolate)" title="Freia (chocolate)">Freia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Marabou_(chocolate)" title="Marabou (chocolate)">Marabou</a>, <a href="/wiki/J._S._Fry_%26_Sons" title="J. S. Fry &amp; Sons">Fry's</a>, and Lacta; gum and cough drop brands <a href="/wiki/Trident_(gum)" title="Trident (gum)">Trident</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dentyne" title="Dentyne">Dentyne</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chiclets" title="Chiclets">Chiclets</a>, <a href="/wiki/Halls_(cough_drop)" title="Halls (cough drop)">Halls</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Stride_(gum)" title="Stride (gum)">Stride</a>; as well as Tate's Bake Shop and powdered beverage brand <a href="/wiki/Tang_(drink_mix)" title="Tang (drink mix)">Tang</a>.
</p><p>The company has an annual revenue of about $26 billion and operates in approximately 160 countries.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> The company ranked No. 117 in the 2018 <a href="/wiki/Fortune_500" title="Fortune 500">Fortune 500</a> list of the largest United States corporations by total revenue.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p><p>Mondelez Canada holds the rights to Christie Brown and Company, which consists of brands such as <a href="/wiki/Mr._Christie" class="mw-redirect" title="Mr. Christie">Mr. Christie</a> and Dad's Cookies. Its head office is in <a href="/wiki/Mississauga" title="Mississauga">Mississauga</a>, Ontario, with operations in <a href="/wiki/Toronto" title="Toronto">Toronto</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hamilton,_Ontario" title="Hamilton, Ontario">Hamilton</a>, Ontario and <a href="/wiki/Montreal" title="Montreal">Montreal</a>, Quebec.

</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(1)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mondelez_International&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History" data-section="1" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-1 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-1">
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Before_Mondelez_International">Before Mondelez International</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mondelez_International&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Before Mondelez International" data-section="2" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Kraft_Foods_Inc.#History" title="Kraft Foods Inc.">Kraft Foods Inc. § History</a></div>
<p>Mondelez International is rooted in the <a href="/wiki/National_Dairy_Products_Corporation" class="mw-redirect" title="National Dairy Products Corporation">National Dairy Products Corporation</a> (National Dairy), which was founded on December 10, 1923, by Thomas H. McInnerney and Edward E. Rieck. The firm was initially set up to execute on a <a href="/wiki/Rollup" title="Rollup">rollup</a> strategy in the fragmented United States <a href="/wiki/Ice_cream" title="Ice cream">ice cream</a> industry.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p><p>In 1924, Kraft Cheese Company was founded and was listed on the <a href="/wiki/Chicago_Stock_Exchange" title="Chicago Stock Exchange">Chicago Stock Exchange</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-kficompany_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kficompany-9">[9]</a></sup> Two years later, it was listed on the <a href="/wiki/New_York_Stock_Exchange" title="New York Stock Exchange">New York Stock Exchange</a>. In 1928, it acquired Phenix Cheese Company, the maker of a <a href="/wiki/Cream_cheese" title="Cream cheese">cream cheese</a> branded as <a href="/wiki/Cream_cheese#Origin" title="Cream cheese">Philadelphia cream cheese</a>, founded by Jason F. Whitney, Sr. and the company changed its name to Kraft-Phenix Cheese Company.
</p><p>In 1930, National Dairy acquired Kraft Phenix. After the acquisition, the combined company retained the National Dairy name and management, though the Kraft Phenix side of the company continued to operate largely independently.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-kficompany_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kficompany-9">[9]</a></sup></p><p>On September 7, 2009, Kraft made a <a href="/wiki/Hostile_takeover" class="mw-redirect" title="Hostile takeover">hostile</a> £10.2 billion takeover bid for the British confectionery group <a href="/wiki/Cadbury" title="Cadbury">Cadbury</a>, makers of <a href="/wiki/Cadbury_Dairy_Milk" title="Cadbury Dairy Milk">Dairy Milk</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bournville_(chocolate_bar)" title="Bournville (chocolate bar)">Bournville</a> chocolate.<sup id="cite_ref-Cadbury_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cadbury-11">[11]</a></sup> On November 9 the company's bid (then £9.8 billion) was rejected by Cadbury, which called it a "derisory" offer.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Kraft renewed the offer on December 4.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> It had significant political and public opposition in the United Kingdom and abroad, leading to a call for the government to implement economic <a href="/wiki/Protectionism" title="Protectionism">protectionism</a> in large-company takeovers.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> On January 19, 2010, Cadbury approved a revised offer from Kraft which valued the company at £11.5 billion ($19.5 billion). Some funds for the takeover were provided by the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Bank_of_Scotland" title="Royal Bank of Scotland">Royal Bank of Scotland</a>, the British partially state-owned bank.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p><p>Cadbury sales were flat after Kraft's acquisition. Despite the Cadbury takeover's helping to boost sales by 30 percent, Kraft's net profit for the fourth quarter fell 24 percent (to $540 million) due to costs associated with integrating the UK business after the acquisition.<sup id="cite_ref-bbc.co.uk_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bbc.co.uk-16">[16]</a></sup> Kraft spent $1.3 billion on integration to achieve an estimated $675 million in annual savings by the end of 2012.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> Kraft increased prices to offset rising commodity costs (for corn, sugar, and cocoa) in North America and Europe. According to Rosenfeld, "We expect it will remain weak for the foreseeable future". Taking into account integration costs, the acquisition reduced Kraft's earnings per share by about 33% immediately after the Cadbury purchase.<sup id="cite_ref-bbc.co.uk_16-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bbc.co.uk-16">[16]</a></sup></p><p>In August 2011 Kraft Foods announced plans to split into two publicly traded companies, an international snack-food company and a North American grocery company.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p><p>The snack-food company, Mondelez International, would be the legal successor of the old Kraft Foods, while the grocery company would be a new company, <a href="/wiki/Kraft_Foods" title="Kraft Foods">Kraft Foods Group</a>. The split was completed in October 2012. It was structured so that Kraft Foods changed its name to Mondelez International and spun off Kraft Foods Group as a new publicly traded company.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> Kraft Foods Group later merged with <a href="/wiki/Heinz" title="Heinz">Heinz</a> to become <a href="/wiki/Kraft_Heinz" title="Kraft Heinz">Kraft Heinz</a>. 
</p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Mondelez_International">Mondelez International</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mondelez_International&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Mondelez International" data-section="3" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>In 2014, the company announced a merger of its coffee business with the Dutch firm <a href="/wiki/Douwe_Egberts" title="Douwe Egberts">Douwe Egberts</a>;<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> the company would be named <a href="/wiki/Jacobs_Douwe_Egberts" title="Jacobs Douwe Egberts">Jacobs Douwe Egberts</a>. The merger was confirmed on May 6, 2014, and completed on July 2, 2015.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p><p>On June 30, 2016, Mondelez made a $23 billion offer to buy its smaller rival, <a href="/wiki/The_Hershey_Company" title="The Hershey Company">Hershey</a>. The half-cash, half-stock deal valued Hershey stock at $107 a share. Hershey's board, however, unanimously rejected the offer.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p><p>In August 2017, it was announced that <a href="/wiki/Dirk_Van_de_Put" title="Dirk Van de Put">Dirk Van de Put</a>, Belgian CEO of <a href="/wiki/McCain_Foods" title="McCain Foods">McCain Foods</a>, would succeed <a href="/wiki/Irene_Rosenfeld" title="Irene Rosenfeld">Irene Rosenfeld</a> as CEO in November 2017.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p><p>On May 6, 2018, Mondelez agreed to buy cookie maker Tate's Bake Shop for approximately $500 million.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> The acquisition was completed in June 7, 2018.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p><p>On June 19, 2019, Mondelez agreed to acquire a majority interest in Perfect Snacks, owner of refrigerated protein bar Perfect Bar.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> The acquisition was completed on July 16.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p><p>On February 25, 2020, Mondelez announced that it was acquiring a majority stake in Toronto-based Give &amp; Go, a maker of two-bite brownies.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> The acquisition was completed on April 3, 2020.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>

<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Deforestation">Deforestation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mondelez_International&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Deforestation" data-section="6" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>In September 2017, an investigation<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> conducted by NGO <a href="/wiki/Mighty_Earth" class="mw-redirect" title="Mighty Earth">Mighty Earth</a> found that a large amount of the cocoa used in chocolate produced by Mondelez and other major chocolate companies was grown illegally in <a href="/wiki/National_park" title="National park">national parks</a> and other protected areas in <a href="/wiki/Ivory_Coast" title="Ivory Coast">Ivory Coast</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ghana" title="Ghana">Ghana</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> The countries are the world's two largest <a href="/wiki/Cocoa_bean" title="Cocoa bean">cocoa</a> producers.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></p><p>The report documents how in several national parks and other protected areas, 90% or more of the land mass has been converted to cocoa.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> Less than four percent of Ivory Coast remains densely forested, and the chocolate companies' laissez-faire approach to sourcing has driven extensive <a href="/wiki/Deforestation" title="Deforestation">deforestation</a> in Ghana as well.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> In Ivory Coast, deforestation has pushed <a href="/wiki/Common_chimpanzee" class="mw-redirect" title="Common chimpanzee">chimpanzees</a> into just a few small pockets, and reduced the country's <a href="/wiki/Elephant" title="Elephant">elephant</a> population from several hundred thousand to about 200–400.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup></p><p>In November 2018, an investigation by <a href="/wiki/Greenpeace_International" class="mw-redirect" title="Greenpeace International">Greenpeace International</a> found that palm oil suppliers to Mondelez International cleared over 70,000 hectares of rainforest from 2015 to 2017.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Wheat_futures_price-fixing_allegation">Wheat futures price-fixing allegation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mondelez_International&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Wheat futures price-fixing allegation" data-section="7" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>In April 2015, the US Commodity and Futures Commission (CTFC) alleged that Mondelez International and its former subsidiary, Mondelez Global, bought $90 million (£61 million) of wheat futures with no intention of taking delivery. According to the CTFC, the purchase raised the price of the commodity and earned the company $5.4 million.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup></p>
