</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(1)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Freeport-McMoRan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History" data-section="1" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-1 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-1">
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Freeport_Sulphur_No._6.jpg" class="image"><noscript><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bf/Freeport_Sulphur_No._6.jpg/220px-Freeport_Sulphur_No._6.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="155" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="660" data-file-height="466"></noscript><span class="lazy-image-placeholder" style="width: 220px;height: 155px;" data-src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bf/Freeport_Sulphur_No._6.jpg/220px-Freeport_Sulphur_No._6.jpg" data-alt="" data-width="220" data-height="155" data-class="thumbimage"> </span></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Freeport_Sulphur_No._6.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Freeport Sulphur No.6 entering <a href="/wiki/Freeport,_Texas" title="Freeport, Texas">Freeport, Texas</a>, harbor, 1923</div></div></div>
<p>The current company was created in 1981 through the merger of <b>Freeport Minerals</b>, formerly <b>Texas Freeport Sulphur Company</b> and <b>McMoRan Oil &amp; Gas Company</b>, becoming Freeport-McMoRan Inc.
</p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_history">Early history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Freeport-McMoRan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Early history" data-section="2" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>Freeport Sulphur Company was founded July 12, 1912 by the eldest son of <a href="/wiki/Swante_M._Swenson" title="Swante M. Swenson">Svante Magnus "E.M." Swenson</a>, <a href="/wiki/Banker" class="mw-redirect" title="Banker">banker</a> Eric Pierson Swenson, with a group of investors, to develop <a href="/wiki/Sulfur" title="Sulfur">sulfur</a> mining at Brian Mound <a href="/wiki/Salt_dome" title="Salt dome">salt dome</a>, along the US <a href="/wiki/Gulf_Coast" class="mw-redirect" title="Gulf Coast">Gulf Coast</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Freeport,_Texas" title="Freeport, Texas">Freeport, Texas</a> was also established in Nov. 1912 to house workers, and serve as a port for Houston, rivaling Galveston and Corpus Christi.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Haynes_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Haynes-4">[4]</a></sup></p><p>Freeport mined <a href="/wiki/Sulphur" class="mw-redirect" title="Sulphur">sulphur</a> along the Gulf Coast using the <a href="/wiki/Frasch_Process" class="mw-redirect" title="Frasch Process">Frasch Process</a>, the patents for which had expired in 1908.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Previously, <a href="/wiki/Union_Sulphur_Company" title="Union Sulphur Company">Union Sulphur Company</a> founder and patent-holder <a href="/wiki/Herman_Frasch" title="Herman Frasch">Herman Frasch</a> had enjoyed a monopoly on the process.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> The company became known as <b>Freeport Sulphur</b>, later changing its corporate name to <b>Freeport Minerals</b>.
</p><p>In 1922, Freeport started producing sulphur from Hoskins Mound in <a href="/wiki/Brazoria_County,_Texas" title="Brazoria County, Texas">Brazoria County, Texas</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Haynes_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Haynes-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>111-117</span></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Freeport_Texas_Company">Freeport Texas Company</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Freeport-McMoRan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Freeport Texas Company" data-section="3" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>Enterprise to support Freeport Sulphur's business and the new town's <a href="/wiki/Infrastructure" title="Infrastructure">infrastructure</a> led to the incorporation of a holding company on September 30, 1913, to join the newer assets with Freeport Sulphur. Officers of the new holding company, <b>Freeport Texas Company</b>, were:
</p>

<li><a href="/wiki/Samuel_McRoberts_(U.S._general)" title="Samuel McRoberts (U.S. general)">Samuel McRoberts</a>, Director</li>
<li>Charles P. Northrop, Director</li>
<li><a href="/w/index.php?title=Charles_A._Stone&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Charles A. Stone (page does not exist)">Charles A. Stone</a>, Director</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Frank_A._Vanderlip" title="Frank A. Vanderlip">Frank A. Vanderlip</a>, Director</li>

</p>

<li>Houston &amp; Brazoz Valley Ry<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></li></ul><p>In 1919, minority stockholders <a href="/wiki/Langbourne_Meade_Williams_Jr." title="Langbourne Meade Williams Jr.">John R. Williams &amp; Sons</a>, First National Bank of Richmond, Virginia vice-president, W. M. Addison, <a href="/wiki/The_News_Leader" title="The News Leader">Benjamin P Alsopp</a>, <a href="/wiki/Equitable_Trust_Company" class="mw-redirect" title="Equitable Trust Company">E. L. <h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="1928_-_1931_shareholder_proxy_fight">1928 - 1931 shareholder proxy fight</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Freeport-McMoRan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: 1928 - 1931 shareholder proxy fight" data-section="4" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>In 1928, shareholder and scion of one of the founding investment firms, John Langbourne Williams &amp; Sons, <a href="/wiki/Langbourne_Meade_Williams,_Jr." class="mw-redirect" title="Langbourne Meade Williams, Jr.">Langbourne Meade Williams, Jr.</a> launched a <a href="/wiki/Proxy_fight" title="Proxy fight">proxy fight</a> for control of the company. In 1929, he then sought help from his former supervisor at <a href="/wiki/Lee,_Higginson_%26_Co." title="Lee, Higginson &amp; Co.">Lee, Higginson &amp; Co.</a>, J.T. Claiborne, who then enlisted clerk <a href="/wiki/John_Hay_Whitney" title="John Hay Whitney">John Hay Whitney</a> — who had become one of the wealthiest men in America following the 1927 death of his father, <a href="/wiki/Payne_Whitney" title="Payne Whitney">Payne Whitney</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Williams eventually gained control of the company from founder Swenson, becoming its president in 1931, with Claiborne as a Vice-President, and Whitney as Chairman. Williams also served as Chairman during 1958 - 1967.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-williams_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-williams-13">[13]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p><p>Williams led the company's diversification, beginning with the purchase of <a href="/wiki/Manganese" title="Manganese">manganese</a> deposits in <a href="/wiki/Oriente_Province" title="Oriente Province">Oriente Province</a>, Cuba.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="1930s">1930s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Freeport-McMoRan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: 1930s" data-section="5" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>In 1932, Freeport Sulphur Company acquired the sulphur rights for Lake Grande Ecaille and vicinity in <a href="/wiki/Plaquemines_Parish" class="mw-redirect" title="Plaquemines Parish">Plaquemines Parish</a>, Louisiana, and escalated the development of sulphur deposits in the Grand Ecaille dome in 1933, still using the <a href="/wiki/Frasch_Process" class="mw-redirect" title="Frasch Process">Frasch Process</a> developed by Dr. <a href="/wiki/Herman_Frasch" title="Herman Frasch">Herman Frasch</a>, who had, in 1895, enjoined the American Sulphur Company into a partnership, forming the <a href="/wiki/Union_Sulphur_Company" title="Union Sulphur Company">Union Sulphur Company</a>, to initiate the first successful sulphur mining at Grand Ecaille, with which Freeport, like other competitors, would compete upon expiry of the Frasch patents in 1908.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> From its earliest inception, sulphur mining was the catalyst that developed <a href="/wiki/Port_Sulphur,_Louisiana" title="Port Sulphur, Louisiana">Port Sulphur, Louisiana</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="1950s">1950s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Freeport-McMoRan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: 1950s" data-section="6" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>The company produced <a href="/wiki/Nickel" title="Nickel">nickel</a> during World War II and <a href="/wiki/Potash" title="Potash">potash</a> in the 1950s.<sup id="cite_ref-fundinguniverse_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fundinguniverse-18">[18]</a></sup> In 1955, Freeport Nickel invested $119 million, of which $100 million came from the U.S. government, into  construction of a nickel-<a href="/wiki/Cobalt" title="Cobalt">cobalt</a> mine at Moa Bay, <a href="/wiki/Cuba" title="Cuba">Cuba</a>, and a refinery at Port Nickel, <a href="/wiki/Louisiana" title="Louisiana">Louisiana</a>. On March 11, 1957, the U.S. government announced a contract to buy nickel and cobalt from the company.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p><p>In 1956, the company formed the Freeport Oil Company.<sup id="cite_ref-fundinguniverse_18-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fundinguniverse-18">[18]</a></sup> In 1958, the company sold an oil discovery near Lake Washington in <a href="/wiki/Louisiana" title="Louisiana">Louisiana</a> for approximately $100 million to <a href="/wiki/Magnolia_Petroleum_Company" title="Magnolia Petroleum Company">Magnolia Petroleum Company</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-fundinguniverse_18-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fundinguniverse-18">[18]</a></sup></p><p>In 1959, Freeport <a href="/wiki/Geologists" class="mw-redirect" title="Geologists">geologists</a> confirmed the 1936 Dutch discovery of the rich Ertsberg copper and gold deposits, now known as the <a href="/wiki/Grasberg_mine" title="Grasberg mine">Grasberg mine</a>, in extremely rugged, remote country in the <a href="/wiki/Jayawijaya_Mountains" title="Jayawijaya Mountains">Jayawijaya Mountains</a> in what was then called the <a href="/wiki/Netherlands_New_Guinea" title="Netherlands New Guinea">Netherlands New Guinea</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-timeline_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-timeline-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="1960s">1960s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Freeport-McMoRan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: 1960s" data-section="7" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<ul><li>In 1960, <a href="/wiki/Fidel_Castro" title="Fidel Castro">Fidel Castro</a> implemented a 25% <a href="/wiki/Ore" title="Ore">ore</a> tax, effectively nationalizing and seizing Freeport's nickel-mining operations in Cuba.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></li></ul><ul><li>In 1961, the company entered the <a href="/wiki/Kaolin" class="mw-redirect" title="Kaolin">kaolin</a> business after purchasing the assets of Southern Clays Inc.<sup id="cite_ref-fundinguniverse_18-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fundinguniverse-18">[18]</a></sup> In 1964, the company formed Freeport of Australia to pursue mining opportunities there and in the surrounding <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Ocean" title="Pacific Ocean">Pacific Ocean</a> region.<sup id="cite_ref-fundinguniverse_18-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fundinguniverse-18">[18]</a></sup></li></ul><h4 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Development_of_the_Ertsberg_deposit">Development of the Ertsberg deposit</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Freeport-McMoRan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Development of the Ertsberg deposit" data-section="8" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h4>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Grasberg_mine" title="Grasberg mine">Grasberg mine</a></div>
<p>In 1967, the company negotiated a contract with the Indonesian government to develop the Ertsberg deposit.<sup id="cite_ref-timeline_21-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-timeline-21">[21]</a></sup> In their <a href="/wiki/Feasibility_study" title="Feasibility study">feasibility study</a>, Freeport geologists estimated that the orebody totaled 33 million tons averaging 2.5% copper, making it the largest above-ground copper deposit ever discovered.<sup id="cite_ref-openingertsberg_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-openingertsberg-24">[24]</a></sup> Construction of an <a href="/wiki/Open_pit" class="mw-redirect" title="Open pit">open pit</a> mine began in May 1970 and in mid-1973 the mine was declared fully operational. Officials at <a href="/wiki/Bechtel" title="Bechtel">Bechtel</a>, the primary project contractor, called mine development at Ertsberg "the most difficult engineering project they had ever undertaken." The challenges included building a 101-kilometre (63 mi) long access road (a project that required boring kilometer long <a href="/wiki/Tunnels" class="mw-redirect" title="Tunnels">tunnels</a> through two mountains) and constructing the world's longest single span <a href="/wiki/Aerial_tramway" title="Aerial tramway">aerial tramway</a>. The tramways were needed to move people, supplies and ore because a 2,000-foot (610 m) cliff separates the Ertsberg mine (at 12,000 feet (3,700 m) elevation) from the mill (at 10,000 feet). Moving copper concentrate from that mill to the shipping port required installation of a 109-kilometre (68 mi) <a href="/wiki/Slurry" title="Slurry">slurry</a> <a href="/wiki/Pipeline_transport" title="Pipeline transport">pipeline</a> — then the world's longest. Mine construction and startup cost about US$200 million. The Ertsberg project was an engineering marvel, but the mine's early financial performance was disappointing. Depressed copper prices and high operating costs kept profits marginal during the 1970s.<sup id="cite_ref-openingertsberg_24-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-openingertsberg-24">[24]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-timeline_21-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-timeline-21">[21]</a></sup></p><p>In 1969, McMoRan Exploration Company was founded, which, in 1981, would merge with Freeport Minerals, formerly Freeport Sulphur, to form Freeport-McMoRan.
</p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="1970s">1970s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Freeport-McMoRan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: 1970s" data-section="9" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>In 1971, the company changed its name to Freeport Minerals Company, (not to be confused with Freeport Minerals Corporation, founded in 1834).
</p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="1980s">1980s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Freeport-McMoRan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: 1980s" data-section="10" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>In 1981, Freeport Minerals Company merged with the McMoRan Oil and Gas Company. The McMoRan Oil and Gas Company was founded in 1967 by three partners, William Kennon McWilliams Jr. ("Mc"), James Robert (Jim Bob) Moffett ("Mo"), who were both <a href="/wiki/Petroleum_geologist" title="Petroleum geologist">petroleum geologists</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Mack_Rankin" title="Mack Rankin">Mack Rankin</a> ("Ran"), "a specialist in land-leasing and sales operations."<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p><p>In 1981, the company formed a 70/30 joint venture with an affiliate of <a href="/wiki/FMC_Corporation" title="FMC Corporation">FMC Corporation</a> to operate the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Jerritt_Canyon_gold_mine&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Jerritt Canyon gold mine (page does not exist)">Jerritt Canyon gold mine</a> near <a href="/wiki/Elko,_Nevada" title="Elko, Nevada">Elko, Nevada</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> In 1985, the company headquarters moved to <a href="/wiki/New_Orleans" title="New Orleans">New Orleans</a>, <a href="/wiki/Louisiana" title="Louisiana">Louisiana</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-williams_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-williams-13">[13]</a></sup> The company also sold a 25% interest in oil and gas assets primarily in the western United States to <a href="/wiki/Britoil" title="Britoil">Britoil</a> for $73.5 million.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p><p>In 1989, the company sold about $1.5 billion in assets to finance development of the <a href="/wiki/Grasberg_mine" title="Grasberg mine">Grasberg mine</a> and the Main Pass offshore sulfur-oil-gas deposit off Louisiana.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="1990s">1990s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Freeport-McMoRan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: 1990s" data-section="11" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>In 1994, the company completed the <a href="/wiki/Corporate_spin-off" title="Corporate spin-off">corporate spin-off</a> of its entire interest in Freeport-McMoRan Copper &amp; Gold, which owned the Grasberg mine.
</p><p>In 1995, RTZ, a predecessor of <a href="/wiki/Rio_Tinto_Group" class="mw-redirect" title="Rio Tinto Group">Rio Tinto Group</a> made a $450 million investment in the company.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p><p>In 1997, <a href="/wiki/IMC_Global" title="IMC Global">IMC Global</a>, a large fertilizer producer, acquired Freeport-McMoRan Inc., the former parent company that now owned the sulfur and fertilizer businesses, in a $750 million transaction. Shareholders of Freeport-McMoRan received shares of IMC Global.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p><p>The Indonesian government asked Freeport to substantiate Bre-X's claims of having found the largest gold mine ever discovered. In 1997, the company announced that its prospective partner <a href="/wiki/Bre-X" title="Bre-X">Bre-X</a> did not have gold reserves at its Indonesian mine, as it had reported.  Bre-X subsequently was exposed as a fraud and went bankrupt.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p><p>In 1998, low commodity prices forced the company to suspend its dividend.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="2000s">2000s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Freeport-McMoRan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: 2000s" data-section="12" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>In 2003, the company was subpoenaed as part of an investigation by anti-trust authorities in the United States, Canada, and Europe into price fixing in the copper industry.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></p><p>On March 19, 2007, the company acquired <a href="/wiki/Phelps_Dodge" title="Phelps Dodge">Phelps Dodge</a> and became the largest copper producer of any public company in the world. The corporate headquarters was moved from <a href="/wiki/New_Orleans" title="New Orleans">New Orleans</a>, <a href="/wiki/Louisiana" title="Louisiana">Louisiana</a> to <a href="/wiki/Phoenix,_Arizona" title="Phoenix, Arizona">Phoenix, Arizona</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="2010s">2010s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Freeport-McMoRan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: 2010s" data-section="13" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>In 2012, the company announced agreements to acquire affiliated companies McMoRan Exploration Company and Plains Exploration &amp; Production Company for a total enterprise value of over $20 billion. The transaction added significantly to the company's <a href="/wiki/Petroleum" title="Petroleum">petroleum</a> assets.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> The transaction was criticized as a <a href="/wiki/Conflict_of_interest" title="Conflict of interest">conflict of interest</a> due to the common ownership of the companies.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> In 2015, the company paid a $137.5 million settlement to resolve claims that executives and directors had conflicts of interest that resulted in the company overpaying in that transaction.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></p><p>In 2014, the company sold its assets in the <a href="/wiki/Eagle_Ford" class="mw-redirect" title="Eagle Ford">Eagle Ford</a> shale to <a href="/wiki/Encana" class="mw-redirect" title="Encana">Encana</a> for $3.1 billion.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> In 2015, the company announced job cuts at its Sierrita Mine in <a href="/wiki/Arizona" title="Arizona">Arizona</a> due to low copper and <a href="/wiki/Molybdenum" title="Molybdenum">molybdenum</a> prices.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup></p><p>On December 28, 2015, the company announced that James R. Moffett would resign as chairman of the company and be replaced by <a href="/wiki/Gerald_J._Ford" title="Gerald J. Ford">Gerald J. Ford</a>. Moffett received $16.1 million in severance pay and cash retirement plans totaling more than $63 million. Moffett continued to consult for the company for annual fees of $1.5 million.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup></p><p>In May 2016, the company sold a 13% interest in its Morenci Mine to <a href="/wiki/Sumitomo_Group" title="Sumitomo Group">Sumitomo Group</a> for $1 billion in cash.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup></p><p>Fourth qtr 2016, Freeport sold its deepwater assets, including the Marlin TLP, and the Holstein and Horn Mountain spars, to Anadarko Petroleum Corp.
</p><p>In August 2017, the company agreed to give a 51% interest in the Grasberg mine to the <a href="/wiki/Government_of_Indonesia" title="Government of Indonesia">Government of Indonesia</a> and build a smelter in exchange for a special permit to operate the mine until 2041.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup></p><p>In 2018, the company ranked at number 176 on the <a href="/wiki/Fortune_500" title="Fortune 500">Fortune 500</a> list.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> During this year, Indonesian President <a href="/wiki/Joko_Widodo" title="Joko Widodo">Joko Widodo</a> also planned to take control of 51% of Freeport Indonesia's equity, effectively handing over control of Freeport Control to Indonesian government. The Indonesian government will need to settle payments of $3.85 billion during the takeover process.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> The Indonesian government finalized the process on December 21, 2018.<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup></p>

<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Grasberg_Mine">Grasberg Mine</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Freeport-McMoRan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Grasberg Mine" data-section="24" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Grasberg_mine" title="Grasberg mine">Grasberg mine</a></div>
<p>The company operates the world's largest and most profitable gold mine, the <a href="/wiki/Grasberg_mine" title="Grasberg mine">Grasberg mine</a> in <a href="/wiki/Papua,_Indonesia" class="mw-redirect" title="Papua, Indonesia">Papua, Indonesia</a>.
</p><p>In 2005, <a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times" title="The New York Times">The New York Times</a> reported that the company paid local military and police generals, colonels, majors and captains, and military units, a total of nearly US$20 million between 1998 and 2004. One individual received up to US$150,000. The payments were meant to secure the reserve. The company responded that the payments were not for individuals, but rather for infrastructure, food, housing, fuel, travel, vehicle repairs and allowances to cover incidental and administrative costs. According to the report, anonymous sources within the company also claimed that company chairman James R. Moffet courted Indonesia's dictator <a href="/wiki/Suharto" title="Suharto">Suharto</a> and "his cronies", cutting them in on deals. Another employee is said to have worked on a program to monitor environmentalists' e-mails and telephone conversations, in cooperation with Indonesian military intelligence officers.<sup id="cite_ref-waste_57-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-waste-57">[57]</a></sup></p><p>The Grasberg mine's tailings "severely impacted" more than 11 square miles (28 km<sup>2</sup>) of rainforest, according to a 1996 Dames &amp; Moore environmental audit.<sup id="cite_ref-spinninggold_58-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-spinninggold-58">[58]</a></sup> The report, endorsed by Freeport, also estimated that during the life of the mine 3.2 billion tons of waste rock—a great part of which generates <a href="/wiki/Acid" title="Acid">acid</a>—will be dumped into the local river system. <a href="/wiki/Overburden" title="Overburden">Overburden</a> (waste rock) from the mine has polluted a nearby lake due to <a href="/wiki/Acid_mine_drainage" title="Acid mine drainage">acid mine drainage</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-spinninggold_58-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-spinninggold-58">[58]</a></sup></p><p>Citing extensive, long-term and irreversible environmental damage in New Guinea, <a href="/wiki/The_Government_Pension_Fund_of_Norway" class="mw-redirect" title="The Government Pension Fund of Norway">The Government Pension Fund of Norway</a> has excluded Freeport-McMoRan from its investment portfolio, following a recommendation from the fund's ethical council.<sup id="cite_ref-59" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup></p>
<h4 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Human_rights_record_in_Indonesia">Human rights record in Indonesia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Freeport-McMoRan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Human rights record in Indonesia" data-section="25" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h4>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Papua_Conflict" class="mw-redirect" title="Papua Conflict">Papua Conflict</a></div>
<p>The company is a signatory participant of the <a href="/wiki/Voluntary_Principles_on_Security_and_Human_Rights" title="Voluntary Principles on Security and Human Rights">Voluntary Principles on Security and Human Rights</a>. However, the company has been accused of funding the Indonesian government to secure its reserve through militaristic oppression of the native <a href="/wiki/Western_New_Guinea" title="Western New Guinea">West Papuan</a> people. 
</p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Environmental_record">Environmental record</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Freeport-McMoRan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Environmental record" data-section="26" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>Based on 2014 data, the <a href="/wiki/Political_Economy_Research_Institute" title="Political Economy Research Institute">Political Economy Research Institute</a> ranked Freeport-McMoRan 13th among corporations emitting airborne pollutants in the U.S. The ranking is based on emission quantities and toxicity.<sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup> In 2012, the company paid a $6.8 million fine to the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_the_Interior" title="United States Department of the Interior">United States Department of the Interior</a> for pollution at the company's Morenci copper mine in southeastern Arizona.<sup id="cite_ref-61" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup></p>
