</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(1)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cadence_Design_Systems&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History" data-section="1" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-1 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-1">
<p>Cadence Design Systems was the result of a merger perfected in 1988 of Solomon Design Automation , co-founded in 1983 by <a href="/wiki/A._Richard_Newton" title="A. Richard Newton">Richard Newton</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alberto_Sangiovanni-Vincentelli" title="Alberto Sangiovanni-Vincentelli">Alberto Sangiovanni-Vincentelli</a> and <a href="/wiki/James_Solomon" title="James Solomon">James Solomon</a>, and <a href="/wiki/ECAD,_Inc." title="ECAD, Inc.">ECAD</a>, co-founded by Glen Antle and Paul Huang in 1982. <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Costello_(software_executive)" title="Joseph Costello (software executive)">Joseph Costello</a> was appointed as CEO from 1988–1997, and Cadence became the largest EDA company during his tenure.
</p><p>Following Costello as CEO were Jack Harding (from 1997–99), Ray Bingham (from 1999-2005), and Mike Fister (from 2005-2008). Following the resignation of Fister, the board appointed Lip-Bu Tan as acting CEO.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> In January 2009, the company confirmed <a href="/wiki/Lip-Bu_Tan" title="Lip-Bu Tan">Lip-Bu Tan</a> as President and CEO. Tan had been most recently CEO of Walden International, a <a href="/wiki/Venture_capital" title="Venture capital">venture capital</a> firm, and remains chairman of the firm. He has served on the Cadence Board of Directors since 2004, where he served on the Technology Committee for four years.
</p><p>In 2013, Cadence celebrated its 25th anniversary. </section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(2)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Products">Products</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cadence_Design_Systems&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Products" data-section="2" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-2 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-2">
<p>The company develops software, hardware and <a href="/wiki/Intellectual_Properties" class="mw-redirect" title="Intellectual Properties">intellectual properties</a> (IP) used to design chips<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>, intelligent systems and <a href="/wiki/Printed_circuit_board" title="Printed circuit board">printed circuit boards</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> as well as IP covering  interfaces, memory, analog, SoC peripherals, data plane processing units, and verification.
</p><p>Cadence products primarily target SoC design engineers and are used to move a design into packaged silicon, with products for custom and analog design, digital design, mixed-signal design, verification, and package/PCB design, as well as a broad selection of IP, and also hardware for emulation and FPGA prototyping. These products allow for broader system design, from the software down to the physical chip design. 
</p><p>It provides solutions that encompass design IP, timing analysis and signoff, services, and tools and methodologies. In addition, it provides system analysis solutions for electromagnetic, electronics, thermal and electromechanical simulation. The company also provides products that assist with the development of complete hardware and software platforms that support end applications.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p><p>Cadence's product offerings  include:

<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Avanti_Corporation">Avanti Corporation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cadence_Design_Systems&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Avanti Corporation" data-section="4" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Cadence_Design_Systems,_Inc._v._Avanti_Corp" title="Cadence Design Systems, Inc. v. Avanti Corp">Cadence Design Systems, Inc. v. Avanti Corp</a></div>
<p>Cadence was involved in a <a href="/wiki/Cadence_Design_Systems,_Inc._v._Avanti_Corp" title="Cadence Design Systems, Inc. v. Avanti Corp">6-year-long legal dispute</a><sup id="cite_ref-bw_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bw-19">[19]</a></sup> with <a href="/wiki/Avanti_Corporation" class="mw-redirect" title="Avanti Corporation">Avanti Corporation</a>, in which Cadence claimed Avanti stole Cadence code, and Avanti denied it.  According to Business Week <i>"The Avanti case is probably the most dramatic tale of white-collar crime in the history of Silicon Valley"</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-bw_19-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bw-19">[19]</a></sup>  The Avanti executives eventually pleaded <i>no contest</i> and Cadence received several hundred million dollars in restitution. Avanti was then purchased by <a href="/wiki/Synopsys" title="Synopsys">Synopsys</a>, which paid $265 million more to settle the remaining claims.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup>  The case resulted in a number of <a href="/wiki/Legal_precedent" class="mw-redirect" title="Legal precedent">legal precedents</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Aptix_Corporation">Aptix Corporation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cadence_Design_Systems&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Aptix Corporation" data-section="5" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>Quickturn Design Systems, a company acquired by Cadence, was involved in a series of legal events with Aptix Corporation. Aptix licensed a patent to <a href="/wiki/Mentor_Graphics" title="Mentor Graphics">Mentor Graphics</a> and the two companies jointly sued Quickturn over an alleged patent infringement.  Amr Mohsen, CEO of Aptix, forged and tampered with legal evidence and was subsequently charged with conspiracy, perjury, and obstruction of justice. Mohsen was arrested after violating his bail agreement by attempting to flee the country. While in jail, Mohsen plotted to intimidate witnesses and kill the federal judge presiding over his case.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup>  Mohsen was further charged with attempting to delay a federal trial by feigning incompetency.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> Due to the overwhelming misconduct, the judge ruled the lawsuit as unenforceable and Mohsen was sentenced to 17 years in prison.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> Mentor Graphics subsequently sued Aptix to recoup legal costs. Cadence also sued Mentor Graphics and Aptix to recover legal costs.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>

<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Denali_Software">Denali Software</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cadence_Design_Systems&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Denali Software" data-section="8" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p><b>Denali Software, Inc.</b> was an <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">American</a> software company, based in <a href="/wiki/Sunnyvale,_California" title="Sunnyvale, California">Sunnyvale, California</a>, now acquired by Cadence.<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> The company produces <a href="/wiki/Electronic_design_automation" title="Electronic design automation">electronic design automation</a> (EDA) software, intellectual property (IP) and design cores and platforms for memory, other standard interfaces and system-on-chip (SoC) design and verification. It has its engineering offices in <a href="/wiki/Sunnyvale,_California" title="Sunnyvale, California">Sunnyvale</a>, <a href="/wiki/Austin" class="mw-redirect" title="Austin">Austin</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bangalore" title="Bangalore">Bangalore</a>. Incorporated in 1996, Denali is headquartered in <a href="/wiki/Sunnyvale,_California" title="Sunnyvale, California">Sunnyvale, California</a> and serves the global electronics industry with direct sales and support offices in North America, Europe, Japan and Asia.

<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Valid_Logic_Systems">Valid Logic Systems</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cadence_Design_Systems&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Valid Logic Systems" data-section="9" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p><b>Valid Logic Systems</b> was one of the first commercial <a href="/wiki/Electronic_design_automation" title="Electronic design automation">electronic design automation</a> (EDA) companies, now acquired by Cadence. It was founded in the early 1980s,<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup> along with <a href="/wiki/Daisy_Systems_Corporation" class="mw-redirect" title="Daisy Systems Corporation">Daisy Systems Corporation</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mentor_Graphics" title="Mentor Graphics">Mentor Graphics</a>, collectively known as DMV. The engineering founders were L. Curtis Widdoes,<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup> Tom McWilliams<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup> and Jeff Rubin,<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup> all of whom had worked on the S-1 supercomputer project at Livermore Labs.
</p><p>Valid acquired several companies such as Telesis (PCB layout),<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup> Analog Design Tools,<sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Calma" title="Calma">Calma</a> (IC layout). In turn, Valid was acquired by Cadence Design Systems in the early 90s.<sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup></p><p>Valid built both hardware and software, for <a href="/wiki/Schematic_capture" title="Schematic capture">schematic capture</a>, <a href="/wiki/Logic_simulation" title="Logic simulation">logic simulation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Static_timing_analysis" title="Static timing analysis">static timing analysis</a>, and packaging. Much of the initial software base derived from <a href="/wiki/SCALD" title="SCALD">SCALD</a> ("Structured Computer-Aided Logic Design"), a set of tools developed to support the design of the S-1 supercomputer at <a href="/wiki/Lawrence_Livermore_National_Laboratory" title="Lawrence Livermore National Laboratory">Lawrence Livermore National Laboratory</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup>  Later, Valid expanded into IC design tools and into <a href="/wiki/Printed_circuit_board" title="Printed circuit board">printed circuit board</a> layout.
</p><p>At first, Valid ran schematic capture on a proprietary UNIX <a href="/wiki/Workstation" title="Workstation">workstation</a>, the <a href="/wiki/SCALD" title="SCALD">SCALDSystem</a>, with static timing analysis, simulation, and packaging running on a VAX or IBM-compatible mainframe. However, by the mid-1980s, general purpose workstations were powerful enough, and significantly cheaper. Companies such as <a href="/wiki/Mentor_Graphics" title="Mentor Graphics">Mentor Graphics</a> and Cadence Design Systems sold software only for such workstations. By 1990, almost all Valid software was also running on workstations, primarily those from <a href="/wiki/Sun_Microsystems" title="Sun Microsystems">Sun Microsystems</a>.
