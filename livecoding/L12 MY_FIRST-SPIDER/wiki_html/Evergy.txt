The company was formed from a merger of <b>Westar Energy</b> of <a href="/wiki/Topeka,_Kansas" title="Topeka, Kansas">Topeka</a> and <a href="/wiki/Great_Plains_Energy" title="Great Plains Energy">Great Plains Energy</a> of Kansas City, Missouri, parent company of <a href="/wiki/Kansas_City_Power_%26_Light" class="mw-redirect" title="Kansas City Power &amp; Light">Kansas City Power &amp; Light</a>. Evergy is the largest electric company in <a href="/wiki/Kansas" title="Kansas">Kansas</a>, serving more than 1,000,000 residential, commercial and industrial customers<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> in the eastern half of the state. Evergy has a generating capacity of 16,000 <a href="/wiki/Megawatt" class="mw-redirect" title="Megawatt">megawatt</a>  <a href="/wiki/Electricity" title="Electricity">electricity</a> from its over 40 <a href="/wiki/Power_plants" class="mw-redirect" title="Power plants">power plants</a> in Kansas and Missouri. Evergy service territory covers 28,130 square miles (72,900 km<sup>2</sup>) in east Kansas and west Missouri. Evergy owns more than 13,700 miles (22,000 km) of <a href="/wiki/Electric_power_transmission" title="Electric power transmission">transmission lines</a> and about 52,000 miles of <a href="/wiki/Electric_power_distribution" title="Electric power distribution">distribution lines</a>.

</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(1)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="History_of_Westar_Energy">History of Westar Energy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Evergy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History of Westar Energy" data-section="1" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-1 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-1">
<p>Westar Energy was the product of a 1992 merger between the two major electric companies in eastern Kansas, Kansas Gas and Electric (KG&amp;E) of <a href="/wiki/Wichita,_Kansas" title="Wichita, Kansas">Wichita</a> and Kansas Power and Light (KPL) of Topeka.
</p><p>KG&amp;E was founded in 1909 when the <a href="/wiki/American_Power_and_Light_Company" class="mw-redirect" title="American Power and Light Company">American Power and Light Company</a> took over electric companies in Wichita, <a href="/wiki/Pittsburg,_Kansas" title="Pittsburg, Kansas">Pittsburg</a> and <a href="/wiki/Frontenac,_Kansas" title="Frontenac, Kansas">Frontenac</a>. Within a decade, it served over 48,000 people in 50 cities and towns. It also provided natural gas to several of the larger cities in its service territory.<sup id="cite_ref-History_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-History-2">[2]</a></sup></p><p>KPL was founded in 1924, and quickly expanded across northeastern Kansas.  In 1983, it merged with The Gas Service Company, a natural gas utility serving customers in Kansas, <a href="/wiki/Missouri" title="Missouri">Missouri</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nebraska" title="Nebraska">Nebraska</a> and <a href="/wiki/Oklahoma" title="Oklahoma">Oklahoma</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-History_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-History-2">[2]</a></sup></p><p>In 1992, KPL merged with KG&amp;E to become Western Resources, with KPL and KG&amp;E as operating companies.  The merger created one of the largest utilities in the Midwest, serving 560,000 electric customers and 1.06 million natural gas customers in three states. In 1996, Western Resources sold its natural gas business to <a href="/wiki/ONEOK" class="mw-redirect" title="ONEOK">ONEOK</a> as <a href="/wiki/Kansas_Gas_Service" title="Kansas Gas Service">Kansas Gas Service</a>; this company is now part of <a href="/wiki/ONE_Gas" title="ONE Gas">ONE Gas</a>. In return, Western Resources acquired a 45 percent stake in ONEOK; it sold this stake in 2003.<sup id="cite_ref-History_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-History-2">[2]</a></sup></p><p>In 2002, Western Resources officially changed its name to Westar Energy, and all of its subsidiaries began doing business under that name.<sup id="cite_ref-History_2-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-History-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(2)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span id="History_of_Kansas_City_Power_.26_Light"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="History_of_Kansas_City_Power_&amp;_Light">History of Kansas City Power &amp; Light</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Evergy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History of Kansas City Power &amp; Light" data-section="2" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-2 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-2">
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Kansas_City_Power_and_Light_Building_1931.jpg" class="image"><noscript><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/70/Kansas_City_Power_and_Light_Building_1931.jpg/220px-Kansas_City_Power_and_Light_Building_1931.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="418" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="473" data-file-height="899"></noscript><span class="lazy-image-placeholder" style="width: 220px;height: 418px;" data-src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/70/Kansas_City_Power_and_Light_Building_1931.jpg/220px-Kansas_City_Power_and_Light_Building_1931.jpg" data-alt="" data-width="220" data-height="418" data-class="thumbimage"> </span></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Kansas_City_Power_and_Light_Building_1931.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The art deco <a href="/wiki/Kansas_City_Power_and_Light_Building" title="Kansas City Power and Light Building">Kansas City Power and Light Building</a> was the former headquarters of the company and was the tallest building west of the Mississippi until 1942, tallest in Missouri until 1976 and tallest in Kansas City until 1986 and is the namesake of the downtown <a href="/wiki/Kansas_City_Power_%26_Light_District" title="Kansas City Power &amp; Light District">Kansas City Power &amp; Light District</a></div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Obama-smith-electric.jpg" class="image"><noscript><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ec/Obama-smith-electric.jpg/220px-Obama-smith-electric.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="112" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="550" data-file-height="281"></noscript><span class="lazy-image-placeholder" style="width: 220px;height: 112px;" data-src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ec/Obama-smith-electric.jpg/220px-Obama-smith-electric.jpg" data-alt="" data-width="220" data-height="112" data-class="thumbimage"> </span></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Obama-smith-electric.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Barack_Obama" title="Barack Obama">Barack Obama</a> in front a KCP&amp;L truck on July 8, 2010, at the <a href="/wiki/Smith_Electric_Vehicles" title="Smith Electric Vehicles">Smith Electric Vehicles</a> plant at <a href="/wiki/Kansas_City_International_Airport" title="Kansas City International Airport">Kansas City International Airport</a>.  KCP&amp;L has bought a fleet of electric powered trucks from the company.</div></div></div>
<p><b>Kansas City Power and Light Company</b> was an electric utility company serving the <a href="/wiki/Kansas_City_metropolitan_area" title="Kansas City metropolitan area">Kansas City metropolitan area</a>.  It was a wholly owned subsidiary of <a href="/wiki/Great_Plains_Energy" title="Great Plains Energy">Great Plains Energy</a> of which it was the biggest component.  The company traces its roots to November 1881 when <a href="/w/index.php?title=Joseph_S._Chick&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Joseph S. Chick (page does not exist)">Joseph S. Chick</a> obtained the exclusive rights to use the Thompson-Houston arc lighting system in the counties of Jackson, Missouri, and Wyandotte, Kansas, for $4,000. The following month, the initial franchise to establish an electric works in the City of Kansas, Mo., was granted to Lysander R. Moore and later assigned to <i>Kawsmouth Electric Light Company</i>. Construction was begun in February 1882 on a power plant on a tract of land at the southeast corner of 8th and Santa Fe Streets in the West Bottoms. Kawsmouth Electric Light Company built quickly and, on Saturday night, May 13, 1882, brought electric illumination to the first 13 customers on the west side of Main Street in the downtown district. In 1885 the company reincorporated as <i>Kansas City Electric Light Company</i>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p><p>Weeks spun off the <i>Edison Electric Light &amp; Power Company</i> to meet residential demand.  An electric war ensued when in 1883 <a href="/wiki/J._Ogden_Armour" title="J. Ogden Armour">J. Ogden Armour</a>, heir to the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Armour_Packing_Company&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Armour Packing Company (page does not exist)">Armour Packing Company</a> purchased the company on May 14, 1900, to power the Metropolitan Street Railway Company and Kansas City Electric Light Company.  Under Armour  the company bought competitors and built a new power plant in 1903, providing steam heat to downtown businesses.  The company focused on the trolley company and in 1911 it went into receivership.  In October 1917, the company spun off the trolley business (which still controlled some power plants) and emerged from bankruptcy as  <i>Kansas City Light &amp; Power Company</i>. In 1917 the company began construction on the Northeast Power station.
</p><p>In June 1919, the company reincorporated again, as <i>Kansas City Power and Light Company</i>. After acquiring the Carroll County Electric Company on July 29, 1922, the reorganized company became Kansas City Power &amp; Light Company, adopting the ampersand and corporate name that continues to this day.  Armour sold his interest in 1923.  Continental Gas &amp; Electric Corporation purchased the controlling interest in 1924 and was part of United Light and Power until United dissolved in 1950.   The Hawthorn Station, situated on the Missouri River, was started in 1948, and the first of two units were completed in 1951. Two other units followed and were fully operational by 1956. Kansas City Power became independent in 1950.  It acquired Eastern Kansas Utilities in 1952.  It was part of a consortium that built <a href="/wiki/Wolf_Creek_Nuclear_Generating_Station" class="mw-redirect" title="Wolf Creek Nuclear Generating Station">Wolf Creek Nuclear Generating Station</a> in <a href="/wiki/Burlington,_Kansas" title="Burlington, Kansas">Burlington, Kansas</a>.
</p><p>On October 1, 2001 a holding company <b>Great Plains Energy Incorporated</b> was established based in Kansas City, Missouri that owned electric utility <i>Kansas City Power and Light Company</i> and Strategic Energy, LLC, an energy management company.
</p><p>It acquired <a href="/wiki/Aquila,_Inc." title="Aquila, Inc.">Aquila, Inc.</a> in July, 2008.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p><p>In September 2009 KCP&amp;L moved into its new building, One Kansas City Place at 1200 Main in KCMO, just 1 block west of Kansas City's Power and Light district. The lease is going to be 23 years long.
</p><p>In 2014, it ranked #855 on the <a href="/wiki/Fortune_1000" title="Fortune 1000">Fortune 1000</a> list.<sup id="cite_ref-Fortune2014_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fortune2014-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(3)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span id="Merger_of_Kansas_City_Power_.26_Light_and_Westar_Energy"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Merger_of_Kansas_City_Power_&amp;_Light_and_Westar_Energy">Merger of Kansas City Power &amp; Light and Westar Energy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Evergy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Merger of Kansas City Power &amp; Light and Westar Energy" data-section="3" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-3 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-3">
<p>In 2016, <a href="/wiki/Great_Plains_Energy" title="Great Plains Energy">Great Plains Energy</a> and Westar announced merger plans,<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> but this proposed merger was rejected by <a href="/wiki/Kansas_Corporation_Commission" title="Kansas Corporation Commission">Kansas Corporation Commission</a> utility regulators as unfavorable to Kansas consumers.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> A new merger plan with Great Plains was announced in 2017.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> As of May 24, 2018, this merger has been approved by both the <a href="/wiki/Missouri_Public_Service_Commission" title="Missouri Public Service Commission">Missouri Public Service Commission</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Kansas_Corporation_Commission" title="Kansas Corporation Commission">Kansas Corporation Commission</a>, with the combined company to be named Evergy.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> KCP&amp;L and Westar became the two operating companies of Evergy.
</p><p>On October 7, 2019; the Westar and KCP&amp;L brands were retired, and the company adopted the <b>Evergy</b> brand across its entire service territory.
