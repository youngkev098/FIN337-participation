<p>The company was formed via the merger of American company Johnson Controls with <a href="/wiki/Tyco_International" title="Tyco International">Tyco International</a>, announced on 25 January 2016. The merger led to the avoidance of U.S. taxes on its foreign market operations and a financial windfall for the CEO of Johnson Controls at that time, <a href="/wiki/Alex_Molinaroli" title="Alex Molinaroli">Alex Molinaroli</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Surane_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Surane-7">[7]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-believe_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-believe-8">[8]</a></sup> As of November 2018<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Johnson_Controls&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, Johnson Control's <a href="/wiki/Corporation_tax_in_the_Republic_of_Ireland#List_of_inversions" title="Corporation tax in the Republic of Ireland">tax inversion</a> to Ireland is the 3rd largest U.S. tax inversion in history.
</p>

<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Johnson_Controls_Super-Sensitive_Thermometer.jpg" class="image"><noscript><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e6/Johnson_Controls_Super-Sensitive_Thermometer.jpg/220px-Johnson_Controls_Super-Sensitive_Thermometer.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="368" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="1952" data-file-height="3264"></noscript><span class="lazy-image-placeholder" style="width: 220px;height: 368px;" data-src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e6/Johnson_Controls_Super-Sensitive_Thermometer.jpg/220px-Johnson_Controls_Super-Sensitive_Thermometer.jpg" data-alt="" data-width="220" data-height="368" data-class="thumbimage"> </span></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Johnson_Controls_Super-Sensitive_Thermometer.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A Johnson Super-Sensitive Thermometer on an air conditioning unit</div></div></div>
<p>In 1883, <a href="/wiki/Warren_S._Johnson" title="Warren S. Johnson">Warren S. Johnson</a>, a professor at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Wisconsin%E2%80%93Whitewater" title="University of Wisconsin–Whitewater">State Normal School</a> in <a href="/wiki/Whitewater,_Wisconsin" title="Whitewater, Wisconsin">Whitewater, Wisconsin</a>, received a patent for the first electric room thermostat. His invention helped launch the building control industry and was the impetus for a new company. Johnson and a group of Milwaukee investors incorporated the Johnson Electric Service Company in 1885 to manufacture, install and service automatic temperature regulation systems for buildings. After Johnson's death in 1911, the company decided to focus on its temperature control business for nonresidential buildings. The company was renamed Johnson Controls in 1974. In 1978, Johnson Controls acquired battery company Globe-Union. In 1985, Johnson Controls acquired automotive seating companies Hoover Universal and Ferro Manufacturing.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p><p>During the 2008–2009 recession, the company's president, Keith Wandell, lobbied Congress for a bailout of the companies that Johnson supplied.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> The Johnson Controls plant in Lakeshore, Ontario, closed in late March 2010 and the property was sold.<sup id="cite_ref-cbc_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cbc-11">[11]</a></sup> In 2013, Stephen Roell retired and <a href="/wiki/Alex_Molinaroli" title="Alex Molinaroli">Alex Molinaroli</a> took his position as CEO and chairman of the board.<sup id="cite_ref-Content_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Content-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Merger_with_Tyco">Merger with Tyco</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Johnson_Controls&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Merger with Tyco" data-section="2" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>In January 2016, Johnson Controls merged with <a href="/wiki/Tyco_International" title="Tyco International">Tyco International</a> to create a company headquartered in Cork, Ireland.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> The new company is called Johnson Controls International plc.<sup id="cite_ref-Globe_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Globe-14">[14]</a></sup></p><p><a href="/wiki/Hillary_Clinton" title="Hillary Clinton">Hillary Clinton</a> condemned the company for wanting to escape United States taxes through the merger after having "begged" the government for financial help in 2008.<sup id="cite_ref-Gilbert_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gilbert-15">[15]</a></sup> The Johnson deal, termed "outrageous"<sup id="cite_ref-Fortune_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fortune-16">[16]</a></sup> by <i><a href="/wiki/Fortune_(magazine)" title="Fortune (magazine)">Fortune</a></i> magazine, qualifies as a <a href="/wiki/Tax_inversion" title="Tax inversion">"super inversion"</a> because Tyco shareholders will own 44% of the company, thus avoiding penalties that the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_the_Treasury" title="United States Department of the Treasury">United States Department of the Treasury</a> has imposed on other inversion deals.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The firm estimated that it would save about <a href="/wiki/United_States_dollar" title="United States dollar">US$</a>150 million a year by avoiding American taxes.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Subsequent_history">Subsequent history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Johnson_Controls&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Subsequent history" data-section="3" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>On October 31, 2016, the former Johnson Controls Automotive Experience division was spun off as a separate, publicly traded company, <a href="/wiki/Adient" title="Adient">Adient</a>, and began trading on the New York Stock Exchange.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> In March 2017, it was announced that <a href="/wiki/Scott_Safety" class="mw-redirect" title="Scott Safety">Scott Safety</a>, its safety gear business, would be bought by <a href="/wiki/3M" title="3M">3M</a> for $2 billion.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p><p>On September 1, 2017, George Oliver was appointed as Chairman and CEO, an acceleration by 6 months from the original plans. <sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(2)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span id="Women.27s_work_rights"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Women's_work_rights">Women's work rights</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Johnson_Controls&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Women's work rights" data-section="4" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-2 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-2">
<p>In 1982, Johnson Controls enacted what it called a "<a href="/wiki/Fetal_protection_policies_in_the_United_States" title="Fetal protection policies in the United States">fetal protection policy</a>", which denied women the right to work on the battery production line because of the potential harm to a <a href="/wiki/Fetus" title="Fetus">fetus</a> they might conceive. Women were allowed to work on the production line only if they could prove that "... their inability to bear children had been medically documented." In April 1984, the <a href="/wiki/United_Automobile_Workers" class="mw-redirect" title="United Automobile Workers">United Automobile Workers</a> sued Johnson Controls on behalf of three employees. These employees were Mary Craig, who had chosen to be <a href="/wiki/Sterilization_(medicine)" title="Sterilization (medicine)">sterilized</a> to avoid losing her job, Elsie Nason, a 50-year-old divorcee, who had suffered a loss of compensation when she was transferred from a high paying job that exposed her to lead, and Donald Penney, who had been denied a request for a <a href="/wiki/Leave_of_absence" title="Leave of absence">leave of absence</a> for the purpose of lowering his blood lead levels because he intended to become a father. <a href="/wiki/United_Automobile_Workers_v._Johnson_Controls,_Inc." title="United Automobile Workers v. Johnson Controls, Inc.">The case</a> was argued before the <a href="/wiki/Supreme_Court_of_the_United_States" title="Supreme Court of the United States">Supreme Court of the United States</a> on October 10, 1990 and was decided on March 20, 1991. The Court ruled in favor of the plaintiffs. This was a landmark ruling because it affirmed that "... it is no more appropriate for the courts than it is for individual employers to decide whether a woman's reproductive role is more important to herself and her family than her economic role."<sup id="cite_ref-UAW_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-UAW-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(3)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Business_units">Business units</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Johnson_Controls&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Business units" data-section="5" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-3 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-3">
<p>The company's operations are segmented into two business units: Building Efficiency and Global WorkPlace Solutions.
</p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Building_Efficiency">Building Efficiency</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Johnson_Controls&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Building Efficiency" data-section="6" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>The Building Efficiency business unit designs, produces, installs and services heating, ventilation and air conditioning systems, industrial refrigeration, <a href="/wiki/Building_management_system" title="Building management system">building management systems</a>, fire and security systems and mechanical equipment for commercial and residential buildings. The brands produced under this business unit are <a href="/wiki/York_International" class="mw-redirect" title="York International">York</a>, TempMaster, Metasys, Panoptix, Frick and Sabroe.<sup id="cite_ref-Form_10-K_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Form_10-K-22">[22]</a></sup> This unit also works with organizations to reduce the energy consumption and operating costs of their buildings.<sup id="cite_ref-Form10-K_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Form10-K-23">[23]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-lightoff_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-lightoff-24">[24]</a></sup> This includes retrofitting existing buildings such as the <a href="/wiki/Empire_State_Building" title="Empire State Building">Empire State Building</a><sup id="cite_ref-Crain_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Crain-25">[25]</a></sup> and working on maximizing efficiency in new construction such as the <a href="/wiki/Burj_Khalifa" title="Burj Khalifa">Burj Khalifa</a> in Dubai.<sup id="cite_ref-Hope_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hope-26">[26]</a></sup> Building Efficiency is the company's longest-running business unit, dating to 1885 when Johnson founded the Johnson Electric Service Company<sup id="cite_ref-Adler_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Adler-27">[27]</a></sup> after patenting the electric <a href="/wiki/Thermostat" title="Thermostat">thermostat</a> in 1883.<sup id="cite_ref-USembassy_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-USembassy-28">[28]</a></sup> As of 2012, the business unit operated from 700 branch offices in more than 150 countries.<sup id="cite_ref-Form10-K_23-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Form10-K-23">[23]</a></sup></p><p>Johnson Controls was one of the defendants in a multimillion-dollar federal court lawsuit in <a href="/wiki/San_Juan,_Puerto_Rico" title="San Juan, Puerto Rico">San Juan, Puerto Rico</a> in a case where 98 people perished and 140 were injured in a <a href="/wiki/Dupont_Plaza_Hotel_arson" title="Dupont Plaza Hotel arson">fire at the DuPont Plaza Hotel</a> and its casino on New Year's Eve, December 31, 1986. The plaintiffs claimed that Johnson Controls sold and installed an energy management system that failed to give early warning of the fire.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> After nine months of trial, the company and its energy management system were absolved of blame when the court issued a directed verdict.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> When this trial was completed the plaintiffs had accumulated approximately $220,908,549.00 in damages as a result of various settlements and a jury verdict against some other defendants.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Global_WorkPlace_Solutions">Global WorkPlace Solutions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Johnson_Controls&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Global WorkPlace Solutions" data-section="7" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>The Global WorkPlace Solutions business unit provides outsourced <a href="/wiki/Facilities_management" class="mw-redirect" title="Facilities management">facilities management</a> services globally.<sup id="cite_ref-MJSverizon_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MJSverizon-33">[33]</a></sup> It also manages corporate real estate on behalf of its customers including acquiring and disposing of property, administering leases, and managing building related projects such as equipment replacements.<sup id="cite_ref-FMWorldbrookfield_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FMWorldbrookfield-34">[34]</a></sup> On September 23, 2015,  <a href="/wiki/CBRE,_Inc." class="mw-redirect" title="CBRE, Inc.">CBRE, Inc.</a> purchased the Global Workplace Solutions business unit, retaining the name "Global Workplace Solutions".<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></p>

<h3 class="in-block"><span id="Coleman_Heating_.26_Air_Conditioning"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Coleman_Heating_&amp;_Air_Conditioning">Coleman Heating &amp; Air Conditioning</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Johnson_Controls&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Coleman Heating &amp; Air Conditioning" data-section="13" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p><b>Coleman Heating &amp; Air Conditioning</b> is a major manufacturing brand of <a href="/wiki/HVAC" class="mw-redirect" title="HVAC">HVAC</a> equipment, and was formerly an independent HVAC <a href="/wiki/Manufacturing" title="Manufacturing">manufacturing</a> company. The company began as a division of the <a href="/wiki/Coleman_Company" class="mw-redirect" title="Coleman Company">Coleman Company</a>  in 1958 and was acquired by <a href="/w/index.php?title=Evcon&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Evcon (page does not exist)">Evcon</a> in 1990, which in turn was acquired by Johnson Controls in 1996.<sup id="cite_ref-67" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-68" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-69" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup>  Of the twelve largest American <a href="/wiki/Furnace" title="Furnace">furnace</a> brand names represented at Gas Furnace Guide, the Coleman brand received an average ranking of 3.7 out of 5 stars.<sup id="cite_ref-70" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="York_International">York International</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Johnson_Controls&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: York International" data-section="14" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p><b><a href="/wiki/York_International" class="mw-redirect" title="York International">York International</a></b> is the final name of a company started in <a href="/wiki/York,_Pennsylvania" title="York, Pennsylvania">York, Pennsylvania</a>, <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">USA</a>, in 1874, which developed the York <a href="/wiki/Brand" title="Brand">brand</a> of <a href="/wiki/Refrigeration" title="Refrigeration">refrigeration</a> and <a href="/wiki/HVAC" class="mw-redirect" title="HVAC">HVAC</a> equipment.<sup id="cite_ref-71" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-72" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-72">[72]</a></sup> The York brand has been owned since August 2005 by Johnson Controls, when it was sold to them for $3.2 billion.<sup id="cite_ref-73" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-73">[73]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-74" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-74">[74]</a></sup> At the time of the acquisition, it was the world's largest independent manufacturer of air conditioning, heating, and refrigeration machinery. Its stock symbol was formerly YRK.
