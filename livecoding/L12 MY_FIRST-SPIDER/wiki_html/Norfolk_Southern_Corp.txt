			<div id="bodyContent" class="content"><div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><script>function mfTempOpenSection(id){var block=document.getElementById("mf-section-"+id);block.className+=" open-block";block.previousSibling.className+=" open-block";}</script><div class="mw-parser-output"><section class="mf-section-0" id="mf-section-0"><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">This article is about the rail company that has existed since 1982. For the historical railway, see <a href="/wiki/Norfolk_Southern_Railway_(1942%E2%80%931982)" title="Norfolk Southern Railway (1942–1982)">Norfolk Southern Railway (1942–1982)</a>.</div>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Not to be confused with <a href="/wiki/Nederlandse_Spoorwegen" title="Nederlandse Spoorwegen">Nederlandse Spoorwegen</a>.</div>

<p>The <b>Norfolk Southern Railway</b> (<a href="/wiki/Reporting_mark" title="Reporting mark">reporting mark</a> <b>NS</b>) is a <a href="/wiki/Railroad_classes" title="Railroad classes">Class I</a> freight railroad in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>. With headquarters in <a href="/wiki/Atlanta" title="Atlanta">Atlanta, Georgia</a>, the company operates 19,420 route miles (31,250 km) in 22 eastern states, the <a href="/wiki/Washington,_D.C." title="Washington, D.C.">District of Columbia</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> and has rights in <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a> over the <a href="/wiki/Albany,_New_York" title="Albany, New York">Albany</a> to <a href="/wiki/Montreal" title="Montreal">Montréal</a> route of the <a href="/wiki/Canadian_Pacific_Railway" title="Canadian Pacific Railway">Canadian Pacific Railway</a>, and previously on <a href="/wiki/Canadian_National_Railway" title="Canadian National Railway">CN</a> from <a href="/wiki/Buffalo,_New_York" title="Buffalo, New York">Buffalo</a> to <a href="/wiki/St._Thomas,_Ontario" title="St. Thomas, Ontario">St. Thomas</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> NS is responsible for maintaining 28,400 miles (45,700 km), with the remainder being operated under trackage rights from other parties responsible for maintenance.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> The most common commodity hauled on the railway is <a href="/wiki/Coal" title="Coal">coal</a> from mines in <a href="/wiki/Indiana" title="Indiana">Indiana</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kentucky" title="Kentucky">Kentucky</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pennsylvania" title="Pennsylvania">Pennsylvania</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tennessee" title="Tennessee">Tennessee</a>, <a href="/wiki/Virginia" title="Virginia">Virginia</a>, and <a href="/wiki/West_Virginia" title="West Virginia">West Virginia</a>. The railway also offers the largest <a href="/wiki/Intermodal_freight_transport" title="Intermodal freight transport">intermodal</a> network in eastern North America.

<p>NS is a major transporter of domestic and export coal. The railway's major sources of the mineral are located in: <a href="/wiki/Pennsylvania" title="Pennsylvania">Pennsylvania</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Cambria_County,_Pennsylvania" title="Cambria County, Pennsylvania">Cambria</a> and <a href="/wiki/Indiana_County,_Pennsylvania" title="Indiana County, Pennsylvania">Indiana</a> counties, as well as the <a href="/wiki/Monongahela_River" title="Monongahela River">Monongahela Valley</a>; <a href="/wiki/West_Virginia" title="West Virginia">West Virginia</a>; and the Appalachia regions of <a href="/wiki/Virginia" title="Virginia">Virginia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kentucky" title="Kentucky">Kentucky</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Tennessee" title="Tennessee">Tennessee</a>. In Pennsylvania, NS also receives coal through interchange with <a href="/wiki/R.J._Corman_Railroad/Pennsylvania_Lines" title="R.J. Corman Railroad/Pennsylvania Lines">R.J. Corman Railroad/Pennsylvania Lines</a> at <a href="/wiki/Cresson,_Pennsylvania" title="Cresson, Pennsylvania">Cresson, Pennsylvania</a>, originating in the "<a href="/wiki/R.J._Corman_Railroad/Pennsylvania_Lines" title="R.J. Corman Railroad/Pennsylvania Lines">Clearfield Cluster</a>". NS's export of <a href="/wiki/West_Virginia" title="West Virginia">West Virginia</a> <a href="/wiki/Bituminous_coal" title="Bituminous coal">bituminous coal</a> begins transport on portions of the well-engineered former <a href="/wiki/Virginian_Railway" title="Virginian Railway">Virginian Railway</a> and the former N&amp;W double-tracked line in Eastern Virginia to its <a href="/wiki/Lambert%27s_Point" title="Lambert's Point">Lambert's Point</a> <a href="/wiki/Coal_pier" title="Coal pier">coal pier</a> on <a href="/wiki/Hampton_Roads" title="Hampton Roads">Hampton Roads</a> at Norfolk. Coal transported by NS is thus exported to <a href="/wiki/Steel_mill" title="Steel mill">steel mills</a> and <a href="/wiki/Power_station" title="Power station">power plants</a> around the world. The company is also a major transporter of auto parts and completed vehicles. It operates <a href="/wiki/Intermodal_freight_transport" title="Intermodal freight transport">intermodal</a> container and TOFC (trailer on flat car) trains, some in conjunction with other railways. NS was the first railway to employ <a href="/wiki/Roadrailer" title="Roadrailer">roadrailers</a> (highway truck trailers with interchangeable wheel sets).
</p><p>Norfolk Southern is the namesake and leading subsidiary of Norfolk Southern Corporation, also based in Norfolk. Norfolk Southern Corporation was incorporated on July 23, 1980, in the Commonwealth of Virginia and is publicly traded on the New York Stock Exchange (NYSE) under the symbol <i>NSC</i>. The primary business function of Norfolk Southern Corporation is the rail transportation of raw materials, intermediate products, and finished goods across the Southeast, East, and Midwest United States. The corporation further facilitates transport to the remainder of the United States through interchange with other rail carriers while also serving overseas transport needs by serving several Atlantic and Gulf Coast ports. As of April 10, 2019, Norfolk Southern Corporation's total public stock value was slightly over $51.6 billion.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p><p>On December 12, 2018, Norfolk Southern announced that it would be relocating its headquarters to <a href="/wiki/Atlanta" title="Atlanta">Atlanta, Georgia</a>; leaving its hometown of Norfolk after 38 years. The move is expected to be completed by the year 2021.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>

</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(1)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Norfolk_Southern_Railway&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History" data-section="1" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-1 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-1">
<p>The system began in 1982 with the creation of the Norfolk Southern Corporation, a <a href="/wiki/Holding_company" title="Holding company">holding company</a> for the <a href="/wiki/Southern_Railway_(U.S.)" title="Southern Railway (U.S.)">Southern Railway</a> (SOU, formed in 1894) and <a href="/wiki/Norfolk_and_Western_Railway" title="Norfolk and Western Railway">Norfolk &amp; Western Railway</a> (N&amp;W, formed in 1881). The new company was given the name of the <a href="/wiki/Norfolk_Southern_Railway_(1942%E2%80%931982)" title="Norfolk Southern Railway (1942–1982)">Norfolk Southern Railway</a>, an older line acquired by SOU in 1974, that primarily served <a href="/wiki/North_Carolina" title="North Carolina">North Carolina</a> and the southeastern tip of <a href="/wiki/Virginia" title="Virginia">Virginia</a>. Headquarters for the new NS were established in <a href="/wiki/Norfolk,_Virginia" title="Norfolk, Virginia">Norfolk, Virginia</a>. The company suffered a slight embarrassment<sup id="cite_ref-auto_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-auto-10">[10]</a></sup> when the marble headpiece at the building's entrance was unveiled, which read "Norfork Southern Railway". A new headpiece replaced the erroneous one several weeks later.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> NS aimed to compete in the eastern United States with <a href="/wiki/CSX_Transportation" title="CSX Transportation">CSX Transportation</a>, formed after the <a href="/wiki/Interstate_Commerce_Commission" title="Interstate Commerce Commission">Interstate Commerce Commission</a>'s 1980 approval of the merger of the <a href="/wiki/Chessie_System" title="Chessie System">Chessie System</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Seaboard_System_Railroad" title="Seaboard System Railroad">Seaboard System</a>.
</p><p>Norfolk Southern's predecessor railroads date to the early 19th century.
</p><p>The SOU's earliest predecessor line was the <a href="/wiki/South_Carolina_Canal_and_Railroad_Company" title="South Carolina Canal and Railroad Company">South Carolina Canal &amp; Rail Road</a>. Chartered in 1827, the South Carolina Canal &amp; Rail Road Company became the first to offer regularly scheduled <a href="/wiki/Train" title="Train">passenger train</a> service with the inaugural run of the <i><a href="/wiki/Best_Friend_of_Charleston" title="Best Friend of Charleston">Best Friend of Charleston</a></i> in 1830.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Another early predecessor, the <a href="/wiki/Richmond_and_Danville_Railroad" title="Richmond and Danville Railroad">Richmond &amp; Danville Railroad</a> (R&amp;D), was formed in 1847 and expanded into a large system after the <a href="/wiki/American_Civil_War" title="American Civil War">American Civil War</a> under <a href="/wiki/Algernon_Sidney_Buford" title="Algernon Sidney Buford">Algernon S. Buford</a>. The R&amp;D ultimately fell on hard times and in 1894, it became a major portion of the new Southern Railway (SOU). Financier <a href="/wiki/J._P._Morgan" title="J. P. Morgan">J. P. Morgan</a> selected veteran railroader <a href="/wiki/Samuel_Spencer_(railroad_executive)" title="Samuel Spencer (railroad executive)">Samuel Spencer</a> as president. Profitable and innovative, Southern became, in 1953, the first major U.S. railroad to completely switch to <a href="/wiki/Diesel_locomotive" title="Diesel locomotive">diesel-electric locomotives</a> from steam.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/City_Point_Railroad" title="City Point Railroad">City Point Railroad</a>, established in 1838, was a 9-mile (14 km) railroad in Virginia that started south of <a href="/wiki/Richmond,_Virginia" title="Richmond, Virginia">Richmond</a> — specifically, <a href="/wiki/City_Point,_Virginia" title="City Point, Virginia">City Point</a> on the navigable portion of the <a href="/wiki/James_River" title="James River">James River</a>, now part of the independent city of <a href="/wiki/Hopewell,_Virginia" title="Hopewell, Virginia">Hopewell</a> — and ran to <a href="/wiki/Petersburg,_Virginia" title="Petersburg, Virginia">Petersburg</a>. It was acquired by the <a href="/wiki/Southside_Railroad_(Virginia)" title="Southside Railroad (Virginia)">South Side Railroad</a> in 1854. After the Civil War, it became part of the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic,_Mississippi_and_Ohio_Railroad" title="Atlantic, Mississippi and Ohio Railroad">Atlantic, Mississippi &amp; Ohio Railroad</a> (AM&amp;O), a trunk line across Virginia's southern tier formed by mergers in 1870 by <a href="/wiki/William_Mahone" title="William Mahone">William Mahone</a>, who had built the <a href="/wiki/Norfolk_and_Petersburg_Railroad" title="Norfolk and Petersburg Railroad">Norfolk &amp; Petersburg Railroad</a> in the 1850s. The AM&amp;O was the oldest portion of the Norfolk &amp; Western (N&amp;W) when it was formed in 1881, under <a href="/wiki/E._W._Clark_%26_Co." title="E. W. Clark &amp; Co.">new owners</a> with a keen interest and financial investments in the <a href="/wiki/Coal" title="Coal">coal</a> fields of Western Virginia and West Virginia, a product which came to define and enrich the railroad. In the second half of the 20th century, the N&amp;W acquired the <a href="/wiki/Virginian_Railway" title="Virginian Railway">Virginian Railway</a> (1959), the <a href="/wiki/Wabash_Railroad" title="Wabash Railroad">Wabash Railway</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/New_York,_Chicago_and_St._Louis_Railroad" title="New York, Chicago and St. Louis Railroad">Nickel Plate Road</a>, among others.<sup id="cite_ref-trainsmag_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-trainsmag-13">[13]</a></sup></p><p>In 1982, the two systems merged and formed the Norfolk Southern Railway.
</p><p>The system grew with the acquisition of over half of <a href="/wiki/Conrail" title="Conrail">Conrail</a>. In 1996, <a href="/wiki/CSX_Transportation" title="CSX Transportation">CSX</a> bid to buy Conrail; Norfolk Southern, fearing that CSX would come to dominate rail traffic in the eastern U.S., responded with a bid of its own. On June 23, 1997, NS and CSX filed a joint application with the <a href="/wiki/Surface_Transportation_Board" title="Surface Transportation Board">Surface Transportation Board</a> (STB) for authority to purchase, divide, and operate the assets of Conrail. On June 6, 1998, the STB approved the NS-CSX application, effective August 22, 1998. NS acquired 58% of Conrail assets, including about 7,200 miles (11,600 km) of track, most of which was part of the former <a href="/wiki/Pennsylvania_Railroad" title="Pennsylvania Railroad">Pennsylvania Railroad</a>. CSX got the remaining 42%. NS began operating its trains on its portion of the former Conrail network on June 1, 1999, closing out the 1990s merger era. The Consolidated Rail Corporation (Conrail) was a 11,000-mile (18,000 km) system formed in 1976 from the <a href="/wiki/Penn_Central_Transportation_Company" title="Penn Central Transportation Company">Penn Central Railroad</a> (1968–1976),<sup id="cite_ref-trainsmag_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-trainsmag-13">[13]</a></sup> and five other ailing northeastern railroads that were conveyed into it, to form a government-financed corporation. Conrail was perhaps the most controversial conglomerate in corporate history. Penn Central itself was created by merging three venerable rivals — the <a href="/wiki/Pennsylvania_Railroad" title="Pennsylvania Railroad">Pennsylvania Railroad</a> (PRR, 1846), the <a href="/wiki/New_York_Central_Railroad" title="New York Central Railroad">New York Central Railroad</a> (NYC, 1831), and the <a href="/wiki/New_York,_New_Haven_and_Hartford_Railroad" title="New York, New Haven and Hartford Railroad">New York, New Haven &amp; Hartford Railroad</a> (NYNH&amp;H, 1872) — as well as some smaller competitors. In 1980, Conrail had become profitable after the <a href="/wiki/Staggers_Rail_Act" title="Staggers Rail Act">Staggers Act</a> largely deregulated the U.S. railroad industry. Norfolk Southern and <a href="/wiki/CSX_Transportation" title="CSX Transportation">CSX Transportation</a> have a <a href="/wiki/Duopoly" title="Duopoly">duopoly</a> over all east–west freight rail traffic east of the Mississippi River.
</p><p>In 2016, a proposed merger that had been months in the pipeline with <a href="/wiki/Canadian_Pacific_Railway" title="Canadian Pacific Railway">Canadian Pacific</a> was abandoned abruptly.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> The proposed merger would have seen the joining of two companies worth over $20 billion each.
</p><p>According to NS's 2018 Annual Report to Investors, at the end of 2018, NS had more than 26,000 employees, 4,100 locomotives, and 54,400 freight cars. At the end of 2018, the transport of coal made up 16% of the total operating revenue of NS, general merchandise (automotive, chemicals, metals, construction materials, agriculture commodities, consumer products, paper, clay, and forest products) made up 59%, and intermodal made up 25% of the total.
</p>

<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Pittsburgh_Line">Pittsburgh Line</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Norfolk_Southern_Railway&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Pittsburgh Line" data-section="5" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Coal_Through_the_Cut_(4933237722).jpg" class="image"><noscript><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2c/Coal_Through_the_Cut_%284933237722%29.jpg/220px-Coal_Through_the_Cut_%284933237722%29.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="330" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="2592" data-file-height="3888"></noscript><span class="lazy-image-placeholder" style="width: 220px;height: 330px;" data-src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2c/Coal_Through_the_Cut_%284933237722%29.jpg/220px-Coal_Through_the_Cut_%284933237722%29.jpg" data-alt="" data-width="220" data-height="330" data-class="thumbimage"> </span></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Coal_Through_the_Cut_(4933237722).jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Two NS trains heading east along the Pittsburgh Line</div></div></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Pittsburgh_Line" title="Pittsburgh Line">Pittsburgh Line</a> is NS's principal east–west line from the Northeast to the Midwest. Running from <a href="/wiki/Harrisburg,_Pennsylvania" title="Harrisburg, Pennsylvania">Harrisburg, Pennsylvania</a>, to <a href="/wiki/Conway,_Pennsylvania" title="Conway, Pennsylvania">Conway, Pennsylvania</a>, it once was the core of the <a href="/wiki/Pennsylvania_Railroad" title="Pennsylvania Railroad">Pennsylvania Railroad</a>'s (PRR) main line. An average day sees 60 to 110 trains of all types. The line is home to the famous <a href="/wiki/Horseshoe_Curve_(Pennsylvania)" title="Horseshoe Curve (Pennsylvania)">Horseshoe Curve</a>.
</p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Chicago_Corridor">Chicago Corridor</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Norfolk_Southern_Railway&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Chicago Corridor" data-section="6" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>At about 150 miles, this is the most direct route for NS trains between Chicago and <a href="/wiki/Fort_Wayne,_Indiana" title="Fort Wayne, Indiana">Fort Wayne, Indiana</a>. It has 16 <a href="/wiki/Passing_loop" title="Passing loop">passing sidings</a> and several stretches of double track.
</p>

<h3 class="in-block"><span id="Pan_Am_Southern.2FPatriot_Corridor"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Pan_Am_Southern/Patriot_Corridor">Pan Am Southern/Patriot Corridor</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Norfolk_Southern_Railway&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Pan Am Southern/Patriot Corridor" data-section="9" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>On May 15, 2008, NS announced that it would join with <a href="/wiki/Pan_Am_Railways" title="Pan Am Railways">Pan Am Railways</a> to create the "Patriot Corridor", an improved rail route between <a href="/wiki/Albany,_New_York" title="Albany, New York">Albany, New York</a>, and the greater <a href="/wiki/Boston" title="Boston">Boston, Massachusetts</a>, area.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> On March 12, 2009, STB approved the deal.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> Each of the two companies now owns 50% of a new company known as <a href="/wiki/Pan_Am_Southern" title="Pan Am Southern">Pan Am Southern</a> (PAS). PAR's trackage between <a href="/wiki/Ayer,_Massachusetts" title="Ayer, Massachusetts">Ayer, Massachusetts</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Mechanicville,_New_York" title="Mechanicville, New York">Mechanicville, New York</a>, was transferred to PAS, and continues to be operated and maintained by PAR's Springfield Terminal Railway Company subsidiary. NS transferred to PAS cash and property valued at $140 million.
</p><p>Planned improvements to the route include upgrades to tracks and signals and new automotive and intermodal terminals.
</p>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(4)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Yards_and_facilities">Yards and facilities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Norfolk_Southern_Railway&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Yards and facilities" data-section="10" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-4 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-4">

<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/List_of_Norfolk_Southern_Railway_lines" title="List of Norfolk Southern Railway lines">List of Norfolk Southern Railway lines</a></div>
<p>Largely an eastern U.S. railway, NS directly owns and operates 35,600 miles (57,300 kilometers) of track in 22 states. It operates four primary hubs in its system: <a href="/wiki/Harrisburg,_Pennsylvania" title="Harrisburg, Pennsylvania">Harrisburg, Pennsylvania</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chicago" title="Chicago">Chicago</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Atlanta" title="Atlanta">Atlanta</a> and maintains facilities across the Eastern US to facilitate operations, including <a href="/wiki/Classification_yard" title="Classification yard">rail classification yards</a>, intermodal yards, and locomotive shops
</p><p>NS has rights to operate its trains with its own crews on competing railroads' tracks. These <a href="/wiki/Arrangements_between_railroads" title="Arrangements between railroads">trackage rights</a> permit NS to operate as far west as <a href="/wiki/Dallas" title="Dallas">Dallas, Texas</a>, on BNSF Railway tracks, as far north as <a href="/wiki/Waterville,_Maine" title="Waterville, Maine">Waterville, Maine</a>, and as far south as <a href="/wiki/Miami" title="Miami">Miami, Florida</a>, on the Florida East Coast Railway tracks. NS locomotives also occasionally operate on competitors' tracks throughout the U.S. and <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a> due to the practice of locomotive leasing and sharing undertaken by the Class I railroads. Not including second, third, and fourth main line trackage, yards, and sidings, NS directly operates 19,420 miles (31,250 kilometres) of track. In addition, NS has direct control over approximately 35,600 miles (57,300 kilometers).
</p>

</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(5)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Environmental_record">Environmental record</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Norfolk_Southern_Railway&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Environmental record" data-section="14" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-5 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-5">
<p>On January 6, 2008, a <a href="/wiki/Graniteville_train_crash" title="Graniteville train crash">NS derailment</a> resulted in a large amount of chlorine and diesel fuel being released into nearby waterways in <a href="/wiki/Graniteville,_South_Carolina" title="Graniteville, South Carolina">Graniteville, South Carolina</a>. In addition, a toxic cloud covered the city resulting in the town being evacuated. Federal <a href="/wiki/Common_carrier" title="Common carrier">common carrier</a> laws prevent railroads from refusing to transport chlorine and similar Toxic Inhalation Hazard (TIH) commodities. Local wildlife was killed, many of the local crops and vegetation were contaminated or killed, nine human deaths were reported, and thousands were injured.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> The company is being taken to court and being fined for violating the <a href="/wiki/Clean_Water_Act" title="Clean Water Act">Clean Water Act</a> and the Federal Superfund law. NS has spent a total of $26 million for the cleanup.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p><p>In early spring of 2008, the state program manager for air quality planning in Georgia, Jimmy Johnston, had been talking to NS about voluntary upgrades to reduce the company's environmental impact. NS is upgrading 3,800 of its locomotives with new technology that is 73 percent more efficient than previous models. The new technology being put into the locomotives is making the ride more fuel efficient and reducing idle time.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p><p>NS has also introduced an experimental battery-electric <a href="/wiki/Switcher" title="Switcher">switcher</a> locomotive, <a href="/wiki/Altoona_Works_BP4" title="Altoona Works BP4">NS 999</a>. This prototype locomotive was developed by Norfolk Southern, in collaboration with the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Energy" title="United States Department of Energy">United States Department of Energy</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Federal_Railroad_Administration" title="Federal Railroad Administration">Federal Railroad Administration</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Pennsylvania_State_University" title="Pennsylvania State University">Pennsylvania State University</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(6)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Labor_Relations">Labor Relations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Norfolk_Southern_Railway&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Labor Relations" data-section="15" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-6 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-6">
<p>In 2019, Norfolk Southern made a plan to lay off over 3,500 workers as a result of <a href="/wiki/Precision_railroading" title="Precision railroading">precision railroading</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(7)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Locomotives_and_rolling_stock">Locomotives and rolling stock</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Norfolk_Southern_Railway&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Locomotives and rolling stock" data-section="16" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-7 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-7">
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Paint_and_colors">Paint and colors</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Norfolk_Southern_Railway&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Paint and colors" data-section="17" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>NS's locomotives are often called "catfish" by railfans, as the stripes are said to look like catfish whiskers.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p><p>The current "Horsehead" paint scheme for NS locomotives is black and white, with yellow grab irons and step-edge highlights. Locomotives feature a rearing horse decal enclosed in the "catfish" stripes on both the nose and rear, which is consistent with marketing campaigns where NS has billed itself as "The Thoroughbred of Transportation".
</p><p>The first few <a href="/wiki/GE_AC44C6M" title="GE AC44C6M">AC44C6Ms</a> features a special version of the Horsehead scheme, which is painted for the D.C. To A.C. Project. The others retain the regular paint job. The GE AC44C6Ms are rebuilt from GE Dash 9-40Cs.
</p><p>In 1994, <a href="/wiki/EMD_GP59" title="EMD GP59">EMD GP59</a> No. 4610 was painted in predecessor Southern colors of green and white with gold trim and was a favorite of railfans.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> The locomotive was repainted in the Norfolk Southern "Operation Lifesaver" paint in February 2012 because of ES44AC #8099 also being in Southern paint.
</p><p>Norfolk Southern painted 20 new-order <a href="/wiki/GE_Evolution_Series" title="GE Evolution Series">ES44ACs</a> and <a href="/wiki/EMD_SD70_series" title="EMD SD70 series">SD70ACes</a> in commemorative heritage paint schemes as part of NS's 30th anniversary celebration in 2012 (more info below in the "Heritage Schemes" section).
</p><p>Norfolk Southern also has many locomotives painted in various versions of the Operation Lifesaver scheme.
</p><p>In February 2015, Norfolk Southern unveiled restored NS 3170 in the Southern Railway "Tuxedo" paint scheme. The 3170 is a <a href="/wiki/EMD_SD40" title="EMD SD40">SD40</a>, the first ordered by the Southern Railway and was retired by Norfolk Southern in 2007. In September 2015, <a href="/wiki/EMD_SD45-2" title="EMD SD45-2">SD45-2</a> 1700 was unveiled wearing its original Erie Lackawanna paint. Another SD40, NS 1580, was set aside for repaint into its original Norfolk and Western paint scheme; however, as of January 2017, it has yet to be repainted.
</p><p>In January 2015, the first of the state-funded "ECO Class" units – painted in a two-tone green, white, and black paint scheme – was completed. "ECO" locomotives thus far (January 2017) include <a href="/wiki/Norfolk_Southern_GP33ECO" title="Norfolk Southern GP33ECO">GP33ECO</a> and SD33ECO; additionally, these units come with 'slug' types: RP-M4C (GP33ECO) and RPU6D (SD33ECO).
</p><p>In November 2011, Norfolk Southern unveiled <a href="/wiki/EMD_SD60" title="EMD SD60">SD60E</a> 6920 – painted in a blue, red, white and black "Honoring our Veterans" paint scheme. In March 2013, Norfolk Southern released NS SD60E 6963, which was painted in a special paint scheme for "GORAIL." In May 2015, Norfolk Southern unveiled another SD60E, number 911 – painted in a red, white, and gold, "Honoring First Responders" paint scheme.
</p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Locomotives">Locomotives</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Norfolk_Southern_Railway&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Locomotives" data-section="18" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>A large majority of Norfolk Southern's locomotives come from the company's many predecessors, which include but are not limited to <a href="/wiki/Norfolk_and_Western_Railway" title="Norfolk and Western Railway">Norfolk and Western</a>, <a href="/wiki/Southern_Railway_(U.S.)" title="Southern Railway (U.S.)">Southern Railway</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Conrail" title="Conrail">Conrail</a>. Of the engines from Norfolk and Western (NW) and Southern, many were equipped with high short hoods. Although these locomotives are aging, a significant number of 'high hoods' still remain on the roster as of January 2017. Norfolk Southern is in the process of getting rid of them by scrapping, rebuilding, or selling the many units on the roster and units that are stored.
</p><p>Historically, NS has only purchased <a href="/wiki/Direct_current" title="Direct current">DC</a> traction <a href="/wiki/Diesel_locomotive" title="Diesel locomotive">diesel locomotives</a>, and was one of the last North American AC-traction hold-outs aside from <a href="/wiki/Canadian_National_Railway" title="Canadian National Railway">Canadian National Railway</a>. In September 2008, however, NS placed its first order for new AC traction locomotives: 23 <a href="/wiki/GE_Evolution_Series" title="GE Evolution Series">GE ES44ACs</a>, numbered 8000-8023. In the years since, NS has purchased several more ES44ACs as well as over 150 <a href="/wiki/EMD_SD70_series" title="EMD SD70 series">EMD SD70ACes</a>.
</p><p>Beginning in 2012, Norfolk Southern began to take delivery of several types of older <a href="/wiki/Electro-Motive_Diesel" title="Electro-Motive Diesel">EMD</a> locomotives from various railroads and leasing companies, <b>including</b> 9 ex-BNSF "tri-clops" <a href="/wiki/EMD_SD60" title="EMD SD60">SD60Ms</a>, 6 ex-ATSF (BNSF) <a href="/wiki/EMD_SD75M" title="EMD SD75M">SD75Ms</a>, the remaining 12 ex-<a href="/wiki/Conrail" title="Conrail">Conrail</a> <a href="/wiki/EMD_SD80MAC" title="EMD SD80MAC">SD80MACs</a> owned by <a href="/wiki/CSX_Transportation" title="CSX Transportation">CSX</a>, a majority of <a href="/wiki/Union_Pacific_Railroad" title="Union Pacific Railroad">Union Pacific</a>'s <a href="/wiki/EMD_SD90MAC" title="EMD SD90MAC">SD9043MACs</a>, and more than 130 SD40-2's from First Union Rail, CIT Group, and Helm Leasing.
</p><p>Norfolk Southern is the only railroad ever to own SD80MACs and SD90MACs simultaneously. Norfolk Southern owns all of the SD80MACs and 100 of the SD90MACs from Union Pacific. Norfolk Southern also acquired 10 SD90MACs from CIT Group in exchange for 15 MP15DCs. The SD90MACs have been rebuilt into <a href="/wiki/EMD_SD70ACU" title="EMD SD70ACU">SD70ACUs</a>. The SD80MACs will eventually enter a similar rebuild program and retain their 20-710G3B engine.
</p><p>Norfolk Southern has a very large program for re-cabbing locomotives. NS has its own designed "Admiral Cab," which they use on their 'standard cab' rebuilds. NS has rebuilt <a href="/wiki/EMD_GP38-2" title="EMD GP38-2">GP38-2s</a>, <a href="/wiki/EMD_SD40-2" title="EMD SD40-2">SD40-2s</a>, ECO units, and many more with the Admiral Cab.
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Norfolk_Southern_locomotives.jpg" class="image"><noscript><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/97/Norfolk_Southern_locomotives.jpg/220px-Norfolk_Southern_locomotives.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="138" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="5078" data-file-height="3182"></noscript><span class="lazy-image-placeholder" style="width: 220px;height: 138px;" data-src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/97/Norfolk_Southern_locomotives.jpg/220px-Norfolk_Southern_locomotives.jpg" data-alt="" data-width="220" data-height="138" data-class="thumbimage"> </span></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Norfolk_Southern_locomotives.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A former Southern Railway SD40-2 with a new Admiral Cab (on the left), being passed by another NS train.</div></div></div>
<p>In 2015, Norfolk Southern began a program to convert aging and unreliable <a href="/wiki/GE_Dash_8-40C" title="GE Dash 8-40C">GE Dash 8-40Cs</a> into <a href="/wiki/GE_Dash_8.5-40CW" title="GE Dash 8.5-40CW">Dash 8.5-40CW</a> units (NS calls them D8.5-40CW). The few units that were upgraded included new cabs, rebuilt and modified engine, electrical upgrades and more. Due to repeated failures, the program was deemed unsuccessful in 2016, and ET44AC units were ordered to replace the un-rebuildable 8-40Cs.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:General_Electric_Locomotive_(16060095980).jpg" class="image"><noscript><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/37/General_Electric_Locomotive_%2816060095980%29.jpg/220px-General_Electric_Locomotive_%2816060095980%29.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="146" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="4592" data-file-height="3056"></noscript><span class="lazy-image-placeholder" style="width: 220px;height: 146px;" data-src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/37/General_Electric_Locomotive_%2816060095980%29.jpg/220px-General_Electric_Locomotive_%2816060095980%29.jpg" data-alt="" data-width="220" data-height="146" data-class="thumbimage"> </span></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:General_Electric_Locomotive_(16060095980).jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A trio of GE demonstrators for the <a href="/wiki/GE_Evolution_Series" title="GE Evolution Series">ET44AC</a></div></div></div>
<p>In 2016, NS bought 46 GE <a href="/wiki/GE_Evolution_Series" title="GE Evolution Series">ET44AC</a> Locomotives, also known as Tier 4 Locomotives, numbered 3600-3646. These are the first Tier 4 road engines purchased, and not immediately stored, by NS. They were purchased as the replacement for the <a href="/wiki/GE_Dash_8-40C" title="GE Dash 8-40C">Dash 8-40C</a> units, many of which are retired and/or scrapped. Norfolk Southern has an order for 34 more units, due for delivery in 2017. The first order was the first order of new locomotives from NS since late 2014, when <a href="/wiki/United_States_Environmental_Protection_Agency" title="United States Environmental Protection Agency">EPA</a> Tier 4 requirements were put in place.
</p><p>In 2016, Norfolk Southern began a rebuild program on the <a href="/wiki/GE_Dash_9-40C" title="GE Dash 9-40C">Dash 9-40C</a> units. The rebuild involved overhaul of the engine, emissions upgrades, a new cab (featuring GE Trip Optimizer, PTC, and NS Cab Signals /  Locomotive Speed Limiter), new electronics, DPU and ECP capabilities, increased weight, and an electric parking brake. Norfolk Southern plans on rebuilding all of the <a href="/wiki/GE_Dash_9-40C" title="GE Dash 9-40C">Dash 9-40Cs</a> (Tophats) and some of the <a href="/wiki/GE_Dash_9-40C" title="GE Dash 9-40C">Dash 9-40CWs</a>. The new locomotives are being classified as <a href="/wiki/GE_AC44C6M" title="GE AC44C6M">GE AC44C6M</a>.
</p>

<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/21st_Century_Steam" title="21st Century Steam">21st Century Steam</a></div>
<p>After the 1982 merger, NS President Robert Claytor retained the Southern Railway's steam excursion program begun in the 1960s by his brother, SOU president <a href="/wiki/W._Graham_Claytor_Jr." title="W. Graham Claytor Jr.">W. Graham Claytor</a>. NS initially used former <a href="/wiki/Chesapeake_and_Ohio_2716" title="Chesapeake and Ohio 2716">Chesapeake and Ohio 2716</a>, which had been modified and decorated as a Southern locomotive for the steam program; however the engine developed with mechanical problems in her fire box after less than a year in excursion service and was replaced by <a href="/wiki/Nickel_Plate_Road_765" title="Nickel Plate Road 765">Nickel Plate 765</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Locomotives_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Locomotives-35">[35]</a></sup></p><p>Merging with the <a href="/wiki/Norfolk_and_Western_Railway" title="Norfolk and Western Railway">Norfolk &amp; Western Railway</a> prompted the steam program to acquire and overhaul <a href="/wiki/Norfolk_and_Western_Railway_class_J_(1941)" title="Norfolk and Western Railway class J (1941)">Norfolk &amp; Western 611</a> in 1982, and <a href="/wiki/Norfolk_and_Western_1218" title="Norfolk and Western 1218">Norfolk &amp; Western 1218</a> in 1987.<sup id="cite_ref-Locomotives_35-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Locomotives-35">[35]</a></sup> These two locomotives and 765 joined the steam program veterans – <a href="/wiki/Southern_Railway_4501" title="Southern Railway 4501">Southern Railway 4501</a>, Savannah and Atlanta Railway 750, <a href="/wiki/Nickel_Plate_Road_587" title="Nickel Plate Road 587">Nickel Plate 587</a>, <a href="/wiki/Louisville_and_Nashville_No._152" title="Louisville and Nashville No. 152">Louisville &amp; Nashville 152</a>, <a href="/wiki/Atlanta_and_West_Point_290" title="Atlanta and West Point 290">Atlanta and West Point 290</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tennessee_Valley_Railroad_610" title="Tennessee Valley Railroad 610">Tennessee Valley Railroad 610</a>, and <a href="/wiki/St._Louis%E2%80%93San_Francisco_1522" title="St. Louis–San Francisco 1522">Frisco 1522</a> – for an extensive series of excursions throughout the late 1980s and early 1990s.<sup id="cite_ref-Locomotives_35-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Locomotives-35">[35]</a></sup></p><p>Norfolk Southern's management under David R. Goode was forced to end the program in late 1994 citing safety concerns, rising insurance costs, the expense of maintaining the steam locomotives, and decreasing rail network availability due to a surge in freight traffic.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> On December 3, 1994, the 611 became the last steam locomotive running on Norfolk Southern's trackage, running her last steam-powered excursion round-trip between <a href="/wiki/Birmingham,_Alabama" title="Birmingham, Alabama">Birmingham, Alabama</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Chattanooga,_Tennessee" title="Chattanooga, Tennessee">Chattanooga, Tennessee</a>. After that, the 611 went on a three-day ferry move from Birmingham to <a href="/wiki/Roanoke,_Virginia" title="Roanoke, Virginia">Roanoke, Virginia</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Wrinn2000pp102-105_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wrinn2000pp102-105-37">[37]</a></sup> She stopped at <a href="/wiki/Atlanta" title="Atlanta">Atlanta, Georgia</a>, for the night on December 5 and next to <a href="/wiki/Salisbury,_North_Carolina" title="Salisbury, North Carolina">Salisbury, NC</a> the next day on December 6.<sup id="cite_ref-Wrinn2000pp102-105_37-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wrinn2000pp102-105-37">[37]</a></sup> Finally, the 611 departed Salisbury and continued her final trip.<sup id="cite_ref-Wrinn2000pp102-105_37-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wrinn2000pp102-105-37">[37]</a></sup> When the locomotive arrived back in Roanoke, 611 had its fire put out for the last time.<sup id="cite_ref-Wrinn2000pp102-105_37-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wrinn2000pp102-105-37">[37]</a></sup></p><p>In June 2010, Norfolk Southern that announced they would run excursions with Southern Railway 4501, <a href="/wiki/Southern_Railway_630" title="Southern Railway 630">Southern Railway 630</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Tennessee_Valley_Railroad_610" title="Tennessee Valley Railroad 610">U.S. Army 610</a> with their new <a href="/wiki/21st_Century_Steam" title="21st Century Steam">21st Century Steam</a> program.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></p><p>The program began in 2011 with excursions in the south powered by 630 and in the north by 765. On February 22, 2013, the <a href="/wiki/Virginia_Museum_of_Transportation" title="Virginia Museum of Transportation">Virginia Museum of Transportation</a> (611's owner) formed a campaign called "Fire Up 611!" to conduct a feasibility study with the goal of returning the 611 to active service and have it join the program.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> The locomotive was removed from her static display from the Virginia Museum of Transportation to the <a href="/wiki/North_Carolina_Transportation_Museum" title="North Carolina Transportation Museum">North Carolina Transportation Museum</a> in 2014 to be overhauled. That same year, TVRM completed their restoration of <a href="/wiki/Southern_Railway_4501" title="Southern Railway 4501">Southern Railway 4501</a> – joining the 21st Century Steam program for the 2015 season and pulling excursions in Tennessee, Virginia, and Georgia. The restoration of 611 was completed in May 2015 and celebrated with a run to <a href="/wiki/Roanoke,_Virginia" title="Roanoke, Virginia">Roanoke, Virginia</a>, where it was originally built. The 611 pulled several excursions in Virginia and was featured in special events at the North Carolina Transportation Museum. In December 2015, Norfolk Southern had concluded their program; however, the 611 continued to run various excursions, hosted by the Virginia Museum of Transportation and the North Carolina Transportation Museum instead of Norfolk Southern across the NS system in <a href="/wiki/Virginia" title="Virginia">Virginia</a> and <a href="/wiki/North_Carolina" title="North Carolina">North Carolina</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> Norfolk Southern currently limits the steam locomotives up to 40 mph (64 km/h) on their system.
</p>

<p>On September 3, 2007, NS launched a television ad featuring a family of gas cans trekking to meet a NS train, meant to underscore the railroad's role in reducing highway congestion. Shot in Virginia's Shenandoah Valley, it features the song "You Don't Need Me" written and performed for NS by <a href="/w/index.php?title=Ravi_Krishnaswami&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Ravi Krishnaswami (page does not exist)">Ravi Krishnaswami</a> of New York and <a href="/wiki/Steve_Kolander" title="Steve Kolander">Steve Kolander</a> of Atlanta.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> On <a href="/wiki/National_Train_Day" title="National Train Day">National Train Day</a> in May 2013, NS premiered a new ad series, using music adapted from "<a href="/wiki/Schoolhouse_Rock!" title="Schoolhouse Rock!">Conjunction Junction</a>" from <a href="/wiki/American_Broadcasting_Company" title="American Broadcasting Company">ABC's</a> <a href="/wiki/Schoolhouse_Rock!" title="Schoolhouse Rock!">School House Rock</a> series, and showing an overhead view of Inman Rail Yard in Atlanta.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup></p>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(10)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Awards_and_recognition">Awards and recognition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Norfolk_Southern_Railway&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Awards and recognition" data-section="24" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-wikimedia-edit-base20 edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-10 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-10">
<p>From 1989 to 2012, NS won the Gold (first-place) <a href="/wiki/E._H._Harriman_Award" title="E. H. Harriman Award">E.H. Harriman Award</a> in Group A (line-haul railroads whose employees worked 15 million employee-hours or more) every single year.<sup id="cite_ref-HttpwwwnscorpcomnscportalnscorpMediaNewsReleasesharrimanhtml_45-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HttpwwwnscorpcomnscportalnscorpMediaNewsReleasesharrimanhtml-45">[45]</a></sup> The award, which recognized the railroads with the lowest casualty rates per 200,000 employee-hours, was discontinued in 2012.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup></p><p>In January 2011, NS Chairman and CEO Wick Moorman was named Railroader of the Year by <i><a href="/wiki/Railway_Age" title="Railway Age">Railway Age</a></i> magazine.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup></p><p>George Vandergriff, along with Wick Moorman, were two of the honorary inductees of the inaugural NS Hall of Fame class.
